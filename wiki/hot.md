- **(NEW) Connolly, Ordóñez & Barker 2012:** 15p — [[connolly-2012-jdm]], [[Judgment and Decision Making (Field)]], [[Lens Model]], [[Policy Capturing]], [[Prospect Theory]], [[Framing Effects]], [[Decision Justification Theory]], [[Appraisal-Tendency Framework]], [[Signal Detection Theory (Decision)]], [[Naturalistic Decision Making]], [[Sunk Cost Effect]], [[Dynamic Decision Making]], [[Electronic Brainstorming]], [[Terry Connolly]], [[Lisa Ordóñez]]. *Handbook of Psychology 2012, Ch. 19. Comprehensive JDM survey. Core dual theme: descriptive (what people do) vs. normative (what they should). Key: [[Prospect Theory]] = dominant descriptive model; reference-point value function, loss aversion, decision weight distortions (low p overweighted). [[Framing Effects]] = same option in gains vs. losses domain → risk averse vs. risk seeking. [[Decision Justification Theory]] (Connolly & Zeelenberg 2002): regret = outcome component (counterfactual comparison) + process component (was decision justified?); process regret priming → better outcomes than outcome regret priming; regret salience reduces reason-based choice errors. [[Appraisal-Tendency Framework]]: fear → risk averse; anger → risk seeking; disgust eliminates endowment effect; incidental emotions carry over to unrelated decisions. [[Signal Detection Theory]]: ROC curve; threshold = f(base rate + error costs); drug-testing example: 1% prevalence + 90% test → P(user|positive) = 8.3%. [[Electronic Brainstorming]]: large EBS > nominal > F2F; production blocking = F2F failure; anonymity + simultaneous input = EBS advantage. [[Sunk Cost Effect]]: prior costs influence decisions; multiple theories (Prospect Theory loss domain, waste aversion, self-justification); may lump distinct phenomena. JDM paradigm shift: heuristics-and-biases waning; NDM/RPD for experts; emotion research expanding.*
- **(2) Tversky & Kahneman 1974:** 8p — [[tversky-kahneman-1974-heuristics-biases]], [[Heuristics and Biases Program]], [[Representativeness Heuristic]], [[Availability Heuristic]], [[Anchoring and Adjustment]], [[Base Rate Neglect]], [[Gambler's Fallacy]], [[Illusion of Validity]], [[Regression to the Mean]], [[Illusory Correlation]], [[Amos Tversky]], [[Daniel Kahneman]]. *Science 1974. Founding paper of the heuristics-and-biases program. Three heuristics for probability judgment: (1) Representativeness: probability assessed by resemblance → base rate neglect (engineer/lawyer study: 70/30 vs 30/70 base rates produce identical judgments when description present; uninformative "Dick" description fully suppresses base rate sensitivity), insensitivity to sample size (hospital study: small hospital has more >60% boy days; most say "same"), law of small numbers, insensitivity to predictability, illusion of validity (redundant inputs ↑ confidence, ↓ accuracy), regression misconceptions (flight instructor fallacy: praise→worse, punishment→better is pure regression, not causal). (2) Availability: frequency/probability assessed by ease of mental retrieval/construction → retrievability bias (famous names judged more numerous), search-set bias (words starting with R vs R in 3rd position), imaginability bias (committees of 2 estimated at 70, committees of 8 at 20; correct: 45 both), illusory correlation (clinical lore "rediscovered" despite contradictory data; Chapman & Chapman 1967/1969). (3) Anchoring and Adjustment: start from anchor, adjust insufficiently → wheel-of-fortune effect (anchor 10 → median 25; anchor 65 → median 45 for %African UN countries), factorial underestimation (8×7×…×1 → 2,250; 1×2×…×8 → 512; correct: 40,320), conjunctive overestimation (prefer .48 conjunctive over .50 simple; planning fallacy: 12 steps at 90% → P=.28), disjunctive underestimation (prefer .50 simple over .52 disjunctive; risk underestimation in complex systems), overconfident probability distributions (true values outside X₀₁–X₉₉ interval ~30% of time vs. expected 2%). Key cross-cutting finding: biases not motivational (persist with financial incentives); biases affect trained researchers; statistical principles not learned from everyday experience because relevant instances are not appropriately coded.*
- **(2) Fulmer, Gerhart & Kim 2023:** 11p — [[fulmer-2023-compensation-performance]], [[Pay-for-Performance]], [[Incentive Effect (Compensation)]], [[Sorting Effect (Compensation)]], [[PFP Intensity]], [[Equal Compensation Principle]], [[Pay Dispersion]], [[Pay Transparency]], [[Ingrid Smithey Fulmer]], [[Barry Gerhart]], [[JiHyun Kim]]. *Personnel Psychology 2023 (75th anniversary issue). 30-year review of compensation→performance research in PP/JAP/AMJ. Task 1: 39 articles; PFP dominates (26/39); theory diversity high but agency + goal-setting gone from 2010–2022; endogeneity stat approaches used in only 4/39 studies; quasi-experiments absent since 2010; 27/39 U.S.-only samples. Task 2: deep dive on PFP effectiveness. Incentive effect: +0.54 SD / ~24% in Kim et al. 2022 meta-analysis (82 ES, N=7,978). Sorting effect: Cadsby et al. 2007 lab experiment: sorting = ~74% of total PFP advantage; Lazear 1986 Safelite: +44% total productivity, 50/50 incentive/sorting split. Annual merit pay (3–4%) vastly understates real PFP intensity — promotion channel is 15% per grade, 48% more promotions per 1-pt rating over 6 years. [[Equal Compensation Principle]]: narrow results-only incentives → distorted effort (Novartis $678M DOJ settlement). Pay transparency: four dimensions; more ≠ better; above-median earners react positively, below-median negatively (Card et al. 2012). [[Pay Dispersion]]: PFP-based dispersion = legitimate + positive; politically-based = illegitimate + negative. Endogeneity = major gap; regression discontinuity, DiD, PSM, IV all recommended. International research = near absent. Future research: PFP intensity construct, holdbacks/clawbacks, merit pay, star performer pay, transparency asymmetry, multi-country samples.*
- **(2) Albarracín et al. 2024:** 10p — [[albarracin-2024-determinants-behaviour-change]], [[Behaviour Change Determinants]], [[Descriptive Norms]], [[Injunctive Norms]], [[Habits (Behaviour)]], [[Behavioural Access Interventions]], [[Social Support (Behaviour)]], [[Reasoned Action Approach]], [[Information-Motivation-Behavioural Skills Model]], [[Correlation-Intervention Gap (Behaviour)]], [[Dolores Albarracín]]. *Nature Reviews Psychology 2024. Meta-review synthesizing meta-analyses of individual and social-structural determinants of behaviour and their intervention efficacy across all domains. Individual targets ordered by increasing intervention efficacy: knowledge (negligible, OR≈1.11) < general skills < general attitudes < beliefs < emotions < behavioural skills < behavioural attitudes < habits (medium, OR≈2.65). Social-structural targets: legal/admin sanctions (negligible) < trustworthiness (negligible) < injunctive norms < monitors/reminders < descriptive norms < material incentives < social support (medium, OR≈2.53) < access (large, OR≈4.89). Core methodological finding: [[Correlation-Intervention Gap (Behaviour)]] — correlational effect sizes systematically exceed intervention effect sizes for every determinant; do not use correlational evidence to select intervention targets. Policy implication: shift from knowledge/attitude campaigns to structural access and social support interventions. US COVID vaccination campaign targeted general attitudes (wrong target); should have focused on access + behavioural skills + social support + incentives.*
- **(2) Yarkoni & Westfall 2017:** 9p — [[yarkoni-westfall-2017-prediction-vs-explanation]], [[Prediction vs Explanation (Psychology)]], [[Overfitting]], [[Training vs Test Error]], [[Bias-Variance Tradeoff]], [[Procedural Overfitting]], [[Cross-Validation]], [[Regularization]], [[Tal Yarkoni]], [[Jacob Westfall]]. +xref [[Replication Crisis]], [[P-hacking]]. *Perspectives on Psychological Science 2017. Central claim: explanation and prediction are statistically in tension — the best explanatory model ≠ best predictive model. Psychology confuses R² (training error) with test error (out-of-sample accuracy). P-hacking reframed as procedural overfitting — analyst capitalizing on sample noise is structurally identical to a flexible model doing the same. Three ML tools psychology should import: (1) Cross-validation — estimate test error without new data; nearly universal in ML, largely absent from psychology; (2) Regularization — deliberate bias (lasso, ridge) to reduce variance; lasso outperforms OLS when p is large relative to N; (3) Bias-variance tradeoff — total error = bias² + variance; explanatory approach minimizes bias, ignores variance; ML minimizes total error. "Decline effect" explained by small-sample overfitting + publication bias, not sloppier research. Big data as overfitting protection. Not arguing against explanation — arguing prediction-focused tools improve explanatory science too.*
- **(2) Nosek et al. 2022:** 11p — [[nosek-2022-replicability-robustness-reproducibility]], [[Robustness (Science)]], [[Replication as Theoretical Commitment]], [[Questionable Research Practices]], [[Prediction Markets (Science)]], [[TOP Guidelines]], [[Psychological Science Accelerator]], [[Simine Vazire]], [[Tom Hardwicke]], [[Anna Dreber]]. +xref [[Brian Nosek]], [[Replication Crisis]], [[Analytic Reproducibility]]. *Annual Review of Psychology 2022. 10-year review of replication evidence. Three R's: reproducibility (same data/analysis), robustness (same data/different analysis), replicability (different data). 307 replications: 64% success / 68% effect size ratio — massive heterogeneity (36%→90% across projects). Replication = theoretical commitment (Nosek & Errington 2020a); "conceptual replication" usually just generalizability test. Replicability predictable pre-replication: prediction markets r=0.52 / ML ≈ markets. Culture change: Rogers diffusion → 5 levels (infrastructure → policy), all necessary, none sufficient. Behavior change exponential (OSF registrations); published audit barely moved (2% data sharing). Key gap: no causal evidence that interventions work.*

## Key Insights

**KM:** Grant 1996 — 4 coordination mechanisms (rules, sequencing, routines, group problem-solving); *common knowledge* → [[Transactive Memory Systems]]. Barney & Felin 2013 — additive vs. emergent aggregation; microfoundations ≠ reductionism.

**Open Science:** OSC 2015: 36% replication rate; effects half magnitude. Nosek 2018: preregistration separates confirmatory/exploratory; garden of forking paths; [[Registered Reports]] = strongest form. LeBel 2018: 4 credibility dims (transparency, analytic reproducibility, analytic robustness, effect replicability). Munafò 2017: 5 reform categories; badges → 10× data sharing. Utopia II: [[Conflict of Interest in Science]] = ordinary psychology under misaligned incentives; solutions: [[Preregistration]], [[Open Workflow]], [[Paradigm-Driven Research]], PLoS ONE model. **Giner-Sorolla 2012:** [[Publication Bottleneck]] = root cause; too many researchers / too few slots → Anna Karenina conjunction rules → [[Aesthetic Standards in Science]] (3 criteria: statistical perfection, narrative conformity, novelty) → [[P-hacking]], [[HARKing]], replication suppression. 1970s solutions failed because they never touched bottleneck. Fix: open bottleneck (carrots: Nosek & Bar-Anan online structure; sticks: mandatory dissemination as IRB condition).

## Cross-Domain Connections

**Nosek 2022 batch:**
- Robustness (Science) ↔ Analytic Reproducibility: robustness = same data / different analysis; both upstream of replicability; LeBel's 4-dim framework maps all three
- Robustness (Science) ↔ P-hacking: fragile findings amplify p-hacking concern; multiverse analysis makes fragility visible
- QRPs ↔ Institutional Accretion (Micelotta): p-hacking/HARKing = accreted field norms via individual pragmatics (not strategic intent); displacement needed to undo them
- Replication as Theoretical Commitment ↔ Paradigm-Driven Research: both operationalize "hold constant except variable of interest"; systematic variation = testable theoretical commitment
- TOP Guidelines ↔ Institutional Isomorphism: policy = coercive isomorphism attempt; fails without normative support (COS levels 3–4 precondition)
- Prediction Markets ↔ Crowdsourcing Science: both aggregate distributed judgment; markets = crowdsourcing epistemic evaluation not labor
- Rogers Diffusion + COS ↔ Institutional Alignment (Micelotta): COS culture change = incremental embedding without displacing journal system = Alignment pathway
- QRPs ↔ Conflict of Interest in Science: QRPs = behavioral expression of Nosek 2012 "ordinary psychology under misaligned incentives"; 2022 adds survey quantification (7,887 respondents)
- 64%/68% aggregate ↔ OSC 2015 36%: gap = heterogeneity by domain/method; not crisis vs. no-crisis; personality replication (Soto) = 90%

**Yarkoni & Westfall 2017:**
- Procedural Overfitting ↔ P-hacking: same phenomenon, two framings — behavioral (QRPs) vs. statistical-learning (overfitting); Y&W framing connects p-hacking to formal theory with principled solutions
- Procedural Overfitting ↔ Institutional Accretion (Micelotta): p-hacking as accreted field norm reinforced by Bias-Variance Tradeoff considerations — each researcher rationally minimizes personal bias at cost of field-level variance
- Bias-Variance Tradeoff ↔ Preregistration: preregistration = high-bias/low-variance analytical commitment; constrains researcher df (↑ bias), reduces capitalization on noise (↓ variance)
- Cross-Validation ↔ Analytic Reproducibility (LeBel): CV = within-study out-of-sample check; analytic reproducibility = across-study same-data check — complementary credibility dimensions
- Overfitting ↔ "Decline Effect": gradual shrinkage of effect sizes as literatures mature = regression to the mean after selective publication of overfit small-sample studies
- Regularization ↔ Multilevel Modeling: MLM = regularized estimation with shrinkage priors; psychologists already use regularization without recognizing it as such
- Prediction vs Explanation ↔ Two Cultures (Breiman 2001): data modeling culture (explanatory) vs. algorithmic modeling culture (predictive); psychology = entirely data modeling; ML = algorithmic

**Giner-Sorolla 2012:**
- Publication Bottleneck ↔ Institutional Isomorphism: aesthetic perfection norms enforced via normative isomorphism (professional socialization through review/editing)
- Publication Bottleneck ↔ Conflict of Interest in Science: same "ordinary psychology under misaligned incentives" argument — Giner-Sorolla and Nosek et al. 2012 independently diagnose same mechanism
- Aesthetic Standards ↔ Motivated Reasoning: p < .05 rituals + narrative conformity pressure operate below conscious awareness; researchers don't experience it as dishonesty
- Publication Bottleneck ↔ Crowdsourcing Science (Uhlmann): both diagnose vertical integration as structurally unsustainable; crowdsourcing is one bottleneck-opening reform
- Aesthetic Standards ↔ Knowledge Retention: if the literature is the organization's knowledge repository, aesthetic-filtered findings = systematically biased knowledge base; open science = quality improvement program

**Albarracín et al. 2024 batch:**
- Correlation-Intervention Gap (Behaviour) ↔ Prediction vs Explanation (Psychology): same structural issue — correlational social science is optimised for explanation, not prediction/intervention; Yarkoni & Westfall's argument applied directly to applied behavioural science
- Correlation-Intervention Gap ↔ HARKing / QRPs: both reflect systematic distortions in how evidence is interpreted and used; policymakers using correlational evidence for interventions = epistemic version of HARKing
- Behaviour Change Determinants ↔ Microfoundations: individual determinants (habits, skills, attitudes) = microfoundational levers; social-structural determinants (access, norms) = macro levers; the debate about which to target mirrors the microfoundations debate
- Behavioural Access Interventions ↔ Institutional Change Pathways: access interventions = environmental restructuring that can produce Accretion (gradual accumulation of habitual behaviour) or Displacement (removing structural barriers for large-scale change)
- Social Support (Behaviour) ↔ Transactive Memory Systems: social support leverages interpersonal networks — same substrate as TMS; high-support groups may also develop stronger TMS
- Habits (Behaviour) ↔ Institutional Accretion: at the population level, widespread habit formation = Micelotta's accretion mechanism; micro-level automated behaviours aggregate to field-level norms without strategic coordination
- Descriptive Norms ↔ Institutional Isomorphism: descriptive norm interventions operate via mimetic isomorphism (modelling what others do); role models = normative isomorphism
- Knowledge (negligible efficacy) ↔ Aesthetic Standards in Science: the "knowledge deficit model" of behaviour change = the naive theory embedded in knowledge-based campaigns; Giner-Sorolla's point that simplistic narrative standards dominate policy just as they dominate science
- Reasoned Action Approach ↔ Motivated Reasoning: RAA assumes people reason about behaviour; motivated reasoning means attitudes often post-hoc rationalise behaviour rather than cause it — partially explains the correlation-intervention gap for attitudes

**Tversky & Kahneman 1974:**
- Base Rate Neglect ↔ Correlation-Intervention Gap (Behaviour): researchers selecting intervention targets via salience of correlational findings are applying representativeness heuristic (high correlation → "looks like" good target); gap is partly a cognitive bias, not just a statistical one
- Gambler's Fallacy ↔ P-hacking / Optional Stopping: intuition that a borderline p-value will "correct" with more data is a gambler's fallacy; drives optional stopping as a QRP
- Illusion of Validity ↔ Aesthetic Standards in Science: results that fit a compelling theoretical narrative produce illusion-of-validity confidence in reviewers and authors; Giner-Sorolla's narrative conformity criterion IS the illusion-of-validity at the field level
- Illusion of Validity ↔ Motivated Reasoning: internally consistent (redundant) evidence feels more compelling; motivated reasoners seek confirming evidence, increasing input redundancy, which increases subjective confidence while decreasing accuracy
- Regression to the Mean ↔ Decline Effect (Yarkoni & Westfall): literature effect-size shrinkage over time is regression at the publication level — initial extreme estimates selected by publication bias regress toward true effects as replication accumulates
- Anchoring and Adjustment ↔ Preregistration: preregistration prevents anchoring on observed results when crafting hypotheses; it removes the anchor
- Anchoring and Adjustment ↔ Planning Fallacy: conjunctive event overestimation from anchoring = the mechanism behind planning fallacy in project management; directly predicts schedule/budget overruns
- Availability Heuristic ↔ Publication Bias: published (available) findings inflate perceived frequency/size of effects; researchers and meta-analysts who rely on available literature are using availability heuristic on a biased sample
- Law of Small Numbers ↔ Replication Crisis: Tversky & Kahneman 1974 directly predicts (52 years early) the replication crisis — researchers over-trust small-sample results and grossly overestimate replicability; this is the availability/representativeness heuristic applied to research design
- Heuristics and Biases Program ↔ Procedural Overfitting (Yarkoni & Westfall): p-hacking as procedural overfitting = the statistical-learning reframing of the anchoring/adjustment + representativeness biases; two descriptions of same phenomenon from different disciplines
