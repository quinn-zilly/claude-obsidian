- **(NEW) Approaches for DEI Evaluation (practice brief):** 3p created — [[approaches-dei-evaluation]], [[DEI Evaluation]], [[Equitable Data Collection]]. Updated: [[index]]. CORE: three-goal framework for inclusive program evaluation. GOAL 1 ENGAGE STAKEHOLDERS: periodic team reflection on systemic inequity + personal biases; incorporate stakeholder feedback at design/enactment/dissemination stages; accessible participation (community locations, off-hours scheduling). GOAL 2 DATA QUALITY: sample reflects full population + subgroups; document analytic decisions + peer QC; watch for reference bias (social norms suppress honest ratings) and mischievous responders; disaggregate analysis to detect differential group impact. GOAL 3 COMMUNICATE ACROSS DIFFERENCES: multiple modalities/formats/languages; disaggregated reporting by race, location, age; honor cultural communication norms (vision wall for Native communities). KEY INSIGHT: DEI-informed evaluation is not just ethics — it directly improves validity. Unrepresentative samples = biased conclusions; aggregated-only analysis can hide harm to subgroups. - **(NEW) Singh 2008 (Job Analysis for a Changing Workplace):** 4p created — [[Singh-2008-Job-Analysis-Changing-Workplace]], [[Strategic Job Analysis (SJA)]], [[KSA Framework]], [[Competency Modelling]], [[Miles and Snow Strategic Typology]]. CORE: traditional JA rooted in Taylorism assumes static jobs, 1-person-1-job, stable KSAs — assumptions violated by dynamic orgs. Singh's SJA = purposeful, systematic, strategy-linked JA process. FOUR-STEP FRAMEWORK: (1) ENV SCAN — Delphi, scenarios, forecasting models, SME workshops; ask SMEs "day in the job of the future"; (2) CURRENT JA — standard JA + team-oriented analysis; team KSAs: coordination, conflict resolution, problem-solving, communication (Campion 1994); generic descriptors preferred; (3) GAP ANALYSIS — compare present vs. predicted future: *no/little difference* → traditional JA + "do nothing" viable (Mintzberg: don't over-reassess); *moderate difference* → iterative redesign; "soft" people skills long-term, technical skills closer to adoption; *significant difference* → SMEs define future behaviors + broad KSAs; (4) VALIDITY EVAL — continuous; link to selection/appraisal outcomes; SME disagreement is validity concern (Dierdorff & Wilson 2005). MILES & SNOW LINK: Defender → traditional JA (stable, narrow, top-down, Taylorist); Prospector → SJA (decentralized, broad skills, multi-skilled, empowerment). LEGAL: SJA defensible if systematic and non-discriminatory; business necessity doctrine; Chavez v. Coors (1999); courts flexible on new methods. SELECTION IMPLICATION: person-strategy fit > person-job fit; "loose fit" — hire adaptable; tight fit inhibits strategy change (Evans 1986). KEY PRIOR SJA WORK: Schneider & Konz 1989 (8-step; foundational), Snow & Snell 1993 (3 staffing models), Sanchez 1994 (assumptions critique + scenarios), Siddique 2004 (competency JA → org performance). - **(NEW) Shrout & Rodgers 2018 (Replication Crisis):** 4p created — [[shrout-rodgers-2018-replication-crisis]], [[Power Analysis (Replication Studies)]], [[Effect Size Heterogeneity]], [[Replication Types Taxonomy]]. Updated: [[Replication Crisis]], [[HARKing]], [[Questionable Research Practices]]. *Annual Review of Psychology 2018, Vol. 69. Review of replication crisis causes and reforms. Nine-question framework. THREE CRISIS TRIGGERS: fraud (Stapel, Hauser), QRP critiques (Ioannidis, Kerr, Simmons), OSC 2015 (36% replication, half effect sizes, social < cognitive). MAIN ARGUMENT: 36% rate is statistically expected — Maxwell et al. (2015) show this formally; standard power analysis is flawed because it uses original (inflated) effect sizes as fixed known quantities. EFFECT SIZE NONLINEARITY: d=0.4→0.3 requires 252 more participants; d=0.3→0.2 requires thousands more; taking midpoint of plausible range systematically underpowers. CORRECTIONS: safeguard power (Perugini et al.) from lower CI bound; predictive power integrating over effect size distribution; Bayesian predictive power using original study posterior. EQUIVALENCE TESTING (to claim no effect): requires 1,000–10,000/group (Maxwell et al.). MAXWELL RECOMMENDATION: replace single-study replication with multiple-study effect distribution estimation. META-ANALYSIS: random effects requires 20+ studies; guard against summarizing p-hacked literature; integrative data analysis for few replications. BAYESIAN: more natural (P(H|data) not P(data|null)); Bayes factors for replication (Verhagen & Wagenmakers 2014); works with small N if prior specified. EFFECT SIZE HETEROGENEITY is theoretically meaningful — model moderators, don't ignore variation; Westfall et al. (2015) crossed random effects; Van Bavel et al. (2016) contextual sensitivity. REPLICATION TYPES CONTINUUM: direct → systematic → conceptual; all informative; failed conceptual = ambiguous (original or context?). KEY REFORMS: preregistration + OSF (most important outcome of crisis); full disclosure of all results; multiple replications over single; sophisticated power analyses; badges (Kidwell et al. 2016). COLLATERAL DAMAGE: early career researchers, minority scholars, applied fields bear costs of policing culture. NHST HISTORY: Fisher (1925) α=0.05 not intended as universal standard; Neyman-Pearson combined → NHST; Fisher's concern was not whether results replicate but using randomization to equate groups. KEY CROSS-LINKS: [[Replication Crisis]] updated with more-sanguine view; [[HARKing]] + [[Questionable Research Practices]] cite Simmons et al. (2011) taxonomy; [[Publication Bias]] as mechanism inflating effect sizes used in power calculations; [[Open Science Framework]] and [[Preregistration]] as structural remedies; [[Meta-Analysis]] and [[Bayesian Statistics in Psychology]] as statistical tools; [[open-science-collaboration-2015]] as primary empirical referent.*

- Goal-Setting Theory ↔ Prospect Theory (K&T): both use reference points; GST's aspiration level = the goal as reference point; exceeding goal → satisfaction (gain domain); falling short → dissatisfaction (loss domain); BUT GST adds aspiration level as a movable motivational force (people reset higher after success); PT does not have this — PT reference point is typically current state, not aspirational
- Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting) ↔ Calibration (Judgment): both concern accuracy of confidence; overconfident self-efficacy on complex tasks → unsystematic strategy search (exactly Kanfer & Ackerman 1989 finding); calibrated SE = knowing when to switch from performance to learning goals
- Goal Conflict ↔ Institutional Change Pathways (Micelotta): organizational goal conflict = intra-individual version of Alignment vs. Displacement tension; when personal goal and org goal misaligned → incompatible action tendencies = micro-level version of institutional pluralism; Seijts & Latham 2000 alignment finding mirrors Micelotta's Alignment pathway
- Situational Interview ↔ Goal-Setting Theory: the interview works because it elicits conscious intentions/goals; Ryan (1970) premise (conscious goals = proximal determinants of action) is the direct validity mechanism; this is unusual — most selection instruments lack a theory of why they predict
- High-Performance Cycle ↔ Motivated Reasoning (Open Science): satisfaction-as-result-of-performance insight has a parallel in science — researchers satisfied with a significant result (reward) → commit to even higher-risk research practices → HPC for QRPs; the cycle is the mechanism by which Aesthetic Standards in Science (Giner-Sorolla) perpetuate themselves

**Cornelissen & Werner 2014:**
- Framing (Organizational Theory) ↔ Framing Effects (Connolly 2012 / Tversky & Kahneman 1974): framing effects = micro-level priming application of prospect theory; C&W show this is just one corner of a much larger multi-level construct
- Framing (Organizational Theory) ↔ Institutional Isomorphism (DiMaggio & Powell): normative isomorphism = transmission of institutional frames across organizations via professional socialization; mimetic isomorphism = adopting field frames from visible others
- Framing (Organizational Theory) ↔ Institutional Change Pathways (Micelotta et al.): framing contests are the agentic mechanism inside Displacement and Alignment pathways; cultural entrepreneurs = institutional entrepreneurs armed with framing theory
- Field Frame ↔ Organizational Field: DiMaggio & Powell's field is the structural arena; Lounsbury et al.'s field frame is the cultural-cognitive settlement that fills it; two constructs are complementary not duplicative
- Institutional Frame ↔ Institutional Logics (Thornton et al. / Micelotta): institutional frames = content/form of logics at the cognitive level; logics are the broader symbolic ordering system; frames are what actors actually activate
- Cognitive Frame ↔ Motivated Reasoning: motivated reasoning = systematic bias in which cognitive frames are activated and sustained; both describe top-down cognition distorting incoming information
- Cognitive Frame ↔ Heuristics and Biases Program: representativeness and availability = biases *in* frame activation; framing effects = gain/loss reference-point effects; both are consequences of top-down schema-driven cognition
- Priming vs Framing ↔ HARKing: HARKing is the product of motivated framing (post-hoc) presented as priming (a priori schema); distinction helps diagnose why structural interventions (preregistration) are needed
- Strategic Framing ↔ Behaviour Change Determinants (Albarracín): framing interventions = emphasis/issue frames that shift perceived salience of a determinant; selecting the wrong frame = Correlation-Intervention Gap at the communication level

- Sensemaking (Organizational) ↔ Knowledge Transfer: sensemaking failure (Weick) = inability to activate or construct a frame adequate to novel cues; tacit knowledge transfer gaps create same problem at organizational level
- Technological Frame ↔ Transactive Memory Systems: TMS assigns who knows what; technological frames determine what a technology *means* and what it's for; both are distributed cognitive representations that coordinate group behavior
