# Question

Discuss how I-O psychology leadership research has changed over the last several decades. In your response, consider the following: (a) describe how the role of “leader” has evolved; (b) specify newer or emergent theories of leadership that you deem relevant or important, and (c) choose one theory that you believe can help leaders improve organizational performance in the 21st century, incorporating research support.

## (a) Evolution of the "Leader" Role

- Across a century of I-O research, the locus of leadership has moved from the person to the process, and from studying leadership only as a cause to also explaining it as an emergent consequence ([[carton-2022-science-of-leadership|Carton, 2022]]). [[lord-et-al-2017-leadership-in-applied-psychology|Lord et al. (2017)]] organize this shift into three waves.
- **Trait paradigm (pre-1948):** leader = a person defined by stable individual differences (intelligence, personality) presumed to confer leadership across situations ([[lord-et-al-2017-leadership-in-applied-psychology|Lord et al., 2017]]).
- **Behavioral wave (1948–1961):** from *who leaders are* to *what they do*; Ohio State isolated initiating structure and consideration. Behaviors out-predict traits and mediate most of the trait–effectiveness relationship ([[derue-et-al-2011-trait-behavioral-leadership-integration|DeRue et al., 2011]]).
- **Contingency wave (1969–1989):** effectiveness reframed as a style × situation match; parallel cognitive turn recast leadership as follower perception (implicit leadership theories) ([[lord-et-al-2017-leadership-in-applied-psychology|Lord et al., 2017]]).
- **Relational/plural wave (1999–present):** leader is co-produced with followers (LMX, followership) and decentered from the single formal individual toward distributed, emergent, team-level leadership ([[lord-et-al-2017-leadership-in-applied-psychology|Lord et al., 2017]]; [[uhl-bien-et-al-2014-followership-theory-review|Uhl-Bien et al., 2014]]).
- **Net evolution:** heroic individual who *possesses* leadership → relational, distributed process co-constructed by leaders and followers and adapted to context ([[carton-2022-science-of-leadership|Carton, 2022]]; [[fletcher-2004-paradox-postheroic-leadership|Fletcher, 2004]]).

## (b) Newer and Emergent Theories

Emergent theories relocate leadership from the individual to social relationships, functions, and processes.

- **Shared leadership** ([[zhu-et-al-2018-shared-leadership-review|Zhu et al., 2018]]): emergent team-level lateral peer influence distributed across members; complements vertical leadership. Meta-analytically related to team effectiveness (ρ = .34), stronger for change-oriented content and complex work ([[wang-et-al-2014-shared-leadership-team-effectiveness|Wang et al., 2014]]).
- **Functional team leadership** ([[morgeson-et-al-2010-leadership-in-teams-functional|Morgeson et al., 2010]]): leadership exists to satisfy team needs across transition/action phases; 15 functions met by four sources (internal/external × formal/informal).
- **Leadership emergence** ([[badura-et-al-2022-leadership-emergence-integrative-review|Badura et al., 2022]]): distal–proximal process of being granted the leader role (informal + formal); gender a pervasive moderator.
- **Followership theory** ([[uhl-bien-et-al-2014-followership-theory-review|Uhl-Bien et al., 2014]]): following is co-constitutive of leadership; role-based ("reversing the lens") and constructionist (claiming–granting) approaches.
- **Functional leadership in diverse teams / LeaD** ([[homan-et-al-2020-leading-diversity-functional|Homan et al., 2020]]): diversity triggers intergroup bias or information elaboration; leaders match person- vs. task-focused behavior to the dominant process.
- **Adaptive leadership theory** ([[derue-2011-adaptive-leadership-complex-adaptive-process|DeRue, 2011]]): leadership as a complex adaptive process (see part c).
- **Caution on construct proliferation:** values-based forms overlap transformational leadership; authentic (ρ = .75) and ethical (ρ = .70) add little incremental variance, servant most defensible ([[hoch-et-al-2018-ethical-authentic-servant-vs-transformational|Hoch et al., 2018]]); split "lumped" constructs into isolable behaviors ([[carton-2022-science-of-leadership|Carton, 2022]]).
- **Critical lens** ([[fletcher-2004-paradox-postheroic-leadership|Fletcher, 2004]]): postheroic/relational leadership is gendered and power-laden; behavioral prescriptions alone risk co-optation.

## (c) Recommended Theory: Adaptive Leadership Theory ([[derue-2011-adaptive-leadership-complex-adaptive-process|DeRue, 2011]])

- **Definition:** leadership as a complex adaptive process of recurring leading–following "double interacts" that co-construct leader/follower identities, relationships, and group-level structures.
- **Corrects four limiting assumptions:** conflating leadership with hierarchy, one-directional influence, leadership as a personal attribute, exogenous environment.
- **Four patterns** (vary on magnitude + dispersion): centralized, distributed, shared, leadership void; groups shift patterns as needs change.
- **Requisite variety (Ashby):** adaptive capacity = variability in the leading–following pattern; dynamic environments reward fluid, distributed patterns over a fixed heroic one — the 21st-century case (knowledge work, complexity, teams).
- **Research support** (from adjacent literatures): shared pattern → team effectiveness (ρ = .34), stronger under complex/novel work ([[wang-et-al-2014-shared-leadership-team-effectiveness|Wang et al., 2014]]); sources differentially fit team needs ([[morgeson-et-al-2010-leadership-in-teams-functional|Morgeson et al., 2010]]); matching leadership to a team's active process improves diverse-team performance ([[homan-et-al-2020-leading-diversity-functional|Homan et al., 2020]]).
- **Hedge:** theory itself is conceptual; direct multilevel tests of double-interact/dispersion dynamics remain limited ([[derue-2011-adaptive-leadership-complex-adaptive-process|DeRue, 2011]]; [[zhu-et-al-2018-shared-leadership-review|Zhu et al., 2018]]).
- **Bottom line:** adaptive leadership is a better bet than fixed-style models because it treats leadership as a distributable capacity that shifts with team needs, and its core prediction has meta-analytic support.
