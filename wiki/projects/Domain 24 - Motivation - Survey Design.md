# Question

You are tasked with creating a survey to assess the extent to which the organization has created the conditions that motivate employees. In addition to popular theories such as goal setting and learning orientation, what are two other motivational theories you can draw on to create survey items? For each of these theories, provide three sample survey items you could build into the survey and how these items are related to the theory.

# Outline

## Theories
Criteria of a "good theory" (Cramer, 2013):
- Comprehensiveness
- Precision and Testability
- Parsimony
- Empirical Validity
- Heuristic value
- Applied Value


**Self-Determination Theory** [[ryan-deci-2000-sdt|(SDT; Ryan & Deci, 2000)]]
- One of the most prominent motivational theories in I-O psychology over the last 3 decades [[kanfer-frese-johnson-2017-work-motivation|(Kanfer et al., 2017)]].
- Made of six parsimonious "mini theories" that combine to form a comprehensive explanation and prediction of human behavior based on internalized motivation and psychological need satisfaction. The two primary mini theories are basic psychological needs theory (BPNT) and Organismic Integration Theory (OIT):
	- BPNT: The pursuit of the three basic psychological needs for competence, autonomy, and relatedness underpins much of human motivation and behavior, and their satisfaction is essential to well-being.
	- OIT: People internalize and are motivated by the values of others in their social context. Different forms of extrinsic motivation and context create different regulatory styles that promote or hinder motivation. Greater autonomous regulation of behavior promotes autonomy satisfaction and motivation. Regulatory styles range from
- Relationships between the psychological constructs and its antecedents/outcomes have been validated across numerous meta-analyses [[ryan-et-al-2022-sdt-meta-review|(Ryan et al., 2022)]].
- The parsimonious nature of each mini theory allows their constructs to be easily applied to survey design.

**Extended Job Characteristics Model** [[humphrey-nahrgang-morgeson-2007-work-design-meta-analysis|(JCM; Humphrey et al. 2007)]].
- The most well-studied work design model/theory in I-O psychology literature [[parker-morgeson-johns-2017-100-years-work-design|(Parker et al., 2017)]].
- The extended JCM is based on the Work Design Questionnaire [[morgeson-humphrey-2006-work-design-questionnaire|(WDQ; Morgeson & Humphrey 2006)]] which identifies a number of job dimensions across 4 broad work characteristics: (a) task, (b) knowledge, (c) social, and (d) contextual.
- Meta-analytic evidence suggests that several work design factors like autonomy, task significance, feedback from the job, and social support are strong predictors of subjective performance and job satisfaction [[humphrey-nahrgang-morgeson-2007-work-design-meta-analysis|(Humphrey et al. 2007)]].
- Work design provides an actionable lever for the organization to proactively improve motivational outcomes [[parker-morgeson-johns-2017-100-years-work-design|(Parker et al., 2017)]].
- Theoretically overlaps with SDT's basic psychological needs of autonomy and relatedness.

## Survey

### SDT Items
The following items are examples of questions measuring the three basic psychological needs identified by SDT. 

1. I have the freedom to make decisions about how to do my work (Autonomy).
2. I have good relationships with other employees in my organization (Relatedness).
3. I am able to do my best work in this job (Competence).

### JCM Items

1. I complete a wide variety of tasks as a part of my job (Task variety)
2. It's easy for me to tell if I have performed my job well (Feedback from the job)
3. There are people at my work who I can turn to for help when I need it (Social support)
