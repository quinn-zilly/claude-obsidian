# Question

Given a program (e.g., leadership development program), what is a step-by-step process you would use to build the program evaluation? Include theory and research to support your answer.
# Outline

## Step 1 – Clarify Purpose, Stakeholders, and Criteria

- Program evaluation is an enterprise for judging a program's worth, not a cumulative field of substantive findings; progress in evaluation comes from advances in conceptualization and method, so a defensible evaluation is judged on the quality of its design rather than on whether the program "worked" ([[Sechrest-Figueredo-1993-Program-Evaluation|Sechrest & Figueredo, 1993]]).
- Fix the purpose first: formative evaluation improves the program while it runs, summative evaluation judges its overall worth. The field drifts toward the easier, less threatening formative mode, so state explicitly which decision the evaluation must inform ([[Sechrest-Figueredo-1993-Program-Evaluation|Sechrest & Figueredo, 1993]]).
- Engage a representative set of stakeholders (leaders, mid-level managers, trainers, participants, and SMEs) to prioritize evaluation questions and secure buy-in ([[noe-2023-needs-assessment|Noe, 2023]]). Recruit and consult stakeholders who reflect the full participant population, including subgroups, and design the evaluation for accessibility ([[Bellwether-2020-approaches-dei-evaluation|Bellwether Education Partners, 2020]]).
- Anchor the criteria in the needs assessment: the organizational, person, and task analyses that justified the program define what it was meant to change and therefore what the evaluation should measure ([[noe-2023-needs-assessment|Noe, 2023]]).

## Step 2 – Specify the Program Theory (Theory-Driven Evaluation)

- Articulate the program theory, the causal model linking the program's inputs and activities to its proximal and distal outcomes ([[coryn-et-al-2011-theory-driven-evaluation-review|Coryn et al., 2011]]).
- Use the theory to evaluate not only whether the program works but how it works by testing the mediators and moderators it implies. This causal-explanation principle distinguishes theory-driven evaluation and lets the evaluator separate implementation failure from theory failure ([[coryn-et-al-2011-theory-driven-evaluation-review|Coryn et al., 2011]]).
- Strong theory partly compensates for weak method by improving treatment specification, measure selection, and predictions ([[Sechrest-Figueredo-1993-Program-Evaluation|Sechrest & Figueredo, 1993]]).
- For a leadership program, identify the specific psychological processes the intervention targets. Effects that operate through recursive processes (e.g., belonging, self-efficacy) can grow over time and depend on context, which informs what to measure and when ([[Walton-2014-Wise-Psychological-Interventions|Walton, 2014]]).

## Step 3 – Select Multidimensional Criteria

- Kirkpatrick's four levels (reactions, learning, behavior, results) provide a heuristic starting taxonomy for outcome criteria ([[alliger-janak-1989-kirkpatrick-levels-thirty-years-later|Alliger & Janak, 1989]]).
- Do not assume the levels form a strict causal hierarchy or a positive manifold: reactions are largely unrelated to learning and transfer, and intercorrelations among the levels are generally modest ([[alliger-janak-1989-kirkpatrick-levels-thirty-years-later|Alliger & Janak, 1989]]).
- Split reactions into affective and utility reactions. Utility judgments ("will this help me do my job?") predict transfer better than affective reactions, and affective "smile sheets" should not be used as surrogates for learning ([[alliger-et-al-1997-training-criteria-meta-analysis|Alliger et al., 1997]]).
- Treat learning as multidimensional (cognitive, skill-based, affective) and extend criteria to higher levels, from individual to team to unit or organizational performance, where the program targets them ([[bell-et-al-2017-100-years-training-development|Bell et al., 2017]]).
- Give each criterion construct validity: explicate the construct and use multiple operationalizations and methods to guard against mono-operation and mono-method bias ([[shadish-cook-campbell-2002-construct-external-validity|Shadish et al., 2002]]).

## Step 4 – Choose a Design for Causal Inference

- Where feasible, randomly assign participants to the program versus a control condition to support internal validity. A valid causal inference requires that the cause precedes the effect, covaries with it, and has no plausible alternative explanation ([[shadish-cook-campbell-2002-statistical-conclusion-internal-validity|Shadish et al., 2002]]).
- When randomization is infeasible, use a quasi-experiment with both a pretest and a nonequivalent control group; the pretest reveals initial group differences and makes selection-based threats (selection-maturation, selection-history, selection-regression) examinable ([[shadish-cook-campbell-2002-quasi-designs-control-and-pretests|Shadish et al., 2002]]).
- Prioritize control by design over statistical adjustment, and predict a coherent pattern of results that few rival explanations could produce. Strengthen the design with double pretests, switching replications, or nonequivalent dependent variables ([[shadish-cook-campbell-2002-quasi-designs-no-control-or-no-pretest|Shadish et al., 2002]]).
- Time the measurement to the theory: because recursive effects can emerge or grow at distal points, include delayed follow-ups rather than only immediate posttests ([[Walton-2014-Wise-Psychological-Interventions|Walton, 2014]]).

## Step 5 – Analyze the Data

- Protect statistical conclusion validity by ensuring adequate power and reliable measures and by reporting effect sizes with confidence intervals rather than relying on dichotomous null-hypothesis significance tests ([[shadish-cook-campbell-2002-statistical-conclusion-internal-validity|Shadish et al., 2002]]).
- Apply statistical adjustment (e.g., propensity scores) only after the best available design controls, and treat it as a supplement rather than a substitute for design ([[shadish-cook-campbell-2002-quasi-designs-control-and-pretests|Shadish et al., 2002]]).
- Disaggregate results by relevant subgroups to assess differential impact and the equity of outcomes ([[Bellwether-2020-approaches-dei-evaluation|Bellwether Education Partners, 2020]]).
- Measure the strength and integrity of the treatment so a null result can be attributed to theory failure rather than to weak or inconsistent implementation ([[Sechrest-Figueredo-1993-Program-Evaluation|Sechrest & Figueredo, 1993]]; [[coryn-et-al-2011-theory-driven-evaluation-review|Coryn et al., 2011]]).

## Step 6 – Judge Generalization and Support Use

- Assess external validity: whether the effect holds across participants, settings, leader levels, and time, including instances not directly studied ([[shadish-cook-campbell-2002-construct-external-validity|Shadish et al., 2002]]).
- Report findings in multiple accessible formats, disaggregated where appropriate, to communicate across stakeholder differences ([[Bellwether-2020-approaches-dei-evaluation|Bellwether Education Partners, 2020]]).
- Plan for use. Much evaluation use is conceptual ("enlightenment") rather than immediate and instrumental, so frame findings to inform ongoing decisions; consider prospective evaluation to forecast likely results before scaling the program ([[Sechrest-Figueredo-1993-Program-Evaluation|Sechrest & Figueredo, 1993]]).
