# Question

Describe the dimensions of organizational justice and how they are related to organizational outcomes. Be able to apply them to specific organizational issues in ways that will maximize justice perceptions among the people who are affected (e.g., implementing a new performance appraisal, conducting employee lay-offs, selecting students for a graduate program)? Additionally, be able to address how fairness may be subjective and how that affects approaches to justice.

# Outline

## Dimensions of Organizational Justice

- Organizational justice is subjective and descriptive (fairness in the eye of the beholder), distinct from philosophy's objective, prescriptive justice; the goal is to measure the effects of justice perceptions ([[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic, 2023]]).
- Justice = perceived adherence to a set of rules (descriptive); fairness = a global perception of appropriateness (evaluative), theorized as downstream from justice ([[colquitt-rodell-2015-measuring-justice-fairness|Colquitt & Rodell, 2015]]).
- [[Four Justice Dimensions|Four-dimension taxonomy]] ([[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic, 2023]]; [[colquitt-rodell-2015-measuring-justice-fairness|Colquitt & Rodell, 2015]]):
    - Distributive: perceived fairness of outcomes, judged against equity, equality, and need rules (Adams).
    - Procedural: fairness of the process, judged against process/decision control plus Leventhal's rules (consistency, bias suppression, accuracy, correctability, representativeness, ethicality).
    - Interpersonal: dignity and respect in treatment (respect, propriety).
    - Informational: adequacy of explanations (truthfulness, justification). Interpersonal and informational were originally "interactional" justice (Bies & Moag; Greenberg split).
- The four dimensions intercorrelate highly (avg r ≈ .49, range .37–.64), which motivates modeling an overall/latent justice construct that is often the more proximal predictor and stabilizes effect sizes ([[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al., 2013]]; [[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic, 2023]]). See [[Justice Measurement Taxonomy]].

## Relationship to Organizational Outcomes

- Justice is a content-based work motive: fairness predicts attitudes, behavior, and health for instrumental, relational, and moral reasons ([[kanfer-frese-johnson-2017-work-motivation|Kanfer, Frese, & Johnson, 2017]]).
- Justice predicts positive affect, satisfaction, commitment, trust, identification, OCB, and task performance, and reduces CWB, turnover intentions, and retaliation ([[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic, 2023]]).
- Primary mechanism is [[Social Exchange Theory|social exchange]]: justice builds relationship quality (trust, commitment, POS, LMX) that mediates justice → task performance and justice → OCB; justice → CWB is not mediated by exchange quality, suggesting a different (e.g., affective/deontic) path ([[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al., 2013]]).
- Employees care about justice for multiple, complementary reasons: instrumental/control, relational (group-value), moral/deontic, and uncertainty management ([[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic, 2023]]).
- Justice operates by source (foci), not only by type: organization-focused justice better predicts org-directed outcomes and supervisor-focused justice better predicts supervisor-directed outcomes, mediated by source-specific exchange (POS, LMX; [[rupp-et-al-2014-multifoci-justice-meta-analysis|Rupp et al., 2014]]). Whether source-matching improves prediction is contested ([[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al., 2013]], found it does not). See [[Multifoci Justice & Target Similarity]].
- Business stakes are concrete: unjustly handled layoffs produced wrongful-termination claims ~20% of the time vs. ~1% when handled justly ([[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic, 2023]]).

## Applying the Rules to Maximize Justice Perceptions

- Faceted justice is intervention-ready because it grounds fairness in specific, actionable rules; design each process to satisfy the relevant rules for the affected people ([[colquitt-rodell-2015-measuring-justice-fairness|Colquitt & Rodell, 2015]]).
- Performance appraisal: procedural (consistent, accurate ratings with an appeal/correctability path and bias suppression), informational (transparent standards and honest justification), interpersonal (deliver feedback with respect), distributive (rating matches contribution).
- Layoffs: procedural (consistent, job-relevant criteria and voice), informational (adequate, honest explanation of the decision), interpersonal (treat departing employees with dignity). Because outcomes are fixed, informational and interpersonal justice are the primary levers and reduce litigation.
- Graduate admissions: procedural (consistent, valid, program-relevant criteria; representativeness via stakeholder input), informational (transparent criteria and feedback), distributive (merit-based/equity).
- To keep evaluative processes fair across subgroups, the most effective bias interventions inhibit the manifestation of bias: making evaluators accountable (g = 0.80) and using structured evaluation (g = 0.51) support the consistency and bias-suppression rules ([[costa-2024-discrimination-interventions|Costa, 2024]]).

## Fairness Is Subjective and Its Implications

- Because justice is perceptual, managing perceptions matters as much as the objective procedure; the same procedure can be judged differently by different people ([[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic, 2023]]).
- People form global fairness judgments quickly from whatever information is available, and early experiences anchor a stable [[Fairness Heuristic Theory|fairness heuristic]] (primacy effect), so first impressions carry disproportionate weight ([[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al., 2013]]; [[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic, 2023]]).
- Reactions are asymmetric: "bad is stronger than good," so injustice evokes stronger, more differentiated reactions than justice (negativity bias; [[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic, 2023]]). See [[Justice–Injustice Asymmetry]].
- Approaches must account for subjectivity: attend to first impressions, communicate transparently (informational and interpersonal justice can shift perceptions even when outcomes are fixed), and treat cultural sensitivity as a moderator, since justice is a universal need but its interpretation varies across cultures ([[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic, 2023]]).
- Who is held accountable shapes the reaction; align interventions with the salient source of (in)justice ([[rupp-et-al-2014-multifoci-justice-meta-analysis|Rupp et al., 2014]]).
