# Training and Development Checklist

## Instructions

Students will produce a (single-spaced) 3-5-page training and development checklist with at least 6 items for every week of class. Make sure you capture the high impact factors in the readings and lecture for each week.

The checklist should include a cover page, and a short introduction (write it in a way that it reads as an introduction to a guide that you will be handing off to another training professional so they can use it). You should write your checklist in enough detail so that someone unfamiliar with the class can understand it. For example, don’t include a checklist item that simply says, “Include adult development principles”. Instead write out the 5 principles of adult learning and briefly define them in a way that someone else could use the items to assess if the quality of their training module.

You should have at least 6 items per week. Include a wide variety of references (at least 4 per week!) to the class readings for each of your checklist items. For each item, include references feel free just this once in your life to write out only the first author not all of them. (First author, et al., year). You can also draw some of the principles from class lectures if desired. References to class slides should be noted as: (Yost, Class Lecture, date). Make sure you don’t rely on only a limited number of sources (e.g., One reading, class lectures). The checklist should provide evidence that you have integrated material from several class readings.


## Introduction

This guide is a working checklist for designing and auditing training and development efforts across the full development cycle: strategy, needs assessment, design, transfer, evaluation, on-the-job development, and career transitions. Each section corresponds to a week of the course and lists concrete, assessable items drawn from the readings. Each item names the source behind it so you can return to the original research when you need depth. Use the checklist to plan a new program, review an existing one, or brief a colleague taking over the work.


## Week 1: Training Overview & Strategy: Training or OD?

*Readings: [[bell-et-al-2017-100-years-training-development]], [[yost-2013-experience-driven-development]], [[kanfer-frese-johnson-2017-work-motivation]], [[argote-2021-organizational-learning]]*

- [ ] Place the intervention on the training-versus-development continuum and confirm the choice fits the problem. Discrete, teachable knowledge and skill gaps fit formal training; capability that must grow through experience fits on-the-job development, since roughly 90% of leader development happens outside the classroom (Yost, 2013).
- [ ] Design formal training as a *catalyst* for development rather than the endpoint. Build in pre-work (boss and stakeholder conversations) and post-work (peer coaching groups, a follow-up session months later) so the classroom accelerates learning that continues on the job (Yost, 2013).
- [ ] Define the training criteria up front for every level the program is meant to affect (individual learning, on-the-job behavior, and team or organizational results), because training effects chain from individual learning to unit performance only when criteria are specified at each level (Bell et al., 2017).
- [ ] Check that the strategy reflects a systems view. Instructional design, trainee characteristics, and organizational conditions all shape outcomes, so a plan that addresses content alone will under-deliver (Bell et al., 2017).
- [ ] Account for learner motivation as a goal-directed resource-allocation process rather than a fixed trait, and specify how the program supports goal choice and goal striving through value, self-efficacy, and environmental support, since motivation governs how much cognitive effort trainees give to learning (Kanfer et al., 2017).
- [ ] Where the goal is organizational capability rather than individual skill, treat it as an organizational-learning problem and decide which process the intervention targets, whether search, knowledge creation, retention, or transfer, since organizations improve at very different rates depending on these processes (Argote et al., 2021).


## Week 2: Needs Assessment

*Readings: [[Singh-2008-Job-Analysis-Changing-Workplace]], [[approaches-dei-evaluation]], [[arthur-et-al-2003-training-meta-analysis]], [[smith-jentsch-2020-smart-investments-training]]*

- [ ] Conduct a three-part needs analysis covering organizational, task, and person analysis before designing anything, because training built on a comprehensive needs assessment produces larger effects than training without one (Arthur et al., 2003).
- [ ] For roles that are changing, use strategic (future-oriented) job analysis: scan the internal and external environment, analyze current jobs, and run a gap analysis between current and predicted future requirements rather than documenting only today's tasks (Singh, 2008).
- [ ] When the gap analysis shows significant change, specify broad enduring abilities such as adaptability, tolerance for ambiguity, learning agility, and problem-solving instead of narrow task lists, and select for "loose fit" so people stay useful as the strategy shifts (Singh, 2008).
- [ ] Before adopting an off-the-shelf program, benchmark its fit: compare the knowledge and skills it targets against your own job analysis, and check face validity using your trainees' actual vocabulary, tasks, and examples (Smith-Jentsch, 2020).
- [ ] Verify that trainees' prior knowledge matches the entry level the program assumes, because a program that worked for one population can fail when the starting knowledge differs (Smith-Jentsch, 2020).
- [ ] Build equity into the needs assessment: gather data from people who represent the full group of interest, ensure subgroup samples are large enough, and check for differential needs across groups so the analysis does not default to the majority's requirements (Bellwether Education Partners, 2020).


## Week 3: Training Design - Theory

*Readings: [[cmu-eberly-learning-principles]], [[kraiger-ford-2021-workplace-instruction]], [[kerr-1995-folly-rewarding-A]], [[frayne-latham-1987-self-management-attendance]]*

- [ ] Surface and check learners' prior knowledge, because accurate prior knowledge is a foundation for new learning while inert or inaccurate prior knowledge actively interferes with it (Eberly Center, n.d.).
- [ ] Organize and sequence content so connections are explicit through coherence, contiguity, and advance organizers, since how knowledge is structured determines whether trainees can retrieve and apply it (Eberly Center, n.d.; Kraiger & Ford, 2021).
- [ ] Engage learners in generating their own learning through retrieval practice and metacognitive prompts rather than passive review, since instruction that follows sound principles outperforms method choice or matching to supposed "learning styles" (Kraiger & Ford, 2021).
- [ ] Design practice for mastery in three phases (acquire component skills, integrate them, and learn when to apply them), and pair practice with timely, specific feedback, because the quality of practice matters more than the quantity (Eberly Center, n.d.; Kraiger & Ford, 2021).
- [ ] Teach self-management and self-regulation skills such as goal setting, self-monitoring, self-reinforcement, and relapse prevention, which causally raised self-efficacy and behavior change in a controlled field study of employee attendance (Frayne & Latham, 1987).
- [ ] Align the surrounding reward system with the behavior the training is meant to produce, because people rationally do what is rewarded; training for behavior B while the organization still rewards behavior A will fail no matter how good the design is (Kerr, 1995).


## Week 4: Training Design - Methods

*Readings: [[cheng-et-al-2019-diversity-training-myths]], [[arthur-et-al-2003-training-meta-analysis]], [[shukla-et-al-2024-transfer-online-training]], [[lacerenza-et-al-2018-team-development-interventions]]*

- [ ] Match the training method to the skill type: lecture and discussion for cognitive content, behavioral role modeling and simulation for interpersonal skills, hands-on practice for psychomotor skills. Mismatches measurably reduce effectiveness (Arthur et al., 2003).
- [ ] Prefer multi-method designs that combine information, demonstration, practice, and feedback over single-method delivery, since combined approaches produce the largest effects (Arthur et al., 2003; Lacerenza et al., 2018).
- [ ] For team-focused training, select the intervention that fits the deficiency, using team training for knowledge and skill gaps and team building or team debriefing for process problems in intact teams, and foster psychological safety so members can discuss errors openly (Lacerenza et al., 2018).
- [ ] For diversity training specifically, use a multicultural approach that acknowledges and appreciates difference rather than a colorblind one, frame it broadly so it does not read as blaming any group, and space it over time rather than delivering a single session (Cheng et al., 2019).
- [ ] Treat trainer competence as a design variable: trainers need professional knowledge of the content and of group dynamics plus genuine interpersonal skill, and visible leadership support raises effectiveness (Cheng et al., 2019).
- [ ] For online or technology-delivered training, assess and address digital literacy and computer attitudes such as anxiety, confidence, and self-efficacy before delivery, since these factors affect transfer in online settings but are absent from offline transfer frameworks (Shukla et al., 2024).


## Week 5: Training Transfer – Sustainable Change

*Readings: [[burke-hutchins-2007-training-transfer]], [[apa-learning-objectives]], [[soderstrom-bjork-2015-learning-vs-performance]], [[ellis-davidi-2005-after-event-reviews]]*

- [ ] Plan transfer across all three factor categories of learner characteristics, intervention design and delivery, and work environment, because only about 40–50% of training investment produces sustained change when any one of them is neglected (Burke & Hutchins, 2007).
- [ ] Secure the work environment for transfer through supervisor and peer support, opportunity to practice on the job, and a transfer climate that expects trained behavior, which are among the strongest predictors of whether skills get used (Burke & Hutchins, 2007).
- [ ] Write behavioral learning objectives that are observable and measurable using action verbs such as describe, demonstrate, or analyze, and avoid vague verbs such as know or understand, because explicit objectives signal the expected post-training behavior and support transfer (APA, n.d.).
- [ ] Use desirable difficulties in practice, such as spacing, variation, and interleaving, even though they slow acquisition and feel harder, because the conditions that make training feel easiest often produce the least durable learning (Soderstrom & Bjork, 2015).
- [ ] Do not treat immediate post-training performance as proof of learning; build in delayed assessment, since learning and performance are dissociable and easy practice can mask poor retention (Soderstrom & Bjork, 2015).
- [ ] Institutionalize structured after-event reviews that examine both successes and failures rather than failures alone, because reviewing both builds richer mental models and greater performance improvement than reviewing failures only (Ellis & Davidi, 2005).


## Week 7: Training Evaluation

*Readings: [[alliger-et-al-1997-training-criteria-meta-analysis]], [[smith-jentsch-2020-smart-investments-training]], [[alvarez-et-al-2004-training-evaluation-effectiveness]], [[avolio-et-al-2010-ROI-leadership-development]]*

- [ ] Distinguish evaluation (measuring outcomes) from effectiveness (explaining why outcomes occurred) and design the study to capture both, since outcome correlations are weak and hard to interpret without the effectiveness variables behind them (Alvarez et al., 2004).
- [ ] Do not treat affective reaction scores ("smile sheets") as evidence of learning; they correlate near zero with learning, whereas utility reactions ("will this help me do my job?") predict transfer better and should be collected as a separate measure (Alliger et al., 1997).
- [ ] Evaluate at the highest criterion level the decision requires, whether reactions, learning, behavior, or results, and remember that behavior and results criteria carry the most information yet are the most underused (Alliger et al., 1997; Alvarez et al., 2004).
- [ ] Rank the strength of your evidence honestly, from anecdote and reactions up to on-the-job behavior and organizational results, recognizing that learning shows what trainees *can* do, not what they *will* do (Smith-Jentsch, 2020).
- [ ] When effectiveness has declined, diagnose the cause systematically by checking for changed training needs, changed implementation, or changed transfer climate, rather than assuming the content simply went stale (Smith-Jentsch, 2020).
- [ ] Estimate financial return with a defensible utility model (RODI = N × T × d × SDy − C), using conservative effect sizes and roughly 40% of annual salary for performance variability, so development is judged as a capital investment rather than a discretionary cost (Avolio et al., 2010).


## Week 8: On-the-Job Development – The Person

*Readings: [[yost-plunkett-2009-derailment]], [[mccauley-yost-2021-comfort-zone]], [[charan-leadership-pipeline-ch1]], [[yost-2013-experience-driven-development]]*

- [ ] Identify the stretch experiences a person needs, such as unfamiliar responsibilities, creating change, high levels of responsibility, managing across boundaries, and dealing with diversity, and confirm the plan moves them to the *edge* of their comfort zone rather than far beyond it (McCauley & Yost, 2021).
- [ ] Build the meta-skills that let people learn from experience, namely feedback-seeking and reflection, along with daily habits organized around anticipating, adapting, and reflecting, because experience alone does not produce development (McCauley & Yost, 2021; Yost, 2013).
- [ ] Confirm that each leadership transition includes the required shift in skills, time application, and work values, because leaders who change their behavior but not their values create pipeline clogs and revert under pressure (Charan et al., 2001).
- [ ] Screen for derailment risk during transitions, where past strengths can become weaknesses, and watch the common patterns: failure to adapt, arrogance, blind spots, and avoiding stretch assignments (Yost & Plunkett, 2009b).
- [ ] Treat openness to learning as the core protective factor, and build self-awareness through 360-degree feedback and pre-move interviews so a person can name what about them will get in the way of where they want to go (Yost & Plunkett, 2009b).
- [ ] Use formal training to activate the manager's role as the primary developer and to help the person capture the lessons of their experiences, rather than expecting the classroom to carry development on its own (Yost, 2013).


## Week 9: On-the-Job Development – Organizational Systems

*Readings: [[yost-2014-leading-from-where-you-are]], [[yost-plunkett-2010-ten-catalysts]], [[beigi-2025-career-success-minority]], [[mccauley-yost-2021-comfort-zone]]*

- [ ] Design on-the-job development initiatives to spread virally by making them strategic, simple, sneeze-able, systemic, and sustainable, so they propagate through talent processes instead of waiting for a top-down mandate (Yost, 2014).
- [ ] Hardwire development into existing HR systems such as succession profiles that include developmental experiences, development plans that label activities as 70-20-10, and onboarding or transition checklists, rather than running it as a standalone program (Yost, 2014; Yost & Plunkett, 2010).
- [ ] Separate goal-setting from development discussions and require development plans whose completion and quality are tracked, because combined meetings crowd development out and unrequired plans go undone (Yost & Plunkett, 2010).
- [ ] Reward managers and leaders for developing people and for navigating critical experiences, since reward systems must reinforce development alongside performance or it will not happen (Yost & Plunkett, 2010).
- [ ] Audit access to stretch assignments for equity, because women and racial or ethnic minority managers receive fewer challenging assignments, and unequal access to development is itself an organizational-system failure (McCauley & Yost, 2021; Beigi et al., 2025).
- [ ] Check that organizational supports counter the visibility penalties minority employees face across networking, mentoring, role models, and developmental assignments, because more inclusive organizations weaken the link between minority status and constrained advancement resources (Beigi et al., 2025).


## Week 10: Career Development & Transitions

*Readings: [[mainiero-sullivan-2005-kaleidoscope-careers]], [[yost-plunkett-2009-transition]], [[charan-leadership-pipeline-ch1]], [[beigi-2025-career-success-minority]]*

- [ ] Treat careers as non-linear and relational rather than a single ladder: people weigh authenticity, balance, and challenge together, with one parameter dominant at each life stage, so retention efforts should address the parameter that currently predominates (Mainiero & Sullivan, 2005).
- [ ] Read "opt-out" and similar career exits as career-fit adjustments driven by advancement opportunity and job satisfaction, the same drivers as for men, rather than as a lack of ambition, and design flexible paths accordingly (Mainiero & Sullivan, 2005).
- [ ] Prepare people for the specific transition ahead, whether joining a company, moving into leadership, taking a stretch assignment, recovering from a significant failure, or living in a new culture, since each of these requires new competencies and giving up old ones (Yost & Plunkett, 2009a; Charan et al., 2001).
- [ ] Have the person contract with their boss on what success means and get to know key stakeholders before chasing early wins, since relationships enable the right wins and unclear success criteria are a common cause of derailment (Yost & Plunkett, 2009a).
- [ ] Use the first 90 days deliberately by setting a development plan and breaking long-term goals into 30–90 day deliverables that build credibility and momentum (Yost & Plunkett, 2009a).
- [ ] Account for unequal access to career resources across minority groups, including mentoring, sponsorship, networks, and visible role models, and build supports that close those gaps rather than assuming a level playing field (Beigi et al., 2025).
