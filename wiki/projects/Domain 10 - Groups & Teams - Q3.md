# Question

Doing research at the team level is different than at the individual level because of levels-of-analysis issues. Describe the additional methodological issues that must be addressed at higher levels (e.g., wording of the items, statistical combination of individual-level scores, statistical combination of individual ratings of the team). Include in your response a discussion of levels of analysis issues with both the predictors and criteria.

# Outline

## Thesis

Team-level research is difficult because a construct's level of origin, its measurement, and its statistical analysis must be aligned; misalignment produces [[Levels of Analysis Fallacies|levels-of-analysis fallacies]] and threatens construct validity ([[kozlowski-klein-2000-multilevel-approach|Kozlowski & Klein, 2000]]; [[klein-kozlowski-2000-micro-to-meso|Klein & Kozlowski, 2000]]). The added methodological work at higher levels is (a) typing each construct, (b) wording items at the correct referent, (c) selecting a composition model to combine individual scores, and (d) justifying aggregation. These issues apply equally to predictors and criteria.

## The core issue: levels of analysis and misalignment

- Findings do not generalize across levels except under restrictive conditions; a relationship can be stronger, weaker, or reversed at a higher level ([[kozlowski-klein-2000-multilevel-approach|Kozlowski & Klein, 2000]]).
- **Two mirror [[Levels of Analysis Fallacies|fallacies]].** Ecological (aggregate relationship generalized down to individuals; typically inflated) and atomistic (individual relationship generalized up to the unit).
- Root problem is misalignment of a construct's level, measurement, and analysis. Remedy is conceptual, not statistical: type every unit-level construct before measuring it.
- **Construct types ([[Global, Shared, and Configural Unit Properties|global, shared, configural]]).** Global originates at the unit level (team size, location); shared converges from members via [[Composition vs Compilation|composition]] (cohesion, collective efficacy, climate); configural captures patterns/dispersion via compilation (diversity, ability configuration). Type dictates how data are measured and combined.

## Wording of the items

- Shared and configural constructs are measured at the level of origin (the individual member) then combined; only global properties are measured directly at the unit level.
- **Item referent matters.** Self-referent ("I am confident I can perform this task") and unit-referent ("I am confident my team can") items capture different constructs; unit-referent wording generally yields higher within-group agreement (less within-unit variance).
- **[[Referent-Shift Consensus|Referent-shift consensus]] ([[chan-1998-composition-models-typology|Chan, 1998]]).** Shifting the referent to the collective before assessing consensus creates a conceptually distinct construct: team efficacy is not the mean of members' self-efficacy.

## Statistical combination of individual scores

- **Choose a [[Chan's Typology of Composition Models|composition model]] ([[chan-1998-composition-models-typology|Chan, 1998]]).** Additive (sum/average regardless of variance), direct consensus (agreement gives meaning), referent-shift consensus, dispersion (within-group variance is the construct, e.g., climate strength), process (higher parameters are analogues of lower ones).
- **[[Composition vs Compilation|Composition vs. compilation]] ([[kozlowski-klein-2000-multilevel-approach|Kozlowski & Klein, 2000]]).** Isomorphic emergence -> shared properties (combine by the mean); discontinuous emergence -> configural properties (combine to capture the pattern—min/max, variance, profile—not the mean). Isomorphism "may be the exception rather than the rule."
- **Justify aggregation ([[Aggregation Justification Indices]]).** Consensus indices ask whether the construct is shared per unit (r_wg, ≈ .70 rule of thumb); consistency indices partition variance and assess reliability of the unit mean (η², ICC(1), ICC(2), WABA). Choice follows theory, not a fixed rule.

## Statistical combination of individual ratings of the team

- When members rate the team itself as the target (e.g., the [[Team Diagnostic Survey]]; [[wageman-hackman-lehman-2005-team-diagnostic-survey|Wageman, Hackman & Lehman, 2005]]), each member is an additional estimate of the team's true score; composites average across both items and members.
- **Reliability of the aggregate.** Group-level reliability can be built from individual alphas via Spearman-Brown; agreement must still be established to treat members as interchangeable raters.
- **Watch the distribution.** Multimodal ratings signal subgroups (wrong level of aggregation), not merely low agreement ([[chan-1998-composition-models-typology|Chan, 1998]]). Some higher-order constructs deliberately have no internal consistency computed because their elements legitimately vary ([[wageman-hackman-lehman-2005-team-diagnostic-survey|Wageman et al., 2005]]).

## Levels of analysis for predictors and criteria

- **Predictors.** Type each predictor and specify the [[Cross-Level Models|cross-level link]]—direct effect, moderator, or frog-pond (relative standing within the unit). A purely between-unit predictor can only explain between-unit variance in an outcome ([[kozlowski-klein-2000-multilevel-approach|Kozlowski & Klein, 2000]]).
- **Criteria.** The only difference between a predictor and a criterion is time ([[cascio-aguinis-2011-criteria-concepts-measurement-evaluation|Cascio & Aguinis, 2011]]), so the same relevance/deficiency/contamination logic applies at the team level. [[Team Effectiveness Criteria|Team effectiveness]] is multidimensional and context-specific with no standard criterion set, spanning multiple constituencies and both tangible outputs and members' reactions ([[mathieu-et-al-2017-century-work-teams|Mathieu et al., 2017]]; [[salas-et-al-2009-wisdom-of-collectives|Salas et al., 2009]]).
- **Type the criterion too.** Team performance can emerge compositionally (average of similar contributions) or compilationally (pattern of differential, interdependent contributions) depending on workflow interdependence ([[kozlowski-klein-2000-multilevel-approach|Kozlowski & Klein, 2000]]). Performance constructs appear largely isomorphic across firm and individual levels ([[marshall-aguinis-beltran-2024-theories-of-performance|Marshall, Aguinis & Beltran, 2024]]), but homology should be argued, not assumed.
- **[[Multilevel Research Design and Sampling|Sampling]].** Testing cross-level relations requires many individuals nested in many units to avoid range restriction on the higher-level construct; shared constructs need within-unit homogeneity plus between-unit variability, configural constructs need units that vary in their pattern.
