# Question

What is competency modeling? What is job analysis? How do they differ from one another? Compare and contrast these approaches to understanding the nature of work. In your response, be sure to address the research-based strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

# Outline

## Job Analysis

- Job analysis (or work analysis) is a systematic process of gathering information about work activities (tasks, duties, responsibilities) and the worker attributes (KSAOs) required to perform them, producing a job description and a job specification ([[Garcia-Izquierdo-et-al-2015-work-analysis-personnel-selection|García-Izquierdo et al., 2015]]).
    - Data are gathered from SMEs (incumbents, supervisors) through qualitative methods (observation, interview) and quantitative instruments (task inventories, PAQ, F-JAS) ([[Garcia-Izquierdo-et-al-2015-work-analysis-personnel-selection|García-Izquierdo et al., 2015]]).
    - Two orientations exist: task-oriented (duties and functions) and worker-oriented (KSAOs); selection purposes favor a worker-oriented analysis ([[Garcia-Izquierdo-et-al-2015-work-analysis-personnel-selection|García-Izquierdo et al., 2015]]).
- Job analysis is descriptive and past-oriented, treating the job as an external object to be measured and describing the typical performance of the average incumbent ([[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2009]]).
- [[Sackett-Walmsley-Laczo-2013-job-and-work-analysis|Sackett et al. (2013)]] frame job analysis as a series of design choices (focus, specificity, data form, taxonomy, source, orientation) whose correct answer depends on the purpose; "one size does not fit all."

## Competency Modeling

- Competency modeling identifies a small set of broadly worded competencies (behavioral themes or critical success factors) tied to organizational strategy, mission, and values, and applied across jobs, ranks, and locations ([[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2009]]; [[Shippmann-et-al-2000-practice-of-competency-modeling|Shippmann et al., 2000]]).
- It is prescriptive and future-oriented, specifying the behavior an organization wants to encourage to execute strategy rather than describing current work ([[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2009]]).
- No consensus definition of "competency" exists, even among authorities in the field, yet adoption is widespread; 75–80% of surveyed organizations reported some competency-based application ([[Shippmann-et-al-2000-practice-of-competency-modeling|Shippmann et al., 2000]]).
- Job-level competency modeling overlaps heavily with worker-oriented job analysis (the difference can be "semantic"); organization-wide competency modeling is conceptually distinct: prescriptive, top-down, and often a change intervention ([[Sackett-Walmsley-Laczo-2013-job-and-work-analysis|Sackett et al., 2013]]).

## Key Differences

- [[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez and Levine (2009)]] contrast the two on six dimensions: purpose (describe vs. influence behavior), view of the job (external object vs. enacted role), focus (job vs. organization), time orientation (past vs. future), performance level (typical vs. maximal), and measurement approach (latent trait vs. clinical judgment).
- The approaches occupy different domains: job analysis is applied measurement, whereas competency modeling is closest to a mechanism of informal, strategic control ([[Sanchez-Levine-2012-rise-fall-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2012]]).
- On the SIOP task force's Level of Rigor Scale, job analysis was rated more rigorous on nearly every evaluative criterion (method of investigation, level of detail, reliability assessment, documentation), except linking to business strategy, where competency modeling was rated stronger ([[Shippmann-et-al-2000-practice-of-competency-modeling|Shippmann et al., 2000]]).

## Job Analysis: Strengths and Weaknesses

- Strengths: methodological rigor, reliability assessment, and documentation ([[Shippmann-et-al-2000-practice-of-competency-modeling|Shippmann et al., 2000]]); it is also the legally required foundation of selection, as any validity defense under the Uniform Guidelines must rest on a job analysis ([[EEOC-1978-uniform-guidelines-employee-selection|EEOC, 1978]]).
- Weaknesses: it ignores strategic variables ([[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2009]]) and assumes jobs are stable objects, an assumption weakened by team-based, boundaryless, and rapidly changing work ([[Singh-2008-Job-Analysis-Changing-Workplace|Singh, 2008]]; [[Sanchez-Levine-2012-rise-fall-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2012]]).
    - It is subject to social and cognitive sources of inaccuracy and faces a criterion problem: there is no external standard by which to confirm that an analysis is "accurate" ([[Sackett-Walmsley-Laczo-2013-job-and-work-analysis|Sackett et al., 2013]]).
    - Some interrater "disagreement" among incumbents reflects legitimate variance in how the job is interpreted and performed rather than error ([[Sanchez-Levine-2012-rise-fall-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2012]]).

## Competency Modeling: Strengths and Weaknesses

- Strengths: it aligns everyday behavior with strategy, provides a common cross-job language useful for succession and career development, has high face validity, and is future-oriented ([[Shippmann-et-al-2000-practice-of-competency-modeling|Shippmann et al., 2000]]; [[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2009]]).
- Weaknesses: definitional ambiguity ([[Shippmann-et-al-2000-practice-of-competency-modeling|Shippmann et al., 2000]]) and lower rigor on data collection, level of detail, reliability, and documentation ([[Shippmann-et-al-2000-practice-of-competency-modeling|Shippmann et al., 2000]]; [[Sanchez-Levine-2012-rise-fall-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2012]]).
    - Broad competencies are fuzzy composites that tend to fail construct-validity and interrater-agreement metrics; [[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez and Levine (2009)]] argue these are the wrong yardstick and that competency modeling should be evaluated by its consequences (its influence on strategic behavior) rather than by content validity.

## Synthesis: Complementary, Not Competing

- Competency modeling and job analysis supplement rather than displace each other; both belong in the HR toolbox, and the appropriate choice depends on the purpose of the analysis ([[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2009]]; [[Sackett-Walmsley-Laczo-2013-job-and-work-analysis|Sackett et al., 2013]]).
- To satisfy Uniform Guidelines job-relatedness requirements, organizations may need to supplement competency modeling with traditional job analysis ([[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine, 2009]]; [[EEOC-1978-uniform-guidelines-employee-selection|EEOC, 1978]]).
- Strategic job analysis offers a middle path: future- and strategy-oriented like competency modeling, but retaining job-analytic method ([[Singh-2008-Job-Analysis-Changing-Workplace|Singh, 2008]]).
