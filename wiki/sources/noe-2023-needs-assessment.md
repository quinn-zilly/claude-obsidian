# Needs Assessment (Noe 2023, Ch. 3)

Chapter 3 of Noe's *Employee Training and Development* (9e). The canonical practitioner-facing treatment of **needs assessment** — the process used to determine whether training is necessary, who needs it, and what tasks/KSAs it should target. Needs assessment is the first step of the instructional design process; if skipped or done poorly, training is misapplied, mis-targeted, or wasted.

## Core Framework

Needs assessment = three analyses, usually run in this priority but not strict order:

| Analysis | Question | See |
|----------|----------|-----|
| **[[Organizational Analysis (Training)]]** | Is training appropriate given strategy, resources, support? | Usually first |
| **[[Person Analysis (Noe Model)]]** | Who needs training? Is the gap a training issue? Are they ready? | With task analysis |
| **[[Task Analysis (Training)]]** | What tasks + KSAOs must training target? | With person analysis |

Person and task analysis run together — can't judge a performance deficiency without understanding the tasks and work environment. An initial org analysis may say "no budget," but if person analysis reveals a widespread strategic skill gap, leaders may reallocate funds.

## Why Needs Assessment Matters

A manager requesting "training" may really need motivation, job redesign, or clearer performance expectations. The assessor's job is to determine whether training is even the right solution. Pressure points ("causes") that suggest training — legislation, lack of basic skills, poor performance, new technology, customer requests, product changes, higher standards, job redesign, customer dissatisfaction — do **not** automatically mean training is the fix. See [[Root Cause Analysis (Training)]].

> [!example] Anesthetic-gas driver
> Driver contaminates a hospital oxygen supply. Cause could be lack of knowledge (→ training), anger over a denied raise (→ HR/discipline), or mislabeled valves (→ work-environment redesign). Only the knowledge gap is a training problem.

## Stakeholders

All stakeholders should participate: company leaders (strategic/holistic view), mid-level managers (unit financial goals, budget), trainers (buy-vs-build, admin), and employees (end users — is learning rewarded/accessible?). **SMEs** (subject-matter experts) supply task and KSAO detail. A **learning council** of executives + key stakeholders prioritizes needs (e.g., Vi, AARP). Job incumbents must be involved — they know the job best and can sabotage adoption if excluded.

## Methods (Table 3.2)

Observation, surveys, interviews, focus groups / [[Crowdsourcing Science|crowdsourcing]], documentation, technology (e.g., eye-tracking glasses at H&H Castings; call-center monitoring), and historical/data reviews. No single method is superior — **use multiple**. Benchmarking against other firms (e.g., ATD forum) also informs type/level/frequency of training.
