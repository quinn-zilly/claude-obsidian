# The Leadership Pipeline — Chapter 1: Six Leadership Passages
**Charan, Drotter & Noel (2001)** — Ch. 1 in *The Leadership Pipeline: How to Build the Leadership-Powered Company*. Jossey-Bass.

## Core Framework

The **Leadership Pipeline model** identifies six passages (transitions) that leaders must navigate as they grow from individual contributor to enterprise manager. Each passage requires changes in three dimensions:

1. **Skills** — new technical and managerial capabilities
2. **Time applications** — how time is allocated (longer time horizons, more delegation)
3. **Work values** — what the leader believes is most important to do

> [!key-insight] The hidden problem
> Leaders who complete a passage behaviorally but not in terms of *values* create pipeline clogs. They do the right things for the wrong reasons and eventually revert to the old level's behaviors under pressure.

## The Six Passages

### Passage 1: Managing Self → Managing Others
- **Shift**: from doing technical work to getting work done through others
- **New skills**: planning, filling jobs, assigning work, motivating, coaching, measuring others' work
- **Value shift**: must genuinely value managerial work, not just tolerate it — must see making others productive as mission-critical
- **Common failure**: high performers reluctant to give up the activities that made them successful; become managers without making behavioral or value-based transition

### Passage 2: Managing Others → Managing Managers
- **Shift**: from some individual contribution to pure management
- **New skills**: selecting first-line managers, assigning managerial work to them, measuring manager progress, coaching managers
- **Strategic shift**: begin thinking beyond function to strategic issues
- **Common failure**: people promoted here having skipped Passage 1 — still value individual contribution; hold first-line managers accountable for technical work rather than managerial work; poison the managerial well

### Passage 3: Managing Managers → Functional Manager
- **Shift**: leading areas outside own experience; communicating through multiple layers
- **New skills**: skip-level communication, valuing unfamiliar functions, team play with other functional managers, resource competition based on business needs
- **Strategic shift**: functional strategy + blending it with overall business strategy
- **Example (Tom)**: operations director struggling to plan beyond immediate requirements and losing touch with line workers — needs coaching and executive development

### Passage 4: Functional Manager → Business Manager
- **Often the most satisfying AND most challenging**
- **Shift**: from functional perspective to profit perspective; from planning to integrating functions
- **Value shift**: from "Can we do it?" to "Will we make money?" and "Is it sustainable?"
- **New demands**: balance short-term (quarterly targets) vs. long-term (3–5 year planning); use staff functions (HR, finance, legal) effectively
- **Common failure**: demeans or underutilizes staff functions → staff deliver halfhearted effort

### Passage 5: Business Manager → Group Manager
- **Shift**: from valuing own business success to valuing *other people's* business success
- **Four new skill clusters**: (1) evaluating strategy for capital allocation; (2) developing business managers; (3) portfolio strategy; (4) assessing core capabilities
- **Common failure**: still wants personal credit; frustrated they can't operate the businesses themselves → undermines business managers' authority or support

### Passage 6: Group Manager → Enterprise Manager (CEO)
- **Shift**: more about values than skills; reinvention of self-concept
- **Characteristics**: long-term visionary thinking + quarter-by-quarter operating mechanisms; external sensitivity; 3–4 high-leverage decisions annually
- **"Letting go"**: must release the pieces (individual products/customers) to focus on the whole
- **Common failure**: CEO fails to view this as a necessary passage; continues running the enterprise as a portfolio of businesses

## Pipeline Clogs

When a leader skips a passage or doesn't complete it fully:
- They bring the wrong skills, time applications, and values to the new level
- They hold subordinates accountable for the wrong things (e.g., technical work instead of managerial work)
- They pass wrong values down the chain — contaminating the pipeline

> [!key-insight] Clogged pipelines cascade
> A clogged passage doesn't just hurt that leader — it degrades performance of everyone below. A CEO who skipped passages diminishes leaders all the way down the line.

## Benefits of the Pipeline Model

1. Reduces emotional stress — pipeline makes skipping passages unlikely (people are placed when ready)
2. Right speed — prevents both ticket-punching (too fast) and career stagnation (too slow)
3. Reduces time to prepare top leaders — no wasted time on duplicate-skill jobs
4. Creates stars from within — don't need external "star" hires to prime the pump

## Application to Small Businesses
- Fewer than 20 people: Passage 1 variant (managing self → owner)
- Growth: adds Passage 2 equivalent
- Group manager level often doesn't exist; absorbed by business manager or functional manager
- Same principles apply; passages compress

## FAQ Highlights
- Model covers ~80–85% of positions; corporate specialists may not fit
- Competency models should be differentiated by layer — most aren't; "one size fits all" is the flaw
- Three biggest pipeline problems: (1) inappropriate work values go unrecognized; (2) senior leaders don't require all leaders to develop others; (3) senior leaders focus on today, not tomorrow
