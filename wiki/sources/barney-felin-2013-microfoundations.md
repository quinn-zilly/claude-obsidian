# Barney & Felin (2013) — What Are Microfoundations?

> [!key-insight] Core Claim
> Microfoundations explain how individual-level factors (actions, cognitions, choices) combine — both additively and emergently — to produce collective-level outcomes. They are not reductionism; they are about causal mechanisms linking levels.

## Bibliographic Details

Jay B. Barney and Teppo Felin. "What Are Microfoundations?" *Academy of Management Perspectives* 27, no. 2 (2013): 138–155.

## Summary

The paper provides conceptual clarity on the term "microfoundations," which had proliferated in organizational theory and strategy without a shared meaning. Barney and Felin distinguish true microfoundational analysis from related but distinct phenomena, and correct four common half-truths about what microfoundations are and require.

### What Microfoundations Are

Microfoundations address the **aggregation problem**: how do individual-level inputs combine to produce collective-level outputs? Two modes:

1. **Additive aggregation** — collective property is the sum or average of individual properties (e.g., average individual learning rates → organizational learning rate)
2. **Emergent aggregation** — collective property is *qualitatively different* from any individual property; arises from interactions and structural configurations (e.g., team trust, organizational routines, culture)

The distinction matters: additive aggregation requires only individual-level data; emergent aggregation requires modeling interaction structures.

### Four Half-Truths Corrected

| Half-Truth | Correction |
|------------|------------|
| "Microfoundations = individuals only" | Microfoundations can be at any level below the focal collective (individual, dyad, team, etc.) |
| "Microfoundations require methodological individualism" | Structural and relational factors (networks, roles) are legitimate microfoundational elements |
| "Collectivist theories have no microfoundations" | All collective theories *implicitly* assume microfoundational mechanisms; making them explicit is the goal |
| "Microfoundations are reductionist" | Emergence is anti-reductionist: the collective cannot be "reduced" to individual parts |

### Relationship to KBV and Dynamic Capabilities

Barney & Felin argue that the knowledge-based view ([[Knowledge-Based View of the Firm]]) and dynamic capabilities research have been largely silent on microfoundations. They call for research that specifies:
- Which individual-level knowledge, skills, or actions contribute to firm-level capabilities
- How those contributions aggregate (additively vs. emergently)
- What structural conditions mediate aggregation

This connects directly to [[Zahra et al. 2020|zahra-2020-knowledge-integration]]'s observation of the micro-macro gap in knowledge integration research.

### Implications

- Empirical research should not treat collective constructs as black boxes but should trace causal mechanisms from individual action to collective outcome
- Mixed-methods approaches are often required: quantitative models for additive aggregation; ethnographic/process research for emergent aggregation
- Theory at the collective level should be explicit about what individual-level inputs are assumed
