# Lefkowitz (2008) — Expand the Values to Match the Ethics

[[Joel Lefkowitz|Lefkowitz's]] central provocation, with apologies to Shakespeare:

> [!quote]
> "The fault, dear colleagues, lies not in our ethics, but in our values."

I/O psychologists are **not unethical** as individuals, Lefkowitz argues — they behave ethically as they see it. The problem is **institutional values**: the field's collective goals and self-image, encoded in the [[Scientist-Practitioner Model]], have drifted toward corporate economic objectives and away from psychology's humanist tradition and societal responsibilities.

Note the distinction from [[Scientism in Social Science|moral psychology]]: ethics ("what is the right thing to do?") and values ("what kind of person/profession shall I be?") are separate. A profession's **values** determine its goals, duties, and what it chooses to study or ignore.

## Five indictments of organizational psychology's implicit values

Since the field publishes no explicit values statement (only vague endorsements of the S-P model), Lefkowitz *infers* its values from its behavior — five interrelated criticisms:

1. **The S-P model replaces psychology's humanist tradition with economic business goals.** Borrowed from clinical psychology (Raimy 1950), but I/O "practice" is driven by free-market/shareholder values, not beneficence. (Church & Burke 1992: practitioners rank "effectiveness/efficiency/productivity" top, "humanizing the workplace / equality / individual welfare" bottom.)
2. **We are overly [[Scientism in Social Science|"scientistic"]].** The "value-free science" claim is a myth — our goals are value-laden, and many "facts" (test bias, criterion relevance, justice) are themselves value choices. Invokes Hayek's "pretence of knowledge" and Ghoshal (2005).
3. **While invariably ethical, we are often amoral.** We avoid *normative* questions ("is it the right thing to do?"). E.g., we study *perceived* procedural/interactional justice but sidestep [[Distributive Justice]] and what distributions *ought* to be fair.
4. **We have a pronounced [[Pro-Management Bias]].** We reflexively take the managerial perspective (employment-at-will, strategic HRM) over employee interests.
5. **We focus myopically on [[Means-Ends Inversion|"means" to the exclusion of "ends"]].** Since Munsterberg (1913) we accept the "hired hand" / technocratic view — competence without concern for consequences. Baritz (1960): *The Servants of Power*.

## The proposed correction: S-P-H

Expand the field's self-image into a [[Scientist-Practitioner-Humanist (S-P-H) Model]]: add explicit humanist values, advocacy of employee rights, and a normative account of how organizations *ought* to behave. The slogan upgrade: from "Good Science–Good Practice" to **"Good Science–Good Practice–Good Works."**

The yardstick is psychology's own [[APA Ethics Code]] preamble — improve the condition of "individuals, organizations, **and** society" (it says *and*, not *or*).

> [!key-insight]
> Lefkowitz reframes the debate away from "value-free objectivity vs. social values" toward: *which* values, under what circumstances, in what proportions, and at what necessary compromises?

## Rebuttal to the obvious objection

Wouldn't a humanist agenda oppose employers? Lefkowitz: (a) balancing stakeholders is what good executives already do; (b) fairness/justice arguably *aid* business objectives; (c) evidence links broader societal concern to corporate financial performance (Orlitzky, Schmidt & Rynes 2003); (d) the rise of CSR / UN Global Compact warrants optimism.

> [!contradiction] Tension with mainstream I/O utility framing
> Much I/O work (and pages like [[avolio-et-al-2010-ROI-leadership-development|ROI of leadership development]], utility analysis) justifies interventions in **financial** terms. Lefkowitz argues this very framing is the symptom — the field has internalized corporate values as if value-neutral. Not a factual conflict; a values-level critique of the field's standard rhetoric.
