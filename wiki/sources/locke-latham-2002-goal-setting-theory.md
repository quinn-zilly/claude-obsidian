# Locke & Latham (2002) — Goal-Setting Theory: A 35-Year Odyssey

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]] | [[wiki/concepts/Goal-Setting Theory|Goal-Setting Theory]]

**American Psychologist**, Vol. 57, No. 9. 13 pages. Canonical mid-career summary of goal-setting theory (GST) across 35+ years of inductive empirical research.

## Core Argument

Conscious goals are the most immediate, proximal determinants of task performance. Difficult, specific goals consistently outperform vague "do your best" instructions and easy goals — across 100+ tasks, 40,000+ participants, 8+ countries, time spans from 1 minute to 25 years.

## Core Findings

**Goal difficulty → performance:** Positive *linear* function (not Atkinson's curvilinear inverse). Harder goals = higher effort + performance. Meta-analytic d = .52–.82 (Locke & Latham 1990). Performance levels off only at ability limits or when commitment lapses (Erez & Zidon 1984).

**Specific hard goals vs. "do your best":** SDG consistently superior; meta-analytic d = .42–.80. "Do your best" has no external referent → idiosyncratic interpretation → wide variance in acceptable performance.

**Goal specificity alone** ≠ high performance (goals vary in difficulty); but specificity reduces performance variance by reducing ambiguity.

## Four Goal Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Direction** | Focus attention/effort toward goal-relevant activities; away from irrelevant ones (Rothkopf & Billington 1979) |
| **Effort** | High goals → greater effort (physical, cognitive, subjective); d robust across task types |
| **Persistence** | Hard goals prolong on-task time; tight deadlines → faster pace (Parkinson's Law effect) |
| **Strategy/Knowledge** | Goals arouse, trigger discovery, or activate task-relevant knowledge and strategies |

Strategy use (4th mechanism) depends on task type:
1. Familiar task → automatized skills applied directly
2. Novel-but-related task → draw from prior strategy repertoire
3. Fully new task → deliberate planning
4. Complex task → performance goals can impede strategy discovery → use **learning goals** instead

## Moderators

### Goal Commitment
- Most critical when goals are difficult (Klein et al. 1999)
- Facilitated by: (a) outcome importance; (b) self-efficacy
- Public commitment enhances via integrity mechanism
- Participation ≠ motivation — primary benefit is **cognitive** (info exchange), not motivational (Locke, Alavi & Wagner 1997). Assigned goals as effective as participative IF rationale given (Latham, Erez & Locke 1988; meta-analytic ES = .11 for participation on performance)
- Monetary incentives → commitment, BUT: task-and-bonus on very hard goals can backfire (goal + self-efficacy drop when reward looks unattainable); piece-rate safer for hard goals

### Feedback
- Goals + feedback > goals alone (Bandura & Cervone 1983; Becker 1978; Erez 1977)
- Feedback without goals → people set own goals in response → mediation
- Negative feedback → self-efficacy determines whether subsequent goals rise or fall

### Task Complexity
- Goal effects smaller on complex tasks (d = .48 complex vs. .67 simple; Wood, Mento & Locke 1987)
- Complex task + performance goal → anxiety → unsystematic strategy search → worse outcomes
- **Fix:** specific challenging *learning* goals on complex tasks (Winters & Latham 1996; Seijts & Latham 2001)
- **Proximal goals** on complex/dynamic tasks: add proximal to distal → higher self-efficacy + profits vs. distal-only or do-best (Latham & Seijts 1999)

## Personal Goals as Mediators of External Incentives

The **motivation hub** (Locke 1991b): personal goals + goal commitment + self-efficacy = proximal conscious determinants of action. These mediate:
- Assigned goals (via self-set goal + self-efficacy)
- Monetary incentives (T. Lee, Locke & Phan 1997) — *some* studies find instrumentality mediates instead
- Feedback effects
- Participative decision-making effects
- Visionary/charismatic leadership effects (Kirkpatrick & Locke 1996)

## Goals and Satisfaction

Goals = reference standard for satisfaction. Exceeding goal → satisfaction proportional to positive discrepancy; falling short → dissatisfaction.

**Paradox:** High-goal people produce most but experience least trial-by-trial satisfaction (bar set high). Motivation to set high goals comes from expected distal outcomes (pride, academic success, career benefits), not anticipated satisfaction with performance.

**High-Performance Cycle** (Locke & Latham 1990): high goals → high performance → rewards (recognition, promotion) → satisfaction + high self-efficacy → willingness to commit to even higher goals. Satisfaction = *result*, not cause, of high performance. Explains why job satisfaction–performance correlation is weak/inconsistent.

## Practical Applications

| Domain | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Logging productivity | +wood cut; truck load 60% → 90% legal weight; $250k saved in 9 months (Latham & Baldes 1975) |
| Engineering/R&D | Higher perf appraisal scores when goals set vs. "do best" |
| Self-regulation / attendance | Training in self-management (F. H. Kanfer methodology) + goal setting → sustained attendance gains (Frayne & Latham 1987, 1989) |
| Selection | Situational interview (Latham et al. 1980) — presents goal/intention dilemmas; highest criterion validity of any interview type |
| Sports + health | Goal setting effective in any self-regulated activity domain |

## New Directions

**Goal conflict:** Personal vs. organizational goal misalignment → incompatible action tendencies → performance decrements (Locke et al. 1994; Seijts & Latham 2000b).

**Learning vs. performance goals:** Harackiewicz et al. 1997 — performance goals improve grades, learning goals improve interest; effects separable and potentially additive (understudied).

**Goals and risk:** Difficult performance goals → riskier strategy selection → sometimes better, sometimes worse outcomes (Knight, Durham & Locke 2001); conditions not yet specified.

**Personality mediation:** Goals + self-efficacy mediate conscientiousness (Barrick et al. 1993), achievement motivation (Matsui et al. 1982), goal orientation (VandeWalle et al. 2001). Assigned goals neutralize LGO/PGO trait effects ("strong situation").

**Subconscious motivation:** GST acknowledged limit — subconscious is a storehouse of values/knowledge; goals may arouse subconscious processing (Wegge & Dibbelt 2000). Conscious motivation > subconscious motives longitudinally (Howard & Bray 1988 AT&T study: conscious promotion goal >> TAT motive pattern); but area needs more research.

## Relation to Other Theories

| Theory | Relation |
|--------|----------|
| **Expectancy Theory (Vroom)** | Apparent contradiction resolved by within- vs. between-goal-level expectancy distinction |
| **Social Cognitive Theory (Bandura)** | Compatible; both emphasize goals + self-efficacy; GST focuses on goal properties; SCT on self-efficacy mechanisms, modeling, societal-level effects |
| **SDT (Ryan & Deci)** | SDT explains *why* people adopt goals (autonomy, competence); GST explains *how* goals work once adopted; complementary |
| **Prospect Theory (K&T)** | Both use reference points; Prospect Theory omits aspiration level; GST aspiration level = distal goal |
| **Control Theory (Carver & Scheier)** | GST rejects control theory's machine model; motivation is discrepancy-*creating* not merely discrepancy-reducing (Bandura 1989) |

## Limitations

- Underspecified role of subconscious motivation
- Less effective on highly complex tasks without learning goal framing
- Goal conflict dynamics understudied
- Most research: individual-level; group/org-level effects exist but less studied

## Key Data Points

- 100+ tasks, 40,000+ participants, 8+ countries, 1 minute–25 years time span
- Meta-analytic d (difficulty): .52–.82; d (SDG vs. do-best): .42–.80
- Complex vs. simple task d: .48 vs. .67
- Participation effect on performance: ES = .11 (Wagner & Gooding 1987)
