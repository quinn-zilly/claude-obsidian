# Sanchez & Levine (2012) — The Rise and Fall of Job Analysis and the Future of Work Analysis

> [!abstract] One-line
> The *first-ever* Annual Review chapter on job-analytic research argues job analysis has lost prestige in top journals even as it remains foundational to every HR application, and calls for reframing the field as **work analysis** aimed at understanding the *experience* of work rather than describing static jobs.

## Core argument

Job analysis is the **preceding step of every application of psychology to HR** (selection, training, appraisal, job design, compensation), yet it has faded from top journals. Morgeson & Dierdorff's compilation shows the share of job-analysis articles in the seven top outlets fell from ~77% (1960–1979) to ~27% (post-2000); *JAP* + *PP* went from a peak of 22 articles (1978–1982) to just 4 (2003–2007). This decline contrasts with the steady flow of predictor-of-performance research — a lopsided emphasis on the **P** side over the **E** side of [[Person–Environment Fit|person–environment fit]].

The authors trace the imbalance partly to validity generalization for [[General Mental Ability (GMA)|GMA]]: the (over)generalized conclusion that detailed job analysis is an unnecessary expense because GMA tests work everywhere. Sanchez & Levine reject the premise, noting O*NET-based ability determinations rely on single-item scales of limited discriminant validity.

## Three types of job-analytic data

The review is organized around the three widely accepted building blocks (cf. Fine & Cronshaw), each evaluated on **reliability, validity, and consequences** (the inferences drawn and the rules used to draw them):

1. **Work activity information** — tasks/functions/duties at molecular→molar levels; task inventories in the tradition of Allen's (1919) "trade analysis"; the 42 [[Generalized Work Activities (GWAs)|generalized work activities]] in O*NET.
2. **Worker attribute information** — the KSAOs inferred as required for performance. Sanchez & Levine insist deriving worker attributes is *intrinsic* to job analysis and is "what makes job analysis a truly psychological endeavor," against Harvey's view that JA should cover only objective/verifiable job aspects.
3. **Work context information** — the environment, situational opportunities and constraints; an **interactional** variable that alters which attributes matter.

## The reframing: from static jobs to the experience of work

> [!key-insight] Legitimate variance, not just error
> The classic assumption that a job is a stable object with fixed properties (a product of **Taylorism** — task standardization + division of labor) holds less well today, where employees are empowered to use discretion. Much interrater "disagreement" among incumbents of the same job title is **not random error** but legitimate, systematic variation in how they interpret *and perform* the job.

This connects job analysis to [[Job Crafting]] (Wrzesniewski & Dutton) and role theory: incumbents are **active agents**, not passive recipients of a work assignment. Evidence: time-spent and task-importance profiles differ between high and low performers (Borman et al. 1992; Sanchez et al. 1998) with tangible correlates (e.g., higher sales for sales-oriented job conceptions). Their recommendation: instead of hiding or eliminating disagreement, job analysis should *embrace* it and study its non-random sources.

## Competencies

Many firms adopted **competency modeling (CM)** in place of job analysis, but the CM–JA distinction "seems still blurry." Citing [[Shippmann-et-al-2000-practice-of-competency-modeling|Shippmann et al. (2000)]], CM is linked to strategic goals but is *less rigorous* than job analysis on data collection, level of detail, reliability assessment, and documentation. Per [[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine (2009)]], JA and CM belong in different domains: **JA is applied measurement; CM is closest to a mechanism of informal control.** CM research should study its true dependent variable — the extent to which CM influences employees' day-to-day behavior along strategic lines — rather than mimicking JA's accuracy/agreement metrics (which broad competencies routinely fail).

## Conclusion & future trends

Framing job analysis as a mere information-gathering tool ("a means to an end," Pearlman & Sanchez) fed its low status. The domain is "turning the corner" toward understanding **work demands as experienced by incumbents**, individually and collectively. Cross-fertilization with the meaning of work, job design, job crafting, strategic change, and interactional psychology is proposed as the route to renewal — the future of **work analysis**.

> [!quote]
> "It is the insight into work demands as experienced by incumbents that turns job analysis into a truly psychological endeavor whose primary goal is precisely to gain an understanding of the psychological requirements of jobs."
