# Sackett, Walmsley & Laczo (2013) — Job and Work Analysis

> [!abstract] One-line
> A *Handbook* chapter framing job analysis as **a series of deliberate choices whose right answer depends on purpose** — "one size does not fit all" — then surveying six modern developments: O*NET, personality-based JA, competency modeling, cognitive task analysis, strategic job analysis, and accuracy.

## Framing: job analysis requires many choices

Job analysis = "systematic procedures for examining, documenting, and drawing inferences about work activities, worker attributes, and work context." The authors defend the label against critics who equate it with rigid written job descriptions; the tools apply to changing work structures too. The chapter's spine is a set of non-orthogonal **design choices**:

| Choice | Poles |
|--------|-------|
| Focus | Activity (work-oriented) vs Attribute (worker-oriented) |
| Specificity | General vs Specific (the "art" is picking the level that fits the purpose) |
| Data form | Qualitative (narrative) vs Quantitative (e.g., [[Position Analysis Questionnaire (PAQ)|PAQ]] 187-item ratings) |
| Taxonomy | Taxonomy-based (PAQ, Fleishman scales) vs Blank slate (job-specific lists) |
| Source | Observer-based vs Informant-based (incumbents/supervisors) |
| Attribute scope | [[KSA Framework|KSA]] vs KSAO (adds personality, attitudes, values) |
| Scope of comparison | Single job vs Job comparison |
| Orientation | Descriptive vs Prescriptive |

> [!key-insight] Purpose drives everything
> The bank-teller example: 20 job titles correlate **0.09–0.21** with "paying & receiving teller" on *activity* ratings but **0.88–0.92** on *ability* ratings. Different descriptors → different conclusions about job similarity. "Which jobs are similar enough to be treated the same?" is unanswerable without knowing the purpose (test battery vs common training program).

## From the DOT to O*NET

The [[Dictionary of Occupational Titles (DOT)|DOT]] (12,000+ jobs) was limited by occupation-specific narratives, task focus, and update cost. The 1993 **APDOT** report blueprinted a replacement built on a **Content Model** (Worker Attributes, Work Context, Labor Market Context, Work Content & Outcomes) and Pearlman's insistence that "skills" is a "Tower of Babel." Result: [[Occupational Information Network (O-NET)|O*NET]] — nine questionnaires, ~300 descriptors, 38 first-order factors → 4 second-order factors, ~1,102 occupations, a public relational database. A 2010 National Research Council panel judged it useful and prioritized data quality and user service.

## Personality-based job analysis

Three approaches to bring personality into JA: (1) direct relevance ratings of personality dimensions (NEO Job Profiler); (2) rating job behaviors linked to a specific instrument (PDI Employment Inventory); (3) general behavior-to-trait linkage tools (**PPRF** — Raymark, Schmit & Guion; 12 dimensions under the Big Five; O*NET "work styles"). Caveat: raters' *own* personalities bias ratings (Cucina et al.), reducible via frame-of-reference training (Aguinis et al.).

## Competency modeling

"Easily the most visible change in the analysis of work in the past 2 decades." Traces to McClelland (1973) "Testing for Competence, Not Intelligence" (whose errors Barrett & Depinet documented). Key positions:

- The claim that CM *replaces* JA rests on the **false premise** that JA only captures tasks. Worker-oriented JA already covers attributes; sociability etc. sit inside KSAO approaches.
- Job-level CM ≈ **worker-oriented job analysis focused on broader (KSAO) characteristics**; the CM–JA difference here can be "semantic."
- **Organization-wide** CM is "conceptually very different": prescriptive, future-tense, top-management vision of what will be valued/rewarded (often a change intervention), not an analysis of current employees.
- Cites [[Shippmann-et-al-2000-practice-of-competency-modeling|Schippmann et al. (2000)]]'s 17-variable comparison: JA more rigorous on evaluative criteria except linking to business goals/strategy.
- Endorses [[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine (2009)]]'s complementary view. Hybrid task+competency ratings (Lievens et al. 2004) raise interrater reliability and between-job discriminability.

> [!quote]
> "It is inappropriate to proclaim competency modeling as a replacement for job analysis, as each approach has a different focus and the appropriateness of either methodology should depend on the purpose of the analysis."

## Cognitive task analysis (CTA)

Approaches to model the **unobservable cognitive processes** behind task performance, focused on expert vs novice differences (PARI, DNA, GOMS, COGNET). A *supplement* to — not replacement for — behavioral task analysis. Limits: time-consuming, poorly standardized, little guidance on going from data to design, and scarce reliability/validity evidence.

## Strategic job analysis (SJA)

Shift from **descriptive** (what is done now) to **predictive** (what will be done) analysis for jobs that may not yet exist (Schneider & Konz). Group-discussion/brainstorming methods dominate; empirical validation is thin. The authors suspect SJA's low profile is "principally a labeling issue" — its strategy focus merges with competency modeling.

## Accuracy in job analysis

Morgeson & Campion's (1997) framework identifies **16 potential social and cognitive sources of inaccuracy** in four categories: social influence, self-presentation, information-processing limits, and information-processing bias. The deep problem is the **criterion problem** — how would we *know* an analysis is accurate? Guion's stance (endorsed): job analysis is **not science**; it is a subjective information-gathering tool. Inflation is larger on less-specific descriptors (competencies/abilities > tasks); analysts show higher reliability than incumbents; generalizability theory (Van Iddekinge et al.) can partition variance sources.
