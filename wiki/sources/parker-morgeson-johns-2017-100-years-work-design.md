# One Hundred Years of Work Design Research — Parker, Morgeson & Johns (2017)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]] | [[Teams & Org Behavior MOC]]

Centennial review in *Journal of Applied Psychology*. Takes a "big picture" view of work design research: maps the field via scientific (bibliometric) mapping, assesses JAP's role over 100 years, evaluates impact on academia/management/practice/policy, and sets a future agenda. Frames the field from McChesney's (1917) first-issue call to "design work that preserves human character."

## Why It Matters

Single best high-level map of the work design field. Shows the major theoretical streams are historically siloed clusters, not substantively distinct — the central argument is that **integration across clusters is the field's biggest opportunity**. Pairs with [[morgeson-humphrey-2006-work-design-questionnaire|the WDQ]] (taxonomy) and [[Work Design (Concept)]] (definition) as the orienting trio for the domain.

## Mapping the Field

From 17,000+ work design articles, narrowed to 5,708 in management/psychology, then 35 "most influential" articles (Table 1). Used VOS (visualization of similarities) bibliometric mapping → **five clusters**, each mapping onto a historical perspective:

| Cluster (color) | Perspective | Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| Red (235 terms, largest) | [[Sociotechnical Systems Theory\|Sociotechnical systems]] & [[Autonomous Work Groups]] | Trist & Bamforth (1951) |
| Green (130 terms) | [[Job Characteristics Model]] | Hackman & Oldham (1975, 1976) |
| Blue (101 terms) | [[Job Demands-Control Model]] | Karasek (1979) |
| Pink (65 terms) | [[Job Demands-Resources Model]] | Demerouti et al. (2001) |
| Yellow (92 terms) | [[Role Theory (Work Design)]] | Kahn et al. (1964); Rizzo et al. (1970) |

> [!key-insight] Central argument
> Clusters reflect **history**, not topic similarity. Because of this, there are likely "important synergies across research areas that are currently not being exploited" — e.g. no reason JCM studies can't examine musculoskeletal or burnout outcomes (clusters 3–4). Integration is the path forward.

## The Five Perspectives (brief)

**Sociotechnical systems & AWGs.** Trist & Bamforth's Longwall coal-mining studies → joint optimization of social + technical systems → [[Autonomous Work Groups]]. Reviews positive (Pasmore et al. 1982) but inconsistent (Wall et al. 1986; Barker 1993 "iron cage") evidence. Broadest, most macro/team cluster.

**Job characteristics model.** Herzberg's motivator-hygiene → Turner & Lawrence (1965) → Hackman & Lawler (1971) → [[Job Characteristics Model|JCM]] (Hackman & Oldham 1975/76/80). Five core characteristics → psychological states → outcomes, moderated by growth need strength. Dominant model; 2nd most-cited JAP article ever. Core characteristics robustly predict outcomes even though the specific mediation/moderation model has weak support (meaningfulness is the key state).

**Job demands-control.** Karasek (1979): demands × control interaction; high-strain = high demands + low control; control buffers demands. Social support added (Karasek & Theorell 1990; Johnson & Hall 1988). Strong main effects, weak/mixed interaction support. Challenge vs hindrance demand distinction (Cavanaugh et al. 2000); Warr's vitamin model (curvilinear effects; Xie & Johns 1995 U-shape).

**Job demands-resources.** Demerouti et al. (2001): generalizes beyond control — any resource can counter demands; dual path (demands → strain/burnout; resources → engagement/performance). Open questions: are demands/resources and engagement/burnout truly distinct?

**Role theory.** Kahn et al. (1964): role conflict, ambiguity, overload. Broader than jobs — roles include emergent/self-initiated elements (Ilgen & Hollenbeck 1991), enabling [[Job Crafting]] (Wrzesniewski & Dutton 2001), role orientation (Parker et al. 1997), role breadth self-efficacy (Parker 1998), personal initiative (Frese et al. 2007).

## Integrative & Contemporary Perspectives

Not yet discrete clusters, but the bridges the authors want built:

- **Interdisciplinary model** (Campion 1988): four design models (mechanistic, motivational, biological, perceptual-motor) with distinct benefit/cost trade-offs.
- **Elaborated model** (Parker, Wall & Cordery 2001) → **[[Work Design Questionnaire|WDQ]]** (Morgeson & Humphrey 2006): 21 characteristics across task/knowledge/social/contextual domains.
- **[[Relational Job Design]]** (Grant 2007): relational architecture connecting workers to beneficiaries → prosocial motivation.
- **[[Learning and Development Perspective on Work Design]]** (Parker 2014): work design drives learning, identity, and development, not just motivation.

## JAP's Role (Centennial Assessment)

- JAP publishes proportionately more work design than peer journals (N≈366, ≈ all six other top-tier combined ≈397).
- Quantity flourished **outside** JAP/top-tier since the mid-1980s (~7x more in the wider field); but for **quality/influence**, JAP leads: ~12% of the top-35 articles, the 2nd-most-cited JAP article of all time (Hackman & Oldham 1975), and ~19% of all work-design citations from just 6.4% of articles.
- JAP map is **narrower**: excludes sociotechnical/AWG, lean, telecommuting, emotional labor, physical-health outcomes — partly because those skew qualitative/case-based, which JAP rarely publishes. Authors argue JAP should embrace broader, more contextualized topics (with rigor).

## Impact Beyond the Discipline

- **Academia:** Miner (2003) rated JCM among only 8 of 73 OB theories high on both validity and utility; sociotechnical also rated well.
- **Other fields:** epidemiology (Whitehall studies, Marmot 1991; Karasek tradition), economics (Holmstrom & Milgrom 1991 — often *not* linked to psychology), ergonomics, nursing, marketing, operations.
- **Management thinking:** ~half of recent HBR articles relevant to work design — but almost none use academic terms ("job design", "job characteristics"). Business books (Reengineering, Future Work, Grant's *Give and Take*) embody it implicitly. Google Ngrams: scientific-management terms peaked ~1918; classic work-design terms peaked early 1980s; teamwork/empowerment/emotional-demands/electronic-monitoring terms rising.
- **Practice & policy:** enriched-design prevalence is overstated by HR surveys; employee surveys show many poor-quality jobs (e.g. only 14.2% in self-directed teams; Vidal 2013: low-autonomy ≈35–41% of US employment). Work design is on most national policy agendas (health/safety + productivity/ageing).

## Future Agenda

Table 2 themes: extended characteristics & configurations; expanded outcomes (learning, identity, ROI, safety, multilevel, trade-offs); alternative mechanisms; context/moderators; antecedents of work design (top-down + bottom-up); proactive focus; dynamic/temporal/multilevel/qualitative/intervention methods.

> [!tip] The authors' reorientation
> Shift from passive ("how does work design affect outcomes?") to **proactive** ("what is the role of work design in *achieving* outcomes and *enabling* adaptation to technological/social change?"). Treat work design as a lever — including a tool to shape identity amid automation — and study it dynamically (it is not static; people craft, events disrupt, contexts shift). Reaffirms the sociotechnical insight: social + technical must be jointly considered. Closes by echoing McChesney: "banish bad jobs to Humdrum Land."
