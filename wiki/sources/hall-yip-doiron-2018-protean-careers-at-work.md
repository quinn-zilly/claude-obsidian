# Hall, Yip & Doiron (2018) — Protean Careers at Work

> [!abstract] One-line
> Integrative review of 122 papers (1996–2017) on the protean career, organized around three empirically supported protean **processes** — identity awareness, adaptability, agency — that translate a protean career *orientation* into psychological success. Introduces the **[[Protean Paradox]]**.

## Core construct

A **protean career** is "a process which the person, not the organization, is managing" (Hall 1976), characterized by the exercise of **self-direction** and a focus on **intrinsic values** in the pursuit of **psychological success**. Named for Proteus (the shape-shifting Greek god); inspired by Lifton's *The Protean Self*. It is an agentic orientation, contrasted with the traditional organization-directed, extrinsic-success career.

Gubler et al.'s (2014) three-way disambiguation, adopted here:
- **PCC** — protean career *concept* (the basic theory, Hall 1976)
- **PCO** — protean career *orientation* (the attitude/mindset: self-directed + values-driven)
- **PCP** — protean career *path* (the objective sequence of moves of a high-PCO person)

Empirical research focuses on PCC and PCO. Briscoe et al.'s (2006) **Protean Career Attitudes Scale (PCAS)** — two dimensions (self-directed α≈.76, values-driven α≈.71) — is the dominant measure and drove the research explosion from 2006.

## The two dimensions and their interaction

**Self-direction** = taking personal agency/responsibility for career decisions. **Values orientation** = making career choices guided by intrinsic values (autonomy, meaning, growth). Key interaction: values orientation *without* self-direction can be **maladaptive/rigid** (Briscoe & Hall 2006); the two components together predict more variance in positive responses to change than either alone (Briscoe et al. 2010; Judge et al. 1999).

## Three protean processes (the review's organizing model)

| Process | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Identity awareness** | A clear sense of personal identity and values — a meta-competency; "path with a heart" / calling; PCO predicts identity clarity and self-efficacy (Hirschi et al. 2017), which mediate PCO → outcomes |
| **Adaptability** | Readiness to cope with changing work conditions (Savickas 1997); PCO predicts active coping and perceived employability; central to the "100-year life" (Gratton & Scott 2016) |
| **Agency** | Capacity to make and impose choices (Bandura 2001); enacted through proactive career behaviors and work engagement |

These processes feed career outcomes: [[Subjective Career Success]], [[Objective Career Success]], and organizational commitment.

## Notable positions

- **Protean ≠ boundaryless.** They are related but independent "new career" orientations; protean is about values/self-direction, boundaryless about mobility. A person can be high-PCO yet low-mobility if their organization fits their values (Harrington & Hall 2007). PCO does *not* reliably predict mobility preferences (Briscoe & Finkelstein 2009).
- **Antecedents** are understudied: proactive personality (distinct from PCO by predictive validity, Herrmann et al. 2015), Openness, promotion regulatory focus, education/managerial experience; women often score higher.
- **Malleability.** Recent evidence challenges the assumption that PCO is a stable trait (Waters et al. 2014; Supeli & Creed 2016).
- **SDT link.** Protean theory and [[Self-Determination Theory]] share a positive view of human motivation; differ in timeframe (lifelong career vs immediate situation).

## Protean Paradox

The review's signature contribution: **greater individual self-direction and values orientation — serving the individual's own purposes — can *also* positively influence the groups and organizations they work in.** Flagged as meriting further investigation. See [[Protean Paradox]].
