# Viswesvaran & Ones (2000) — Perspectives on Models of Job Performance

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> A review of contemporary models of job performance. Job performance is **an abstract, multidimensional construct** — "scalable actions, behavior and outcomes that employees engage in or bring about that are linked with and contribute to organizational goals." The many hypothesized facets (task, [[Organizational Citizenship Behavior|OCB]], counterproductivity) are all **positively correlated** (a *positive manifold*), implying a substantive [[General Factor in Job Performance|general factor]] that is *not* explained by rater halo alone. Companion conceptual piece to [[viswesvaran-schmidt-ones-2005-general-factor-job-performance|Viswesvaran, Schmidt & Ones (2005)]], which supplied the meta-analytic evidence.

## A framework for classifying performance models

The authors cross two dichotomies (**developmental context** × **occupational focus**) to yield four model types:

| | Limited to specific occupations | Applicable across jobs |
|---|---|---|
| **Stand-alone dimensions** | Too numerous to review | Borman & Motowidlo (1993); Organ (1994) |
| **Dimensions developed as a set** | Borman & Brush (1993); Hunt (1996) | [[Campbell Model of Job Performance|Campbell (1990)]]; Viswesvaran (1993) |

## Stand-alone dimensions applicable across jobs

Three broad families:

1. **[[Task Performance]]** — proficiency on formally recognized, role-prescribed activities that feed the organization's technical core (Borman & Motowidlo 1993).
2. **[[Organizational Citizenship Behavior|OCB]]** — discretionary, extra-role behavior aggregating to organizational effectiveness (Organ 1988). Related constructs: organizational spontaneity (George & Brief 1992), extra-role behavior (Van Dyne et al. 1995), prosocial organizational behavior (Brief & Motowidlo 1986).
3. **[[Counterproductive Work Behavior|Counterproductive behaviors]]** — deviance (Robinson & Bennett 1995: property / production / personal aggression / political), withdrawal, social loafing, integrity-test-relevant behaviors (Ones, Viswesvaran & Schmidt 1993).

## Dimension sets for specific occupations

- **Service entry-level** (Hunt 1996): nine generic dimensions (adherence to confrontational rules, industriousness, thoroughness, schedule flexibility, attendance, off-task behavior, unruliness, theft, drug misuse).
- **Managers**: Borman & Brush (1993) 18 dimensions → 4 broad (leadership/supervision; interpersonal/communication; technical/mechanics; useful behaviors/skills); Conway (1999).
- **Military** (Project A): Campbell, McHenry & Wise (1990) five dimensions across 19 Army jobs; Borman et al. (1985) allegiance / teamwork / determination.

## Dimension sets applicable across all jobs

- **[[Campbell Model of Job Performance|Campbell (1990)]]** — eight distinct dimensions (job-specific task proficiency; non-job-specific task proficiency; written/oral communication; demonstrating effort; personal discipline; facilitating peer/team performance; supervision; management/administration). Treated as distinct (small true-score correlations).
- **Viswesvaran (1993)** — **lexical hypothesis** applied to performance: cull *all* 486 performance measures used in ~80 years of literature; two raters grouped them into **ten dimensions** (overall performance, productivity, effort, job knowledge, interpersonal competence, administrative competence, quality, communication competence, leadership, compliance with rules). Cumulating >300 studies, the ten showed a **positive manifold**.
- **Murphy (1990)** — four dimensions: downtime behaviors, task performance, interpersonal, destructive behaviors.
- **Borman & Motowidlo (1993)** — task + [[Contextual Performance|contextual performance]].
- **Bernardin & Beatty (1984)** — performance as the *record of outcomes* (contrast with Campbell's behavior-not-outcomes stance); six Kane (1986) dimensions: quality, quantity, timeliness, cost-effectiveness, need for supervision, interpersonal impact.

## The general factor

> [!key-insight] Positive manifold → substantive general factor
> Cumulating >300 studies, **over 50% of variance is shared** across dimensions — comparable to the general factor in cognitive-ability factor analyses (Jensen 1986). The authors argue much of it is **substantively meaningful, not mere halo**. But a general factor does **not** make the dimensions redundant: specific variance is useful for specific purposes (e.g., designing training). Structure is a **hierarchy** — general factor at apex, dimensions below, subdimensions under those. Appropriate level of specificity depends on the assessor's purpose (Wallace 1965).

See [[General Factor in Job Performance]] for the halo-vs-substance controversy and its 2005 resolution.

## Measurement issues

- Job performance is an **abstract construct**: you can only point to *manifestations*, which may change job to job even as the *dimension* generalizes (analogous to domain sampling).
- Assessment relies on **objective counts** or **subjective judgment (ratings)**. Judgmental assessment from knowledgeable, motivated raters can have high criterion relevance, but opportunity to observe (Rothstein 1990) affects it.
- The claim that "rater disagreement is not error" (Murphy & Cleveland 1995) is rejected: **interrater reliability is foundational** — without consistency of measurement, validation collapses and there is no science of work behavior.
- **[[Dynamic Criteria|Dynamic criteria]]**: mean change, rank-order change, or changing correlations over time. Barrett, Caldwell & Alexander (1989) found scant evidence for rank-order/correlational change. Typical vs maximal performance (DuBois et al. 1993) — see [[Typical vs Maximal Performance]].

## Antecedents of performance dimensions

- Hunter (1983): cognitive ability → job knowledge → job performance → supervisory ratings.
- McCloy, Campbell & Cudeck (1994): all individual-differences variables act via declarative knowledge, procedural knowledge, or motivation.
- Conscientiousness has **multiple pathways** (Ones & Viswesvaran 1996): more time on task, more OCB, more attention to detail / vicarious learning.
- Bottom line: **each dimension is complexly determined jointly by ability and personality**; the two most likely common determinants across dimensions are **general cognitive ability and conscientiousness**.
