# Stepping to the Edge of One's Comfort Zone
**McCauley & Yost (2021)** — Ch. 8 in *The Age of Agility* (Harvey & De Meuse, eds.), Oxford University Press.

## Core Argument

Stretch experiences are the primary driver of leader development. Learning agility relates to stretch experiences in three ways simultaneously: as **antecedent** (learning-agile leaders seek them out), **moderator** (learning agility buffers negative consequences and amplifies positive ones), and **outcome** (repeated stretch experiences build learning agility itself).

Organizations can support this by: (1) making stretch experiences visible and valued, (2) staffing for development, and (3) building daily learning habits.

## Key Concepts

- **Stretch experience**: any experience requiring knowledge, skills, or perspectives beyond what the individual currently has — from major role changes to daily on-the-job challenges
- **Five challenge types** (Ohlott, 2004): unfamiliar responsibilities, creating change, high levels of responsibility, managing across boundaries, dealing with diversity
- **Equity caveat**: women and African American managers receive fewer challenging assignments; access to stretch is unequal

## Table 8.1 — Learning Agility & Stretch Experiences

| Antecedent (who seeks them) | Moderator (who learns most) | Outcome (what develops) |
|---|---|---|
| Change readiness | Emotional regulation | Self-insight |
| Curiosity / experimental | Self-efficacy | Resilience |
| Learning goal orientation | Learning goal orientation | Flexibility, feedback seeking, self-efficacy |

## Three Organizational Strategies

### 1. Shine a Light
- Build a **stretch experience framework** (e.g., McCauley 2006 developmental map) — list stretch experiences alongside classes in development plans
- Highlight in **formal programs**: action learning, back-home projects, continuous learning groups (e.g., Heineken first-time supervisor model)
- Reinforce via **culture**: senior exec modeling, reward structure, measurement (track stretch assignment impact, not just training ROI)

### 2. Make Stretch Experiences Happen
- **Equip bosses**: teach managers to identify development needs, use job moves / temp assignments / responsibility reshaping; manage stretch risk
- **Staff for development**: deliberate person–job misfit; use succession planning, development planning, rotational programs (e.g., GE Corporate Leadership Staff)
- **Design new experiences**: IBM Corporate Service Corps (1-month global pro bono assignment); Microsoft emerging-market HQ rotations

### 3. Encourage Daily Learning — 8 Heuristics
Organized around **Anticipating → Adapting → Reflecting**:

**Anticipating:**
a. *"What's my story today?"* — frame challenge as hero's journey; growth mindset (Dweck)
b. *Turn performance into development* — bundle stretch into current work goals
c. *Create a cue* — environmental trigger that directs attention to learning

**Adapting:**
a. *Live your life as a working hypothesis* — treat every day as an experiment; prototype small
b. *Sharpen the saw* — recharge (exercise, sleep, social support) for sustained effectiveness
c. *"What else is going on?"* — step onto the balcony (Heifetz & Laurie), see the system

**Reflecting:**
a. *Think kaizen* — continuous improvement; after-event reviews; don't call them postmortems
b. *Count your blessings* — celebrate small wins; gratitude practices build confidence and spread prosocial behavior

## Future Directions
- Better delineation of **basic** vs. **advanced** learning agility facets (current measures conflate learning process with high-potential advancement)
- Longitudinal studies tracking individuals through stretch experiences
- Identifying which LA facets matter most for which stretch experience types
