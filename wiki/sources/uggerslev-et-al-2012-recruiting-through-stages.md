# Uggerslev, Fassina & Kraichy (2012) — Recruiting Through the Stages

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> Meta-analysis (**232 studies, 250 samples, 3,518 coefficients, N = 108,632**) using **semipartial correlations from regression on meta-analytic matrices** to compare the *relative strength* and *incremental variance* of **seven** recruiting-predictor categories on **[[Applicant Attraction|applicant attraction]]** across **[[Recruitment Stages (Barber)|Barber's three stages]]**. Headline: **[[Perceived Fit (Recruitment)|perceived fit]] is the strongest predictor of attraction** (large effect, zero-order *and* incremental) — but a **nonsignificant** predictor of actual **job choice**. **[[Recruiter Behaviors|Recruiter behaviors]]** carry the **second-largest incremental variance**, concentrated at the **early stages**. **Organizational characteristics** matter more at Stage 2 (maintaining status); **recruitment-process characteristics** grow more predictive as stages advance. **Job characteristics** account for the most unique variance in **job-choice** decisions. Framed by the **[[Elaboration Likelihood Model|elaboration likelihood model]]**: early = low-EL heuristics (recruiter cues), later = high-EL scrutiny (job/org info, procedural justice).

## The three practical questions

Reworded as budgeting questions: (a) should firms allocate recruiting dollars differently by stage? (b) **where should a firm spend its first recruitment dollar?** (c) where should it spend its subsequent (incremental) dollars?

## Seven predictor categories (Barber 1998)

Job characteristics · organizational characteristics · [[Recruiter Behaviors|recruiter behaviors]] · recruitment-process characteristics · [[Perceived Fit (Recruitment)|perceived fit]] (P-J and P-O) · perceived alternatives · hiring expectancies.

## Theory: ELM across stages

The **[[Elaboration Likelihood Model]]** predicts *why* predictor importance shifts. Early on, applicant **ability** (little info) and **motivation** (skimming many options) are low → **low EL** → reliance on simple cues/heuristics (recruiter friendliness, a usable website — signals of working conditions, per **[[Signaling Theory (Recruitment)|signaling theory]]**). As applicants enter and stay in the pool, ability and motivation rise → **high EL** → effortful scrutiny of job/org information and procedural justice, and recruiter cues fade. Hence:

- **H1**: job/org/process/alternatives predict attraction more at *later* stages.
- **H2**: recruiter behaviors predict attraction more at *earlier* stages.
- **H3**: job, org, and fit are stronger predictors than recruiter/process.
- **H4**: recruiter and process account for *unique* variance beyond job/org/fit.

## Key results

**Relative strength (averaged across stages, Cohen benchmarks r ≥ .10/.24/.37 = small/med/large):**

| Predictor | Effect on attraction |
|---|---|
| **Perceived fit** | **Largest — large effect** (strongest zero-order *and* incremental) |
| Organizational characteristics | 2nd; medium (> all but job & fit) |
| Job characteristics | 3rd; medium |
| Recruiter behaviors / process / hiring expectancies | medium, not different from each other |
| Perceived alternatives | **nonsignificant** |

**Incremental variance on attraction** (model R² = .50): **perceived fit ΔR² ≈ .397** (dominant); **recruiter behaviors** next (ΔR² ≈ .096, sr² = .084); process third. **Job characteristics add ~no unique variance** to attraction. Supports **H4**: the categories firms *control* (recruiter behaviors, process) add real incremental value beyond fit.

**Stage moderation (H1/H2):** stage is a significant moderator. Recruiter behaviors' unique contribution is **larger at Stage 1** than Stage 2 (H2 = limited support — Stage-3 data too sparse). **Organizational characteristics more predictive at Stage 2.** Process characteristics become stronger as stages progress. Fit is strongest at *both* Stage 1 and 2 (no fit×stage difference between them).

**Job choice** (the behavioral outcome): confidence intervals overlap — **no predictor is clearly strongest**; **fit is nonsignificant**; **job characteristics** carry the most unique variance. Attraction ≠ choice.

**Moderators:** job characteristics more predictive in **field** studies; recruiter behaviors, process, hiring expectancies, and alternatives produced **larger effects in the lab**. (Also tested single-source bias and publication status.)

## Practical implications (the recruiting-dollar answer)

- **First dollar → foster [[Perceived Fit (Recruitment)|perceptions of fit]]** through message content (fit feedback; tailoring info to applicant preferences; RJPs and site visits; ethical emphasis on *true* fit).
- **Incremental dollars → recruiter behaviors early** (train/select for **personableness** — the single strongest recruiter behavior), then **organizational characteristics** (Stage 2) and **recruitment process** (later stages).
- Good news: firms can't easily change org size/location/image, but **can** train recruiters, build better web tools, and improve process. Within job characteristics, **the job itself matters more than total compensation** ("more than being shown the money"); within org characteristics, **image beats specific attributes**.
