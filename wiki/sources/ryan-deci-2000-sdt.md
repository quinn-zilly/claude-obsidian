# Ryan & Deci (2000) — SDT Overview

*American Psychologist*, Vol. 55, No. 1, pp. 68–78. Canonical overview of [[Self-Determination Theory]].

## Core Argument

Human activity/passivity not fixed by disposition or biology — largely a function of social context. SDT specifies which conditions foster vs. thwart three innate psychological needs ([[Autonomy (SDT)]], [[Competence (SDT)]], [[Relatedness (SDT)]]). Need satisfaction → self-motivation + well-being. Need thwarting → diminished motivation + ill-being.

## Three Parts of the Paper

### 1. Intrinsic Motivation & CET

[[Cognitive Evaluation Theory]] (Deci & Ryan 1985): subtheory explaining variability in intrinsic motivation.

Key findings:
- Positive feedback → ↑ intrinsic motivation (mediated by perceived competence; Vallerand & Reid 1984)
- Competence alone not sufficient — must co-occur with sense of **autonomy** (internal perceived locus of causality)
- All expected tangible rewards contingent on task performance **reliably undermine** intrinsic motivation (Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999 meta-analysis; N = large; despite Eisenberger & Cameron 1996 contrary claims)
- Threats, deadlines, directives, pressured evaluations, imposed goals → ↓ intrinsic motivation (external locus)
- Choice, feeling acknowledgment, self-direction → ↑ intrinsic motivation
- Autonomy-supportive teachers → greater student intrinsic motivation, curiosity, challenge-seeking
- Controlling teachers → lose initiative; learn less effectively on conceptual/creative tasks
- **Relatedness** plays distal supporting role: secure attachment → more exploratory behavior (Frodi et al. 1985); cold/uncaring teachers → ↓ intrinsic motivation (Ryan & Grolnick 1986)

> [!key-insight] CET Summary
> Social environments support or thwart intrinsic motivation via the three needs. Autonomy + competence = primary levers. Relatedness = necessary background condition.

### 2. Self-Regulation of Extrinsic Motivation & OIT

[[Organismic Integration Theory]] (Deci & Ryan 1985): details forms of extrinsic motivation and conditions promoting internalization/integration.

**Self-determination continuum** (left → right):

| Type | Regulatory Style | Locus | Processes |
|------|-----------------|-------|-----------|
| Amotivation | Non-regulation | Impersonal | Nonintentional, lack of control |
| Extrinsic | External regulation | External | Compliance, rewards/punishments |
| Extrinsic | Introjected regulation | Somewhat external | Ego-involvement, contingent self-esteem |
| Extrinsic | Identified regulation | Somewhat internal | Personal importance, conscious valuing |
| Extrinsic | Integrated regulation | Internal | Congruence, synthesis with self |
| Intrinsic | Intrinsic regulation | Internal | Interest, enjoyment, inherent satisfaction |

**Key distinction**: Autonomy ≠ independence. Autonomy = volition; can accompany collectivist or dependent acts.

**Facilitating integration**: Three conditions needed:
1. **Relatedness** — internalization begins because behaviors are valued by significant others one wants to feel connected to
2. **Competence** — efficacy supports adopting valued activities
3. **Autonomy** — critical for integration specifically; deep holistic processing (Kuhl & Fuhrmann 1998) requires freedom from excessive external pressure

**Applied outcomes of greater internalization:**
- Education: more engagement, better performance, lower dropout, higher quality learning
- Health care: medication adherence, weight loss maintenance, glucose control, addiction treatment
- Other domains: religion, physical exercise, political activity, environmental activism, intimate relationships

### 3. Psychological Needs and Well-Being

Basic psychological need definition: energizing state where satisfaction → health/well-being; deprivation → pathology/ill-being.

**Intrinsic vs. extrinsic aspirations** (T. Kasser & Ryan 1993, 1996):
- Intrinsic goals (affiliation, personal growth, community) → directly satisfy needs → ↑ well-being
- Extrinsic goals (wealth, fame, image) → at best indirectly satisfy needs → ↓ well-being indicators
- Replicated in Russian sample (Ryan, Chirkov et al. 1999) — cross-cultural generalizability

**Cold, controlling maternal care** → materialistic orientations in teens (Kasser, Ryan, Zax & Sameroff 1995) — need deprivation → substitutive extrinsic goal pursuit.

**Within-person daily fluctuations**: Reis, Sheldon et al. (in press) — all three needs independently predicted daily well-being variability.

## Implications

Applied everywhere: education, health care, work, sport, religion, psychotherapy. Practitioners who attend to need support vs. deprivation can diagnose alienation vs. engagement and facilitate both achievement and well-being.
