# Pierce & Aguinis (2013) — The Too-Much-of-a-Good-Thing Effect in Management

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> Proposes a **formalized [[Meta-Theory (Management)|meta-theory]]** — the **[[Too-Much-of-a-Good-Thing Effect|too-much-of-a-good-thing (TMGT) effect]]** — to reconcile a growing pile of countertheoretical findings across management. Claim: **all** seemingly monotonic positive antecedent→outcome relations reach a **context-specific inflection point** ($I$), after which they turn asymptotic or negative, producing overall **curvilinearity** (usually inverted-U). Rooted in the philosophical **[[Doctrine of the Mean]]** (Aristotle's Golden Mean, Confucius). The field's default "more is better" linear assumption is the deficient heuristic the meta-theory corrects.

## The paradox it resolves

For a beneficial antecedent $X$ and outcome $Y$, three theses each seem tenable but can't all be true at once:

- **Thesis A:** more $X$ → more $Y$ ($r_{xy} > 0$)
- **Thesis B:** more $X$ → no change ($r_{xy} = 0$)
- **Thesis C:** more $X$ → less $Y$ ($r_{xy} < 0$)

Resolution (Poole & Van de Ven methods 2 & 4): separate the theses **by level of $X$**. A holds when $X < I$; B holds near $X \approx I$; C may hold when $X > I$. If Thesis C confirms → **inverted U**; if not → **asymptotic**. The location of $I$ is context-specific and is the domain of relation-specific theorizing.

## Eight illustrative examples

| Subfield | Example | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| OB | Leadership (initiating structure & consideration) | Curvilinear; extremes ↑ grievances/turnover (Fleishman 1998; Ames & Flynn 2007) |
| OB | Conscientiousness | Inverted-U with performance; inflection lower for low-complexity jobs (Whetzel et al. 2010; Le et al. 2011) |
| HRM | Enriched [[Job Characteristics Model\|job design]] | Diminishing then negative returns; moderated by growth-need strength (Champoux; Xie & Johns 1995) |
| HRM | Experience in selection | Curvilinear; inverted-U for low-complexity, asymptotic for high-complexity jobs (Sturman 2003) |
| Entrepreneurship | New venture planning | Diminishing returns; inflection ≈ 136–143 hrs prep (Chrisman et al. 2005) |
| Entrepreneurship | Firm growth rate | Too-fast growth → failure; excessive growth in 70% of major failures (Probst & Raisch 2005) |
| Strategy | Diversification | Inverted-U with performance (Lang & Stulz 1994; Qian et al. 2008) |
| Strategy | Organizational slack | "Positive up to a point, then negative" (Bourgeois 1981; Tan & Peng 2003) |

Plus many "additional illustrations": OCB, org identification, self-efficacy, creativity, passion, board size (5–7 optimal), R&D spend, product variety, trust/autonomy, group size.

## Modeling the effect

TMGT is a **special case of moderation where $X$ moderates its own relation with $Y$**:

$$ Y = b_0 + b_1 X + b_2 X^2 + e $$

with the inflection point at $-b_1 / 2b_2$. A fuller model combines a true moderator $Z$ with the quadratic:

$$ Y = b_0 + b_1 X + b_2 Z + b_3 XZ + b_4 X^2 + e $$

## Shape follows Aristotle's taxonomy

Aristotle distinguished **actions** and **passions** (subject to the mean → inverted-U) from **things good in themselves** (e.g., general mental ability, wisdom → asymptotic, no downturn). This predicts *which* curvilinear shape to expect.

## Implications

Theory development should specify not just *whether* $X$ relates to $Y$ but *where* the inflection point sits and *what shape* the curve takes. Expands the role of moderators. For practice: reject reflexive "maximize the antecedent"; identify context-specific optima.

> [!note] Contrast with linear-model defenders
> Hastie & Dawes (2001) and Einhorn & Hogarth (1975) argue linear models efficiently approximate monotonic phenomena and that ignoring nonlinearity aids practical utility. Pierce & Aguinis concede linear models can be more practical in narrow cases (e.g., predictive judgment; cf. [[Mechanical vs Clinical Prediction]], [[Bootstrapping (Improper Linear Models)]]) but argue understanding curvilinearity is critical overall.
