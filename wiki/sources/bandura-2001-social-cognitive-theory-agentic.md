# bandura-2001-social-cognitive-theory-agentic

Nav: [[index]] | [[Social Cognitive Theory]] | [[Learning & Development MOC]]

> [!abstract] Source summary
> Bandura's synthesizing statement of **social cognitive theory (SCT)** framed around **[[Human Agency|human agency]]**: "To be an agent is to intentionally make things happen by one's actions." Reviews the paradigm shifts in psychology (behaviorism → linear computationalism → connectionism), argues each granted humans little agentic capability, and positions SCT's **emergent interactive agency** against eliminative materialism. Lays out the four **[[Human Agency|core features of agency]]**, the **[[Modes of Human Agency|three modes of agency]]** (personal, proxy, collective), **[[Agentic Management of Fortuity]]**, and **[[Triadic Reciprocal Causation]]**.

## Core argument

Bandura rejects reductive accounts (cognition as subpersonal network activation; consciousness as epiphenomenon) — "Why would an epiphenomenal consciousness that can do nothing evolve and endure?" People are **agents of experiences, not just undergoers of experiences**. Thought is an *emergent* brain activity that exerts **downward causation**; emergent properties differ qualitatively from constituents (Bunge's water analogy: fluidity ≠ aggregate of H and O).

## Four core features of human agency

Detailed on [[Human Agency]]:

1. **Intentionality** — a proactive commitment to a future course of action (not mere prediction). Requires coordinated plans; joint action requires shared intentions.
2. **Forethought** — temporal extension of agency; future events represented cognitively become current motivators. Anchors [[Outcome Expectancies|outcome expectations]] and hierarchically structured (distal + proximal) goals. See [[Forethought and Anticipatory Self-Guidance]].
3. **Self-reactiveness** — self-regulation linking thought to action via self-monitoring, personal standards, and corrective self-reactions. Includes **moral agency** (inhibitive + proactive) and its dark twin, [[Moral Disengagement]].
4. **Self-reflectiveness** — the metacognitive capacity to reflect on one's own functioning. **Efficacy beliefs are the foundation of human agency** — the most central, pervasive mechanism; they govern choice, effort, persistence, and resilience.

## Three modes of agency

See [[Modes of Human Agency]]: **direct personal**, **proxy** (get others with resources/expertise/power to act on one's behest — relies on perceived social efficacy), and **collective** (socially coordinated interdependent effort; see [[Collective Efficacy]]). Collective efficacy is an **emergent group-level property**, not the sum of members' efficacy — yet "there is no group mind" independent of individuals' beliefs and actions.

## Key claims

- **Self-efficacy occupies a pivotal role in SCT's causal structure** because it affects adaptation both directly and through other determinants (goals, outcome expectations, affect). Cites meta-analyses (Multon et al. 1991; Stajkovic & Luthans 1998; Holden 1991).
- **Agentic management of fortuity**: chance encounters shape life paths, but people convert fortuity into opportunity via cultivated interests/competencies. "Chance favors only the prepared mind" (Pasteur). See [[Agentic Management of Fortuity]].
- **Triadic reciprocal causation**: personal, behavioral, and environmental factors are interacting determinants (see [[Triadic Reciprocal Causation]]). Three environment types: imposed, selected, constructed.
- **Rejects contentious dualisms**: personal agency vs. social structure; self-efficacy vs. communality; individualism vs. collectivism. Efficacy is valued not for individualism but because it is vital regardless of whether goals are pursued individually or collectively. Gandhi cited as commanding self-efficacy in service of collective/communal ends.
- **Culture**: efficacy beliefs are functionally valuable across individualistic and collectivistic cultures; culture shapes *how* they are developed and exercised. People perform best when psychological orientation is **congruent** with the social system.

## Key insight

> [!key-insight]
> Efficacy beliefs are the foundation of human agency: "Unless people believe they can produce desired results and forestall detrimental ones by their actions, they have little incentive to act or to persevere in the face of difficulties." This is the through-line connecting SCT to [[Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)]], [[schunk-usher-2012-social-cognitive-theory-motivation|Schunk & Usher's motivation account]], and the metacognitive deficits in [[kruger-dunning-1999-unskilled-and-unaware|Kruger & Dunning]].
