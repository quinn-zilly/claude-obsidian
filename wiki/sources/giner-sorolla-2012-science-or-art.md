# Giner-Sorolla (2012) — Science or Art?

*Perspectives on Psychological Science* 7(6): 562–571

## Core Argument

The replication crisis in psychology is a **structural, information-economic problem**, not primarily a moral one. The bottleneck (too many studies, too few publication outlets) forces researchers to master the *art* of producing perfect-looking results. This aesthetic pressure — not just fraud or intentional misconduct — is the root cause the 1970s solutions failed to address and why they failed again.

> [!key-insight] The Publication Bottleneck as Root Cause
> Articles cannot pass through by showing theoretical meaning and methodological rigor alone; their results must *appear* to support the hypothesis perfectly. This favors aesthetic criteria over scientific ones.

## The Two Crises Framework

Giner-Sorolla draws an explicit parallel between the **1970s methods crisis** and the **2012 replication crisis**:

| 1970s Crisis | 2012 Crisis |
|---|---|
| Lykken (1968) — single significant results unconvincing | Simmons et al. (2011) — 60% false positives via analytic flexibility |
| Cyril Burt fraud; replications suppressed | Stapel fraud; Bem precognition (9 studies) |
| Post-crisis fix: require multi-study papers | Multi-study papers easily fabricated (Stapel) or HARKed |
| Journals for null results founded (died quickly) | Journal of Articles in Support of Null Hypothesis (1–7 articles/year) |

The post-1970s fix (requiring multiple studies) increased the study count in JPSP from 2.20 (2000) to 3.33 (2008), but did not solve the underlying problem — it just raised the aesthetic bar.

## Three Aesthetic Criteria Distorting Science

### 1. Statistical Perfection (p < .05)

The p < .05 threshold has become a **ritual hallmark** (Gigerenzer, 2004), a symbol of perfection rather than an inferential tool. The opening thought experiment makes this vivid: a three-study paper with joint p = .0074 is objectively stronger evidence than a two-study paper with joint p = .012, yet the latter looks better because each individual study clears .05.

Practices that create the *appearance* of perfection without the reality (from Simmons et al. 2011):
- Drop non-significant measures/studies that trend in the right direction
- Try multiple analyses, covariates, moderators; report only those that "work"
- Run additional participants after failing to reach significance
- Apply one-tailed tests selectively

These practices inflate false-positive rates to ~60%.

### 2. Narrative Conformity (HARKing)

Research papers must conform to an "easily processed, self-promoting narrative format." The particular temptation is [[HARKing]] — representing post-hoc hypotheses as a priori. Kerr (1998) identified this; Giner-Sorolla notes it continues undiminished. The pressure is **aesthetic and presentational**: admitting a midcourse correction is a "fatal flaw" narratively even when scientifically honest.

> The p-value is only valid for a pre-specified test. A hypothesis derived from data it will "test" has no valid inferential meaning.

### 3. Novelty (suppressing replication)

Novelty has an inverted-U effect on preference (Sluckin et al. 1983): journals require enough novelty to be interesting. This creates a **moral hazard**: direct replications (the least novel paper) are effectively unpublishable, even in small journals. Even nonreplications are easier to publish because they suggest boundary conditions. The cost is that independent replication — the crucial check on validity — is structurally excluded.

## The Publication Bottleneck

The bottleneck is quantified via SPSP attendance vs. publication slots in social/personality psychology:
- SPSP attendance: 812 (2000) → 1,500 (2003) → 3,500 (2010) → ~4,000 (2012)
- Articles per head: 1.32 (2003) → 0.90 (2010)

Under the **Anna Karenina principle** (Bornmann & Marx, 2012): when evaluating papers in a very tight market, "nothing wrong with it" beats "highest overall excellence." Aesthetic flaws (messy results, acknowledged uncertainty, replication studies) become disqualifying in a conjunction-rule evaluation. This is where novelty and statistical perfection enter as tie-breakers.

## Why Prior Solutions Failed

The root cause — information economics — was never addressed. Under tight constraints:
- It is irrational to report mistakes or bury studies
- Replication outlets wither because publishing in them is career-negative
- "Smarter criteria" (replace p < .05 with effect sizes, etc.) still leave room for suppression and have repeatedly failed to stick (example: effect size reporting still inconsistently required a decade after Wilkinson 1999 recommendation)

## Solutions Proposed

Giner-Sorolla rejects both "smarter criteria" and "more journals for messy results" as insufficient. He argues for **opening the bottleneck** — creating professionally recognized arenas for all results:

**Voluntary (carrots):** Following [[nosek-bar-anan-2012-scientific-utopia-i|Nosek & Bar-Anan (2012)]], an online post-publication review structure where sharing results (regardless of significance) acts as a tiebreaker in career evaluation, with indirect benefits (recognition, reputation, timeliness).

**Compelled (sticks):** If psychology doesn't self-regulate, biomedical research precedents suggest top-down regulation: funding agencies could treat failure to disseminate all results as research fraud; IRB approval could be conditioned on full reporting.

> [!key-insight] The Fairness Argument
> Two researchers with equally brilliant ideas may get different empirical results by chance. It is fair that the lucky one gets the Nobel. But when the bottleneck is narrow enough that *only* researchers who reliably produce significant results can survive, the system selects for aesthetic skill over scientific accuracy. This gives researchers moral cover for "cleaning up" results — and structural pressure overrides individual ethics.
