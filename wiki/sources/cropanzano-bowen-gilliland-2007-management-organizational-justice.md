# Cropanzano, Bowen & Gilliland (2007) — The Management of Organizational Justice

> [!key-insight]
> A **practitioner-facing** synthesis: justice is not just measured, it must be **managed**. Frames justice as the "glue" that binds employees to employers and a source of **sustainable competitive advantage** (a "culture of justice"). Walks five managerial tasks — hiring, reward systems, conflict management, layoffs, performance appraisal — and shows how to build fairness into each.

## Core framing

Businesses are economic institutions but not *only* economic. Justice = "members' sense of the moral propriety of how they are treated." A **descriptive** (not prescriptive) concept: I/O scientists study what people *believe* is just, not what philosophically *is* just. Producing justice requires managers to **take the employee's perspective**.

Managers confuse **outcome favorability** (judgment of personal worth) with **outcome justice** (judgment of moral propriety). They are distinct, correlated only .19–.49.

## Why employees care (three reasons)

- **Long-range benefits (control model)** — justice lets employees predict/control future outcomes; quasi-economic self-interest (Tyler & Smith).
- **Social considerations (group-value model)** — just treatment signals respect and belonging; the more committed you are, the more injustice hurts (Brockner et al. 1992: most-committed reacted worst to layoffs).
- **Ethical considerations** — people care about justice as morally right; will take risks / seek retribution even when only *witnessing* injustice (see [[Deontic Justice Perspective]]).

## Three components (Table 1)

See [[Four Justice Dimensions]] / [[Distributive Justice]] / [[Procedural Justice]] / [[Interactional Justice]]. This paper uses the **three-component** structure (interactional = interpersonal + informational).

- **Distributive** — appropriateness of outcomes. Three allocation rules: **equity** (to each by contribution → individual motivation), **equality** (to each the same → esprit de corps), **need** (to each by urgency). Rooted in Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* → Adams' (1965) equity theory. Over-reward effect (Greenberg 1988 office-status study).
- **Procedural** — appropriateness of the allocation process. Leventhal's rules: consistency, lack of bias, accuracy, representation, correction, ethics. Enables the **[[Fair Process Effect]]** and institutional legitimacy.
- **Interactional** — appropriateness of treatment from authorities: **interpersonal** (dignity, courtesy, respect) + **informational** (truthful, adequate justifications).

## The interaction of components (three bites at the apple)

The three components **interact**: ill effects of injustice are mitigated if *at least one* component is upheld. Goldman (2003): employees filed discrimination legal claims most when distributive, procedural, AND interactional justice were all low; any one being high dropped litigation risk. → Organizations get "three bites at the apple."

## Consequences of justice

Meta-analytically established (Cohen-Charash & Spector 2001; Colquitt et al. 2001; Viswesvaran & Ones 2002):

- Builds **trust and [[Organizational Commitment|commitment]]** (procedural-justice↔trust up to r ≈ .60).
- Improves **[[Task Performance|job performance]]** — stronger in the field than lab; interactional justice → high-quality relationships → performance.
- Fosters **[[Organizational Citizenship Behavior|OCBs]]** — reciprocated only to the party that treated one justly (Liden et al. 2003 contingent-worker study: procedural justice from the *contracting firm*, not the *agency*, drove OCB toward the firm).
- Builds **customer satisfaction/loyalty** — "spillover" of just treatment to customers (Bowen, Gilliland & Folger 1999; Masterson 2001).

## Building justice into five management systems (Table 2)

1. **Selection / job candidates — the justice paradox.** The most valid screening tools (cognitive ability, personality tests) are seen as *least* fair; unstructured interviews (low validity) feel *fairest*. Fixes: use work samples / simulations (valid *and* fair); concrete test items; combined structured+unstructured interviews; interactional justice (honest info, timely feedback).
2. **Reward systems — the two-factor model.** Balance individual motivation (equity) vs. group cohesion (equality). High wage dispersion hurt collaboration/productivity (Pfeffer & Langton 1993). Procedural + interactional justice let firms maintain external equity despite internal inequality (McFarlin & Sweeney 1992; Greenberg 1993 pay-cut study: gentle 90-min announcement → 80% less theft, 15× fewer resignations vs. curt 15-min).
3. **Conflict management — you don't have to win.** Arbitration is autocratic and risks distributive injustice, but any one justice component present improves overall appraisal. "If you can't give people the outcome they want, at least give them a fair process."
4. **Layoffs — softening hardship.** Distributive injustice is a given; procedural + interactional justice reduce derogation and litigation. Lind, Greenberg, Scott & Welchans (2000): 66% of unjustly-treated layoff victims considered litigation vs. 16% of justly-treated. Apologies help (contra legal advice). Survivor guilt reduced by good explanations + advance warning.
5. **Performance appraisal — the [[Due Process Approach to Appraisal|due-process approach]].** Move from the "test metaphor" to due process: **adequate notice**, **just hearing**, **judgment based on evidence** (Folger, Konovsky & Cropanzano 1992). Taylor et al. (1995): due-process system preferred despite *lower* ratings. Caution: added safeguards can raise expectations (Mesch & Dalton 1992 grievance-spike).
