# Kraiger & Ford 2021 — The Science of Workplace Instruction

**Full title:** The Science of Workplace Instruction: Learning and Development Applied to Work
**Authors:** [[Kurt Kraiger]] (U. Memphis), [[J. Kevin Ford]] (Michigan State)
**Venue:** *Annual Review of Organizational Psychology and Organizational Behavior*, 2021, Vol. 8

## Core Argument

I-O psychology training research has drifted from its roots in learning theory. The authors propose a new *science of workplace instruction* that bridges the science of training (organizational factors, effectiveness) and the science of learning (cognitive mechanisms, encoding/retrieval). The bridge is **instructional principles** — empirically supported propositions that guide training design regardless of method or medium.

## Training Systems Framework

Two interlocking frameworks:

**Figure 1 — Full system:**
- **Instructional events** (principles, methods, media) → **Learning events** (encoding, organizing, retrieving) → **Learning outcomes** (affective, knowledge, skills) → **Instructional outcomes** (transfer, performance, effectiveness)

**Figure 2 — Core elements (intersection model):**
- Three foundational elements: **Learner**, **Instructional Principles**, **Training Delivery**
- Research frontiers lie at the intersections: Learner × Principles, Principles × Delivery, Learner × Delivery

## The Learner

- Science of learning: ignores individual differences; principles generalize across learners
- Science of training: individual differences (motivation, GMA) as predictors/moderators
- Science of workplace instruction: individual variability matters only insofar as it triggers design interventions; well-designed instruction works for *all* learners

## Five Core Instructional Principles

See [[Instructional Principles]] for full table. Summary:

1. **Organize content** — coherence, contiguity, advanced organizers
2. **Optimize sequencing** — scaffolding, adaptive difficulty, interleaving
3. **Engage learner in own learning** — generative effects, metacognitive prompts, retrieval practice (testing effect)
4. **Conduct effective practice** — variability, spaced practice, identical elements
5. **Develop past initial mastery** — knowledge of results, feedback/feedforward, overlearning

**Key meta-finding:** Training methods show large heterogeneity in effect sizes across studies. Much of this variance is explained by whether sound instructional principles were incorporated — not by which method was used.

## What Doesn't Work

**Learning styles (meshing hypothesis):** No empirical support. Pashler et al. (2008) found no evidence; Aslaksen & Lorås (2018) meta-analysis confirmed no replicable effect. Well-designed instruction works for all modality preferences.

**Age-specific instruction:** Older adults do show cognitive processing differences, but ATI (aptitude-treatment interaction) effects are inconsistent. Age-inclusive instruction using standard principles benefits all age groups, possibly more for older learners.

**Aptitude-treatment interactions generally:** 50+ years of research, inconsistent findings. Kowollik et al. (2010) meta-analysis: small, inconsistent interactions for GMA × structure. Not cost-effective to design for ATIs.

## Self-Directed Learning

Organizations push more responsibility to learners — but learners are systematically poor self-regulators:
- Prefer massed practice over spacing (metacognitive illusion)
- Believe rereading beats retrieval practice
- Overconfident in mastery

Implications: Don't just give learner control; design principles *into* the system with structured choice (scaffolded perceived control). See [[Self-Directed Learning]].

## Synthetic Learning Environments

VR, simulations, serious games show meta-analytic effectiveness for physical/skill tasks; mixed/null for cognitive tasks. Key insight: **media ≠ method effectiveness**. Clark (1994): "media will never influence learning." Sitzmann et al. (2006): web-based vs. classroom equivalent when design attributes held constant. See [[Synthetic Learning Environments]].
