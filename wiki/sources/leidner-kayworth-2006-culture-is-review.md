# Leidner & Kayworth (2006) — Culture in IS Research

> [!abstract] One-line
> A review of 82 IT-culture studies that merges the previously separate national-culture and organizational-culture IS literatures under a **values-based** lens, distills **six research themes**, and builds a new **theory of IT-culture conflict** (system, contribution, vision) whose resolution drives gradual cultural change.

*MIS Quarterly* 30(2), June 2006, pp. 357–399. [JSTOR](https://www.jstor.org/stable/25148735).

## Why it matters

Culture (national, organizational, group) shapes whether IT is adopted, how it is used, and what outcomes it produces — yet "culture" is notoriously hard to research given ~164 competing definitions (Kroeber & Kluckhohn 1952). The paper's move is to standardize on **values** as the tractable layer of culture (per Schein's three levels: assumptions → values → artifacts), then use that lens to integrate two literatures that had evolved in isolation.

## Method

Review of **82 articles** (51 national-level / 31 organizational-or-subunit) from 38 journals, two dissertations, one working paper, searched 2003 and updated 2004. Over 60% of national-culture studies used one or more of [[Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions]].

## The six themes of IT-culture research

1. **Culture & IS development (ISD)** — value differences yield different design approaches and different risk/escalation behavior (e.g., uncertainty avoidance → project-risk perception; individualism → willingness to report bad news).
2. **Culture, IT adoption & diffusion** — uncertainty avoidance generally dampens adoption; the dominant construct that emerges is **fit** between a group's values and the values embedded in the technology. See [[Cultural Fit]].
3. **Culture, IT use & outcomes** — same IT is used differently across cultures (Hong Kong social use vs. US informational use of the Internet); at the org level the question shifts to *which* values predict satisfaction/success.
4. **Culture, IT management & strategy** — national values shape IS service philosophies, employment structures, and information-ethics behavior (e.g., software piracy higher in individualistic societies — Husted 2000).
5. **IT's impact on culture** — under-studied; most work treats culture as stable. Exceptions (Madon 1992; Walsham 2002; Doherty & Doig 2003) show IT can gradually transform values.
6. **IT culture** — the values a group attributes to IT itself ("IT values"). IT is not value-neutral; it is symbolic and values-laden (Robey & Markus 1984; Scholz 1990). See [[IT Culture]].

## The contribution: theory of IT-culture conflict

The paper's central theoretical contribution is [[IT-Culture Conflict]] — three conflict types arising among **group values**, **values embedded in a specific system**, and **IT values**:

- **System conflict** — group values vs. values embedded in a *specific* IT.
- **Contribution conflict** — group values vs. the group's general *IT values* (drawn from Bourdieu's theory of capital/distinction).
- **Vision conflict** — general IT values vs. values embedded in a specific system.

Five proposition-sets follow (P1a–d system; P2a–d contribution; P3a–d vision; P4 managers can reduce all conflict by promoting shared IT values; P5 conflict emergence + resolution produces cultural change over time). Resolution reorients values — IT thereby "infuses" culture, starting at lower (subunit) levels before national values shift.

## Notable secondary findings

- **Kaarst-Brown's five IT cultural patterns** (fearful, controlled, revered, demystified, integrated) — an *assumptions*-based (vs. values-based) account of IT culture.
- ~2/3 of empirical studies used Myerson & Martin's **integration perspective** (culture as uniform/persistent), neglecting subgroup conflict and contradictory outcomes.
- A taxonomy of **information/IT values** as polar pairs (Table 4 in the original).

> [!key-insight]
> The field had been asking "does culture predict *whether* IT is adopted?" Leidner & Kayworth argue culture better predicts the *timing*, *breadth*, and *objective* of adoption — and that the higher-leverage question is how value **conflicts** surface and resolve.
