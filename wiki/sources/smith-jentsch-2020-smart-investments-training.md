# Smith-Jentsch (2020) — Making Smart Investments in Training

## Summary

A practitioner-facing roadmap for making evidence-based training investment decisions. Organized around three problems: (1) estimating whether off-the-shelf training will work for your organization, (2) diagnosing why previously effective training has declined, and (3) protecting training investments proactively. Core thesis: organizations consistently overlook implementation details that determine whether training produces returns.

> [!key-insight] Core Claim
> "The devil is in the details." Training ROI is determined not just by the content of training but by the specific implementation conditions under which effectiveness was established — and whether those conditions can be replicated.

---

## Part 1: Will It Work for Us? (Inter-Organizational Benchmarking)

### Evidence Standards
Rank evidence quality (weakest → strongest):
1. Anecdotal / interview quotes
2. Reactions (enjoyment, perceived usefulness, self-confidence)
3. Learning (knowledge, attitudes, skills — pre/post or vs. control group)
4. On-the-job behavior (specific to training content)
5. Results (sales, productivity, readiness)

**Critical caveat**: Learning reflects what employees *can do*, not what they *will do*. On-the-job behavior evidence is necessary for transfer.

Best evidence = random assignment + control group + quantitative measures.

### Key Benchmarking Dimensions

**Training Needs Fit:**
- Job analysis comparison: Are KSAs required the same?
- Face validity: Do materials look relevant to *your* trainees' vocabulary, tasks, examples? (Smith-Jentsch: using "team" vs. "crew" drove immediate disengagement)
- Prior knowledge match: Are your trainees at the same pre-training level as those who benefitted?

**Implementation Fit:**
- Internal marketing approach (who advocated for it, how it was communicated)
- Length, number of sessions, mode of delivery
- Opportunities for active practice (role plays, simulations) — the first thing organizations cut
- Class size and social composition
- Instructor characteristics (credibility, familiarity, belief in training)
- Degree of customization (terminology, examples, case studies)

> [!key-insight] Active Practice Warning
> Research shows active practice producing verbal responses is critical for skill acquisition. Reducing practice (e.g., asking for volunteers while the rest observe) is highly likely to reduce effectiveness — and those who volunteer are least likely to need practice.

## Part 2: Why Has Our Training Stopped Working? (Intra-Organizational Benchmarking)

### Diagnostic Framework — Three Root Causes

**1. Training Needs Have Changed**
- Task procedures streamlined or changed
- Supporting technologies added
- Co-located → virtual teams
- New hire KSA profiles shifted (selection changes, applicant pool changes)
- Upstream pipeline courses changed (creating redundancies, omissions, or conflicts)

**2. Implementation Has Changed**
- Instructor turnover: initial instructors have mission ownership; successors "fix what isn't broken"
- Physical/social setting changed (on-site vs. off-site; class size; intact teams vs. strangers)
- Mode shift to e-learning: breaks intellectual flow, adds distractions, reduces interaction
- Contingent workforce replacing permanent trainers

**3. Transfer Environment Has Changed**
- Performance appraisal criteria no longer include training-related competencies
- Timing of training relative to job experience changed (Navy officers example: classroom before shipboard experience was less effective)
- Supervisory support weakened — key moderator of transfer; supervisors need familiarity with what their subordinates were trained on

## Part 3: Protecting Training Investments (Proactive Recommendations)

Smith-Jentsch's 20 recommendations (condensed):

**Look Before You Leap (vendor due diligence)**
- Require quantitative evidence; ask about sample, design, what was measured
- Compare your employees' characteristics and tasks to prior beneficiaries
- Ask about implementation variations and their effects

**Know Thyself (internal monitoring)**
- Measure KSAs in formal appraisal systems to identify who needs what
- Continually monitor training effectiveness with consistent metrics across cohorts

**Connect the Dots (cross-functional communication)**
- Formal communication between training program owners in the same pipeline
- Alert training when recruitment/selection changes affect new-hire competencies
- Consider training impact before changing performance appraisal or job design

**Document Your Recipe for Success**
- Record *why* key delivery decisions were made (length, practice types, timing)
- Invest in train-the-trainer programs; video-record the training in purest form
- Document instructor changes over time

**Set the Stage for Transfer**
- Measure and reward behaviors you train
- Provide supervisors with complementary training
- Develop customized job aids for on-the-job application
