# Sechrest & Figueredo (1993) — Program Evaluation

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]] | [[Home MOC]]

*Annual Review of Psychology* chapter (44:645–674). Reviews program evaluation not by cataloguing findings (deliberately — see [[Evaluation Is Not a Field]]) but through the lens of **behavioral / social ecology**: the discipline's evolution is a *selective ecological adaptation* to a changing social environment, not just an inexorable march of scientific progress. Written as a partially complementary companion to Shadish, Cook & Leviton's *Foundations of Program Evaluation* (1991). OCR'd from a 32-page scanned PDF (the vault's `.md` extraction was empty).

## Two world-views in collision

Sechrest & Figueredo add two "ecological" forces to the field's standard history.

- **Newtonian vs. Darwinian paradigms.** Social science inherited a *physicalistic, mechanistic, elementistic, universalistic* paradigm (Fechner, Weber, Wundt) — the ideal of the randomized controlled single-variable experiment, "maxmincon" (Kerlinger), institutionalized Fisherian null-hypothesis testing. This paradigm has *chronically inadequate external validity* and is "catastrophically inappropriate to a biological science" (Mayr). Evaluators trained for the "icy depths of interplanetary space" instead found a **Darwinian jungle**: everything alive, competitive, strategic, deceptive — because information is power. Their memorable path-model illustration: an intervention *I₁* aimed at determinant *D₁* to reduce outcome *O₁* ignores that *O₁* has other determinants *D₂/D₃*, that *I₁* may affect them counterproductively, and that outcomes carry different utilities *U₁/U₂/U₃* for different stakeholders. Straight-line Newtonian thinking fails; regulating the system requires ecological (Darwinian) thinking. See [[Ecological Paradigm in Evaluation]].
- **Postindustrial social revolution.** The field launched (1960s) just as society shifted from an industrial/Taylorite mode to a postindustrial, cybernetic, information-based mode. Consequences: the "rational model" of centralized policymaking gave way to *decentralized, multi-stakeholder* decision-making; the producer/consumer line blurred (consumer feedback became integral); the evaluator moved from impartial outside "intelligence agent" to an embedded *participant and stakeholder*; heterogeneity of treatment implementation stopped being decried as a threat to validity and became an exploitable *source of variance*.

## Evaluation is not a field

Evaluation is an *enterprise* for deciding worth, not a field of inquiry: no cumulative substantive body of knowledge attaches to it, so reviewing "programs found effective" would satisfy only idle curiosity. Progress is measured by advances in **conceptualization and method**, not by positive/negative findings. The authors document weak cumulation (few recent citations in evaluation texts vs. a health-psychology comparison) and note core journals now prefer methodological/conceptual articles, relegating actual evaluation reports to the "fugitive literature." See [[Evaluation Is Not a Field]].

## The qualitative movement — a skeptical reading

Interest in qualitative evaluation grew into a "movement" (Patton 1978/1980; Guba & Lincoln's "fourth generation evaluation"). Sechrest & Figueredo argue this partly reflects **despair** at maintaining an analytic program amid social complexity — *unwarranted*, given advances in multivariate methods. They reject the qualitative-vs-quantitative *either/or*: the more useful distinction is Popper's **context of discovery vs. context of justification** ("boldness in conjecture, austerity in refutation"); some qualitative researchers are fixated at the discovery stage. They fault the field for not standardizing qualitative methods (praising grounded theory and GAO case-study frameworks as exemplary because they are *specifiable*). Illustrative case: the **PUSH/Excel** evaluation — the quantitative team found no consistent program across sites; House (qualitative) argued they were wrong to demand cross-site consistency, but never showed the program worked. "Matters of values rather than facts."

## Shadish et al.'s five fundamental issues

Sechrest & Figueredo adopt Shadish, Cook & Leviton's (1991) scheme as the field's organizing frame — see [[Five Fundamental Issues of Evaluation]]:

1. **Social programming** — how programs develop and change → leads to [[Theory-Driven Evaluation]] (Chen; Chen & Rossi). Strong theory can compensate for weak method (Lipsey): better treatments, measures, and predictions; saves conceptual and statistical degrees of freedom. "For many social problems… the poverty of theory will be as evident as the theory of poverty."
2. **Knowledge construction** (epistemology) — the quantitative/qualitative dispute; early evaluation assumed *complementary* use of both (Webb et al. "Approximations to knowledge"); what's new is the insistence that qualitative can *substitute* for quantitative.
3. **Valuing** — can evaluation be value-free? Some (Guba & Lincoln, House, Sirotnik) say never and urge partisan, justice-serving evaluation; the authors warn this risks the same partisanship that discredited politically biased program reviews. "Program evaluation may have to begin with values, but it will have to demonstrate objectivity if it is to retain any credibility at all."
4. **Knowledge use** — utilization has been perennially disappointing; much use is *conceptual* ("enlightenment," Weiss) rather than direct. Almost nothing is known about utilization at state/local or private-sector levels.
5. **Evaluation practice** — especially [[Formative vs Summative Evaluation|formative vs. summative]] (Scriven 1967). The authors detect a **drift toward formative** (easier, more comforting, less threatening to the sponsoring organization). Summative evaluation is hard, slow, and often finds near-zero effects (Rossi's "metallic laws": expected impact ≈ 0).

## Program development and prospective evaluation

- **Evaluation succession model** (Tharp & Gallimore 1979): iterative build-test-feedback over ~10 years produced a highly effective reading program for Hawaiian children (like *Sesame Street*'s development). Under-emulated because it demands long funding commitment; the funder said "*We are going to solve this problem*," not "let's see if this idea works." Lesson: learn from mistakes rather than discarding programs (cf. shock incarceration).
- **[[Prospective Evaluation]]** (GAO/PEMD): forecasting a program's likely results *before* implementation. "Prevention is better than cure." Example of foreseeable failure: New York's pay-relatives-to-house-children scheme collapsed on runaway costs.

## The case for multivariate quantitative methods

The review's constructive core: quantitative methods have "caught up" (causal modeling with latent variables, growth-curve analysis, regression-discontinuity, common-factor measurement models). Guidance:

- **Multiple predictors** — beware multicollinearity (unstable, even sign-flipped coefficients) and *alpha slippage* (capitalization on chance). Prefer **common-factor modeling** over stepwise regression for data reduction.
- **Multiple outcomes** — structural-equation / confirmatory path analysis needs strong theory; simultaneous canonical analysis needs little theory but yields uninterpretable composites; **sequential canonical analysis** (Gorsuch & Figueredo) is a middle path that keeps outcomes' separate identity.
- **Multiple utilities** — utility (impact *beyond* the immediate outcome) is the least-developed area; benefit-cost returns disappoint because each extra path multiplies effects down (.5 × .5 = .25; .25 variance → .06). Utilities should be specified and sensitivity-analyzed across stakeholder groups (critical multiplism, Shadish 1989).
- **Multiple replications** — meta-analysis is limited in evaluation (few programs tested at many sites); more useful for understanding a literature than for pooled effect sizes.

Shift advocated: from **ruling out** rival hypotheses (Popperian falsification) toward **ruling in** causes (Einhorn & Hogarth; Cordray) — causality as a *positive* inference, which puts more weight on theory. Also revives Sechrest et al.'s (1979) call to quantify **[[Strength and Integrity of Treatment]]** (dose-response; guards against Type II error in [[Statistical Conclusion Validity]]).

> [!key-insight]
> Program evaluation is not "applied social science with peculiar problems" but a discipline whose problems *expose the failure of mainstream social science* — its Newtonian experimental paradigm has poor external validity in a Darwinian, multi-stakeholder social ecology. The remedy is not a flight to qualitative methods but *stronger theory plus modern multivariate models* that rule causes **in**.
