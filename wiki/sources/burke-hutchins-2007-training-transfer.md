# Burke & Hutchins (2007) — Training Transfer: An Integrative Literature Review

> [!key-insight] Core Claim
> Training transfer is determined by three factor categories: **learner characteristics**, **intervention design & delivery**, and **work environment**. Only ~40–50% of training investments result in sustained performance change. Transfer research must move toward multidimensional, multilevel models assessed with direct behavioral outcomes.

## Overview

Integrative review of ~170 empirical articles across management, HRD, adult learning, psychology, and performance improvement literatures. Follows Torraco (2005) integrative review methodology. Updates Baldwin & Ford (1988) and Ford & Weissbein (1997).

**Transfer problem scope:** Georgenson (1982) — 10% of training → behavioral change. Saks (2002) — 40% fail immediately post-training; 70% falter at 1 year; only 50% of training investments yield org/individual improvement.

## Three-Factor Taxonomy

### 1. Learner Characteristics

| Variable | Empirical Support |
|---|---|
| Cognitive ability | Strong (rc = .43, Colquitt et al. 2000) |
| Self-efficacy | Strong (multiple studies); malleable |
| Pretraining motivation | Strong (r = .45, Facteau et al. 1995) |
| Anxiety / negative affectivity | Strong negative correlation |
| Perceived utility | Strong |
| Organizational commitment | Strong (rc = .45–.61) |
| Career planning | Moderate (rc = .30) |
| Conscientiousness | Mixed (rc = .29 but inconsistent) |
| Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation | Mixed |
| Locus of control | Mixed |
| Motivation to learn/transfer | Minimal direct evidence |
| Extroversion | Minimal direct evidence |

**Key mechanisms:** Self-efficacy is malleable → interventions targeting mastery experiences, modeling, verbal persuasion, arousal increase transfer. High organizational commitment = interested learner who applies new knowledge.

### 2. Intervention Design and Delivery

| Variable | Empirical Support |
|---|---|
| Learning goals | Strong (r = .37, Kontoghiorghes 2001) |
| Content relevance | Strong (r = .61/.45 immediate/1-month, Axtell et al. 1997) |
| Practice & feedback | Strong |
| Behavioral modeling | Strong; mixed models (positive + negative) > positive only |
| Error-based examples | Strong (firefighters, pilots studies) |
| Self-management strategies | Mixed (relapse prevention inconsistent) |
| Active learning | Minimal empirical evidence despite widespread advocacy |
| Overlearning | Moderate; "half-life" = 19 days for cognitive tasks |
| Needs analysis | Minimal empirical despite strong conceptual |
| Technological support (EPSS) | Minimal — critical gap |

**Transfer paradox (van Merrienboer et al. 2006):** Repeated exact-task practice + frequent feedback enhances mastery but does *not* carry to transfer. Whole–part approach + scaffolding better for transfer of complex tasks.

**Self-management:** Goal-setting, action plans, relapse prevention (RP) have conceptual support. RP has 20 years research but inconsistent results — methodological issues (low N, self-report, indistinct comparators).

### 3. Work Environment Influences

| Variable | Empirical Support |
|---|---|
| Transfer climate | Strong (rc = .37, Colquitt et al. 2000); moderates posttraining interventions |
| Peer support | Strong (B = .65, Chiaburu & Marinova 2005) |
| Supervisory support | Strong but mixed; widely replicated |
| Opportunity to perform | Strong |
| Strategic link | Minimal empirical |
| Accountability | Minimal empirical |

**Transfer climate definition (Rouiller & Goldstein 1993):** Situations and consequences that inhibit or facilitate use of trained skills. Features: cues prompting skill use, consequences for correct/incorrect use, peer/supervisor incentives and feedback.

**Key instrument:** LTSI (Holton, Bates & Ruona 2000) — 16-factor, 68-item diagnostic assessing learner/training/org factors. Validated internationally. Does not directly measure transfer outcomes.

## Research Gaps and Future Directions

Three overarching recommendations:
1. **Directly assess transfer** as criterion variable — not intentions or motivation proxies
2. **Validate organizational transfer practices** empirically — many current "best practices" are case-based only
3. **Multidimensional, multilevel models** — systemic rather than linear; combine individual + intervention + org factors

**Specific gaps:**
- Metacognition and goal orientation: link to transfer (not just learning) understudied
- Active learning methods: advocated but not empirically linked to transfer
- EPSS and e-coaching: anecdotally supported only
- Needs analysis → transfer outcome: link not established
- Trainees' prior experience: negligible coverage
- Accountability: theorized via Schlenker's (1997) responsibility triangle; untested

## Methodological Critique

Overreliance on: self-report measures, short-term single-source data, lab settings, linear models. Progress since Baldwin & Ford (1988): multi-source feedback, longer retention intervals. Remaining issue: personality/motivation research often measures *intentions* not *transfer outcomes*.
