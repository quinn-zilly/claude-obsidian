# Argote & Miron-Spektor (2011) — From Experience to Knowledge

Navigation: [[sources/_index|Sources]] | [[Organizational Learning]] | [[Learning Curves]]

## Core Claim

Organizational learning is a cycle in which **experience interacts with context to create knowledge**, which in turn modifies the context and shapes future experience. Decomposing this cycle into active context (members, tools, tasks and their networks) and latent context provides a tractable framework for understanding when, how, and what organizations learn.

## Purpose

Perspective essay synthesizing theoretical frameworks for organizational learning. Published in *Organization Science* as part of a special issue marking 20 years since the journal's foundational 1991 special issue on organizational learning. Provides a conceptual map of the field and identifies promising future research themes.

## Key Contributions

### The Core Theoretical Framework

Organizational learning = **Experience × Context → Knowledge → (modifies) Context**

The cycle is continuous. Knowledge acquired is embedded in the context and changes future experience.

**Experience** — what transpires as the organization performs its task; typically measured as cumulative task performances (surgeries performed, devices assembled, products designed).

**Context** has two layers:
- **Active context** — members, tools, and tasks (and their networks) — the elements that *act*. These are the primary mechanisms through which learning occurs and knowledge is created, retained, and transferred.
- **Latent context** — structure, culture, identity, strategy, incentives, interorganizational relationships — shapes *which* members, tools, and tasks are in play, but cannot itself take action.

### The Active Context: Members, Tools, Tasks

Drawn from McGrath & Argote's (2001) organizational framework:

- **Members** — primary learning agents; also serve as individual knowledge repositories
- **Tools** — technology, databases, codified processes; can store and transfer knowledge independent of individuals
- **Tasks** — routines and task sequences that encode procedural knowledge collectively

**Networks among them:**
- Member-member → social network (who learns from whom)
- Task-task → interdependencies among tasks
- Member-task → division of labor
- Member-tool → who uses what
- Task-tool → which tools serve which tasks
- Member-task-tool → full specification of how work is performed

> [!key-insight] Knowledge repositories are distributed
> Knowledge is not stored in a central location. It is embedded simultaneously in individual members (can leave), tools (can be replaced), routines (can atrophy), and transactive memory systems (can dissipate). Organizational "forgetting" happens when any of these repositories degrades.

### How the Latent Context Enables Learning

Examples from the literature:
- Shared superordinate identity → greater knowledge transfer (Kane et al., 2005)
- Trust between members → more knowledge sharing (Levin & Cross, 2004)
- Psychological safety → promotes experimentation and learning (Edmondson, 1999)

These latent factors do not learn directly, but they determine whether the active context is set up to facilitate or inhibit learning.

### Knowledge Dimensions

- **Tacit vs. explicit** — tacit is difficult to articulate; explicit is codifiable
- **Declarative vs. procedural** — know-what vs. know-how
- **Causal ambiguity** — extent to which cause-effect relationships are understood (affects transfer difficulty)
- **Demonstrability** — ease of showing a solution is correct
- **Codifiability** — whether knowledge can be written down

### Levels of Analysis

The learning cycle operates at individual, group, organizational, and interorganizational levels. Individual learning is necessary but not sufficient — knowledge must be embedded in a supraindividual repository (routine, TMS, database) to count as organizational learning.
