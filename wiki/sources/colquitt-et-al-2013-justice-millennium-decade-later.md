# Colquitt et al. (2013) — Justice at the Millennium, a Decade Later

> [!key-insight]
> A meta-analytic test of two lenses on **why justice predicts behavior**: the dominant [[Social Exchange Theory]] lens and an emerging **affect** lens. Drawing on **493 independent samples**, it confirms that social-exchange-quality indicators (trust, [[Organizational Commitment|commitment]], [[Perceived Organizational Support|POS]], [[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)|LMX]]) **mediate** justice→performance and justice→OCB — but **not** justice→CWB. Several popular assumptions (focus-matching, event-vs-entity) get overturned.

## Setup

Updates the turn-of-millennium justice meta-analyses (Colquitt et al. 2001; Cohen-Charash & Spector 2001) a decade on, when the literature had ~3× more studies. The review is built around **four justice dimensions** (see [[Four Justice Dimensions]]): procedural, distributive, interpersonal, informational. Maps SET's three pillars onto justice:

- **Resource** = justice itself (symbolic, particularistic; per [[Foa & Foa Resource Theory]])
- **Rule** = reciprocity ([[Norm of Reciprocity]])
- **Relationship quality** = trust, commitment, POS, LMX

## Key Findings

### Social exchange mediation (the core result)
- Justice→**task performance** and justice→**OCB** were significantly **mediated** by social-exchange-quality indicators (trust, commitment, POS, LMX).
- Justice→**CWB** was **not** mediated by exchange quality — exchange indicators relate weakly to CWB. Suggests CWB "may arise from an altogether different type of mediator," echoing Dalal (2005): OCB and CWB are *not* opposite ends of one continuum.

### Direct effects beyond exchange
A number of justice→behavior links were **direct** (unmediated by exchange quality): procedural, distributive, and informational justice had direct negative links with CWB; distributive justice fostered task performance beyond exchange quality; informational justice→OCB also went through some other mechanism. Conclusion: **even if SET is the most predictively valid lens, it need not be the only one.**

### Focus-matching is overrated (H3/H4)
Contrary to the **target-similarity model** (Lavelle, Rupp & Brockner 2007), focus-matching relationships (e.g., supervisor-focused justice → supervisor-targeted OCB) were **not** reliably stronger than non-focus-matching ones. Instead **supervisor-focused justice tended to be stronger in general**, regardless of target — possibly because supervisors are more salient/observable. Coworker-targeted (OCBI/CWBI) relationships were similar in magnitude to authority-targeted ones, despite coworkers not setting justice levels.

### Event vs. entity makes little difference
Justice conceptualized as a discrete **event** yielded relationships similar to justice as a global **entity** — supporting the view that people form global justice judgments quickly from whatever information is available (Lind's fairness heuristic).

### Affect lens
Justice→performance relationships were also **mediated by state positive and negative affect**, with the relevant affect dimension varying across justice and performance variables. Justice was moderately (negatively) related to state negative affect — "justice seems to make people less upset." Integrating the [[Affective Events Theory|affect]] and exchange lenses is flagged as a rare, valuable future direction.

### Dimension intercorrelations
The four justice dimensions correlated **.37 to .64, average .49** — high, motivating latent/aggregate modeling (see [[Justice Measurement Taxonomy]]).

## Why It Matters for the Vault

The empirical keystone connecting [[cropanzano-mitchell-2005-social-exchange-theory|Cropanzano & Mitchell (2005)]]'s conceptual SET pillars to hard meta-analytic estimates, and the bridge from justice to the [[Affective Events Theory]] already in the vault. Its measurement appendix is elaborated in the companion chapter [[colquitt-rodell-2015-measuring-justice-fairness|Colquitt & Rodell (2015)]].
