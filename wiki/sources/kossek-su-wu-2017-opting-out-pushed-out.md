# Kossek, Su & Wu (2017) — "Opting Out" or "Pushed Out"?

> [!abstract] One-line
> An integrative multilevel review that reframes women's career inequality: the "opting-out" (pull) and "pushed-out" (push) explanations are **not rival hypotheses but coexisting, interconnected forces**, best unified through a single cross-level mechanism — the **climate for gender inclusion**.

A conceptual review in *Journal of Management* that consolidates three under-integrated literatures on women's underrepresentation in leadership — the **career preference**, **gender bias**, and **work–family** perspectives — and argues each is incomplete alone. What look like women's individual "choices" are shaped by the social contexts they are embedded in, so pushes and pulls cannot be cleanly separated.

---

## The core construct

**[[Women's Career Equality]]** = the degree to which women, relative to men, (a) have **equal access to and participation** in career opportunities and (b) experience **equal intrinsic** (job/life/family satisfaction) **and extrinsic** (pay, promotion) work and nonwork outcomes. It is a **multilevel** phenomenon, measurable at individual and collective (societal, occupational, organizational, group) levels, and its indicators include **[[Vertical and Horizontal Gender Segregation|vertical and horizontal gender integration]]**.

Framing facts (as of 2015): women are ~half the population but <5% of CEOs and 19% of board members; 40% of the global workforce but only 22% of expatriates (a key advancement path), partly because 90% of women professionals vs 50% of men are in dual-career marriages.

## The three perspectives

| Perspective | Core claim | Push or pull | Intervention target |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Career Preference Perspective (Gender)]]** | Gendered paths follow from women's interests/values/goals × work-environment fit ([[Person–Environment Fit|P–E fit]]); women "opt out" of low-communion environments (e.g., STEM) | Pull ("opt out") | Redesign jobs/culture for communal/relational fit; mentoring |
| **[[Social Role Theory (Gender)|Gender bias & stereotype]]** | Explicit + implicit bias, descriptive/prescriptive prejudice, and **[[Role Congruity Theory|backlash]]** exclude women; internalized via **[[Stereotype Threat]]** | Push ("pushed out") | Reduce bias in selection/appraisal; cluster hiring; reduce evaluation ambiguity |
| **Work–family** | Incompatible work/family roles + the **[[Ideal Worker Norm]]**, overwork, **[[Flexibility Stigma]]**, and the **[[Motherhood Wage Penalty]]** penalize women's care load | Push + pull | Increase work–family resources, control over hours; remove flexibility/family-structure bias |

The paper's central move: **stop splitting these disciplinary narratives.** Each perspective identifies necessary-but-insufficient conditions; bias interventions must succeed *first* for career-preference or work–life interventions to work.

## The linking mechanism: climate for gender inclusion

**[[Climate for Gender Inclusion]]** is the proposed cross-level construct linking antecedents to career-equality outcomes. It has **three ordered dimensions**:

1. **Fairness & nondiscrimination** for women in access, process, and outcomes — the foundational, necessary-but-insufficient dimension (most-studied).
2. **Culture & strategy that leverage women's talents** via high P–E fit (learning-and-effectiveness paradigm; Ely & Thomas).
3. **Workplace support** for women's values, voices, and needs (belongingness + psychological safety; tangible and psychological support).

Fairness must be in place before talent-leveraging and support can be enacted. Climate operates through attraction-selection-attrition: women select into and stay in contexts experienced as gender-inclusive.

## Nested context

National socioeconomic and cultural context (masculinity/femininity values, patriarchy, leave policy, legal institutions) shapes the rigidity of gender roles and which perspective is most explanatory. The review notes most literature is written from a **Western** viewpoint — "career equality" is itself a culturally Western notion.

## Future-directions agenda (5 themes)

1. Stop splitting disciplinary narratives; sequence interventions (bias first).
2. Treat opting-out and pushed-out as coexisting cross-level dynamics.
3. Seriously model national socioeconomic influences.
4. Improve measures of inclusion climate, career-equality outcomes, and implicit/explicit bias.
5. Close the research-to-practice gap; **rigorously evaluate interventions** (esp. bias training, "the most common and least scientifically evaluated" intervention — can backfire).

> [!contradiction] Work–family conflict framed as a women's issue vs the meta-analytic near-null
> Kossek et al. build their work–family perspective on the premise that women carry disproportionate work–family conflict (WFC) and are penalized for it. [[shockley-et-al-2017-gender-work-family-conflict|Shockley et al. (2017)]] meta-analytically find **men and women barely differ in reported WFC** (near-null overall). The two are *reconcilable*: Kossek's mechanism is about **attributions and penalties** (women are *perceived* as higher-WFC and stigmatized — Hoobler et al. 2009), not necessarily about women *reporting* higher WFC. Both papers cite Hoobler et al. and the flexibility stigma. See [[Gender Similarity in Work–Family Conflict]].
