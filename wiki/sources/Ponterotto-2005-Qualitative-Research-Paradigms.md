# Ponterotto (2005) — Qualitative Research Paradigms: A Primer

Introductory article to a *Journal of Counseling Psychology* special issue on qualitative methods. Argues counseling psychology is in a gradual paradigm shift from quantitative dominance toward methodological pluralism, and gives researchers a working map of philosophy of science so they can "locate" their inquiry within a paradigm.

## Core Argument

North American (counseling) psychology has been dominated by [[Positivism]] and [[Postpositivism]] and their quantitative methods. To use qualitative methods well, researchers must understand the [[Philosophy of Science Parameters|philosophy-of-science parameters]] that anchor each [[Research Paradigm]]. Otherwise they risk **"postpositivizing"** constructivist methods — importing postpositivist habits (literature-driven standardized interviews, pre-set sample, pre-set theme categories, frequency counts) into studies that were meant to be constructivist. "Round peg into a square hole."

## The Paradigm × Parameter Matrix

The article's central contribution is mapping four paradigms across five parameters (adapted from Guba & Lincoln 1994).

> [!key-insight] The 4×5 map
> | Parameter | [[Positivism]] | [[Postpositivism]] | [[Constructivism–Interpretivism]] | [[Critical–Ideological Paradigm]] |
> |---|---|---|---|---|
> | **Ontology** | naïve realism (one true reality) | critical realism (one reality, imperfectly known) | relativism (multiple constructed realities) | reality shaped by power relations |
> | **Epistemology** | dualist / objectivist | modified dualist / objectivist | transactional / subjectivist | transactional, subjectivist, dialectic |
> | **Axiology** | values excluded | values contained | values bracketed, not eliminated | values central, emancipatory |
> | **Rhetoric** | objective, "scientific" | objective, "scientific" | first-person, personalized | first-person, value-laden |
> | **Methodology** | experimental, etic/nomothetic | experimental/quasi, etic/nomothetic | naturalistic, idiographic/emic | dialogic + dialectic, idiographic/emic |

See [[Philosophy of Science Parameters]] for definitions of each parameter and [[Idiographic vs Nomothetic]] / [[Etic vs Emic]] for the cross-cutting distinctions.

## Locating Two Qualitative Approaches

Ponterotto demonstrates "locating" by analyzing the two most-used approaches in *JCP* (1989–2003 content analysis):

- **[[Consensual Qualitative Research]] (CQR)** — Hill et al. (1997, 2005). Falls between postpositivism and constructivism, leaning postpositivist: consensus/auditor model implies one approximal reality (postpositivist ontology); semistructured 30–60 min interviews; brackets bias (constructivist axiology). Classed as postpositivist–constructivist.
- **[[Grounded Theory]] (GT)** — Glaser & Strauss (1967). The constructivist-leaning version (Charmaz, Rennie) dominates counseling psychology: no single agreed reality, lengthy (up to 3 hr) interviews, theoretical sampling, first-person rhetoric with participant "voice." Classed as predominantly constructivist.

## Field Snapshot (15-year JCP content analysis)

Of 825 articles (1989–2003): 81% quantitative, 5% qualitative, 3% mixed-method, 3% conceptual, 8% invited comments. Of 49 qualitative / "Big Q" mixed studies: 21 constructivist, 9 postpositivist, 17 postpositivist/constructivist, 2 constructivist/postpositivist. A separate survey: only 10% of counseling psychology programs required a qualitative course; median 10% of dissertations were qualitative.

## Closing: Researchers as Bricoleurs

Ponterotto closes by framing counseling researchers as **[[Bricoleur|bricoleurs]]** (Denzin & Lincoln) — "Jack of all trades" who deploy whatever methods are at hand, integrating across paradigms (as CQR does). He predicts the field will move toward philosophical and methodological pluralism.
