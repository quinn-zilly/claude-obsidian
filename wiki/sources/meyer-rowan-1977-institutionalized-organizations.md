# Meyer & Rowan (1977) — Institutionalized Organizations: Formal Structure as Myth and Ceremony

John W. Meyer and Brian Rowan. "Institutionalized Organizations: Formal Structure as Myth and Ceremony." *American Journal of Sociology* 83, no. 2 (1977): 340–363.

The founding paper of the "new institutionalism" in organizational sociology. Its 1983 sequel, [[dimaggio-powell-1983-institutional-isomorphism]], extends this argument into the field-level theory of [[Institutional Isomorphism]].

## Core Argument

Formal organizational structure is not primarily a rational tool for coordinating work. In modern societies, many structures arise as reflections of [[Rationalized Myth|rationalized institutional rules]] in the institutional environment. Organizations adopt these structures **ceremonially** to gain legitimacy, resources, stability, and survival prospects — *independent of whether the structures make the work more efficient*.

The result: structure tracks the myths of the environment, not the demands of the technical core.

## The Two Faces of Structure

Meyer & Rowan draw a sharp line between:

- **Formal structure** — the blueprint (offices, programs, policies, the "table of organization"); a rational *theory* of how activity fits together.
- **Actual work activities** — what people do day to day.

Prevailing theories (Weber, Thompson, Blau) assumed formal structure exists because it efficiently coordinates complex technical/relational networks. But an earlier generation of empirical researchers (Dalton, Homans, Downs) repeatedly found a **gap** between the formal and the informal — organizations are [[Loose Coupling|loosely coupled]] (March & Olsen, Weick 1976). That gap is the puzzle the paper sets out to explain.

## Institutional Source of Structure

Where prevailing theory treats legitimacy as *given*, Meyer & Rowan make it the explanandum. Modern societies are saturated with [[Rationalized Myth|rationalized myths]] — professions, programs, technologies, and techniques presented as the rational means to social ends. These myths have two properties:

1. **Rationalized and impersonal** — they cast social purposes as technical ones and prescribe rulelike means.
2. **Beyond the discretion of any participant** — taken for granted as legitimate, *apart from* evaluation of their actual impact on outcomes.

Organizations incorporate these myths as structural elements (a personnel department, an R&D unit, an accounting function) because doing so is what counts as proper, rational, and modern — and avoids charges of negligence.

## Key Propositions

> [!abstract] The five propositions
> **P1.** As rationalized institutional rules arise in given domains of work activity, formal organizations form and expand by incorporating those rules as structural elements.
> **P2.** The more modernized the society, the more rationalized institutional structure in given domains and the greater the number of rationalized institutional domains.
> **P3.** Organizations that incorporate institutionalized myths are more legitimate, successful, and likely to survive.
> **P4.** Because attempts to control and coordinate activities in institutionalized organizations lead to conflicts and loss of legitimacy, elements of structure are **decoupled** from activities and from each other.
> **P5.** The more an organization's structure derives from institutionalized myths, the more it maintains elaborate displays of confidence, satisfaction, and good faith — internally and externally.

## The Central Tension

Ceremonial conformity to institutional rules **conflicts with efficiency criteria**. To coordinate and control activity tightly (inspection, evaluation, output measurement) is to expose inconsistency and inefficiency, which sacrifices legitimacy. Organizations resolve this with two interrelated devices:

### Decoupling (P4)
See [[Decoupling]]. Organizations buffer their formal structure from the messiness of technical work: inspection and evaluation are minimized or ceremonialized, goals are made vacuous and categorical (hospitals "treat" rather than "cure"; schools "produce students," not "learning"), output data is rendered invisible, activities are delegated to professionals, and coordination happens informally. Firms in an industry look alike *in structure* (shared institutional origin) while varying widely *in practice*.

### Logic of Confidence and Good Faith (P5)
See [[Logic of Confidence]]. Decoupled organizations are not anarchies — daily activity proceeds in an orderly way because internal participants and external constituents extend confidence and good faith. Borrowing Goffman (1967), confidence is maintained through **avoidance, discretion, and overlooking**. Everyone assumes everyone else is acting properly; that assumption is what lets a decoupled structure function.

## Significance

- Inverted the dominant efficiency account of bureaucracy: legitimacy, not technical performance, often drives structure.
- Supplied the foundation DiMaggio & Powell (1983) built into a field-level mechanism account ([[Institutional Isomorphism|coercive / mimetic / normative]]).
- Made [[Decoupling]] and ceremonial conformity standard analytic vocabulary across organizational theory, education research, and public administration.
