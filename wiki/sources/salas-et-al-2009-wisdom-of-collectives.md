# Salas et al. 2009 — The Wisdom of Collectives

Nav: [[index]] | [[sources/_index|Sources]] | [[Teams & Org Behavior MOC]]

## One-Line Summary

A conceptual review that updates the [[ABCs of Teamwork Competencies|teamwork competencies]] first proposed by Cannon-Bowers, Tannenbaum, Salas & Volpe (1995), reorganizing the knowledge, skills, and attitudes of teamwork against a decade of empirical evidence and framing effective teams as producing an emergent "wisdom of collectives."

## Core Argument

"Wisdom of collectives" = the increased capacity for performance afforded by the *interactions* of team members — an **emergent** property that is greater than (or at least different from) the summed output of individuals. The chapter's job: document the teamwork mechanisms that let this emergence happen, and refresh the competency taxonomy that names them.

Three stated goals: (1) overview the current science of teams; (2) update the 1995 teamwork competencies (KSAs) with empirical support ratings; (3) set a research agenda.

## Key Definitions

- **Team** — two or more individuals who adaptively and dynamically interact through specified roles toward shared, valued goals; **interdependency** is the defining feature; expertise is typically heterogeneous and distributed.
- **Taskwork competencies** — KSAOs for individual task performance (no interdependent interaction required).
- **Teamwork competencies** — KSAOs needed to function within an interdependent team.
- **Teamwork** — the "dynamic, simultaneous and recursive enactment of process mechanisms" that inhibit or contribute to performance (Salas, Stagl, Burke & Goodwin, 2007).
- **Team performance** — multilevel process combining individual taskwork *and* teamwork processes. Teamwork is *nested within* team performance.
- **Team effectiveness** — evaluation of performance outcomes against criteria. Hackman's (1987) three dimensions: (1) stakeholder quality/quantity standards, (2) member satisfaction, (3) whether interaction strengthened the group's capacity to keep working together.

## The Model of Teamwork — the "Big Five"

Full page: [[Big Five of Teamwork]]. Salas, Sims & Burke (2005) propose five core components plus three coordinating mechanisms:

**Five components:** team leadership, adaptability, mutual performance monitoring, backup behavior, team orientation.
**Three coordinating mechanisms:** shared mental models, closed-loop communication, mutual trust.

Key nuance the chapter stresses: **accuracy of mental models matters more than sharedness** (Edwards, Day, Arthur & Bell, 2006) — no gain from sharing *inaccurate* models. And mutual performance monitoring backfires if perceived as "keeping tabs" to dodge blame rather than as a performance aid.

## Alternative Organizations of Teamwork

- **Rousseau, Aubé & Savoie (2006)** — organized 29 frameworks under **action regulation theory** (Frese & Zapf, 1994): orientation → execution → evaluation → adjustment. Two macro-categories: *regulation of team performance* and *management of team maintenance*. Introduces **time** into the structure — different behaviors dominate different phases. See [[Teamwork Processes (Marks et al. Taxonomy)]].
- Contrast: the Big Five aims for **parsimony**; Rousseau et al. aim for **inclusiveness**.

## Evolution of the IPO Model — Time in Teamwork

The Input–Process–Output (IPO) model is criticized as too "static." Two directions of critique:

1. **Too coarse for short-term performance.** Marks, Mathieu & Zaccaro (2001) introduce **performance episodes** — "distinguishable periods of time over which performance accrues and feedback is given." Teams pursue overlapping goals; IPO should cycle at the episode, not the whole team lifespan.
2. **Deemphasizes long-term development.** Kozlowski et al. (1999): nested IPO cycles compile team knowledge/skill over time.

**Ilgen et al. (2005) IMOI** — Input–Mediator–Output–Input. P→M signals that more than processes drive outcomes (e.g., [[Emergent States (Teams)|emergent states]]); the extra I signals cyclicality (outputs of one episode feed inputs of the next). Dynamical-systems views (Arrow, McGrath & Berdahl, 2000) are noted as promising but "in their infancy."

## The Updated Competencies (ABCs)

Full page + table: [[ABCs of Teamwork Competencies]]. The chapter's central update reframes competencies as **A**ttitudes, **B**ehaviors, **C**ognitions (KSAOs), each rated for empirical support (`+` substantial, `~` moderate, `x` none).

- **Attitudes** (much new since 1995): team/collective orientation `+`, team/collective efficacy `+`, team cohesion `+`, mutual trust `+`; and newer: [[Psychological Safety|psychological safety]] `~`, team learning orientation `~`, team empowerment `~`, team reward attitude `~`, team goal commitment `~`.
- **Behaviors** (most measurable, most studied): mutual performance monitoring `+`, adaptability `+`, backup/supportive behavior `+`, implicit coordination `+`, closed-loop communication `+`, conflict resolution `+`, task-related assertiveness `+`, intrateam feedback `+`, planning `+`, coordination `+`, team leadership `+`, problem solving `+`; shared/distributed leadership `~`, mission analysis `~`, problem detection `~`, motivation of others `~`.
- **Cognitions** (rooted in shared-mental-model theory): cue–strategy associations `+`, accurate [[Shared Mental Models|problem models]] `+`, [[Transactive Memory Systems|transactive memory]] & team situation awareness `+`, team mission/objectives/norms `+`; multiteam-system (MTS) coupling `~`.

`task organization` was dropped/subsumed into coordination.

## Mechanisms of Expert Team Performance (Table 3.2)

Adapted from Salas, Rosen, Burke, Goodwin & Fiore (2006). Expert teams: hold shared mental models; optimize resources by learning/adapting (self-correct, compensate, reallocate, discuss errors); have clear-but-not-rigid roles; hold a clear, valued, shared vision; run a **prebrief → performance → debrief** discipline; have strong (often shared) leadership; develop trust, "teamness," and collective efficacy; manage/optimize outcomes (fewer errors, better decisions); and cooperate/coordinate.

## Research Agenda ("Where do we go from here?")

1. **Shared cognition needs precise, reliable, valid metrics** — the construct remains fuzzy and hard to measure.
2. **Take time seriously** — more longitudinal / time-based studies; wisdom unfolds across time scales.
3. **Move beyond self-report** — need richer "anthropological," qualitative, observational studies of teams "in the wild" (aligns with naturalistic decision making).

## Why This Matters (for the wiki)

This is the **taxonomic anchor** for teamwork competencies in the vault — it names and rates the ABCs and the Big Five that downstream sources (e.g., [[lepine-et-al-2008-teamwork-processes|LePine et al. 2008]] on the Marks taxonomy) test empirically. Bridges the older military/aviation team-training tradition (Salas lineage) with modern emergent-state and multilevel views.
