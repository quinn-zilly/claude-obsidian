# Fulmer et al. (2023) — Compensation and Performance

**Citation:** Fulmer, I. S., Gerhart, B., & Kim, J. (2023). Compensation and performance: A review and recommendations for the future. *Personnel Psychology, 76*, 687–718. https://doi.org/10.1111/peps.12583

Published in the 75th anniversary issue of *Personnel Psychology*. Open access (CC BY-NC-ND).

---

## Purpose

Two tasks:

1. **Trend analysis** — map 30+ years of pay→performance research (1990–2022) in *Personnel Psychology*, *Journal of Applied Psychology*, and *Academy of Management Journal* (39 articles total). Track theory use, methods, sample scope, endogeneity approaches.
2. **Substantive deep dive** — [[Pay-for-Performance]], [[Pay Dispersion]], [[Pay Transparency]], pay equity, endogeneity, international issues, drawing on the broader literature.

**Scope exclusions:** executive compensation (top 5 / board), turnover outcomes (unless high-performer turnover), benefits, indirect pay.

**Motivation:** Prior comprehensive reviews cover the literature to ~1990 (Gerhart & Milkovich, 1992; Milkovich & Wigdor, 1991); 75th PP anniversary prompted a 30-year update.

---

## Key Definitions

> **Compensation** (Gerhart & Milkovich, 1992, p. 484): "any direct or indirect payments to employees, such as wages, bonuses, stock, and benefits."

**[[Pay-for-Performance]] (PFP):** Classified on two dimensions — (1) results-oriented vs. behavior-oriented performance measures; (2) individual vs. aggregate level of performance measurement. A third dimension, **[[PFP Intensity]]**, captures the magnitude of the pay-performance relationship.

**[[Incentive Effect (Compensation)]]:** How PFP changes the *behavior* of current workforce — primarily through motivation.

**[[Sorting Effect (Compensation)]]:** How PFP changes *who* the workers are — lower-productivity workers leave, higher-productivity workers are attracted and retained.

**[[Pay Dispersion]]:** Variation/differentiation in pay or unequal pay among members of a group. Horizontal = same-job peers; vertical = across hierarchy levels.

**[[Pay Transparency]]:** Four dimensions — (1) pay outcome information; (2) pay determination information; (3) policies on employee pay discussions; (4) communication modes/channels.

## Trends in the Literature (Task 1)

### Volume
39 articles across PP/JAP/AMJ from 1990–2022: 13 in the 1990s, 15 in the 2000s, 11 in 2010–2022. Confirms longstanding observation that compensation is under-researched relative to practitioner interest (Gupta & Shaw, 2014; Markoulli et al., 2017).

### Theory Use (Table 2)
PFP topics dominate (26 of 39 studies). Most-used theories:

| Theory | Count |
|---|---|
| Expectancy | 7 |
| Agency | 6 |
| Equity | 5 |
| Goal-setting | 5 |
| Tournament/Sorting | 5 |

**Key trend:** Agency and goal-setting theory have disappeared from 2010–2022 research. Only equity and expectancy appear more than once in the most recent period. The review recommends reviving these frameworks for new phenomena (e.g., applying goal-setting theory to pay transparency and digital goal-sharing contexts).

### Performance Measurement
Results-based measures used ~2× more often than behavior-based overall; but 2010–2022 shows near-equal use.

### Endogeneity — a persistent gap
Statistical approaches to endogeneity used in only **4 of 39 articles** over 30 years. Quasi-experiments, common in 1990s–2000s, **absent since 2010**. Most studies rely on OLS regression without addressing selection, simultaneity, or omitted variable bias.

### International scope
27 of 39 studies used U.S.-only samples. Only 1 used between-country variance to test substantive hypotheses.

## PFP and Performance (Task 2)

### Employees and Employers Both Believe PFP Works
Surveys across U.S., China, and Russia show employees rank job performance as the most important pay determinant. Pay satisfaction correlates with perceived PFP use (r = .31; Williams et al., 2006). Virtually all private-sector organizations use some PFP.

### Incentive Effects Are Real and Meaningful
Kim et al. (2022) meta-analysis (82 effect sizes, N = 7,978): performance +0.54 SD (~24%) under incentive vs. fixed pay. Even accounting for higher payouts in incentive conditions, net advantage ≈ 20%.

### Sorting Effects Are Large and Often Ignored
Cadsby, Song & Tapon (2007) lab experiment: PFP advantage increased from **10%** (random assignment) to **38%** (free worker choice of pay condition) — implying sorting = ~74% of the total effect. Research using one-shot random assignment thus *substantially underestimates* total PFP impact.

### Lazear (1986) — the Safelite Benchmark
Safelite Glass conversion to piece rates: total productivity +44%. ~22% from incentive effect (same workers, before vs. after), ~22% from sorting (workforce composition change). Both effects equal in magnitude.

### PFP Is Measured Too Narrowly
Annual merit increases are typically 3–4%, with 85% of employees rated 3 or 4 on a 5-point scale — effectively decoupling pay from performance. But the **promotion channel** is large: median mid-point progression between adjacent pay grades = 15%; 3 promotions ≈ 1.52× pay increase. A 1-point higher performance rating (4-pt scale) was associated with **48% more promotions over 6 years** (Gerhart & Milkovich, 1989).

### Non-Normal Performance Distributions
O'Boyle & Aguinis (2012) and Aguinis & O'Boyle (2014): some jobs have power-law (Paretian) performance distributions. Rating systems and merit budget constraints may decouple pay from performance for "stars," creating poaching risk.

## PFP Intensity, Trade-offs, and Unintended Consequences

The [[Equal Compensation Principle]] (Milgrom & Roberts, 1992): when different performance goals are not incentivized equally, employees focus on what is most incentivized and neglect the rest.

Potential pitfalls of high-intensity PFP tied solely to results:
- Excessive risk-taking
- Excessive within-firm competition
- Under-attention to quality, service, long-term outcomes
- Gaming of included metrics

**Novartis example:** $678M DOJ settlement over improper incentives to doctors. Response: results-only payout replaced with combined results + behavioral values ratings; PFP intensity capped at 35% of compensation.

**Mitigations:** Add behavior-based performance measures alongside results-based; reduce PFP intensity on results alone; do not make exceptions for below-standard performance (weakens perceived pay-performance link for all peers).

## Pay Transparency

More transparency ≠ uniformly better performance (contra Lawler, 1967's early expectation).

**Key moderator — relative pay standing:**
- Above-median earners react positively to transparency
- Below-median earners react negatively (more dissatisfaction, higher turnover intentions; Card et al., 2012)

**Employee misperceptions under secrecy:** Employees systematically **overestimate** peer pay (causing felt under-reward inequity) and **underestimate** pay at higher job levels (reducing promotion incentive).

**Performance effects:** Most studies show a positive transparency → motivation/performance relationship (Belogolovsky & Bamberger, 2014; Brown et al., 2022), via both incentive and sorting.

**Individual differences:** Equity sensitivity (Bamberger & Belogolovsky, 2010); status-seeking / social dominance motivation → Compensation Activation Theory (Fulmer & Shaw, 2018).

**Transparency asymmetry:** Open research question — are employee reactions to *reducing* transparency as negative as reactions to *increasing* it?

---

## Pay Dispersion

**Key finding — source matters:** PFP-caused dispersion is perceived as more legitimate than politically-based dispersion, and is associated with better performance, less turnover, and attraction of high performers (Shaw, 2014; Trevor et al., 2012; Kepes et al., 2009). Equal pay for unequal performance = perceived inequity under equity theory.

**Recommendation:** Future pay dispersion research should clarify what it adds beyond studying PFP dispersion directly, since dispersion effects appear largely driven by whether dispersion is performance-based.

---

## Endogeneity

Endogeneity sources: omitted variables, simultaneity, selection bias, measurement error. OLS does not address simultaneity, selection, or measurement error — only omitted variables (partially).

**Preferred design approaches:** Experiments (gold standard); quasi-experiments (absent since 2010). **Preferred statistical approaches** (rare in the literature): instrumental variables, fixed effects, Heckman correction, regression discontinuity, difference-in-differences, propensity score matching.

---

## Pay Equity and Gender/Race Gaps

- Women earn 82% of men's median earnings (up from 60% in 1980). Blau & Kahn (2017): 62% explained by experience, occupation, industry; 38% unexplained.
- Race gaps vs. white men: Black men 81%, Hispanic men 74%, Asian men 131%.
- **Access discrimination** (occupational sorting) vs. **valuation discrimination** (unequal pay in same job).
- Women are 45% of S&P 500 employees but only 6% of CEOs; promotion is a key mechanism of the gender pay gap.

---

## International / Country Differences

National culture effects on equity vs. equality allocation rule are small: Fischer & Smith (2003) meta-analysis (14 countries), r = .065. Zhou & Martocchio (2001): performance explained 64.2% of bonus variance in both China and U.S.; country × performance interaction explained only 1.1%. Country effects ≠ culture effects — country is an upper bound on any single country characteristic.

## Practical Recommendations (Table 3)

| Area | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| PFP intensity | Monitor for unintended consequences; graduated payouts warrant closer monitoring |
| Performance measurement | Think beyond annual merit; include bonuses tied to specific goals; communicate promotion pathways |
| Holistic measurement | Add behavior-based measures to reduce risk of equal-compensation violations |
| Sorting | Factor sorting effects into PFP design; high performers prefer stronger PFP |
| Star performers | Ensure payout distribution can reflect extreme performance distributions |
| Exceptions | Avoid i-deals for below-standard performance — weakens the PFP signal for peers |
| Transparency | Proceed carefully; mixed evidence; more transparency ≠ better outcomes for all |

## Future Research Recommendations (Table 4)

### Theoretical Foundations
- Revive agency and goal-setting theory for new phenomena (digital goal-sharing, clawbacks, complex multi-PFP systems)

### Understudied Aspects of PFP
- **PFP intensity** — beyond present/absent; study the magnitude and shape of pay-performance gradient
- **Holdbacks and clawbacks** — delay or recovery of PFP payouts to align with long-term performance; no research on employee-level programs
- **Performance measurement** — more research combining results-based AND behavior-based measures
- **Merit pay** — the most common PFP form is under-studied relative to individual incentive plans
- **Extreme performance distributions** — designing PFP for "star + solid majority" workforces
- **Pay dispersion** — clarify what it adds beyond PFP dispersion

### Pay Communication and Transparency
- Contextual factors (individual, situation) determining when transparency helps vs. hurts motivation, performance, sorting
- Transparency asymmetry (increasing vs. decreasing transparency)
- Whether transparency reduces pay inequities, through what means, and at what cost
- Effects of increased public pay information availability (internet) on social comparisons and employee mobility

### Methodological
- **Endogeneity** — revive quasi-experiments; use regression discontinuity, diff-in-diff, propensity score matching, IV
- **International samples** — multi-country designs testing whether PFP effectiveness varies across countries
