# Zahra, Neubaum & Hayton (2020) — Knowledge Integration

Navigation: [[sources/_index|Sources]] | [[Knowledge Integration]] | [[Knowledge-Based View of the Firm]]

## Core Claim

Knowledge integration is the raison d'etre for the existence of the firm (Grant, 1996; Kogut & Zander, 1992), yet despite widespread scholarly attention the construct remains "equivocally defined and measured," producing a confusing array of conceptualizations that undermine theoretical and practical usefulness.

## Purpose

Integrative review that bridges micro-level (individual/group) and macro-level (organizational) perspectives on knowledge integration. The authors survey existing definitions, propose their own, and show how the micro-macro interplay can enrich future research.

## Key Contributions

### Zahra et al.'s Definition of Knowledge Integration

> "Knowledge integration is an organizational capability for creating novel combinations of different strands of knowledge, which have utility for solving organizational problems, from component knowledge sourced from within and beyond the organization, and across time, and which derive from individual and group contributions, facilitated by both formal and social processes."

### Six Dimensions of Knowledge Integration (Figure 1)

The authors distilled the literature into six common dimensions that any definition must address:

1. **What is it?** — ability, mechanism, or process
2. **Who does it?** — companies, decision-makers, project teams
3. **What is done?** — absorbing, constructing, encoding, combining
4. **What is integrated?** — capabilities, component competencies, technological/market knowledge
5. **What are the sources?** — alliance partners, customers, past projects, external knowledge
6. **What are the outcomes?** — explorative/exploitative strategies, innovation, recombination

### Knowledge Taxonomy

Four properties of knowledge shape how it can be integrated:

- **Tacit vs. explicit** — tacit knowledge is harder to transfer; integration depends on surfacing implicit understanding
- **Declarative vs. procedural** — know-what vs. know-how
- **Component vs. architectural** — knowledge of parts vs. knowledge of how parts connect (Henderson & Clark, 1990)
- **Core vs. integrative** — foundational knowledge vs. knowledge of how to integrate activities (Helfat & Raubitschek, 2000)

### Micro-Level Perspectives

- **Individual cognition** — knowledge encoded in individual memory; integration requires attention and mindful re-encoding
- **Transactive Memory Systems (TMS)** — distributed memory across group members; who knows what and how to access it
- **Group routines** — shared patterns of action that encode integrated knowledge implicitly

### Macro-Level Perspectives

- **Organizational boundaries** — integration occurs across internal units and external alliances
- **Capabilities perspective** — combinative capabilities (Kogut & Zander), architectural competence (Henderson & Cockburn), dynamic capabilities (Teece et al.)
- **Knowledge-based view** — firm exists to integrate specialist knowledge (Grant, 1996)
- **Knowledge management** — explicit systems for capturing, sharing, applying knowledge
- **Organizational learning** — learning as ongoing knowledge integration across levels

### Key Shortcomings Identified

- Lack of conceptual precision undermines cumulative theory-building
- Micro and macro perspectives rarely talk to each other
- Measurement approaches are inconsistent across levels
- Most studies ignore multi-level dynamics
