# Hough, Oswald & Ock (2015) — Beyond the Big Five

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> An *Annual Review* manifesto arguing that a **paradigm shift is needed** in organizational personality research: a sole focus on broad Big Five factors **obscures important relationships** and cannot describe the complexity of personality at work. The authors evaluate **four taxonomic approaches** to personality structure — the **[[Five-Factor Model|FFM]]**, the **[[HEXACO Model|HEXACO model]]**, **circumplex models**, and **[[Nomological-Web Clustering|nomological-web clustering]]** — and argue for moving *beyond* hierarchical models toward refined, facet-level, criterion-connected structures, better criterion constructs, and **[[Synthetic Validity|synthetic validation]]**. Motto: **explore depth first so that any resulting breadth is more meaningful.**

## The periodic-table analogy

A mature science needs good taxonomies. The authors use the **periodic table of elements** as the ideal: elements are basic/distinct/universal; not all present in all environments; bond to form molecules; same elements in different quantities form different molecules; decompose unchanged; the table can grow. By analogy, the basic unit of personality is the **facet/trait**; **[[Compound Traits (Personality)|compound traits]]** (integrity, service orientation) are "molecules" — the same basic traits in different concentrations (Ones & Viswesvaran 1996, 1998); the structure should be stable yet open to revision.

## Brief history

Roots in Galton's (1884) **lexical hypothesis** → Allport & Odbert (1936) cataloged 17,953 trait words → Cattell reduced to 35 → Fiske (1949) first found five factors → **Tupes & Christal (1961/1992)** identified today's FFM (surgency, agreeableness, dependability, emotional stability, culture); Goldberg (1993) called them the "true fathers." Organizational personality research surged after **Project A** (Army Research Institute), whose signature move was summarizing validities **by construct** (both predictor and criterion sides) rather than by measure — a permanent advance.

## Criticisms of the Big Five

The Big Five is useful but limiting. It is:

1. **Method-bound** — discovery/confirmation depend on factor analysis; "what you discover depends on what variables you include" (Costa & McCrae: varimax axes depend entirely on variable selection). "Prestructuring" (Block 1995) casts doubt on claimed universality. The lexical approach finds *poor* cross-language similarity; the Big Five is **neither universal in English/Western cultures nor across languages**.
2. **Missing variables** — not a comprehensive map. Table 1 lists dozens of missing constructs (honesty/humility, Machiavellianism, narcissism, psychopathy, social insight, sensation-seeking, risk-taking, religiosity, masculinity/rugged individualism, RIASEC interests, etc.).
3. **Relationships obscured** — Project A found that summarizing validities *by construct* (predictor × criterion) revealed useful validities that construct-blind summaries (Guion & Gottier 1965; Schmitt et al. 1984) had missed. Ghiselli (1966), who linked traits to jobs theoretically, found validities in the .20s that the FFM organizing scheme did not reproduce for job proficiency.
4. **Low construct validity of the factors** (facets cross-load; relationships are more **lattice-like than strictly hierarchical**, Loehlin & Goldberg 2014).

## Importance of facets

A "mountain of evidence" shows that, depending on the criterion, **many facets have higher criterion-related validity than their parent factor** ([[Facet-Level Personality Measurement]]). Facet placement is itself ambiguous across models (Costa & McCrae's 30 facets vs Saucier & Ostendorf's 18 vs Soto & John's 15 — disagreeing on warmth, anxiety, adventurousness). See the **[[Bandwidth-Fidelity Dilemma]]**: the broad-vs-narrow question is *not a dilemma to resolve but a tension between theory (understanding) and practice (prediction)*; synthetic validity lets narrow predictors be tailored and assembled into batteries.

## Four structural approaches

- **[[Five-Factor Model|FFM]]** — hierarchical; useful "security blanket" but limiting.
- **[[HEXACO Model|HEXACO]]** — six factors incl. Honesty-Humility; also hierarchical but *more comprehensive* (covers Machiavellianism, narcissism, psychopathy, etc.); Emotionality ≠ Neuroticism (lacks the anger component); H predicts CWB. Still missing many variables; whether HEXACO beats the Big Five on *good* performance criteria is unresolved.
- **Circumplex models** (e.g., AB5C, Hofstee et al. 1992) — traits arrayed on a circle; allow cross-loadings; but 2-dimensional (two factors at a time), so cannot easily represent 3-factor compounds like integrity.
- **[[Nomological-Web Clustering]]** — non-hierarchical; group traits by shared *profiles* of linear and nonlinear relationships (with other traits, criteria, moderators, subgroup differences, malleability). An *open network* continuously revisable. Uses generalizability theory + synthetic validity as operationalizing tools.

## Synthetic validation

**[[Synthetic Validity|Synthetic validity]]** = a more flexible version of meta-analysis: accumulate vast personality × criterion validities, then tailor new performance models and predictor equations to new work configurations, informed by personality-based and criterion-based job analysis. The metaBUS project (Steel, Bosco, Uggerslev — coding ~all published org-science findings across 3,000+ variables) is cited as revolutionary infrastructure.

## Research & practice agenda

Update personality *and* criterion taxonomies; develop nomological-web clustering; examine moderators/mediators/differential validity/subgroup mean differences; use person-centered *and* variable-centered analyses; don't neglect construct validity or reliability in the Big-Data era; study compound traits; grow a databank of specific personality × criterion validities; use synthetic validation and rolling validation for changing work.
