# Rupp, Shao, Jones & Liao (2014) — The Utility of a Multifoci Approach

> [!key-insight]
> Meta-analysis (**k = 647, N = 235,682**) arguing that justice should be modeled by **source/foci** (supervisor vs. organization) rather than only by **type** (distributive/procedural/interactional). Source-based ("multifoci") justice **out-predicts** type-based justice for **matched** outcomes, target-similar effects exceed target-dissimilar ones, and **source-specific social exchange mediates** the link. Directly answers the [[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al. (2013)]] meta-analysis, which reached the opposite conclusion.

## The argument

Justice **"types" (DJ, PJ, IJ) were never originally perceptual targets** — they are *sets of normative rules* perceivers use to judge whether a **specific accountable party** is fair (Lind & Tyler 1988). Modeling justice without a "face" (accountable party) misses the phenomenon. Draws on:

- **Normative rules** — DJ (Adams equity/equality/need), PJ (Thibaut & Walker process control; Leventhal's 6), IJ (Bies & Moag → interpersonal + informational).
- **Moral accountability / fairness theory** (Folger & Cropanzano 2001) — justice reactions are targeted at the party held responsible; blame attribution.
- **Bandwidth-fidelity** (Cronbach) — prediction is optimized when predictor and criterion **specificity match**; aligning justice *source* with outcome *target* = "[[Multifoci Justice & Target Similarity|target similarity]]" (Lavelle, Rupp & Brockner 2007).
- **[[Social Exchange Theory]]** — fairness from a specific party engenders source-specific exchange (POS with org, [[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)|LMX]] with supervisor), driving reciprocation back to that party.

## Hypotheses & results (all supported)

- **H1** — Multifoci (source-based) justice more strongly predicts outcomes at matched sources than type-based justice. **Supported**: organizationally-focused justice was the stronger predictor of org-directed outcomes; supervisory-focused justice the stronger predictor of supervisor-directed outcomes (non-overlapping CIs on betas). Usefulness analyses: source-over-type incremental variance > type-over-source for 16 of 18 outcomes.
- **H2** — Target-similar effects > target-dissimilar effects. **Supported** (with expected small cross-foci effects retained). E.g., supervisory justice → supervisory satisfaction r = .64 vs. org justice → supervisory satisfaction r = .41; LMX: SJ r = .65 vs. OJ r = .42.
- **H3** — Multifoci justice → matched outcomes is **mediated by source-specific social exchange** (POS, LMX, trust, commitment, identification). **Supported** — direct effects attenuated when exchange added; more than a methodological artifact.

## Method notes

- 530 papers, 647 studies, >12,236 correlations; two major sources (supervisor, organization) × 18 target-specific outcomes.
- First-party, individual-level justice only (excluded third-party and climate-level effects).
- Combined **meta-analysis + hierarchical multiple regression + path analysis** (following Colquitt et al. 2001).
- Job performance coded as a **supervisor-directed** outcome.
- 40% of measures were type-specific but **source-ambiguous** — the crux: type-based estimates mix conflicting sources.

## Contradiction with Colquitt et al. (2013)

> [!contradiction] Conflicts with [[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al. (2013)]]
> Colquitt et al. (2013), examining **bivariate correlations**, concluded that **matching source to target does NOT improve prediction** over the unmatched DJ/PJ/IJ framework, and that supervisor-focused justice is simply stronger in general. Rupp et al. (2014), using **multiple regression + path analysis** on largely overlapping data, conclude the **opposite** — matching *does* help, mediated by source-specific exchange. Per [[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic (2023)]] the divergence is method-driven: Rupp used source-based (not type) justice, overall justice per source, path analysis, more liberal-vs-conservative coding, and more studies (647 vs. 493; 213 shared). Unresolved; both remain cited.

(2013)]] page already present. Anchors the source-specific exchange mediation story (LMX/POS) in hard estimates.
