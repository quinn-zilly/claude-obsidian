# The Work Design Questionnaire (WDQ) — Morgeson & Humphrey (2006)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]] | [[Work Design Questionnaire]] | [[Teams & Org Behavior MOC]]

The foundational development-and-validation paper for the [[Work Design Questionnaire]]. Argues that existing work design measures (the JDS, the MJDQ) were narrow, incomplete, and psychometrically problematic, then builds a single parsimonious instrument that integrates **motivational, social, and contextual** features of work. Validated on **540 incumbents across 243 distinct jobs**.

## Motivation for the Study

The authors identify three reasons a comprehensive measure was needed:

1. **Measurement gap.** The dominant [[Job Characteristics Model|JDS]] (Hackman & Oldham 1980) taps only ~5 motivational characteristics and has questionable internal consistency (Taber & Taylor 1990) and unstable factor structure (Idaszak & Drasgow 1987). Campion's MJDQ was broader but Edwards et al. (1999) showed it was better described by 10 factors than 4 and still missed key characteristics like autonomy.
2. **Design restriction.** With only a few characteristics measured, redesign decisions are highly restricted; a richer set enables more fine-grained, trade-off-aware changes.
3. **Theoretical stagnation.** Work design theory had slowed since Hackman & Oldham (1975/76) and Campion (1988); few measures spanned the full range, so perspectives stayed parochial. A broad measure could stimulate integrative theory.

## Methodology — Identifying Work Characteristics

- Searched PsycInfo and ABI–Inform plus the O*NET occupational requirements domains.
- Identified **107 work characteristic terms** discussed or measured in the literature.
- Independently sorted terms into homogeneous categories (monothetic classification adapted from Fleishman & Quaintance 1984), then reconciled — e.g., "work scheduling" and "decision-making authority" were folded into **autonomy**.
- Result: **18 work characteristic categories** organized into the three-component (Morgeson & Campion 2003) framework — motivational (subdivided into task + knowledge), social, and contextual.

## The 18 Characteristics (4 domains)

> [!abstract] WDQ structure
> Motivational characteristics split into **task** and **knowledge** requirements (Campion & McClelland 1993); plus **social** and **contextual** domains.

**Task (motivational) — 5:**
autonomy (3 facets: work scheduling, decision making, work methods), task variety, task significance, task identity, feedback from job.

**Knowledge (motivational) — 5:**
job complexity, information processing, problem solving, skill variety, specialization.

**Social — 4:**
social support, interdependence (initiated + received), interaction outside the organization, feedback from others.

**Contextual (work context) — 4:**
ergonomics, physical demands, work conditions, equipment use.

## Item-Writing Principles

- Mix of existing (17%), adapted (33%), and new (50%) items; all in the Appendix.
- Simple 5-point strongly disagree–strongly agree scale (complex scales add construct-irrelevant variance; Harvey et al. 1985).
- Positively worded (except the job complexity scale and one ergonomics item), to avoid the negative-wording factor artifacts seen in the JDS.
- Minimum 4 items per construct (3 per sub-dimension where dimensions exist, e.g. autonomy = 9 items).
- Items refer to the job itself, not the incumbent's reaction; grouped by construct for psychometric advantage.

## Factor Structure (CFA, EQS)

Five models compared via confirmatory factor analysis:

| Model | χ² | df | χ²/df | SRMR | RMSEA | CFI |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4-factor | 19010 | 2839 | 6.70 | .12 | .11 | .40 |
| 18-factor | 5686 | 2678 | 2.12 | .06 | .05 | .89 |
| 19-factor (split interdependence) | 5280 | 2659 | 1.99 | .06 | .04 | .90 |
| 20-factor (split autonomy) | 5435 | 2639 | 2.06 | .06 | .05 | .90 |
| **21-factor** | **5027** | **2618** | **1.92** | **.06** | **.04** | **.91** |

> [!key-insight] 21 factors, not 18
> Although 18 *categories* were defined a priori, the best-fitting model splits **autonomy → 3 facets** and **interdependence → 2 directions**, giving **21 distinct scales**. The 4-factor "reductionist" model (task/knowledge/social/contextual) fit poorly — work design is far more differentiated than the four broad buckets.

## Reliability

- Average internal consistency (α, r-to-z transformed) = **.87**; only **ergonomics** fell below .70 (.64).
- Interrater reliability (ICC2) and agreement (r_wg) generally support aggregating to the job level.
- Three scales showed near-zero interrater reliability: **feedback from job, feedback from others, initiated interdependence** — flagged for follow-up research.

## Construct Validity (Hypotheses 1–4)

Tested WDQ scales against archival **DOT and O*NET** measures (independent sources):

- **H1 (supported):** task characteristics largely *unrelated* to cognitive-ability descriptors; knowledge characteristics *positively* related (specialization the lone exception). Knowledge chars related to cognitive ability significantly more strongly than task chars.
- **H2 (partial):** interaction outside org related to people/communicate GWA (H2c ✔); initiated/received interdependence *not* (H2a, H2b ✘); social support and feedback from others largely unrelated as predicted (H2d partial, H2e ✔).
- **H3 (fully supported):** all four work-context characteristics related to archival physical descriptors in the predicted directions (strongest WDQ–archival links overall).
- **H4 (mostly supported):** professional jobs higher in knowledge chars + autonomy (7 of 8); nonprofessional jobs higher in physical demands, lower in work conditions; "human life" occupations higher in task significance; sales higher in interaction outside org.

## Relationships to Outcomes (Hypotheses 5–6)

> [!key-insight] Resolving the satisfaction vs. cost trade-off
> Both **task** and **knowledge** characteristics predicted **satisfaction** (H5a, H5b ✔). But only **knowledge** characteristics predicted **training** requirements (H5c ✔; H5d for compensation not significant). So a redesign can raise satisfaction via *task* characteristics — which are largely unrelated to training/compensation costs — sidestepping the classic motivational trade-off.

- **H6 (supported):** **social support** incrementally predicted satisfaction (+6% R² beyond the 10 motivational characteristics) and was **negatively** related to training and compensation requirements — i.e., supportive jobs are more satisfying *and* cheaper to train for.

## Seven Contributions (per the Discussion)

1. Most comprehensive work design measure available (40+ years integrated).
2. Corrects known measurement problems (response scale, negative wording).
3. Uniformly high reliability vs. JDS (avg .87 vs. JDS ~.68; Taber & Taylor 1990).
4. Rigorous CFA support for a 21-factor model (most prior work used EFA).
5. Meaningful links to independent O*NET/DOT databases (objective job properties).
6. Detects expected occupational differences (useful for classification/compensation).
7. Opens new theory: trade-offs can be avoided by emphasizing the right characteristics.

## Limitations

- Same-source (incumbent) data for both WDQ and satisfaction → possible inflation (mitigated by simultaneous-regression logic; all other tests used independent sources).
- Sampling idiosyncrasies (many managers), though 243 distinct jobs is a strength.
- Only direct relationships examined; moderators (e.g., individual differences) left for future work.
