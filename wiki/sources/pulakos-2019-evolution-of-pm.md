# The Evolution of Performance Management: Searching for Value

**Pulakos, E. D., Mueller-Hanson, R., & Arad, S. (2019).** *Annual Review of Organizational Psychology and Organizational Behavior, 6*, 249–271.

> [!key-insight] Core Argument
> Nearly a century of effort — rating formats, rater training, MBO, forced distribution, 360s, competency models, automation — has produced PM systems that are **costly, detested, and have virtually no measurable impact on performance**. The value is not in better *measurement*; it is in day-to-day **manager and employee behavior** (clear expectations, real-time feedback, coaching, agile goals). The recent wave of PM reform (Deloitte, GE, Adobe, Accenture, Microsoft) is the field's overdue response.

## The cost/value indictment
- CEB (2012): average manager + employee spend ~250 hrs/yr on PM; ~$30M/yr for a 10,000-person firm. Deloitte: ~2M hours/yr.
- CEB (2012), 23,339 ratings / 40 orgs: business units with highly-rated employees were **no more profitable** than those with low-rated employees.
- Culbert & Rout (2010): the review is a "pretentious, bogus practice." A *Washington Post* headline: "basically every single person hates performance reviews."

## Performance evaluation: three shaky assumptions
See [[Three Assumptions of Performance Rating]]. Underlying all rating research:
1. Everyone has a **stable "true" performance** level. — But rater–ratee *relationship* differences produce *real* behavioral differences; interrater reliability sits around .50 (Viswesvaran et al. 1996), raising doubt that "true performance" even exists across raters.
2. Raters **can** rate accurately. — Managers can sort capable/failing/exceptional but cannot make the nuanced, multi-level judgments overengineered scales demand.
3. Raters are **motivated** to rate accurately. — [[Murphy & Cleveland]]'s four competing goals (task, interpersonal, strategic, internalized) mean accuracy is often *not* the driver. Managers rationally inflate to keep good-but-imperfect employees engaged, and retrofit ratings to pay decisions.

## History in brief
Graphic scale (Paterson 1922) → behavioral anchors (Smith & Kendall 1963; BARS; Latham & Wexley 1977) → [[Landy & Farr]] (1980) moratorium on format research → **forced-choice/IRT** revival (Bartram 2007) yields better ratings but managers hate it (can't manipulate outcomes) → MBO (Drucker) → **[[Forced Distribution]]** ("rank and yank," Jack Welch/GE; use fell 49%→14%, 2009–2011) → **[[360-Degree Feedback]]** → **[[Competency Modeling]]** (McClelland; Boyatzis; Klein). Automation made PM heavier and more disconnected from actual management, not better.

## The reform: two strategies
1. **Streamline the formal system** — cut low-value steps; the ratings debate (keep vs. drop). Nuance: CEB (2016) found rating-less orgs report *lower* engagement/fairness — but that study conflated "dropped ratings + added check-ins" with "just dropped ratings." Do **not** remove ratings without replacing them with expectations + regular feedback. See [[Rating-less Reviews]].
2. **Drive effective PM behavior** — the higher-leverage path. CEB (2004) and Google's Project Oxygen (Bryant 2011) converge on the same manager behaviors: set clear expectations, revise them in real time, give daily informal feedback, check in, coach. These behaviors are learnable.

> [!key-insight] Behavior change is hard
> Real change needs experiential, on-the-job learning (DeRue et al. 2012; Ericsson), a **learning (not performance) orientation** (Boyatzis's Intentional Change Theory), and attachment to a concrete performance goal. Mindfulness may help managers/employees give and receive feedback less defensively (neuroscience: reviews trigger automatic threat responses — Rock 2008).

## CEO (Center for Effective Organizations) evidence
Ledford et al. (2016), 244 companies: 97% adopted ongoing feedback, 51% rating-less reviews, 27% crowdsourced feedback. The **combination of all three** had the most impact on strategic alignment, motivating/developing employees, and rewarding top talent. New practices outperformed traditional ones — but durability is unknown.

## Contradiction / tension with the wiki
> [!contradiction] Does "true performance" exist?
> Pulakos et al. lean toward **no** stable cross-rater true score (rater–ratee relationship differences are real, not just error). This is a stronger skeptical stance than the classic psychometric framing in [[denisi-murphy-2017-100-years-appraisal|DeNisi & Murphy (2017)]], which treats accuracy as elusive-to-measure but conceptually meaningful. [[schleicher-2019-pm-effectiveness-review|Schleicher et al. (2019)]] revisit exactly this as one of their three "pressing questions."
