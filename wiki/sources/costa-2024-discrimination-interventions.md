# Costa (2024) — Bias-Intervention Effectiveness Meta-Analysis

*Journal of Applied Psychology*, advance online publication (Aug 29, 2024). [doi:10.1037/apl0001215](https://dx.doi.org/10.1037/apl0001215). McIntire School of Commerce, University of Virginia.

Meta-analysis of **70 articles / 208 effect sizes** from the past two decades testing interventions to reduce bias. Introduces a conceptual taxonomy mapping the **attitude dimension** each intervention primarily targets, derived from the **[[Tripartite Model of Attitudes|tripartite theory of attitudes]]** (cognition / affect / behavior) and the **[[Attitude Dimension Consistency|principle of compatibility]]**.

## Central Thesis

Interventions are most effective at shifting a bias outcome when the **attitude dimension targeted matches the dimension measured** ("attitude dimension consistency"). A cognitively-focused intervention best shifts cognitive outcomes (stereotyping); a behaviorally-focused one best shifts behavioral outcomes (discrimination).

## Key Findings

- **Passive interventions are largely ineffective at changing behavior** — short-term education or reminders of bias processes barely move discriminatory behavior.
- **Behavior-targeting interventions are the most effective class** — those that directly inhibit the *manifestation* of bias.
- Workplace behavioral outcomes overall: interventions reduce discrimination by ≈ **¼ of a SD** (*g* = 0.27, 95% CI [0.196, 0.344]) vs. control — helpful but **modest**.

**Highest-impact intervention types (behavioral outcomes):**

| Intervention | Category | *g* |
|---|---|---|
| **Making individuals accountable** | inhibit bias manifestation | **0.80** |
| **Intervening on social norms** | inhibit bias manifestation | 0.58 |
| Leveraging structured evaluation | disrupt stereotype processing | 0.51 |
| Imagined contact | update affective states | 0.47 |
| Multicultural experience | update affective states | 0.46 |
| Majority signaling by targets (e.g., "whitening" résumés) | disrupt stereotype processing | 0.44 |

> [!key-insight] Match the dimension — except for affect
> Cognitive→cognitive and behavioral→behavioral matches boosted effectiveness. **Affective matches did not** benefit similarly: emotional responses are deeply ingrained, harder to measure reliably, and more subject to social-desirability bias, so dimension-matching confers no clear affective advantage.

## Why It Matters

Reconciles prior mixed findings. Example: Mousa (2020) found intergroup contact (an "affective" treatment) between Christian and Muslim youth in postwar Iraq shifted **behavior** via a newly introduced **social norm** (a behavioral mechanism), not via increased outgroup information (cognitive). The taxonomy explains *why* some interventions outperform: alignment between the mechanism engaged and the outcome measured.
