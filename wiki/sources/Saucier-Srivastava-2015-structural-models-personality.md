# Saucier & Srivastava (2015) — What Makes a Good Structural Model of Personality?

Nav: [[index]] | [[sources/_index]] | [[Personality at Work MOC]]

> [!abstract] One-line
> A critical review of personality-structure models via the **lexical approach**: the [[Five-Factor Model|Big Five]] is useful but its cross-cultural support is weaker than assumed; **one-, two-, and three-factor** structures are more robustly replicable, and the **Big Six** may generalize better beyond Northern-European languages.

## The units problem

Following Allport, the foundational question of personality psychology is the **[[Units Problem (Personality)|units problem]]**: what are the constructs to describe personality, and how are they organized? Definitions are consequential — they shape variable selection. The authors distinguish three views:

- **Realist** (Allport, Five-Factor Theory): traits are latent, biologically-driven dispositions that exist objectively in the person.
- **Constructivist** (Mischel): trait descriptions largely reflect perceiver biases/lay theories; the strong version (traits have little validity) collapsed empirically.
- **Functionalist** (their preferred middle ground): personality descriptions are perceptual units that **serve a purpose for perceivers** — "important to whom, and for what?" Descriptors exist because people make decisions (whom to hire, marry, trust).

## Eight criteria for a "good" structural model

Social importance · predictive validity · comprehensiveness · reliability/cross-time stability · generalizability across data types · generalizability across cultures (**universality**) · established causal basis · linkage to a plausible theory.

The **[[Lexical Hypothesis|lexical approach]]** — deriving structure from natural-language descriptors within each language separately — engages most criteria at once and imposes the demanding **cross-cultural generalizability** test.

## The radial hierarchy of factor emergence

A key synthesis (Figure 13.1): factors emerge in a **radial hierarchy** — see [[Personality Structure Hierarchy]].

- **1 factor** → general **Evaluation** (good vs. bad; the "Big One").
- **2 factors** → **Social Self-Regulation** (getting along / communion) vs. **Dynamism** (getting ahead / agency). As replicable as the one-factor, and impervious to variable-selection effects.
- **3 factors** → conscientiousness (task self-regulation) splits from sociomoral self-regulation, plus dynamism. One-to-three-factor structures are the **most cross-culturally replicable**.
- **5 factors** → the Big Five, but recovered cleanly mainly in **Germanic/Slavic** languages; **intellect–openness** and (secondarily) **neuroticism** show poor cross-cultural validity.
- **6 factors** → the **Big Six / HEXACO-like** structure (adds Honesty–Humility); less bound to N-European languages, may exceed Big Five generalizability. See [[HEXACO Model]].

Beyond six factors, no replicable independent structure has appeared (Big Seven failed to replicate).

## Evidence on stability & agreement (mostly Big Five)

- **Rank-order stability** rises with age (retest ≈ .50 young adulthood → ≈ .70 later).
- **Mean-level change**: people become more conscientious, emotionally stable, socially dominant across adulthood (Roberts et al. 2006).
- **Cross-observer agreement** exceeds chance but is imperfect (~.30 from brief acquaintance); no data source is a gold standard — Vazire's **self–other knowledge asymmetry (SOKA)** model integrates this.
- A limiting factor is **situation-related variance**: if stability is at the level of *trait-in-situation*, cross-time/cross-observer stability is attenuated (echoing [[Person–Situation Debate|Mischel's critique]]).

## Why it matters for the vault

The definitive statement that the **Big Five is one useful cut, not the ground truth** — the lexical/structural counterweight to FFM-centric selection research. Feeds the "beyond the Big Five" tension already live in [[Personality at Work MOC]] and grounds [[HEXACO Model]] and [[Five-Factor Model]] historically.
