# Fischhoff & Broomell 2020 — Judgment and Decision Making

Annual Review of Psychology, 2020. Comprehensive state-of-the-field review covering judgment, preference, choice, individual differences, life-span development, and applied decision science.

## Core Argument

Decision science integrates **task analysis** (what is objectively required) with **behavioral research** (what people actually do) + **interventions** (how to improve decisions). This tripartite integration — analytical, descriptive, prescriptive — is the field's distinctive identity.

The field progressed by analyzing tasks before describing behavior: formal standards (Bayesian inference, utility theory) reveal when behavior deviates and why.

## Structure

### Judgment (Accuracy + Consistency)

**Accuracy** studied three ways:

1. **Knowledge/Literacy tests** — measure what people know; rarely grounded in task analysis of what people *need* to know for decisions
2. **Calibration** — confidence matches accuracy; people tend to be overconfident on hard tasks, underconfident on easy ones; scoring rules (Murphy & Winkler weather forecasters) can improve calibration with feedback
3. **Pooling (Wisdom of Crowd)** — aggregate judgment > any member when judgments correlate with criterion but not with each other; lacks explanatory transparency

**Consistency** studied via:
- Bayesian inference as standard — conjunction fallacy, base-rate neglect (links to [[tversky-kahneman-1974-heuristics-biases|Tversky & Kahneman 1974]])
- Dempster-Shafer as alternative — handles incomplete hypothesis sets; "known unknowns"
- Emerging trend: multi-method, open-ended tasks to capture how people *naturally* think about uncertainty (lay concepts of confidence vs. likelihood)

### Preferences

No accuracy criterion for preferences (unlike judgment). Consistency standard = utility theory axioms.

- **Violations**: reference-point sensitivity ([[Prospect Theory]]); irrelevant options reversing preference ordering (decoy effect)
- **Constructed preferences**: people don't have stable preferences for unfamiliar choices — they construct them from context cues; abstract elicitation methods (standard gambles) generate high exclusion rates
- **Sacred values**: nonrational but legitimate; violate trade-off axiom; studied with mixed methods; linked to genocide resistance, anti-extremism

### Choice

Two strategies: experiments (piecemeal, factor-varying) vs. **modeling** (statistical weight estimation).

**Cumulative Prospect Theory (CPT)** as canonical model:
- Parameters: loss aversion, risk tolerance, probability weighting, choice stochasticity
- Problem: parameters are highly variable across studies; one meta-analysis found weak evidence of loss aversion overall (Walasek et al. 2018)
- **Decision by Sampling (DbS)** model (Stewart et al. 2006): estimates loss aversion from memory-sampling of prior comparisons; task features affect which comparisons are sampled

**Description vs. Experience gap**:
- Description-based: summary probabilities → CPT predictions
- Experience-based: learn from trials → underweight small probabilities (opposite CPT)
- Broomell & Bhatia (2014): information-theoretic proof that experience stimuli cannot estimate CPT parameters in principle

### Individual Differences

- Early research found almost none; focus on general tendencies discouraged standardization
- Breakthrough: **DOSPERT scale** (Domain-Specific Risk-Taking) — risk tolerance is domain-specific, not general
- **Decision-Making Competence (DMC)** (Fischhoff's own work): tasks drawn from experimental literature; Y-DMC (age 18) correlated with real-world outcomes (drug use, antisocial behavior, sexual behavior) in CEDAR longitudinal study
- Y-DMC → A-DMC correlation at age 30 = 0.50 (stable individual differences)
- Strongest predictor of DMC: neighborhood disadvantage at age 10 (persists after controlling for IQ)

### Life Span

- **Adolescents**: by mid-teens (15–16) have adult-level cognitive decision skills; less emotional control; higher need for experimentation
  - Folk claim of "invulnerability bias" often wrong: teen marijuana use better predicted by correct belief that it might be too good to stop (affective forecasting failure), not overconfidence
  - [[Fuzzy-Trace Theory]] (Reyna): gist extraction improves teen health decisions
- **Elderly**: retain basic skills absent health impairment; decline on complex mental operations; working memory decline = strongest predictor (structural equation modeling)
- Future: compare decisions *across lifespan contexts*, not just decision *skills*

### Applications

**Improving individual decisions**:
- Libertarian paternalism (Thaler & Sunstein): manipulate choice architecture toward "rational" choice — only appropriate when individual analysis done
- Warning about biases → limited effect; people lack cognitive structures or consider themselves immune
- Conditions for learning: prompt feedback, unambiguous criteria, incentives, instruction in unintuitive processes

**Good Judgment Project** (IARPA-sponsored):
- Thousands of forecasters, hundreds of geopolitical events, scoring-rule feedback
- Results: brief training + feedback → sustained improvement; remote interaction helps; **superforecasters** identified as stable individual difference; self-selected participants already better calibrated than typical lab sample

**Night Shift (ED physician transfer decisions)**:
- 60% of severely injured patients not transferred appropriately from local EDs
- SDT analysis: some physicians have good discrimination / poor decision rules; others opposite
- Serious games (narrative engagement vs. analogical reasoning) → improved performance vs. standard ACS training, sustained 6 months

**Cost-Risk-Benefit Analysis**:
- Expert judgment elicitation: scoring rules, day-long structured interviews (Morgan 2017)
- **Psychometric risk perception**: dread + uncertainty dimensions (Slovic); these are often treated as irrational but can reflect legitimate public concerns
- SDT applied to policy: false-negative rates for triage, phishing vulnerability, sexual intent perception
- FDA restructured drug evaluation around decision-science benefit-risk framework
- Climate communication: involved from Carter admin; mainstream role only from late George W. Bush era

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| Task analysis | Formal characterization of what a decision requires before studying behavior |
| Calibration | Match between confidence and accuracy |
| Constructed preference | Preferences inferred/built at decision time, not retrieved from stable storage |
| DMC | Decision-making competence — measurable, stable individual difference |
| Superforecasters | Individuals with stable superior calibration identified in Good Judgment Project |
| Applied basic psychology | Testing theories in real-world settings (Baddeley 1979) |
| Basic applied psychology | Domesticating real-world phenomena for lab research |
