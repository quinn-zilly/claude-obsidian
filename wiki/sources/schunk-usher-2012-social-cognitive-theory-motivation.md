# schunk-usher-2012-social-cognitive-theory-motivation

Nav: [[index]] | [[Social Cognitive Theory]] | [[Learning & Development MOC]]

> [!abstract] Source summary
> A handbook chapter operationalizing Bandura's **[[Social Cognitive Theory]]** for **motivation**. Motivation = "the process whereby goal-directed activities are energized, directed, and sustained." SCT is contrasted with instinct/trait and behaviorist accounts: thoughts, beliefs, and emotions are the central processes underlying motivation. Built on **[[Triadic Reciprocal Causation]]**, it emphasizes vicarious, symbolic, and self-regulatory processes and treats **[[Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)|self-efficacy]]** as the especially critical motivational influence.

## Conceptual framework

- **[[Triadic Reciprocal Causation]]** — reciprocal interactions among personal, behavioral, and social/environmental factors (worked through with self-efficacy as the running example).
- **[[Enactive and Vicarious Learning]]** — learning by doing (from consequences) vs. by observing models. Vicarious learning is an efficient alternative that spares people undesirable consequences.
- **Learning vs. performance** — SCT (unlike behaviorism) separates new learning from performance of previously learned actions; learned behavior may not be demonstrated (motivation, incentives, social pressures intervene).
- **Three human capabilities**: vicarious, symbolic, and self-regulatory processes.

## Vicarious processes

Three types (Bandura 1986): **response facilitation** (modeled acts as social prompts — not learning), **inhibition/disinhibition** (models strengthen/weaken tendencies, often moral/legal or emotional), and **observational learning** (four sub-processes: attention, retention, production, motivation). Model characteristics matter: perceived **similarity** (age, gender for appropriateness; **competence** dominates for actual learning), **mastery vs. coping models** (coping models — who struggle then improve — help learners with prior difficulties more), and cognitive modeling.

## Key motivational processes

Goals and self-evaluations of progress, **[[Outcome Expectancies|outcome expectations]]**, **values**, **[[Social Comparison|social comparisons]]**, and **self-efficacy**. Perception of goal progress sustains self-efficacy and motivation.

## Self-efficacy: sources and effects

The chapter's centerpiece. **Four [[Sources of Self-Efficacy|sources]]**: mastery experience (most influential), vicarious experience/social comparison, verbal/social persuasion, and physiological/emotional states. Sources do not act automatically — they are cognitively *interpreted*, weighted differently by individuals (e.g., girls and African American students weighted social persuasion more in one study). **Effects**: task choice, effort, persistence, self-regulation, and achievement; also career choice (social cognitive career theory). Cites **Multon et al. (1991)** (self-efficacy ↔ academic performance and persistence) and **Stajkovic & Luthans (1998)** (28% gain in work performance).

## Key insight

> [!key-insight]
> Self-efficacy is a **key determinant** of motivation and achievement — but not the only one: "No amount of self-efficacy" substitutes for requisite skills, positive values, and outcome expectations. Given those, self-efficacy is decisive for choice, effort, and persistence. This chapter is the applied bridge from [[bandura-2001-social-cognitive-theory-agentic|Bandura's agentic SCT]] to the vault's [[Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)|goal-setting]] and [[Learning & Development MOC|learning]] clusters.
