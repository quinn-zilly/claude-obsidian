# Seibert, Akkermans & Liu (2024) — Understanding Contemporary Career Success: A Critical Review

> [!abstract] One-line
> A critical review that pushes back on the field's drift toward subjective-only career success, argues [[Objective Career Success|OCS]] remains important, consolidates the [[Subjective Career Success|SCS]] construct, organizes predictors into seven theoretical perspectives, and sets a five-part future agenda (sustainable careers, career shocks, marginalized groups, alternative employment, career equality).

## Framing

Career = "the evolving sequence of a person's work experiences over time" (Arthur et al. 1989). **Career success** = the positive material and psychological outcomes resulting from those experiences (Judge et al. 1995). The authors write from the boundary between two "invisible colleges" — **organizational psychology (OP)** (internal processes, career-specific constructs, subjective outcomes; European/vocational leaning) and **organizational behavior (OB)** (workplace behaviors, general OB constructs, objective outcomes; US/management/economics leaning) — arguing the two have progressed on parallel tracks studying the same phenomenon and should cross-pollinate.

## Conceptual claims

### Revaluing objective career success
The field, especially OP, has exaggerated the deficiencies of OCS (salary, rank, promotions, occupational status). Their defense: objectivity is a virtue (circumvents common-method variance); OCS constructs like salary are a *direct measure of a career goal*, not a deficient measure of career performance (Campbell & Wiernik 2015); the de-emphasis on OCS may be a rhetorical strategy normalizing the loss of stable organizational careers (Inkson et al. 2012); OCS stays crucial for **equity** among marginalized groups and non-traditional workers. Meaning-focus may be a "WEIRD-sample luxury." **Recommendation: include both OCS and SCS unless there is a well-articulated reason not to.**

### Consolidating subjective career success
SCS research has reached **saturation** — eight recurring dimensions emerge across the major conceptualizations (Dries et al. 2008; Shockley et al. 2016; Briscoe et al. 2021 / 5C group): financial, advancement, interpersonal relations, challenge & mastery, meaning & impact, self-development, career opportunities & control, and the work-life interface (plus an overall satisfaction assessment). The new multidimensional scales are mostly used as organizing schemes, not in empirical work — most studies citing them still use Greenhaus et al.'s (1990) unidimensional career-satisfaction scale. Open problems: construct **contamination/overlap** (SCS correlates .61 with job satisfaction; dimensions overlap with job characteristics, work-family balance); dimensionality/weighting; measurement-design choices (Table 2). Prescription: fine-grained SCS for specific predictors/interventions; short global SCS for broad theory.

## Seven predictor perspectives (Section 4)

Human capital theory; internal & external labor markets (tournament/path-dependent promotion, ILM career systems); sponsorship / developmental support / developmental networks (contest vs sponsored mobility); stable and malleable individual differences; career self-management behaviors; plus gender and career equality. A recurring critique: HCT and the contest/sponsorship model function as **theoretical catch-alls** used to legitimize variables rather than generate unique hypotheses.

## Five-part future agenda (Section 5)

1. **[[Sustainable Career]]** — reconcile OP (happiness) and OB (productivity) by adding **health**: a career is sustainable when it dynamically balances happiness × health × productivity over the life course (De Vos et al. 2020).
2. **[[Career Shocks]]** — disruptive positive or negative events that trigger deliberation about career change (Seibert et al. 2013; Akkermans et al. 2018); complements the field's over-agentic, over-planned view.
3. **Marginalized/underrepresented groups** — apply identity theory and stigma theory; intersectionality of race × gender.
4. **Alternative employment arrangements** — gig/platform/contingent work along Spreitzer et al.'s (2017) three flexibility dimensions; choice vs necessity moderates outcomes.
5. **Career equality** — women close the performance gap but not the reward gap (links to [[Gender Gap in Rewards vs Performance]]).
