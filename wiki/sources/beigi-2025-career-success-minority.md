# Beigi et al. (2025) — Career Success and Minority Status: A Review and Conceptual Framework

## Citation

Beigi, M., Shirmohammadi, M., & [co-authors] (2025). Career success and minority status: A review and conceptual framework. *Journal of Management*. Advance online. DOI: 10.1177/01492063251342190

## Summary

Systematic review of career success literature among minority groups — women, racial/ethnic minorities, persons with disabilities, LGBTQ+ individuals. Screened 6,570 records; reviewed 337 articles. Introduces a conceptual framework anchored in resource management theory (Spurk et al., 2019) and the "Bodies Out of Place" concept (Puwar, 2004). Core argument: minority status operates through **complex visibility** to constrain career advancement resources and capital, producing career success disparities.

## Authors

- [[Mina Beigi]] — University of Southampton; lead author
- [[Melika Shirmohammadi]] — University of Houston

## Theoretical Anchors

- **Resource Management Framework (Spurk et al., 2019):** career success emerges from resources; minority status disrupts resource access and accumulation
- **"Bodies Out of Place" (Puwar, 2004):** racialized/gendered norms define whose bodies "belong" in which spaces; minority status triggers hyperscrutiny and exclusion mechanisms in workplaces that assume a white/male/able-bodied default

## Conceptual Framework

Two pathways from minority status to career success disparity:

**Path A → B → C:** Minority status → **Complex Visibility** → Constrained access to career advancement resources → Career success disparity

**Path A → D → E:** Minority status → Complex visibility modulating individual resources (human + psychological capital) → Career success

**Moderator:** Organizational inclusivity moderates the minority status → complex visibility link (more inclusive orgs attenuate the visibility penalty)

## Complex Visibility (core construct)

Three interacting states — see [[Complex Visibility]]:

1. **[[Hypervisibility]]** — excess scrutiny (hyperscrutiny) + tokenism; minority individual stands for entire group; mistakes amplified, successes attributed to group quota
2. **[[Invisibility (Career)]]** — not seen, not heard; contributions overlooked; excluded from informal networks
3. **[[Managed Visibility]]** — behavioral adaptation: concealing identity, code-switching, strategic self-presentation to navigate hypervisibility/invisibility tensions

## Career Advancement Resources

Four categories of organizational/social resources that minority status disrupts access to:

| Resource | Mechanism of disruption |
|---|---|
| Networking | Informal networks are racialized/gendered; minority members excluded from key social circles |
| Mentoring | Fewer same-group senior mentors; cross-group mentoring often less effective |
| Role models | Scarcity of visible minority executives reduces aspirational modeling and career optimism |
| Training & development | OJD assignments (stretch roles, turnarounds) disproportionately withheld or offered on worse terms |

## Individual Resources (Capital)

- **Human capital:** education, skills, experience — affected by constrained access to developmental assignments
- **Psychological capital:** self-efficacy, resilience, career optimism — depleted by sustained hypervisibility/invisibility; can be buffered by mentoring and role models

## Subjective Career Success Dimensions (expanded)

Beyond objective metrics (pay, promotion), minority career success requires four additional dimensions — see [[Subjective Career Success]]:

1. **Authenticity** — ability to bring full self to work without identity concealment
2. **Survival** — managing threat without derailing career
3. **Collective good** — contributing to community / paving the way for others
4. **Adjustability** — capacity to adapt across contexts while maintaining core identity

## Key Phenomena

- **[[Glass Cliff]]** — minority individuals appointed to leadership in crisis/high-risk contexts; set up for failure; see especially women and racial minorities in CEO appointments
- **Savior effect** — variant of glass cliff; minority executive hired to "save" struggling unit
- **Tokenism** — one or few minority members used symbolically; hypervisibility consequence
- **Pay gap** — ~20% global gender pay gap; wider for racial/ethnic minorities and intersectional identities
- **[[Outsider-Within Status]]** — insider structural position with outsider experiential reality (Collins, 1986); structural inclusion ≠ felt belonging

## Four Minority Groups

| Group | Key phenomena |
|---|---|
| Women | Glass ceiling, glass cliff, maternal wall, pay gap, hypervisibility in male-dominated fields |
| Racial/ethnic minorities | Racial pay gap, invisibility in leadership pipeline, code-switching demands |
| Persons with disabilities | Invisibility (impairment not acknowledged), disclosure dilemma, accommodation negotiation |
| LGBTQ+ | Identity concealment, managed visibility most prominent, heteronormative workplace assumptions |

## Intersectionality Note

Framework acknowledges intersectionality (multiple minority identities compound) but treats groups separately for clarity. Most reviewed literature is single-axis; intersectional research is identified as a gap.

## Methodology

- **Protocol:** PRISMA-compliant systematic review
- **Database search:** Business Source Complete, PsycINFO, Web of Science, Scopus
- **Records screened:** 6,570 initial → 337 final included articles
- **Period:** Primarily 2000–2024
- **Unit of analysis:** Empirical and conceptual articles on career success for one or more of four minority groups

## Connections to Wiki Domains

- **[[On-the-Job Development]]** (Leadership Dev): training & development resource deprivation = OJD denial; stretch assignments are the most powerful OJD catalyst (Yost & Plunkett); withholding them from minority employees explains career development gaps structurally
- **[[Learning Agility]]** (Leadership Dev): hypervisibility and threat-laden environments suppress learning agility expression; psychological safety is prerequisite for agility
- **[[Organizational Learning]]** (KM): org-level learning capacity is constrained when minority employees mask knowledge/perspective via managed visibility; diversity as epistemic resource
- **[[Motivated Reasoning]]** (Open Science): same psychological mechanism — ordinary cognition under misaligned incentives — explains why managers don't see their exclusion behavior as biased
- **[[Institutional Isomorphism]]** (Inst. Theory): normative isomorphism in professions (shared hiring criteria, credential filters, informal networks) reproduces exclusion patterns across organizations even without explicit discrimination
