# Judge & Kammeyer-Mueller (2012) — Job Attitudes

**Citation:** Judge, T. A., & Kammeyer-Mueller, J. D. (2012). Job attitudes. *Annual Review of Psychology, 63*, 341–367.

First-ever dedicated Annual Review of Psychology chapter solely on job attitudes (prior reviews treated it in service of other topics).

## Core Argument

Job attitudes are evaluations of one's job expressing feelings, beliefs, and attachment. They are among the most studied constructs in organizational psychology (33,348 PsycINFO records as of writing). They matter because (a) work is central to identity and waking life, (b) they predict important behaviors, and (c) they are a window into social attitudes more broadly.

> [!key-insight] Key Insight
> Job attitudes have both **traitlike** (stable, dispositional) and **statelike** (within-person, fluctuating) properties. ESM research shows ~1/3–1/2 of variance in job satisfaction is *within-person*, meaning one-shot designs miss substantial variance.

## Structure of the Review

### 1. What Are Job Attitudes?

**Definition:** Evaluations of one's job expressing feelings (affect), beliefs (cognition), and attachment. Cognition and affect are inseparable in practice — neuropsychology confirms reciprocal brain-region relationships.

**Attitude vs. related constructs:**
- *Perceptions/descriptions* (e.g., role clarity, most org. justice scales) — lack explicit evaluative appraisal; antecedents to attitudes, not attitudes themselves
- *Intentions* (planned behavior) — shaped by opportunities and social norms beyond attitudes
- *Motivation/engagement* — reflects energy direction, not evaluative state

**Hierarchy:** Global job attitude > overall job satisfaction/org. commitment > facets (pay, supervision, coworkers, work, promotion).

Job attitudes can be treated as *latent* (top-down: overall attitude causes facet correlations) or *manifest* (bottom-up: facets compose overall). Both are valid depending on research purpose.

### 2. Discrete Job Attitudes

See [[Job Satisfaction]] and [[Organizational Commitment]] for detailed treatments.

**Job Descriptive Index (JDI)** — Smith et al. (1969): 5 facets (work, supervision, coworkers, pay, promotion). Meta-analytic inter-facet correlations ≈ r = .20–.30. *Work satisfaction* is most strongly correlated with global satisfaction and with motivation.

**Pay satisfaction** — 4 dimensions: pay level, benefits, pay raise, structure/administration. Satisfaction based on discrepancy between received and deserved pay. Social comparisons strong: internal comparisons rc = .94, external rc = 1.00 (Williams et al. 2006).

**Organizational commitment** — Three-component model (affective, normative, continuance; Meyer et al. 2002) criticized by Solinger et al. (2008): affective commitment is an attitude toward the *organization*, normative/continuance are attitudes toward *behaviors* (staying/leaving). Affective commitment has highest validity for predicting performance.

### 3. States and Traits

**[[Affective Events Theory]]** (Weiss & Cropanzano 1996): Job features shape distribution of job events → events trigger affect → affect drives state-like behaviors (OCB, withdrawal); cognitive evaluations drive judgment-driven behaviors (turnover).

ESM research: nearly as much within-person variability in job attitudes as in moods/emotions. Three research categories:
1. Moods/emotions as antecedents or consequences of job attitudes
2. Within-person variability from workplace events
3. Within-person job attitude variation predicting within-person behavior variation

### 4. Dispositional Antecedents

See [[Dispositional Antecedents of Job Attitudes]] and [[Core Self-Evaluations]].

Key findings:
- Staw et al. (1986): childhood affective disposition (ages 12–14) correlated r = .34 with job satisfaction at ages 54–62
- Arvey et al. (1989): monozygotic twins reared apart showed consistent job satisfaction levels
- Big Five: neuroticism (rc = −.29), extraversion (rc = .25), conscientiousness (rc = .26) → job satisfaction (Judge et al. 2002)
- **CSEs** (self-esteem, generalized self-efficacy, locus of control, emotional stability): rc = .37 with job satisfaction; most useful predictor vs. Big Five or PA/NA frameworks

### 5. Situational Antecedents

See [[Situational Antecedents of Job Attitudes]].

- **Job characteristics** (Hackman & Oldham 1976): skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, feedback → satisfaction. Subjective perceptions more predictive than objective analyst ratings.
- **Social environment**: coworker support meta-analytically related to satisfaction, involvement, and commitment (Chiaburu & Harrison 2008); social network ties cluster in job-related affect
- **Leadership**: leader consideration rc = .78 with subordinate satisfaction; initiating structure rc = .33. Transformational leadership buffers emotion regulation → dissatisfaction link.
- **Org. practices**: distributive justice rc = .79 with pay satisfaction; person–org. value congruence → positive attitudes
- **Time**: "honeymoon-hangover" pattern — high initial attitudes decline after hire; org. commitment also declines among newcomers

### 6. Outcomes

See [[Job Performance (Outcomes of Job Attitudes)]].

- **Task performance**: meta-analytic relationship with job satisfaction (Judge et al. 2001); Riketta (2008) confirms satisfaction → performance direction stronger than reverse
- **Citizenship (OCB)**: strong link, esp. supervisor satisfaction (rc = .45); personality–citizenship link mediated by satisfaction
- **Withdrawal/counterproductivity**: satisfaction → turnover intentions → turnover (mediated); job satisfaction more predictive of turnover for high-ability/high-mobility employees
- **Creative performance**: uncertain; activated moods (positive or negative) linked to creativity; dissatisfied employees more creative with high continuance commitment + support
- **Organizational performance**: unit-level satisfaction/engagement → customer satisfaction, productivity, profit, turnover, safety (Harter et al. 2002)

## Key Takeaways

1. Job attitudes = social attitudes, among the most central given work's role in life
2. Affect and cognition both matter; inseparable in practice
3. Multilevel: traitlike (between-person) and statelike (within-person)
4. ESM shows day-to-day satisfaction variance is real, not error
5. Work satisfaction facet most predictive of global satisfaction
6. Personality (esp. CSEs) important; CSEs most consistent dispositional predictor
7. Social environment (coworkers, leadership) as important as job content
8. Bandwidth-fidelity principle: match attitude specificity to behavior specificity

## Future Issues Raised

- Causal direction between job attitudes and moods/emotions
- Integration of state and trait perspectives
- Time-frame effects on temporal variation
- Alternatives to self-report measurement
- Which org. interventions produce durable attitude improvements
