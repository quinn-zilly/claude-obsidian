# Pichler (2012) — The Social Context of Performance Appraisal and Appraisal Reactions

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> First quantitative (meta-analytic) review of the social context of performance appraisal. Across 69 samples from 62 studies, **rater–ratee relationship quality is the strongest correlate of appraisal reactions** (composite Mr ≈ .66) — stronger than appraisal participation or rating favorability. Using meta-analytic structural equation modeling (MASEM), a **relational model** (Model 3) — where relationship quality has a *direct* path to reactions on top of its indirect paths through participation and ratings — fits far better than an independent-antecedents model or a purely instrumental model. The relationship quality → reactions link is **not** moderated by rating favorability or participation.

## Context & Purpose

Performance appraisal research had shifted from a psychometric focus (rating errors, accuracy) toward the **social context** of appraisal and **[[Appraisal Reactions|employee reactions]]** as criteria (Levy & Williams 2004; Murphy & Cleveland 1995). Three predictors were known to relate to reactions — [[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)|rater–ratee relationship quality]], appraisal participation, and rating favorability — but the literature was unclear on *how* these interrelate. Pichler integrates [[Social Exchange Theory]] with [[Procedural Justice]] theory to test competing process models.

Composite **relationship quality** = four regularly-measured constructs: overall relationship quality (LMX), supervisor trust, supervisor support, supervisor satisfaction.

## Three Competing Models

The paper's core contribution is testing three models of the appraisal process via MASEM (single-item indicators, ML estimation, harmonic-mean N = 107 [studies-weighted]).

| Model | Claim | Fit |
|---|---|---|
| **1 — Independent Antecedents** | Relationship quality, participation, rating favorability are separate, parallel predictors of reactions | Poor: χ²(3)=37.63, CFI=.72, RMSEA=.33, SRMR=.24 |
| **2 — Instrumental** | Relationship quality matters *only* because it drives participation + favorable ratings (no direct path) | Poor: χ²(2)=34.33, CFI=.69, RMSEA=.39, SRMR=.14 |
| **3 — Relational** | Relationship quality is antecedent to participation + ratings **and** has a direct path to reactions | **Good: χ²(1)=1.93 (p=.16), CFI=.99, RMSEA=.09, SRMR=.04, R²=.49** |

> [!key-insight] Model 3 wins
> The best-fitting model integrates *both* instrumental and relational propositions from procedural justice: participation and rating favorability matter, **but** relationship quality carries meaning above and beyond any instrumental effect. See [[Relational Model of Appraisal Reactions]].

## Meta-Analytic Correlations (Table I)

Relationship-quality subdimensions with appraisal reactions all cluster around Mr ≈ .60:

| Relationship | N | k | Mr |
|---|---|---|---|
| Relationship quality ↔ reactions | 1,356 | 8 | .60* |
| Supervisor satisfaction ↔ reactions | 2,235 | 11 | .60* |
| Supervisor support ↔ reactions | 633 | 3 | .58* |
| Supervisor trust ↔ reactions | 1,103 | 8 | .60* |
| **Composite relationship quality ↔ reactions** | **4,042** | **23** | **.66*** |
| Relationship quality ↔ performance ratings | 2,092 | 15 | .30* |
| Relationship quality ↔ participation | 2,492 | 12 | .45* |
| Participation ↔ performance ratings | 3,413 | 17 | .25* |
| Participation ↔ reactions | 10,467 | 42 | .48* |
| Rating favorability ↔ reactions | 10,880 | 36 | .39* |

\* p < .05, random-effects models (all effect sizes significantly heterogeneous).

## Moderation Tests (Table III)

- **Appraisal participation did NOT moderate** the relationship quality → reactions link (B=.32, R²=.12, k=12, n.s.). RQ2 asked whether participation *enhances* or *substitutes for* relationship quality — answer: neither, robustly.
- **Rating favorability did NOT moderate** it either (B=.33, R²=.21, k=12, n.s.), disconfirming Hypothesis 1 (which predicted RQ→reactions would be stronger when ratings are favorable).

Heterogeneity remains (Q=105.09*), so unmeasured moderators exist — but not these two.

## Key Findings

1. The social context matters: all relationship-quality constructs strongly relate to reactions.
2. Relationship quality is the **dominant** predictor — larger than participation or favorability, even controlling for them.
3. The appraisal process is **relational, not merely instrumental** — but instrumentality (participation, favorable outcomes) still matters and shouldn't be dismissed.
4. The RQ→reactions relationship is robust — unmoderated by the two obvious instrumental candidates.

## Practical Implications

- Ratees react more positively to appraisals — **regardless of how favorable the rating** — when they have a good working relationship with their supervisor.
- Interventions should target supervisor–subordinate relationship quality and supervisor supportiveness, not just rating accuracy.
- Rater training should cover *delivering feedback in a supportive, participatory way*, complementing rater-error / accuracy training.
- Positive reactions matter downstream: motivation to improve, fairness perceptions, and reduced grievance/discrimination-claim risk.

## Limitations

- Effect sizes almost entirely **cross-sectional, percept–percept** data → possible inflation; limits process/causal inference. Calls for longitudinal designs.
- Composite reactions variable used (fairness, accuracy, utility, satisfaction, motivation collapsed) — differential predictors of distinct reaction types not tested.
- Scope limited to **supervisor-driven, administrative** reviews; does not necessarily generalize to [[360-Degree Feedback|360-degree]] / developmental feedback.
- Tenure (a known voice moderator; Avery et al. 2011) too rarely measured to test.

## Cited-by / Connects to

- Extends [[Social Exchange Theory]] into the appraisal process by showing exchange *quality* predicts favorable treatment (participation, ratings) rather than the reverse ordering common in org SET models.
- Empirically supports the **relational / group-value model** over the **instrumental model** of [[Procedural Justice]] for appraisal — consistent with Tyler (1989, 1994) and Cawley et al. (1998).
