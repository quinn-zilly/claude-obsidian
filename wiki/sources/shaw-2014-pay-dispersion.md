# Shaw (2014) — Pay Dispersion

> [!abstract] One-line
> The definitive *Annual Review* synthesis of pay-dispersion research: the direct dispersion–performance relationship is unpredictable, but once you separate **explained (legitimate)** from **unexplained (residual)** variation, the field becomes coherent — legitimate dispersion helps, residual dispersion hurts.

Review of the performance-, turnover-, and attitude-related outcomes of **[[Pay Dispersion]]** ("spread, range, variation, inequality" in pay within [horizontal] and across [vertical] jobs/levels). Focus on the prior ~15 years of organizational-psychology/OB research, selectively drawing from economics.

---

## The framing conundrum

Pfeffer & Langton (1993): *"a theoretical dilemma in the literature concerning reward allocations."* Two camps:

- **Dispersion helps** — motivation theories (expectancy/[[Pay-for-Performance]]), tournament theory, and [[Distributive Justice|equity theory]] (proportional pay = fair). Wide spreads raise effort/competition and drive **[[Sorting Effect (Compensation)|workforce sorting]]**.
- **Dispersion hurts** — compression fosters cohesion, trust, cooperation; dispersion undermines internal equity, especially under interdependence (Frank 1984; Levine 1991).

Shaw's resolution: the debate dissolves once dispersion is decomposed. See **[[Explained vs Unexplained Pay Dispersion]]**.

---

## Three stylized facts (Summary Evaluation)

> [!key-insight] The stylized facts
> 1. **Performance.** The *direct* dispersion–performance link (org/team/individual) is hard to predict a priori. But **explained/legitimate dispersion** (from incentives, seniority, education, tenure, historical performance) is generally **positively** related to productivity, safety, and performance; the **residual (unexplained) variation** — once legitimate inputs are partialled out — is **negatively** related, especially at the team level.
> 2. **Turnover.** Greater dispersion is positively related to overall turnover, most clearly for **vertical** dispersion. Horizontal effects are murkier, but the **tournament/sorting** prediction holds: well-communicated performance-based dispersion retains good performers and pushes out poor ones (Shaw & Gupta 2007).
> 3. **Attitudes.** Least-developed literature. Apparent contradictions (Pfeffer & Langton negative vs. Clark et al. positive) likely stem from studies inadvertently stripping out legitimate variation, leaving only residual dispersion — and from ignoring the employee's **own pay standing** (reactions differ by position in the distribution; Trevor & Wazeter 2006).

---

## Key theoretical engines

- **[[Tournament Theory (Compensation)]]** — wide, convex prize spreads motivate effort and competition, and sort the workforce (winners stay, losers leave). Heavily tested in sports samples (golf, racing, hockey).
- **[[Distributive Justice|Equity theory]]** — dispersion is fair when outcomes match commensurate inputs; horizontal *equality* under unequal effort actually *violates* equity.
- **Work interdependence** — the classic moderator (dispersion supposedly worse under interdependence), but Shaw argues its empirical support is weak and it should be **superseded by identifiability**.

---

## The signature agenda item: identifiability

> [!key-insight] Beyond interdependence → identifiability
> Shaw argues the real underlying contingency is **[[Identifiability of Individual Inputs]]**, not interdependence. Nearly all work is interdependent; what matters is whether individual contributions can be *reliably observed and assessed*. Where reliable markers exist (goals, assists), performance-based dispersion works even under high interdependence; where contributions can't be accurately gauged, dispersion invites negative reactions.

Other agenda items: an **international/cross-cultural** view of what counts as "legitimate" variation; better mechanism (mediator) studies; attention to pay-system communication/transparency.
