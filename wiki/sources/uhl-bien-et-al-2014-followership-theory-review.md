# Followership Theory: A Review and Research Agenda

Nav: [[index]] | [[Leadership MOC]]

Agenda-setting review in *The Leadership Quarterly* arguing that leadership research has given followers "short shrift." Since "there is no leadership without followers," the authors formalize [[Followership Theory]] — **the study of the nature and impact of followers and following in the leadership process**.

## Historical Treatment of Followers

The review traces a progression (their Table 1) in how leadership theory has treated followers:

1. **Leader-centric** (Taylor's scientific management, trait, behavior, contingency, charismatic/transformational) — followers as recipients or moderators of the leader's influence, or as a "situational" factor to be managed.
2. **Follower-centric** (romance of leadership, Meindl; implicit leadership theories) — followers *construct* leaders and leadership through attribution and schema.
3. **Relational views** (Lord's connectionist processing; Follett's "power with"; Hollander's relational view; LMX; Klein & House's "charisma on fire"; Padilla et al.'s **toxic triangle** of destructive leaders + susceptible followers + conducive environments) — leadership as mutual influence.
4. **Followership** (the new frontier) — privileging the role of the follower in its own right.

> [!key-insight] What most surprised the authors
> Leadership scholars have *long* agreed leadership is a process co-occurring in leader–follower interaction — yet the systematic study of followership was almost entirely missing (only ~14% of *LQ* articles 1990–2008 even mentioned "follower"; "followership" a handful).

## Two Frameworks for Studying Followership

- **Role-based approach** — "reversing the lens" (Shamir 2007). Studies followership as a rank/role (subordinate); treats **follower characteristics and behaviors as the independent variables** and leader outcomes as dependent. Covers follower typologies (Zaleznik; Kelley's exemplary/sheep/yes-people; Chaleff's *courageous follower*; Kellerman's engagement types; Lipman-Blumen's toxic-enabling followers), **follower role orientations** (Carsten et al. 2010: passive vs. proactive/co-production schemas, enacted contingent on climate), **[[Implicit Followership Theories]]** (Sy 2010), and followership behaviors (obedience, resistance, proactivity, upward influence tactics).
- **Constructionist approach** — followership and leadership are **co-constructed in relational interaction** and need not align with formal hierarchy. Anchored by [[Leadership Identity Construction|DeRue & Ashford's (2010) claiming–granting model]], Shamir's [[Co-Production of Leadership|co-production]], Collinson's post-structuralist follower identities (conformist / resistant / dramaturgical selves), and Fairhurst & Uhl-Bien's discursive approach.

## Defining Boundaries: Follower ≠ Employee

For a construct to qualify as followership it must be conceptualized/operationalized (a) *in relation to* leaders or the leadership process, and/or (b) in contexts where individuals hold follower positions or identities. Followership theory is **not** "leadership from the follower's perspective." The formal theory specifies three construct classes: **followership characteristics**, **followership behaviors** (obeying, deferring, voicing, resisting, advising), and **followership outcomes** (individual, relational, and leadership-process outcomes).

> [!note] Shamir's boundary on "fully shared" leadership
> Shamir (2012): "If it is fully shared, I suggest we don't call it leadership because the term loses any added value." For a phenomenon to be leadership, some actors must exert **disproportionate non-coercive influence** — a useful tension with [[Shared Leadership]].
