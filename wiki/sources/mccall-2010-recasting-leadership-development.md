# McCall (2010) — Recasting Leadership Development

Nav: [[index]] | [[Learning & Development MOC]] | [[Morgan McCall]]

> [!abstract] One-line
> A *SIOP* focal article arguing that **experience — not genetics, training, or business school — is the primary source of learning to lead**, and that the reason this well-established knowledge is so rarely applied lies in executives' results-drive and HR's parochialism, not in gaps in knowledge.

## Seven "sure bets" about experience and leadership development

1. **To the extent it is learned, leadership is learned from experience.** Twin studies (Arvey et al., 2006, 2007) attribute ~30% of leadership role occupancy to heredity — leaving the majority to experience.
2. **Certain experiences matter more than others.** Recurring types across studies: early work experiences, short-term assignments, major line assignments, other people (esp. very good/very bad bosses), hardships, plus miscellany like training. The folklore [[70-20-10 Rule]] traces to McCall et al. (1988) / Lindsey et al. (1987) data (no original published source for the exact ratios).
3. **Experiences are powerful because of the *challenges* they present** — the unexpected, high stakes, complexity, pressure, novelty (McCauley, Ruderman, Ohlott & Morrow, 1994). See [[Developmental Experiences]].
4. **Different experiences teach different lessons.** Identify the challenges in an experience → deduce what it can teach (start-up vs turnaround differ in required learning).
5. **Jobs and assignments can be made more developmental** by adding the known power-elements — challenge + feedback (DeRue & Wellman, 2009) + sometimes coaching — without changing jobs.
6. **People can get the experiences they need despite obstacles** — whoever controls assignments controls development; matching needs to opportunities is a matter of **intentionality**.
7. **Learning takes place over time and is dynamic** — nonlinear, full of serendipity, dead ends, do-overs. Timing matters (career transitions; readiness to learn). Failure to grow new strengths or address weaknesses can cause [[Derailment]].

## Why the money is on other horses

Despite the sure bets, HR retreats to the "safer haven" of integrated competency models, 360-feedback, performance management, and training. McCall's **heresy**: the culprit is not the [[Knowing-Doing Gap|knowing–doing gap]] in the usual sense (enough is known) but three deep obstacles:

- **Beliefs about what people can(not) learn** — the "you have it or you don't" assumption undermines using experience for development, and reduces "throwing people into fires" to **testing rather than developing** (see [[Competency Models vs Experience (Leadership Development)]]).
- **Short-term results pressure** — development is long-term; the pressure to pick the proven candidate over the one who'd learn most is often overwhelming, and risks derailing talent later.
- **Misunderstanding the true cost of development** — visible HR expenditures (training, coaching, tuition) are easy to measure and cut; the *real* cost is opportunity cost of the learning curve, and the *return* is long-term leadership quality — hard to assess. This makes HR programs "excellent and easy targets for cost cutting."

A fourth issue ties them together: **development competes with other priorities unless it is embedded in strategic business needs.**

## Imperfect strategies (put experience at the center)

- **Go with the flow rather than fight it** — executives already accept challenging assignments; work *with* their language of talent (Tables 1–2 decode succession-planning "lingo" into derailment / challenging assignments / availability). Run two succession events: one for replacement planning, one to select *experiences* high-potentials need.
- **Embed development in business strategy** — link key business challenges to the experiences talent should get; build "what can/can't be learned here" charts per business/function to justify cross-boundary moves.
- **Use ongoing business initiatives, not HR processes** — e.g., Ghosn's cross-functional teams at Nissan, Gerstner's task forces at IBM: already-powerful learning events needing only "a little tweaking" (influence who's chosen, add feedback/reflection).
- **Create a new role — the "wise counselor"/business partner** — someone credible and knowledgeable who nudges who-gets-what-experience in real time; reports to line managers with a dotted line to HR. "The very informality of the nudging and tweaking process is what makes it work."
- **Focus on learning *from* experience, not just having it** — reflection is not managers' strength (Mintzberg). Biweekly "what did you do / what did you learn" contacts made executives' experience richer ("I never knew that asking dumb questions could make so big a difference"). "Much of development is a matter of attention."
- **Shift the focus to mastery** — reframe leadership development as acquiring the **expertise** to meet the [[Five Demands of Leadership]], paralleling deliberate-practice research (Ericsson): mastery of a large K/S domain, over a lifetime, requiring intentional effort/practice, teachers at inflection points, and "playing on edge."

## The five demands of leadership

Thousands of experience-lessons reduced to **[[Five Demands of Leadership|five demands]]** every leader faces: (1) setting/communicating direction, (2) aligning critical constituencies, (3) developing an executive temperament, (4) setting/living values, (5) growth of self and others. This avoids the "monotheistic search for one best style"; the demands can be met in remarkably different ways (equifinality rules — Kelleher, Welch, and Mulcahy all succeeded with distinct styles).

## Provocative conclusion

"Could it be that **selection is ultimately the key to development**?" — select/promote leaders who (a) see leadership as business-critical, (b) accept that talent can learn to lead, (c) believe it's learned through experience, (d) hold a long-term perspective — and they'll model development and hold others accountable. Three research priorities: better understanding of **potential**, of **transitions/passages**, and of **what enhances or obstructs learning from experience** (esp. reflection).
