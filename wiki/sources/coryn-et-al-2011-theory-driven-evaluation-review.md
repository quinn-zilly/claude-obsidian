# Coryn et al. (2011) — A Systematic Review of Theory-Driven Evaluation Practice

> [!abstract] One-line
> A systematic review of **45 case examples** of [[Theory-Driven Evaluation]] (TDE) published 1990–2009, coded against a derived set of five [[Core Principles of Theory-Driven Evaluation|core principles]], to test whether TDE *practice* actually enacts what TDE *theory* prescribes.

## Context

TDE rose to prominence with Chen's 1990 book *Theory-Driven Evaluations*. It has drawn both strong advocates and vocal critics (notably Scriven and Stufflebeam), but before this review little systematically-derived evidence existed to substantiate or repudiate either side's claims. The authors replicate the logic of Miller & Campbell's (2006) review of empowerment evaluation, but the phenomenon under study is TDE practice.

## Method

Multistage sampling of mainstream journals and books (dissertations, tech reports, conference papers excluded). Broad scan yielded 205 candidate works → screening → **final sample of 45 codable case examples**. Average of ~10 TDE-related articles/chapters published per year, of which only ~2/year were adequately-detailed, codable cases. Two-coder pairs; interrater agreement *p* = .91, κ = .87.

The five [[Core Principles of Theory-Driven Evaluation|core principles]] were derived deductively + inductively from major theoretical writers (Chen, Donaldson, Rogers, Weiss, White) and vetted by leading scholars. Principles are **situational, not absolute** criteria.

## Key Findings

- **Settings**: ~47% health interventions, ~27% educational programs, remainder crime/safety, transport, environment, business. School-age children the most common target population (31%).
- **Why TDE was chosen**: overwhelmingly **ideological** (73%) — evaluators declared TDE "more scientifically sound" than method-driven alternatives. Far less often: guiding program development (13%), stakeholder involvement (11%), cost/time reduction (one case). No evidence funders/sponsors drove the choice.
- **Program theory formulation**: most theories drawn from existing theory/research; stakeholder-derived (implicit) theory also common. Gap between the rich menu of prescribed sources (Donaldson's four; Patton's deductive/inductive/user-oriented) and thinner practice.
- **Principle 5 (causal explanation) is the demarcating principle** — using program theory to explain *how* (mediation/moderation), not just *whether*, distinguishes TDE from other evaluation forms, yet is the least consistently enacted.

## The Advocate–Critic Debate

> [!quote] Weiss (1997a)
> "The idea of theory-driven evaluation is plausible and cogent, and it promises to bring greater explanatory power to evaluation. However, problems beset its use…"

- **Critics** — Scriven ("Minimalist Theory," 1998): the evaluator's job is to determine *whether* programs work, not *how*; substantive theory is "a luxury… not essential for 99% of all evaluations" (aspirin analogy). Stufflebeam (2001): validated program theories rarely exist; ad hoc theory-building wastes resources and creates conflict of interest (evaluators evaluating their own theory). Coryn: TDE writers tell you *what* to do but not *how*.
- **Advocates** — Chen, Donaldson, Rogers: causal *explanation* has high value for replication, improvement, and policy; a TDE can distinguish **implementation failure** vs **theory failure** vs **unsuitable context** (Pawson & Tilley's realist evaluation).
- **Logical tension** flagged: if a theory is a fixed testable model, one cannot logically "test" unanticipated side effects outside the model — yet identifying side effects is a TDE aspiration.
