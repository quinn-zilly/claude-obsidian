# Hough & Johnson (2013) — Use and Importance of Personality Variables in Work Settings

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> A comprehensive handbook chapter arguing that **personality variables, once dismissed as unrelated to work performance, are now well-established determinants** of individual, team, and organizational outcomes. The organizing thesis: **criterion-related validity is high when predictor and criterion are conceptually matched, and low when they are not** — so the field must link *specific* personality facets to *specific* criteria rather than correlating everything with everything. Six sections: (a) structure of personality, (b) process models of how personality determines performance, (c) construct validity + magnitude of criterion-related validity, (d) demographic subgroup differences, (e) measurement methods, (f) intentional distortion (faking). Updates and extends [[hough-oswald-ock-2015-beyond-the-big-five|Hough & Furnham (2003)]] with dozens of new meta-analyses (Tables 9.1–9.6).

## Structure of personality

Early research correlated *all* personality variables with *all* criteria; most correlations were near-zero, creating the false impression that **personality is generally unrelated to performance**. The corrective insight: **the trait must be relevant to the criterion**, and both predictor and criterion should be conceptualized as constructs (Hough & Schneider 1996).

- The dominant taxonomy is the **[[Five-Factor Model]]** (Big Five), derived via the **lexical approach** — robust and generalizable across rating sources, some cultures/languages, and rotation methods, but criticized as insufficiently comprehensive and too heterogeneous.
- The **HEXACO** six-factor model adds Honesty/Humility; across four cross-cultural samples the six factors account for **10–15% more variance in predicting workplace delinquency** than the FFM.
- **[[Facet-Level Personality Measurement|Lower-order facets of the same Big Five factor often correlate very differently with job performance]]** — relationships masked by broad measures (Hough 1992). Facet scores can be aggregated up into broad factors or into higher-order **[[Compound Traits (Personality)|compound variables]]**.
- **[[Nomological-Web Clustering]]** (Hough & Ones 2001): an alternative to factor analysis — group personality variables by *similarity in their patterns of relationships with variables outside the personality domain* (e.g., criteria), consistent with convergent/discriminant construct validity (Campbell & Fiske 1959).
- **[[Compound Traits (Personality)|Compound traits]]** = combinations of basic traits that need not covary, combined to maximize prediction of a specific criterion (integrity, customer-service orientation, employee reliability, managerial potential).

## Process models of the personality → performance link

The past decade saw a surge in models specifying *how* personality influences performance (moderators + mediators). See [[Personality–Performance Process Models]].

- **Johnson (2003)**: performance is determined directly by knowledge, skill, motivation, **+ work habits** (Johnson's addition). Personality is an *indirect* determinant that works through these direct determinants. Splits motivation into proactive cognitive processes, online (self-regulation) processes, and psychological motives.
- **Barrick, Mitchell & Stewart (2003)**: three motivational mediators — accomplishment striving, status striving, communion striving; situational demands (cooperative vs competitive) moderate which mediates.
- **[[Trait Activation Theory|Tett & Burnett (2003) trait activation theory]]**: traits are activated by trait-relevant situational cues; trait-consistent behavior counts as performance only when the organization values it. Predicts positive trait–performance correlations when expression meets work demands, negative when it interferes.
- **Johnson & Hezlett (2008)** integrated these into a single high-level general model in which personality is a determinant of *every* direct determinant of performance, plus a potential moderator of many paths.

## Criterion-related validity (Tables 9.1–9.6)

> [!key-insight] The matching principle
> **Validities are high when predictor and criterion are conceptually matched, low when they are not.** E.g., Sales Effectiveness is predicted strongly by Achievement and Dominance (and less by Dependability, Sociability, Rugged Individualism), but *not* by Emotional Stability, Agreeableness, or Openness.

Illustrative corrected validities (ρ): Global **Conscientiousness** ≈ .23 for overall job performance (Barrick & Mount 1991), and Conscientiousness correlates most highly with overall performance across many jobs — but **facets diverge**: Achievement and Dependability are differentially important by job. Conscientiousness–CWB ≈ −.35; Agreeableness–interpersonal-CWB ≈ −.46. **Compound variables show the highest validities when the criterion is complex.**

## Demographic subgroup differences

- **Ethnicity** (Foldes et al. 2008; Hough et al. 2001): at the *factor* level, Black/Hispanic/Asian/American-Indian means are similar to White means (avg |d| ≈ 0.06). But at the *facet* level, differences emerge: Blacks score similarly to Whites on **Dominance** but ~0.30–0.40 *lower* on **Sociability** (both facets of Extraversion). At the factor level these facet differences are obscured.
- **The Dominance "win–win" argument**: because Dominance correlates with many criteria (job performance, sales, managerial effectiveness, leadership, getting ahead) while Sociability does not, and because Blacks ≈ Whites on Dominance, using a *facet-level* Dominance measure yields **higher validity AND lower adverse impact** than a broad Extraversion measure. This directly contradicts advice to measure at the factor level.
- **Gender**: women score higher on Dependability (a Conscientiousness facet) and Agreeableness; men higher on Emotional Stability and Dominance. **Age**: 40+ vs <40 differences minor except older people higher on Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, and especially Dependability.

## Measurement methods

- **Self-report Likert** — easy to build, good validity, but susceptible to faking.
- **Forced-choice** — pairs statements of equal social desirability to reduce faking; risk = **ipsativity** (scores are relative to one's own other traits, not to other people — a serious problem for selection). Mitigated by many scales or same-trait pairs.
- **Computer adaptive personality tests** (NCAPS, TAPAS) — use an **ideal-point** model (Thurstone) rather than dominance-response; NCAPS avoids ipsativity by pairing same-trait statements; TAPAS uses multidimensional pairwise preference (MDPP) IRT. Less fakable, similar validity, precise, secure (good for unproctored internet testing).
- **Conditional reasoning tests** (James 1998) — look like reasoning tests but tap ego-protective justification mechanisms (e.g., hostile attribution bias → aggression). CRT-Aggression r ≈ .26 for CWB. Easily faked once purpose is known.
- **Other-ratings** — well-acquainted observers are accurate (Connelly & Ones 2010 meta-analysis: **other-ratings yield higher validity than self-ratings**), suggesting observers see targets more accurately than targets see themselves. Multiple observers → potentially very high validity. Pragmatic obstacles in selection settings.

## Intentional distortion (faking)

Key findings that have stood the test of time (see [[Intentional Distortion (Faking)]]):

- People *can* fake when instructed (especially in the negative direction); distortion is **larger in directed-faking studies than in real high-stakes settings**.
- Applicant factor structures do *not* collapse the way instructed-faking structures do.
- **Faking does not appear to be corrosive of criterion-related validity** — predictive validities with applicants seem unaffected (e.g., Ones et al. 1993 integrity-test meta-analysis, r ≈ .29 for CWB, N = 93,092).
- Applicants score higher than incumbents, but less than commonly thought (moderate differences on ES and Conscientiousness only; Birkeland et al. 2006).
- **Warnings + consequences reduce distortion; warnings alone (without consequences) do not** (Dwight & Donovan 2003). Warnings don't harm criterion validity.
- Corrections to content scales based on social-desirability ("Unlikely Virtues") scores do NOT improve content-scale–criterion relationships — and may differentially disadvantage Hispanics (who score ~0.5 SD higher on Unlikely Virtues).

## Path forward

Cumulate knowledge systematically under a standard **criterion taxonomy** and build a **[[Synthetic Validity|synthetic validation]] database** linking specific predictor and criterion constructs (Johnson et al. 2010, "validation is like motor oil: synthetic is better"). This enables tailored, a priori prediction equations for any job.
