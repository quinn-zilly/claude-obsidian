# Parker, Van den Broeck & Holman (2017): Work Design Influences

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design Influences Framework]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]]

> [!abstract] One-line
> A multilevel synthesis that flips the usual question — instead of "what does work design cause?", it asks **"what causes work design?"** — and organizes the answer into a framework of contextual and individual influences operating through managers' and employees' motivation, KSAs, and opportunity.

## Core contribution

High-quality [[Work Design (Concept)|work design]] (job autonomy, variety, social support, reasonable demands) is a known driver of well-being, attitudes, and performance — yet poorly designed jobs persist. Mainstream research models work design as the **independent variable** at the start of a causal chain to outcomes (see the meta-analysis [[humphrey-nahrgang-morgeson-2007-work-design-meta-analysis|Humphrey, Nahrgang & Morgeson 2007]]). This paper treats work design as the **dependent variable** and reviews the disparate, multi-disciplinary literature on its antecedents using an integrative [[Work Design Influences Framework|multilevel framework]].

It complements the companion centennial review [[parker-morgeson-johns-2017-100-years-work-design|Parker, Morgeson & Johns (2017)]] (same lead author, same year), which mapped the field's five historical perspectives. Where that paper looks at the *shape* of work design research, this one looks at the *causes* of work design itself.

## The framework

Work design arises from solving two "universal problems of organizing" (Puranam et al., 2014): **dividing labor** and **integrating effort**. These choices are made through two routes:

- **Formal decision-making** by those in authority (CEOs, managers, team leaders).
- **Informal, emergent, and social processes** — employee-led [[Job Crafting|job crafting]], i-deals, role-making, group norms.

Both routes are governed by the [[Ability-Motivation-Opportunity Model|ability-motivation-opportunity (AMO)]] of the actor: their motivation, their knowledge/skills/abilities (KSAs), and their opportunity (including power).

Contextual influences operate at four levels, via three mechanisms each (direct; indirect via managers; indirect via employees):

| Level | Key influences | Strongest evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Higher-level external** | Globalization & market liberalization; national economy (GDP, unemployment); national culture; national institutions (trade unions, employment policy, training systems); [[Institutional Isomorphism\|institutional regimes & isomorphism]]; occupations | National institutions & institutional regimes (M–H); rest weak/case-study |
| **Organizational** | Strategy ([[Strategic HRM]]); high-involvement HR practices; operational uncertainty; technology (SBTC/RBTC); organizational design (bureaucracy, restructuring) | Reasonably strong (esp. strategy, operational uncertainty, technology, org design) |
| **Local context (work group)** | Group composition/heterogeneity; interdependence; group autonomy; leadership | Strongest for group autonomy & leadership; interdependence & composition scarce |
| **Individual** | Demographics (age, gender, ethnicity); competence/learning; personality/motivation; job crafting | Mostly low; works largely via crafting/i-deals and via signaling to managers |

## Key mechanisms (selected findings)

- **International supply chains** transmit coercive pressure: client firms in developed markets selecting suppliers on price can "coerce" cost-minimization → poor work designs. But organizational counter-choices (codes of conduct, long-term collaboration) plus national institutions can "turn the boat."
- **National economy**: high GDP + low unemployment → tighter labor markets that motivate and enable managers to design enriched work and boost employees' power to demand it. 15-country European evidence (1995–2010) supports this.
- **National culture**: Hofstede-based arguments (uncertainty avoidance, power distance → preference for formalized/centralized jobs) are only weakly and inconsistently supported.
- **Institutions** ([[Institutional Isomorphism|neo-institutional theory]]): coercive, mimetic, and normative isomorphic pressures shape work design — though direct evidence on work design (vs. HR/operational practices) is thin. Social democratic [[Institutional Regimes & Employment Regime Theory|employment regimes]] (Nordic) show the most enriched work ("Nordic advantage").
- **Technology**: rarely determines work design per se; effects depend on managerial choices, employee skill, and operational uncertainty. SBTC (skill-biased) and RBTC (routine-biased) technical change frame divergent effects on high- vs. low-skill and routine vs. non-routine jobs.
- **Operational uncertainty** (sociotechnical/contingency theme): high uncertainty → enriched jobs (autonomy, variety) cope better with variability; low uncertainty → standardization.
- **Work group**: self-managing/[[Autonomous Work Groups|autonomous teams]] raise individual job enrichment, but can also raise demands and breed self-imposed normative control (Barker's "concertive control"). Interdependence cuts both ways (support vs. constrained individual autonomy).
- **Individual**: demographic attributes act as **signals** to managers (per the Circular & Dynamic Model; Clegg & Spencer 2007). Age, gender, ethnicity, migrant status linked to poorer work design, partly via discrimination; personality/proactivity shapes crafting.

## Evidence-quality verdict

Strongest: organizational factors + national institutions/regimes. Weakest: global/international, national economy/culture, occupational. Across the board, researchers tend to **infer rather than measure** the mediating mechanisms, and there is little systematic comparison of the *relative* potency of global vs. national vs. occupational vs. organizational factors. Morgeson et al. (2010): how context affects work design "represents an open playing field."

## Why it matters for the vault

This is the antecedents-side companion to the work-design cluster. It bridges [[Teams & Org Behavior MOC|work design]] with [[Institutional & Strategy MOC|institutional theory and strategy]] — making explicit how [[Institutional Isomorphism|isomorphic pressures]], [[Strategic HRM|SHRM]], and the [[Ability-Motivation-Opportunity Model|AMO model]] feed into the design of jobs. It also reinforces the [[parker-morgeson-johns-2017-100-years-work-design|100-years paper's]] call for a **multilevel** theory of work design.
