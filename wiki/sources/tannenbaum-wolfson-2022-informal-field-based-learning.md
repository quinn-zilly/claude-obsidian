# Tannenbaum & Wolfson 2022 — Informal (Field-Based) Learning

Nav: [[index]] | [[sources/_index|Sources]]

## One-Line Summary

Annual Review synthesizing what is known about **informal field-based learning (IFBL)** — intentional, self-directed on-the-job learning — and offering the **CAM-OS framework** (Capability, Awareness, Motivation, Opportunity, Support) as leverage points for promoting healthy IFBL.

## Core Argument

Most workplace learning happens informally: estimates range from 70% to more than 90% of competency acquisition occurring outside formal training and development (T&D). US organizations spent ~$204B on formal T&D in 2018, yet the bulk of actual learning is "in the field." IFBL is defined (per Wolfson et al. 2018, *J. Appl. Psychol.* 103) as "engaging in intentional, self-directed behavior aimed at learning new, work-oriented, and organizationally valued content outside of a formal learning program."

IFBL comprises three learning behaviors:

- **Feedback / reflection-based learning** — seeking feedback, coaching, debriefing experiences
- **Vicarious learning** — observing others, asking experts, watching demonstrations
- **Experimentation / new experiences** — trying tasks in novel ways, trial-and-error, novel assignments

## Situating IFBL — Three Continua

Learning constructs vary along three dimensions (adapting Cerasoli et al. 2014 and Wolfson et al. 2018). IFBL sits at the **informal, self-directed, and intentional** ends:

| Continuum | IFBL position |
|---|---|
| Formal ↔ Informal | Informal |
| Other-directed ↔ Self-directed | Self-directed |
| Incidental ↔ Intentional | Intentional |

Intentionality is what distinguishes IFBL from incidental learning (a by-product of other activity, sometimes unconscious). It lets individuals and organizations be purposeful rather than hoping learning happens serendipitously.

## The CAM-OS Framework

Whether employees engage in IFBL, and whether it yields desired outcomes, is a function of **personal readiness** and **situational readiness**:

**Personal readiness** (learner-level):

- **Capability** — competencies to extract value from opportunities (observation, question-asking, feedback-seeking, metacognition/self-reflection). Focus on teachable informal-learning skills rather than GMA.
- **Awareness** — "learning attunement": knowing what/when/how to learn in the environment, and which landmines to avoid.
- **Motivation** — willingness to exert discretionary effort. Linked to learning goal orientation and promotion focus; more situationally malleable than personality.

The three likely operate **compensatorily / in tandem** — a deficiency in one may be offset, and possessing two may build the third.

**Situational readiness** (organizational-level):

- **Opportunity** — the chance to engage, described along four sub-dimensions (Figure 4): **richness** (barren→rich), **slack** (time/resources; may be curvilinear), **malleability** (fixed→highly malleable; job crafting), **appropriateness** (detrimental→constructive).
- **Support** — symbolic and tangible: work environment/climate (esp. [[Psychological Safety]]), manager/leader support, peer support, organization practices (rewards, promotions, staffing).

Relatively stable **individual characteristics** (personality, propensity such as learning goal orientation) influence engagement but are less actionable than the five readiness factors. Surface demographics show no consistent effect.

## Outcomes

Meta-analytically (Cerasoli et al. 2018), informal learning is positively related to affective outcomes (satisfaction, engagement, commitment, lower job insecurity), cognitive outcomes (knowledge and skill acquisition, sharing), and performance / talent-pipeline outcomes. But IFBL is **not always positive** — it can create safety hazards, distract from core work, be perceived as overreach, or increase stress ("the dark side of IFBL").

## Practical Tools

- **Field Learning Needs Analysis (FLNA)** — analogue of a training needs analysis, but identifying on-the-job learning needs/opportunities via interviews or focus groups.
- **Red-Yellow-Green (RYG) chart** — categorizes tasks: red = do not learn by trial-and-error; yellow = only with an experienced person / safety net; green = safe to try. Helps avoid detrimental IFBL.

The CAM-OS framework is applied through three stakeholder perspectives — **senior leaders** (strategic: where/how to invest, balance formal vs informal), **managers/team leaders** (encourage/facilitate healthy IFBL, avoid detrimental IFBL), and **individual employees** (career: be a better on-the-job learner). ## Relation to Formal Training

CAM-OS shares elements with formal-training models (science of training, ability-motivation-opportunity HRM framework), but the emphasis differs. In formal T&D learning objectives and experiences are prespecified; IFBL relies **completely** on on-the-job opportunities that the learner must often identify or create. Thus awareness and capability matter far more in IFBL. The formal transfer-of-training literature focuses on *applying what was taught* (proximal transfer), not fostering new, emergent, self-directed learning — a "semipermeable membrane" that training and debriefs can help bridge.

> [!key-insight]
> IFBL reframes development from something organizations *deliver* to something they *enable*. The high-leverage move is not more content but shaping the five readiness factors — especially opportunity and psychological safety.

## Methodological Notes for Future Research

IFBL is unplanned and fluid, so it resists classic pre/post testing and observation. The authors recommend **event-sampling methodology**, qualitative/diary methods, and **longitudinal designs** with baselines and repeated KSAO measures. Self-report is preferable for self-reflection and personal readiness; other-report suits observable feedback-seeking and environmental richness.
