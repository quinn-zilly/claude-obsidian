# Colquitt & Rodell (2015) — Measuring Justice and Fairness

> [!key-insight]
> The canonical review of **how** justice gets operationalized. Two definitional moves anchor it: **justice = perceived adherence to rules** (descriptive), **fairness = a global perception of appropriateness** (evaluative), with fairness theoretically "downstream" from justice. From those two distinctions plus a dimensionality distinction comes a **2×2 [[Justice Measurement Taxonomy|taxonomy]]** of measurement approaches.

## Fundamental Issues: Three "Conversions"

Any justice measure must be tailored along three axes:

1. **Focus** — organization-focused vs. supervisor-focused (bold portion of items). Sometimes dictated by research question, sometimes by theoretical lens (social exchange logic flows more seamlessly when benefit focus matches reciprocation target). But [[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al. (2013)]] found focus-matching "makes little difference empirically" — only 3 of 36 correlations were stronger for focus-matching; supervisor-focused justice is just stronger in general.
2. **Context** — selection, performance evaluation, compensation, dispute, change, etc. Most scales tailor easily; occasionally a context introduces a new rule (e.g., Gilliland's job-relevance rule for selection tests).
3. **Experience bracketing** — single event → multiple events → entity. George & Jones (2000): constructs get meaning through a bracketing process. Extensive bracketing shifts a measure from assessing *events* ("trees") to *entities* ("forests").

## The Justice Rules (Table 8.1)

The descriptive backbone — see [[Four Justice Dimensions]] for the full rule list. Procedural (8 rules: process/decision control, consistency, bias suppression, accuracy, correctability, representativeness, ethicality), distributive (equity, equality, need), interpersonal (respect, propriety), informational (truthfulness, justification).

## The 2×2 Taxonomy (Table 8.2)

Cross **emphasis** (descriptive justice vs. evaluative fairness) with **dimensional distinctions** (kept vs. collapsed):

|  | Assessing Justice | Assessing Fairness |
|---|---|---|
| **Dimensions kept** | Faceted Justice | Faceted Fairness |
| **Dimensions collapsed** | Latent Justice | Overall Fairness |

- **Faceted Justice** — Moorman (1991) first; **Colquitt (2001)** the modern standard four-factor scale (Box 8.1). Strength: grounds the phenomenon in specific, *actionable* rules (intervention-ready). Drawback: very high inter-dimension correlation → multicollinearity ("bouncing betas").
- **Latent Justice** — model the four dimensions as indicators of a second-order latent factor (Colquitt & Shaw 2005, loadings .64–.86). Appropriate when hypotheses don't reference specific dimensions; consistent with exchange-based logic that dimensions are functionally substitutable. A "growth stock."
- **Overall Fairness** — single global scale ("Overall, I'm treated fairly"). Already realizing growth; entity-structured by default.
- **Faceted Fairness** — least growth potential; the border between interpersonal fairness and overall fairness is "blurry" (the multiple meanings of *treat*).

## When to Use Which (Summary)

Faceted and latent **justice** work best as **independent variables**; **fairness** approaches work well as **mediators** or **manipulation checks** (fairness = the "sentiment that encourages reciprocation," Blau). Exception: studies predicting *rule adherence itself* (e.g., training supervisors to be just) use justice measures even as dependent variables.

## Four Emerging Measurement Issues

1. **Anticipations / expectations** — anticipatory justice (Shapiro & Kirkman) and justice expectations (Bell et al.) capture *future* fairness; measures assess the past, but employees judge events before they occur.
2. **Within-person methodologies** — [[Experience-Sampling Methodology|ESM]]; justice varies day-to-day. Greenberg's structural/social split predicts procedural/distributive are more stable, interpersonal/informational more variable over time (confirmed: Hausknecht et al. 2011; Holtz & Harold 2009).
3. **Multiple sources** — most field studies use 2 sources (employee + supervisor), risking common-method bias. Employee–supervisor agreement on justice rule adherence is modest (interpersonal r = .26, informational r = .46; Zapata et al. 2013). Coworker reports / justice climate offer a route around source-bound mediation.
4. **Operationalizing injustice** — some argue injustice "moves the needle," not justice (Rupp & Spencer). Current scales suffer "continuum truncation" — they sample rule adherence, not violation.
