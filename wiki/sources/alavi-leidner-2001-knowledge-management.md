# Alavi & Leidner (2001) — Knowledge Management Systems

Navigation: [[sources/_index|Sources]] | [[Knowledge Management]] | [[Knowledge-Based View of the Firm]]

## Core Claim

Knowledge management systems (KMS) — IT-based systems that support the creation, transfer, and application of knowledge — should be grounded in the rich multi-disciplinary literature on organizational knowledge. Effective KMS design requires clarity about what knowledge is before deciding how to manage it.

## Purpose

Comprehensive review of KM literature from IT, strategic management, and organizational theory, synthesized into a process view of organizational knowledge management with attention to IT's role at each stage. Published in MIS Quarterly (2001), highly cited as a foundational reference in both IS and KM research.

## Key Contributions

### Five Perspectives on Knowledge

The authors identify five distinct ways knowledge has been conceptualized, each implying different KMS design priorities:

| Perspective | View of Knowledge | KMS Implication |
|---|---|---|
| State of mind | Personal understanding | Support individual learning and internalization |
| Object | Storable, manipulable thing | Build repositories and retrieval systems |
| Process | Simultaneous knowing and acting | Support processes and know-how application |
| Access to information | Condition of having information | Provide access and connections to expertise |
| Capability | Capacity for effective action | Enable performance and competence building |

### Data-Information-Knowledge Hierarchy (and its critique)

Common view: data → information → knowledge. But Tuomi (1999) inverts this: knowledge must exist before information can be formulated, and before data can be measured. Knowledge is not at the top of a stack — it is a prerequisite for everything below.

Alavi & Leidner's working definition: **knowledge is information processed in the mind of individuals** — it is personalized, related to facts, procedures, concepts, interpretations, and judgments. Information becomes knowledge once processed; knowledge becomes information once articulated.

### The Four KM Processes (Process View)

The authors organize KM around four organizational processes:

1. **Knowledge Creation** — individual and organizational learning, R&D, knowledge combination
2. **Knowledge Storage and Retrieval** — organizational memory, databases, expert systems
3. **Knowledge Transfer** — communication, training, socialization, IT-mediated sharing
4. **Knowledge Application** — decision support, performance support, expert systems

IT can support each stage differently. The process view avoids treating KMS as monolithic.

### Knowledge Taxonomies Used

- **Tacit vs. explicit** (Polanyi; Nonaka & Takeuchi) — tacit knowledge is experiential, context-specific, hard to articulate; explicit is codifiable and transferable
- **Individual vs. collective** — knowledge can reside in persons or in shared routines, culture, databases
- **Declarative, procedural, causal, relational** — different facets of what is "known"

### Research Issues Identified

- How does IT affect knowledge creation, not just storage and retrieval?
- How do social and cultural factors mediate KMS adoption?
- How should organizations measure the value of knowledge?
- When do KMS support innovation vs. entrench existing knowledge?
