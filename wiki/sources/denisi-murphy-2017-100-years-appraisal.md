# Performance Appraisal and Performance Management: 100 Years of Progress?

**DeNisi, A. S., & Murphy, K. R. (2017).** *Journal of Applied Psychology, 102*(3), 421–433.

> [!key-insight] Core Argument
> Reviewing 100 years of performance-appraisal (PA) research — mostly through the lens of the *Journal of Applied Psychology* — the answer to "100 years of progress?" is "yes and no." We learned a great deal about **individual-level** appraisal (rating formats don't matter much; traditional rater-error measures are poor criteria; context matters enormously), but the field has **never demonstrated that improving individual performance improves firm-level performance**. That link is "an elusive goal."

## Structure of the review

DeNisi & Murphy sort PA research into **eight substantive areas** and trace each, focusing on the 1970–2000 "heyday":

1. **Scale formats** — dominant early line (>40% of PA articles in 1970s). Graphic scales, ranking, forced-choice, weighted checklists, critical-incidents → BARS. [[Landy & Farr]]'s (1980) review effectively ended the format search: format has only modest effects on rating quality. Not wasted, though — it forced the field to anchor judgments behaviorally.
2. **Criteria for evaluating ratings** — the "holy trinity" of [[Rater Error Measures]] (halo, leniency, central tendency). Murphy & Balzer (1986) showed rater errors are uncorrelated with accuracy → ended reliance on error measures as the quality criterion.
3. **Training** — error-avoidance training can *reduce* accuracy; [[Frame-of-Reference Training]] (Bernardin & Buckley 1981; Woehr 1994) became the dominant, effective method.
4. **Reactions to appraisal** — justice/fairness perceptions (Cawley, Keeping & Levy 1998 meta: participation predicts reactions); "due process" appraisal (Folger et al. 1992; Taylor et al. 1995).
5. **Purpose of rating** — Cleveland, Murphy & Williams (1989): appraisals serve multiple, conflicting purposes; rater goals shape ratings (Murphy et al. 2004).
6. **Rating sources** — self, peer, supervisor; foundation for [[360-Degree Feedback]]. Sources differ systematically; self-ratings most lenient.
7. **Demographic effects** — lab (vignette) studies overstate race/sex/age bias; field effects are small. Called "one of the real success stories" of JAP research.
8. **Cognitive processes** — spiked in 1980s (Feldman 1981; Landy & Farr 1980), then faded by mid-90s as researchers realized appraisal is not a pure judgment task but a motivated act in a social context.

## The performance-management turn

PA is a discrete, infrequent, formal evaluation. **Performance management (PM)** is the broader set of activities — feedback, goal setting, incentives, training — aimed at improving performance and aligning it with strategy. PM research is much thinner. Three categories: (a) whole-process descriptions (mostly books — Aguinis 2013; Pulakos et al. 2012); (b) single-component studies (feedback, goals, incentives — e.g., ProMES, Pritchard et al. 1988); (c) firm-level HR-bundle studies (Huselid 1995).

> [!key-insight] The unanswered question
> The "?" in the title = the **individual-to-firm gap**. DeNisi & Smith (2014): no empirical evidence that improving individual performance improves firm performance. Establishing that link is "a critical priority."

## Prescriptions for future research

JAP should publish PM research that is (a) conducted in real organizations, (b) with real stakes, (c) assessing distal *and* proximal context, (d) measuring performance at individual, group, *and* firm levels. "Stop the moaning" about the research–practice gap and rebuild links to practice.
