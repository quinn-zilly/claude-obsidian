# Connolly, Ordóñez & Barker (2012) — Judgment and Decision Making

Chapter 19 in Weiner's *Handbook of Psychology* (2nd ed.). Comprehensive survey of JDM field for organizational/industrial psychology audience. Authors: [[Terry Connolly]] (Arizona), [[Lisa Ordóñez]] (Arizona), Steven Barker.

## Core Argument

JDM is inherently dual: descriptive (what people do) and normative/prescriptive (what they should do). Neither strand can be pursued in isolation — serious descriptive work surfaces normative questions; prescriptive models require psychological realism to be actionable.

## Structure

1. Normative vs. descriptive themes
2. Inference processes (lens model, MCPL, policy capturing, heuristics & biases)
3. Prediction (anchoring, availability, representativeness, overconfidence, hindsight bias, idea generation)
4. Preferences (utilities, MAUT, comparison processes, choice rules)
5. Single-choice decisions (SEU, Prospect Theory, framing, non-SEU models, signal detection)
6. Emotion and decision making (visceral emotions, cognitive emotions / regret, DJT)
7. Multiple related decisions (information search, sunk costs, dynamic DM)
8. Multiple decision makers (groups, negotiation, GDSS)

## Key Content

### Inference

**[[Lens Model]]** (Brunswik 1952 → Hammond 1955): cues → judgment; both judge and environment simultaneously modeled. [[Policy Capturing]] = regression model of individual judge; widely used, widely abused — cue orthogonality violates representative design; judges use few cues, linear, low test-retest reliability, low inter-judge agreement.

**MCPL**: learning from outcome feedback is "learning the hard way" (Klayman 1988); real-world tasks harder than lab; outcome feedback not the key to skill acquisition.

**[[Heuristics and Biases Program]]** (Tversky & Kahneman): representativeness, availability, anchoring — systematic errors vs. normative model. Paradigm waning: grew into error catalog; normative models questioned; some "errors" = subjects solving different problem. See [[tversky-kahneman-1974-heuristics-biases]] for founding paper.

### Prediction

- **Anchoring and adjustment**: insufficient adjustment (Bolger & Harvey 1993); inadequate search effort mechanism (Epley & Gilovich 2006)
- **Availability**: vivid/recent events overweighted; recent experience with disaster → overestimate probability; performance appraisals biased by events in prior 3 months
- **Representativeness**: gambler's fallacy; non-regressive predictions; base rate neglect (drug-testing example: 1% prevalence + 90% accurate test → P(user|positive) = 8.3%); Israeli flight instructor fallacy = pure regression misread as causal
- **[[Overconfidence]]**: confidence intervals too narrow (~45% miss rate vs. expected 2%); stable across domains; men > women in investment; declines with experience and with "think of ways I could be wrong" instructions
- **[[Hindsight Bias]]** (Fischhoff & Beyth 1975): "knew it all along"; impedes learning from outcomes; warning doesn't help; source-confusion debiasing (Marks & Arkes 2010) may help
- **Confirmation bias**: seek confirming evidence, not disconfirming; Wason (1960) four-card problem
- **Idea generation / brainstorming**: nominal groups > F2F brainstorming (production blocking); large EBS (electronic) > nominal groups; F2F still popular due to intuitive appeal + group well-being benefits

### Preferences

- Preferences constructed, not retrieved (Fischhoff 1991): framing, timing, order, context all shift stated preferences
- **[[Multiattribute Utility Theory]]** (MAUT): compensatory; additive; most normatively complete
- **Satisficing** (Simon 1955): accept first acceptable option; aspiration level may shift
- **Lexicographic / EBA** models: non-compensatory; sequential attribute comparison
- **Effort-accuracy tradeoff** (Payne, Bettman & Johnson 1993): more effortful rules = better choices but higher cost

### Single-Choice Decisions

- **SEU** (Savage 1954): subjective probabilities + subjective utilities; normative standard
- **[[Prospect Theory]]** (Kahneman & Tversky 1979): reference-point value function; loss aversion (losses steeper than gains); risk aversion in gains domain / risk seeking in loss domain; probability weighting (low p overweighted, high p underweighted); explains Allais paradox
- **[[Framing Effects]]**: identical options framed as gains vs. losses shift risk preferences; attribute framing (75% lean vs. 25% fat)
- **[[Signal Detection Theory (Decision)]]** (TSD): ROC curve; false positive vs. true positive tradeoff; threshold setting depends on base rate + error costs; underused in organizational research
- **Non-SEU models**: [[Image Theory]] (Beach 1990); SP/A model (Lopes 1987); [[Naturalistic Decision Making]] / RPD (Klein)

### Emotion and Decision Making

- Negative affect → more analytic; positive → more heuristic (general valence view)
- **[[Appraisal-Tendency Framework]]** (Lerner & Keltner 2000/2001): distinct emotions with distinct appraisal dimensions → different decision effects; fear (uncertainty/low control) → risk averse; anger (certainty/high control) → risk seeking; disgust eliminates endowment effect
- Integral vs. incidental emotions: incidental emotions (no normative relevance) carry over to unrelated decisions
- **Regret and [[Decision Justification Theory]]** (Connolly & Zeelenberg 2002): two components — *outcome regret* (counterfactual comparison) + *process/self-blame regret* (was decision justified?). Action effect (Kahneman & Tversky 1982): commission > omission regret. Time reversal: short-run regrets action, long-run regrets inaction (Gilovich & Medvec 1994). Regret priming → more careful decisions; process regret priming → better performance than outcome regret priming. DJT explains reason-based choice effects (decoy, accept/reject, most-important-attribute).

### Multiple Related Decisions

- **Information search**: confirmation bias; positive-test strategy (Klayman & Ha 1987) = generally appropriate heuristic; subjects make costly errors in info purchase; unstructured interviews = massive overpurchase of decision-irrelevant information
- **[[Sunk Cost Effect]]**: prior unrecoverable costs influence later decisions; theater ticket study (Arkes & Blumer 1985); foot-in-door; escalation of commitment (Staw & Ross 1989; bank loan officers); conflicting explanations: Prospect Theory loss function / waste aversion / self-justification
- **[[Dynamic Decision Making]]**: multiple interventions over time; feedback delays impede learning; system dynamics → oscillations; expert performance often RPD-based not deliberative

### Multiple Decision Makers

- Information flow problems: message tuning, curse of knowledge, transparency illusion, uneven communication, common information effect
- **Hidden profile tasks** (Stasser 1988): unique information not surfaced; rank-ordering helps
- Groups not clearly better/worse than individuals; depends on demonstrability of solution
- Escalation of commitment stronger in groups (collective commitment)
- **GDSS / electronic brainstorming**: computerized systems overcome F2F production blocking; anonymity increases idea quantity and controversial ideas
