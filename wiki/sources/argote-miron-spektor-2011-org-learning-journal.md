# Argote & Miron-Spektor (2011) — Organizational Learning: From Experience to Knowledge

> [!key-insight] Core Claim
> Organizational learning is the process by which experience is transformed into knowledge. This transformation is mediated by context (the active and latent context in which learning occurs) and involves three subprocesses: knowledge creation, retention, and transfer.

> [!note] Distinct from Book Chapter
> This is the **journal article** version published in *Organization Science* 22(5). A separately-ingested source covers the **book chapter** version ([[argote-2011-org-learning-experience]]). The journal article is the primary theoretical statement; the book chapter may have minor differences in framing or examples.

## Bibliographic Details

## Framework

### The Experience → Knowledge Pathway

The core model is: **Experience × Context → Knowledge**

- **Experience** is the raw input; it includes direct participation in activities and indirect exposure (observation, organizational records)
- **Context** mediates how experience is encoded:
  - *Active context*: the current task environment, team composition, available tools, and ongoing relationships
  - *Latent context*: accumulated residue of prior experience — stored routines, norms, culture, physical environment
- **Knowledge** is the output: the residue of experience that affects future behavior

### Three Subprocesses

| Subprocess | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Knowledge Creation** | Generation of new knowledge from experience, including experimentation, trial-and-error, and analogical transfer from other domains |
| **Knowledge Retention** | Embedding knowledge in organizational repositories so it persists beyond individual memory — includes people, tools, routines, structures |
| **Knowledge Transfer** | Movement of knowledge across units, boundaries, and time; includes both within-organization transfer and inter-organizational learning |

These three subprocesses are dynamically linked: retained knowledge shapes what new experiences are recognized as learning opportunities; transferred knowledge both enriches the receiving unit's context and modifies the sending unit's.

### Learning Curves

The paper grounds the framework in decades of empirical research on **learning curves** — the observation that performance improves at a decreasing rate as cumulative experience accumulates (see [[Learning Curves]]). The framework explains *why* learning curves occur (active context improvements, routine development) and *why* they vary across organizations (differences in latent context, retention effectiveness, transfer capabilities).

### The Role of Context

A key theoretical contribution is distinguishing active from latent context:
- Active context shapes which experiences are salient and how they are interpreted in real time
- Latent context is the "shadow" of organizational history — what the organization already knows shapes what new experience means

This makes learning inherently path-dependent: the same experience produces different learning in organizations with different histories.
