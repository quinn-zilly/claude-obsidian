# Marshall, Aguinis & Beltran (2024) — Theories of Performance: A Review and Integration

> [!abstract] One-line
> An integrative review of 15,535 articles (1946–2022, 44 journals) that distills 239 performance-related theories into six meta-theoretical constructs and one isomorphic equation: the [[CORE Model of Performance]] — **P = C + O + RE** (Capacity + Opportunity + Relevant Exchanges).

## The Problem

Performance is the central dependent variable across management, yet there is little consensus on what it *is*. Two symptoms:

- **Siloed literatures.** Firm-level (strategy) and individual-level (OB) performance are studied in parallel, using different language for similar phenomena — a driver of the micro–macro divide.
- **"Theorrhea" (Antonakis 2017).** A never-ending search for novelty produces theories that are, per Cronin et al. (2021), "disorganized, disconnected, contradictory, and redundant." The antidote is **parsimony**, not more theory.

## Method

Integrative review combining computer-aided techniques and human coding:

1. Top-10 journals across six SCImago management categories → 44 journals; Web of Science topic search for "performance" → **15,535 articles**.
2. Keyword cluster analysis (bibliometrix, MCA, 4 clusters) — clusters split mainly by **level of analysis**, confirming the silo.
3. KWIC search for "theory" in single-cluster abstracts (N=5,190) → 248 theories; 9 removed → **239 unique performance theories**.
4. Coded each theory on two dimensions: **level** (firm vs. individual) × **intra- vs. inter-agent**. Named six meta-theoretical constructs factor-analysis style.

## Six Meta-Theoretical Constructs

| | Intra-agent (Capacity) | Structural (Opportunity) | Inter-agent (Relevant Exchanges) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Firm** | Capabilities | Structures | Transactions |
| **Individual** | KSAOs | Roles | Relationships |

Exemplar theories per construct — Capabilities: resource-based, dynamic capabilities, upper echelons. Structures: institutional, contingency, network. Transactions: agency, stakeholder, resource dependence. KSAOs: conservation of resources, self-determination, [[Social Identity Theory]]. Roles: [[Trait Activation Theory]], role congruity, role theory. Relationships: social exchange, leadership, signaling, LMX.

## The CORE Model (key contribution)

The six constructs are **isomorphic across levels** — firm capabilities mirror individual KSAOs, structures mirror roles, transactions mirror relationships. Raised one level of abstraction, each pair collapses into a CORE component:

$$P = C + O + RE$$

- **Capacity (C)** = firm capabilities + individual KSAOs — the maximum performance potential given current resources.
- **Opportunity (O)** = firm structures + individual roles — the social situation enabling performance / knowledge sharing.
- **Relevant Exchanges (RE)** = firm transactions + individual relationships — exchanges between agents for a performance purpose.

Performance is thus a **multicomponent dynamic system**, not a process *or* an outcome. (See [[CORE Model of Performance]] for the full treatment.)

## Stop / Start Recommendations

**Stop:** studying performance in silos; aiming for illusory (seemingly-novel) theoretical contributions; thinking dichotomously about process-vs-outcome.
**Start:** with the big picture (ask "is my question about C, O, or RE?"); exploring intersections among constructs; cross-level micro–macro collaboration (e.g., macro scholars' endogeneity tools + micro scholars' experimental designs).

## My Notes / Contradictions

> [!contradiction] Language vs. substance
> Marshall et al. treat the micro–macro divide as *largely linguistic* — the same latent system described in different words. [[campbell-wiernik-2015-modeling-assessment-work-performance|Campbell & Wiernik (2015)]] make the parallel claim at the individual level (the latent structure of performance is invariant across levels/cultures/functions). The two reviews reinforce each other but stop short of a joint empirical test.

> [!note] Where Campbell's model sits in CORE
> Campbell & Wiernik's eight-factor model is a *content* model of individual performance-as-behavior. In CORE terms it lives inside the individual-level **Capacity/KSAOs → Roles → Relationships** slice, but CORE deliberately abstracts above behavioral content. The two are complementary, not competing.
