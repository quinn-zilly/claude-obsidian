## Summary

Argues that scientific research horizontally distributed across many contributors provides substantial complementary value to the standard small-team "vertically integrated" model. Crowdsourcing aims to maximize available resources, promote inclusiveness and transparency, and increase rigor and reliability. The two models should coexist, not compete.

## Core Distinction

**[[Vertical Integration (Science)]]** — standard model: small team conceives, designs, collects, analyzes, writes. Full research pipeline within one lab. Philosopher-scholar model. Credit via authorship on single paper.

**[[Horizontal Distribution (Science)]]** — crowdsourcing model: numerous researchers each carry out specific components under a coordination team. Resources, ownership, expertise distributed across many contributors.

## 2×2 Typology of Crowd Science

| | Low Communication (Curated Contributions) | High Communication (Crowd Collaboration) |
|---|---|---|
| **Select Experts** | Crowdsourcing designs/analysis, prepub replication, Many Labs | Coordinated analyses, expert peer review, intervention contests, Polymath, StudySwap |
| **Open to Anyone** | Prediction markets, CREP, citizen science | Crowd writing, open peer review, idea generation |

## Three Benefits of Crowdsourcing

1. **Enabling big science:** research designs that exceed single-lab capacity; vary stimuli/procedures rather than holding them constant; high-powered, precise studies; flip planning from "what can we do?" to "what's the best way?"

2. **Democratizing science:** Matthew effect means early advantages compound; grant funding overallocated to elite institutions; crowdsourcing opens participation to researchers from less-resourced institutions and underrepresented groups; talent is globally distributed

3. **Assessing robustness:** crowdsourcing uniquely suited to evaluate reliability and generalizability; multilab designs vary populations and procedures; failure to replicate tends to be *consistent* across labs (not explained by moderators)

## Key Empirical Reference Point

Aggregating 6 major replication initiatives (190 effects): crowdsourced teams successfully replicated 90 (47%). Non-replicable effects fail consistently across labs and demographics — not explained by hidden moderators. Expertise level (senior vs. junior researcher) does not predict replication success.

## Crowdsourcing Across the Research Lifecycle

| Stage | Crowd mechanism |
|---|---|
| Ideation | Polymath projects, hypothesis contests (Jia et al., 2018) |
| Assembling resources | Science Exchange, StudySwap |
| Study design | Same hypothesis → multiple independent designs (Landy et al., 2018) |
| Data collection | Mechanical Turk, citizen science, PSA (300+ labs) |
| Data analysis | Same dataset → multiple analyst teams (Silberzahn et al., 2018: 29 teams, radical effect-size dispersion) |
| Pre-publication replication | Pipeline Project (Schweinsberg et al., 2016): 6/10 replicated |
| Writing | Crowd writing with coordination team |
| Peer review | Open/crowd review; 677-day avg. submission-to-publication in current system |
| Replication of published findings | Many Labs, OSC 2015, CREP |
| Deciding future directions | Prediction markets (Dreber et al., 2015; Camerer et al., 2016, 2018): aggregated predictions accurately anticipate replication results |

## Silberzahn et al. (2018) — Crowdsourcing Data Analysis

29 analyst teams, same dataset ("Do dark-skin-toned soccer players get more red cards?"), 29 distinct specifications → effect-size estimates ranging from slightly negative to large positive; 69% significant, 31% nonsignificant. Illustrates: research conclusions are contingent on defensible analytic choices.

## Wisdom of Crowds — and Its Limits

Crowds of independent agents balance individual biases in aggregate (Galton, 1907). But "wisdom of crowds" is spoiled by peer influence (Lorenz et al., 2011): more communication → more conformity risk. Low-communication crowd designs preserve analytic independence; high-communication designs enable learning but risk herding.

## Structural Reforms Proposed

1. **Grant funding:** distribute more evenly (returns higher outside elite institutions); link renewal to open science practices (preregistration, data sharing, replications)
2. **Author contribution statements:** CRediT taxonomy — conceptualization, data curation, writing, visualization; replace authorship heuristics (first/last author position)
3. **Selection/promotion criteria:** reward open science contributions and replication; examine project workflows (OSF)
4. **Pedagogy:** integrate crowd projects into methods classes (CREP, Pipeline Project); gives students middle authorship on real papers
5. **Publication criteria:** require independent replication for top outlets; shift emphasis from novelty to rigorous verification
6. **Infrastructure:** StudySwap, Psychological Science Accelerator (300+ labs), OSF, Registered Reports (now at scores of journals)
