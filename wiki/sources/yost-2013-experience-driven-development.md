# Training and Experience-Driven Development (Yost, 2013)

**Source:** Yost, P. (2013). Training and experience-driven development. In *The ASTD Handbook* (p. 57). ASTD Press.

## Core Argument

Starts from assumption that **90% of leader development occurs outside the classroom** → forces a fundamental reframing of training's purpose.

**Outside-in model**: training is not the point — it is a *catalyst* to drive leadership development outside the classroom. Formal training becomes an *accelerator* for on-the-job development, not the primary mechanism.

Contrasts with typical model where training is center-of-action and "transfer" is a post-training concern. The word *transfer* itself signals the problem — it implies training is the locus of learning.

## Three Critical Functions of Classroom Training

1. **Highlight key OJD experiences** — what is the short list of experiences most critical to a leader's development?
2. **Develop meta-skills** — feedback-seeking, reflection — that promote growth *in* the experiences
3. **Activate manager role** — remind managers they are the primary developers of their direct reports

## The Development Continuum (Figure 57.1 / 57.2)

Yost describes a spectrum from formal training to full OJD:

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Formal Training** | Reflection on key experiences; identification of future experiences to pursue; managers build dev plans for direct reports; senior leaders share their own experience lessons |
| **Transfer of Training** | Pre/post design with explicit catalysts: pre-training boss conversations, client/employee meetings brought into workshop; post-training peer coaching groups + 6-month follow-up class |
| **Action Learning** | Real business problems as the vehicle; development happens along the way; senior-sponsored short-term projects |
| **Mentoring / CoPs** | No classroom needed; learning through relationships; development embedded in daily work via mentor guidance |
| **Special Assignments / Job Rotations** | Stretch assignments in undeveloped areas; pre-planned sequences = formal rotation programs; classroom becomes prep/debrief catalyst only |
| **Job Assignments** | The extreme: trial-by-fire, edge-of-comfort-zone experiences where learning is required to survive (McCall et al. 1988 "Lessons of Experience") |

## Key Insight: Inverting the Transfer Problem

Standard model: design training → add pre/post conditions to support transfer.

Yost's model: start with experience → ask how training can accelerate the development that is *already* happening.

> "Even the word 'transfer' implies that the center of the action is the training program rather than the reality that the training program is one event among many significant events in a leader's development."

## Practical Design Implications

**Pre-training catalysts:**
- Leaders meet with boss to discuss career next steps
- Leaders meet with clients/employees before workshop; bring that intel in

**Post-training catalysts:**
- Follow-up class 6 months later (share lessons learned)
- Peer coaching groups formed in class → continue independently

**In-class design:**
- Senior leaders share their own experience lessons
- Workshop format: leaders work on *real world problems*, coach each other
- Build dev plans for 2 direct reports during class (practice OJD activation)
