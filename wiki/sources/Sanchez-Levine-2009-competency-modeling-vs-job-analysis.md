# Sanchez & Levine (2009) — TJA vs CM

## Thesis

**Competency Modeling (CM) and Traditional Job Analysis (TJA) supplement, not displace, each other** — both belong in the HR toolbox. The either/or debate is misframed. CM fills a void TJA leaves: infusing **strategic concerns** into day-to-day employee behavior. TJA and CM pursue fundamentally different goals; advocates on both sides overlook this.

CM has replaced TJA in many orgs for many HR applications, yet no professional consensus on how they differ; no agreement on definition of "competency" or CM methodology. Critics call CM "watered-down job analysis" (Barrett & Callahan 1997; Pearlman 1997) or say strategic JA does everything CM does (Sackett & Laczo 2003). Authors reject both.

## Six Dimensions (Table 1)

See [[TJA vs CM (Six Dimensions)]].

| Dimension | Traditional Job Analysis | Competency Modeling |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| **Purpose** | Describe behavior | Influence behavior |
| **View of the job** | External object to be described | A role to be enacted |
| **Focus** | Job | Organization |
| **Time orientation** | Past | Future |
| **Performance level** | Typical | Maximum |
| **Measurement approach** | Latent trait | Clinical judgment |

### 1. Purpose — describe vs influence
TJA measures/understands work assignments (applied measurement domain; informs staffing, training, compensation). CM raises **situation strength** — loudly signals "behavioral themes" (critical success factors / strategic performance drivers) to create a strong org climate (Bowen & Ostroff 2004). CM's closest theory referents: org culture & social control. Small number of core competencies, unambiguously worded in business language, cutting across jobs/ranks/locations. TJA is burdened with long lists of tasks and psychologically-worded KSAOs — deep but hard to communicate.

### 2. View of the job — object vs role
TJA treats job as objectively verifiable external entity (nomothetic, across-incumbent "average"). But the job is a **social construction / role** (Katz & Kahn 1978) — interpreted and enacted differently by each incumbent. Performance is a by-product of role interpretation. CM's idiographic approach targets interpretation, signaling themes aligned to strategy.

### 3. Focus — job vs organization
TJA: only the job, its formal responsibilities/equipment. CM: certain behavioral themes should touch performance across **all** jobs (employer brand, employee value proposition). Examples: FedEx "discretionary effort," Disney cleanliness/detail, GE "inclusiveness," Microsoft "success factors." Competencies form a common cross-job language that simplifies succession/career development. O*NET tries a universal occupational language but uses psychological jargon ("deductive reasoning") harder for end-users.

### 4. Time orientation — past vs future
TJA descriptive, rooted in past (job as done to date; source = incumbents, bottom-up flow). CM prescriptive, future-oriented (job as it *should* be enacted; source = strategic planners, top-down flow). TJA = operational capabilities for survival; CM = dynamic/evolutionary capabilities for growth/change (VA's Personal Mastery, Creative Thinking).

### 5. Performance level — typical vs maximal
TJA describes typical performance of average incumbent (transactional psychological contract; minimum for base salary; task performance — Borman & Motowidlo 1992). CM induces maximal performance (relational contract; contextual/prosocial performance; on-brand behavior). CM guides those who've met basic KSAO requirements to next level.

### 6. Measurement approach — latent trait vs clinical judgment
The debate's root: mistaken "latent trait reading" of competencies. Critics (Pearlman 1997) say competencies are fuzzy multi-domain concoctions failing construct validity (no clean convergent/discriminant nomological net). Authors' counter: **CM's aim is NOT construct-valid latent traits** — competencies are **fuzzy composites**, best treated as **global/clinical judgments** combining many constructs into one score (like assessment center / structured interview overalls). So internal-consistency reliability is not a useful CM metric; interrater agreement has a role but not as an accuracy index.

## Cross-Fertilization / Recommendations

TJA and CM enrich each other. TJA's shortcoming: ignores strategic variables. **Strategic job analysis** (Schneider & Konz 1989: present vs future ratings) concerns strategy *formulation*; CM concerns strategy *execution* — so SJA cannot replace CM (contra Sackett & Laczo).

- **Deriving competencies**: first SME panel (top strategists + operations-savvy) translates strategy → competencies. Werbel & DeMarie (2005) criteria: durable human capital, competitive-advantage-capturing, tacit/hard-to-imitate, valuable in knowledge economy. Strategy formulation should **precede** CM.
- **Evaluating CM**: designing staffing solely around strategic competencies is risky — person-org fit has null-to-small validity for performance/turnover (Arthur et al. 2006). Asking SMEs whether a strategic competency "applies" to a job is misguided ("acrobatics on an icy floor") — strategic competencies apply to all jobs by definition. Content validation is **not** the best CM evaluation; evaluate CM by its **consequences** — how much it shaped behavior along strategic lines (controlled experiments/quasi-experiments).
- **Job-relatedness & the law**: the practice of rating competency-job relevance was fueled by desire to comply with [[EEOC-1978-uniform-guidelines-employee-selection|Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection (1978)]] job-relatedness provisions. To comply, org may need to **supplement CM with TJA** (unless work isn't organized around fixed job titles). TJA's KSAOs serve as "enablers" (Ruggeberg 2007) for scientific selection.
- **Behavioral incidents**: critical incident technique + frame-of-reference training to translate competencies → job-level behavioral indicators. Disagreement stimulates panel discussion (like assessment-center assessors).

## Key Quote

> "Whereas traditional job analysis focuses on describing and measuring the requirements of work, CM creates a conduit to influence day-to-day employee performance along strategic lines." (p. 61 / conclusion)
