# Soderstrom & Bjork (2015) — Learning Versus Performance

Nav: [[sources/_index]] | [[Learning vs Performance Distinction]] | [[Desirable Difficulties]]

## Citation

Soderstrom, N. C., & Bjork, R. A. (2015). Learning versus performance: An integrative review. *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 10*(2), 176–199. https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691615569000

## Core Argument

Learning and performance are **dissociable**. The same manipulation can improve performance while harming learning, or impair performance while boosting learning. Instructors and learners who use short-term performance as a proxy for learning systematically choose inferior learning strategies.

> [!key-insight] The Central Paradox
> Conditions that produce the most errors during acquisition are often the very conditions that produce the most learning. The conditions that make training feel easiest are often those that produce the least durable, least transferable learning.

## Key Definitions

**Learning** = relatively permanent changes in behavior or knowledge supporting long-term retention and transfer.

**Performance** = temporary fluctuations in behavior or knowledge observable during or immediately after acquisition.

## Foundational Studies (Learning Without Performance)

- **Latent learning** (Blodgett 1929; Tolman & Honzik 1930) — rats learned a maze during free exploration when no reinforcement was present; revealed by later incentive. Learning occurred when performance was stagnant.
- **Overlearning** (Krueger 1929) — continued practice past mastery increased long-term retention; the overlearning group recalled more at 28 days.
- **Fatigue** (Adams & Reynolds 1954; Stelmach 1969) — fatigue suppressed performance but not learning; post-rest retention caught up.

## Distribution of Practice

**Massed practice** → rapid acquisition performance but faster forgetting.
**Distributed (spaced) practice** → slower acquisition but better long-term retention and transfer.

Motor: Baddeley & Longman (1978) — distributed > massed for postal workers' keystroke retention. Learners in the distributed group *reported feeling less satisfied* even though they learned more.

Motor: Shea & Morgan (1979) — blocked > random during acquisition, but random > blocked on 10-min and 10-day retention tests (**contextual interference effect**).

Verbal: Spacing effect is "one of the most robust and reliable effects in all of memory research." 93.33% of students incorrectly endorse massed study (McCabe, 2011).

See [[Spacing Effect]] for full detail.

## Variability of Practice

Variable conditions during acquisition → more performance errors but better long-term retention and transfer.

Motor: Kerr & Booth (1978) — children who tossed beanbags from varied distances (2 ft, 4 ft) outperformed a group who only practiced from 3 ft when *tested at 3 ft*.

Verbal: Smith et al. (1978) — studying in different rooms → 21% better recall in a neutral test location.

Mechanism: variable practice broadens the range of memory cues associated with the target skill/knowledge, facilitating later retrieval in varied contexts.

## Retrieval Practice

**Testing effect**: retrieval practice is itself a potent learning event — not merely an assessment.

Motor: Physical guidance reduces errors during training but impairs long-term retention vs. unguided practice. Learners who practice without guidance must reload the motor program themselves.

Verbal: SSST vs. STTT (Hogan & Kintsch 1971) — repeated studying beat repeated testing after 5 min; reversed at 2 days. Roediger & Karpicke (2006) — restudied group recalled 80% at 5 min; tested group recalled ~70%. After 1 week: restudied ~40%, tested ~60%.

**Generation effect**: generating items from memory > reading items for retention, even when generation fails.

**Failed generation enhances learning** (Kornell et al. 2009) — attempting to retrieve (even incorrectly) activates semantic networks that facilitate later encoding.

See [[Testing Effect]] for full detail.

## Metacognition

Learners systematically mistake short-term performance for long-term learning.

**Judgment of Learning (JOL)**: prospective predictions of future recall. JOLs are heavily influenced by encoding fluency and retrieval fluency — both misleading cues.

**Stability bias** (Kornell & Bjork 2009): tendency to believe current accessibility will remain stable across time; insensitivity to retention interval.

**Illusions of competence**: learners predict massed study will produce better 1-week recall, even immediately after experiencing the opposite in an experiment (Kornell & Bjork 2008).

Survey data (Karpicke et al. 2009): 84% of students rank rereading as a primary strategy; only 11% use retrieval practice.

See [[Judgment of Learning]] for full detail.

## Theoretical Perspectives

1. **New Theory of Disuse** (Bjork & Bjork 1992) — [[New Theory of Disuse]]: storage strength vs. retrieval strength. High retrieval strength → less gain in storage strength. Forgetting can foster learning.

2. **Schema Theory** (Schmidt 1975) — variable practice sensitizes learners to the general motor program underlying a skill.

3. **Reloading Hypothesis** (Lee & Magill 1983) — spacing forces reloading of motor commands; this effort is the potent learning event.

4. **Desirable Difficulties** (Bjork 1994) — [[Desirable Difficulties]]: manipulations that appear difficult during acquisition (spacing, variability, testing) enhance long-term retention and transfer. Key caveat: difficulties become *undesirable* when the learner cannot overcome them.

## Implications

- Long-term retention tests, not just acquisition performance, are required to evaluate learning interventions.
- Trainers should incorporate spacing, variability, and retrieval practice even though these *feel* less effective.
- Metacognitive sophistication — knowing what effective learning looks like — must be explicitly taught.
- The learning–performance distinction applies equally to motor and verbal learning, and across the lifespan.

- [[On-the-Job Development]] ↔ Desirable Difficulties: stretch assignments that feel hard may be optimal for leadership learning; OJD researchers should measure long-term skill development, not just immediate performance ratings
- [[Learning Agility]] ↔ Learning vs. Performance: learning agility = stable trait capacity to extract lessons from experience; individuals with high agility may tolerate performance dips during desirable-difficulty conditions
- [[Knowing-Doing Gap]] ↔ Metacognitive illusions: both describe failure to act on known best practices; here the mechanism is fluency-based misattribution rather than motivational
- [[Replication Crisis]] ↔ Illusions of Competence: researchers, like learners, may trust immediate "performance" signals (p < .05) over long-term cumulative evidence; also connects to [[Aesthetic Standards in Science]]
- [[Organizational Learning]] ↔ Spacing Effect: organizations that train in long, massed sessions may be optimizing performance during training, not long-term learning
- [[Knowledge Transfer]] ↔ Testing Effect: retrieval practice as a transfer mechanism; testing as tacit knowledge externalization
