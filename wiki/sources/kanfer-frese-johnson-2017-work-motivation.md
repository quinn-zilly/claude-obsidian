# Kanfer, Frese & Johnson (2017) — Work Motivation: A Century of Progress

Nav: [[index]] | [[wiki/concepts/Work Motivation Meta-Framework (Kanfer)|Meta-Framework]]

**Journal of Applied Psychology**, Vol. 102, No. 3. 18 pages. Centennial review of work motivation in JAP. Organizes 100 years of theory and research using a tripartite meta-framework; identifies trends and 10 future directions.

## Core Argument

Work motivation is best understood as a **dynamic, goal-directed, resource allocation process** that unfolds over time, experience, and place. Goals are the nexus connecting the "why" (needs, motives) with the "how" (purposive action). Three cross-cutting trends across the century: (1) primacy of goals, (2) importance of goal striving processes, (3) motivation as active resource allocation rather than passive response.

## Meta-Framework: Content × Context × Process

### Content-Based Theories (What motivates?)

**Universal Motives:**
- *Need fulfillment* (Maslow 1943): five hierarchical needs; little empirical support for prepotency; lingering popularity despite weak evidence
- *Intrinsic motivation / SDT* (Deci & Ryan 1985): autonomy, competence, relatedness; extrinsic rewards undermine IM when contingent (Deci et al. 1999 meta-analysis; but Eisenberger et al. 1999 found no such effect in applied settings); IM stronger for quality than quantity performance; IM + prosocial motivation synergistic (Grant 2008a)
- *Justice motives* (Adams 1965; Thibaut & Walker 1975): equity theory → procedural/interactional justice; fairness predicts attitudes, behavior, health; instrumental, relational, and moral reasons for caring about fairness

**Trait-Based Motives:**
- *Achievement/power/affiliation* (McClelland 1961): achievement motive → challenging goals + higher performance; motive research waned in 1970s
- *Personality traits (Big Five)*: conscientiousness + neuroticism = most reliable job performance predictors (Barrick & Mount 1991); traits → performance via motivational mediators (goals, SE)
- *Core self-evaluations* (Judge, Locke & Durham 1997): self-esteem + generalized SE + emotional stability + locus of control → effort, persistence, outcomes
- *Approach/avoidance motivation* (Kanfer & Heggestad 1997; Elliot & Thrash 2002): approach = BAS, extraversion; avoidance = BIS, neuroticism; clearer motivation links than Big Five
- *Goal orientation* (Dweck 1986): LGO (incremental beliefs, progress-based SE) → performance; PGO-prove = weak; PGO-avoid = detrimental (Payne et al. 2007 meta-analysis)
- *Regulatory focus* (Higgins 1997): promotion = gains-maximizing; prevention = losses-minimizing; prevention predicts safety; promotion predicts productivity + well-being (Lanaj et al. 2012 meta-analysis)

### Context-Based Theories (Where does motivation operate?)

**From extrinsic rewards to internal motivation:** Scientific management (Taylor 1911) → Herzberg's two-factor theory (hygienes vs. motivators) → job characteristics model

**Job Characteristics Model** (Hackman & Oldham 1975, 1976): 5 features (skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, feedback) → 3 psychological states (meaningfulness, responsibility, knowledge of results) → internal work motivation. Task meaningfulness = strongest mediator (Humphrey et al. 2007 meta-analysis). Extended to include relational/social features.

**Group/Team level:** Social loafing, social context effects; cross-level team variables (cohesion, climate, shared identity) → individual motivation states; team research growing but integration with individual motivation sparse

### Process-Based Theories (How does motivation operate?)

**Goal Choice subsystem:**
- *Expectancy/VIE theory* (Vroom 1964): valence × instrumentality × expectancy → motivational force; supported 1950s–70s but methodological issues (within-person theory tested between-person)
- *Theory of planned behavior* (Ajzen 1991): extends VIE with subjective norms; meta-analysis largely supportive but BCQ issues

**Goal Striving subsystem:**
- *Self-regulation* (Bandura 1977; Carver & Scheier 1981; Kanfer 1977): three interrelated activities = self-monitoring, self-evaluation, self-reactions; self-efficacy moderates behavioral intentions → action
- *Action regulation theory* (Frese & Zapf 1994): plans as bridge between goal choice and striving; mental simulation of actions; planning aids performance via deeper thinking, Plan B/C development, and stability in uncertain environments

**Integrative approaches:**
- *Goal setting theory* (Locke & Latham 1990): leading theory of work motivation; specific + difficult + feedback + commitment = highest performance; direction, effort, persistence, strategy mechanisms; task complexity moderates (d = .48 complex vs .67 simple; Wood et al. 1987); learning goals > performance goals on complex tasks
- *Resource allocation theory* (Kanfer & Ackerman 1989; Naylor et al. 1980): motivation = allocation of limited attentional resources across goals in response to demands, affect, incentives, feedback; beneficial effects only when sufficient resources for self-regulation
- *Resource depletion / ego depletion* (Baumeister et al. 1998): finite self-regulatory resource → sustained self-control depletes → task/citizenship behaviors suffer, deviant behaviors rise; recovery via positive events, autonomy, respites, mastery, sleep; but basic tenets challenged (Carter et al. 2015 meta-analysis)

**Rubicon Model** (Heckhausen 1991): vivid metaphor for deliberation (goal choice) → implementation (goal striving) boundary; goal planning = bridge across Rubicon

## Key Themes and Debates

**Mechanistic vs. nonmechanistic theories:** Hull's S-R model vs. Tolman's cognitive maps; at core: can motivation account for agency? Bandura's self-efficacy theory = nonmechanistic; cybernetic control theory = mechanistic (discrepancy-reducing); debate on high SE → higher vs. lower subsequent effort reconciled by Schmidt & DeShon (2010): SE + ambiguity interaction (high ambiguity → SE negatively related to effort; low ambiguity → positively)

**Multiple goals:** Real-world individuals distribute effort across multiple goals; resource allocation across goals underspecified; Schmidt & DeShon 2007 on incentive × goal progress effects on allocation

## Ten Future Research Directions

1. Better theory–methodology matching (within-person longitudinal designs)
2. Affect and motivation integration (cognition given primacy; affect as supporting role insufficient)
3. Implicit vs. explicit motivation systems (implicit motives explain unique variance; new measurement tools)
4. Temporal dynamics (cyclical motivation, temporal footprint, occupational future time perspective)
5. Motivational resources (what resources are; depletion boundary conditions; alternative explanations)
6. The self in motivation (autobiographical self, possible selves, personal/relational/collective self-construals)
7. Proactive engagement (vigor + dedication + absorption; job crafting; intersection with well-being)
8. Motivation across the life span (age-related motive shifts; motivation *to* work vs. *during* work)
9. Contextual influences (socio-cultural norms; team dynamics; community embeddedness; globalization)
10. Neuroscience + technology integration (embodied cognition; human–system integration; high-stakes work)

## Key Data Points

- Cerasoli, Nicklin & Ford (2014): IM → quality performance (not quantity); stronger when extrinsic rewards only indirectly tied to performance
- Payne et al. (2007): LGO enhances performance; PGO-prove weak; PGO-avoid detrimental (meta-analysis)
- Humphrey et al. (2007): task meaningfulness = strongest job characteristics mediator
- Wood, Mento & Locke (1987): goal effects d = .48 (complex) vs. .67 (simple)
- Lanaj, Chang & Johnson (2012): regulatory focus and work outcomes meta-analysis
