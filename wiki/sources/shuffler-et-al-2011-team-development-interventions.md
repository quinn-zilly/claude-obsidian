# Shuffler et al. 2011 — Team Development Interventions

Nav: [[index]] | [[sources/_index]]

## Core Argument

Team development interventions are **not one size fits all**. Two dominant types — team building and team training — target different needs, use different mechanisms, and produce different outcome profiles. Science-based design is essential; absent it, interventions range from ineffective to harmful. Core claim: *there's a science for that* — and it must be applied correctly.

## Background

Teams investigated in workplace since 1920s; modern global economy ↑ demand for high-performing teams. Effective team = obtains specified level of performance via **taskwork** (doing the actual task) + **teamwork** (team-level attitudes, behaviors, cognitions).

Three teamwork factor categories (Salas et al. 2008):
- **Attitudes**: mutual trust, cohesion, collective efficacy
- **Behaviors**: backup behavior, team leadership, information exchange
- **Cognitions**: shared mental models, transactive memory, metacognition

Science base includes: team KSA frameworks (Cannon-Bowers et al. 1995), team selection, team task analysis, team leader development, performance measurement systems, diagnostic feedback.

## Two Intervention Types

### Team Building
Designed to improve interpersonal relations and social interactions; also addresses goal achievement and task accomplishment. For *existing* teams with *current problems*.

Four approaches (Buller & Bell 1986):
1. **Goal setting** — individual and team-level goals; strongest effect
2. **Interpersonal relationships** — trust development, conflict resolution
3. **Role clarification** — communication about role specification; second strongest
4. **Problem solving** — identifying solutions to task-related problems

**Key caveat**: requires team members to be willing and able to diagnose their own problems; outside facilitation needed but member buy-in essential.

### Team Training
Theoretically based instructional processes targeting team KSAs; used both *prior to* and *during* performance. Prepares teams before they break down.

Four strategies (Table 4):
| Strategy | Purpose | Targets |
|---|---|---|
| Cross-training | Learn teammates' duties | Shared knowledge, mutual monitoring, backup |
| Team self-correction training | Diagnose own breakdowns internally | Mutual monitoring, communication, leadership |
| Team-coordination training | Improve shared mental models | Backup, monitoring, shared understanding |
| Crew Resource Management (CRM) | Apply well-tested tools (sims, role play) | Communication, briefing, decision making, adaptability, situation awareness |

**Key caveat**: resource-intensive; requires needs analysis to identify KSAs → learning objectives → design strategy. Cannot shortcut the design process.

## Empirical Evidence (Table 5)

Meta-analysis results (ρ values):

| Outcome | Team Training ρ | Team Building ρ |
|---|---|---|
| Cognitive | **0.42** | 0.13 |
| Affective | 0.35 | **0.44** |
| Process | 0.44 | 0.44 |
| Performance | **0.39** | 0.26 |
| All combined | 0.34 | 0.31 |

Sources: Klein et al. (2009) for team building (20 studies); Salas, DiazGranados et al. (2008) for team training.

Key finding: **team training > team building overall**, especially cognitive and performance outcomes. **Team building ≈ team training for process and affective outcomes**. Complementary, not competing.

Real-world example: surgical team training across 74 facilities → **lower mortality rates** (Neily et al. 2010).

## Three Key Takeaways

1. **Distinct strategies for distinct needs** — match intervention to team problem; don't default to one type
2. **Team building** = fix current problems (affect, trust, role clarity); **Team training** = build capability before problems emerge (cognition, performance)
3. **Science-based design is non-negotiable** — team building is frequently misused/trivialized; only effective when designed systematically

## When to Use Each (Table 6)

| Use team building when… | Use team training when… |
|---|---|
| Addressing current breakdowns | Building/refreshing KSAs before performance |
| Need ↑ trust/cohesion | Need ↑ team performance via teamwork |
| Need ↑ communication/coordination | Need ↑ cognitive outcomes (shared knowledge) |
| Need to reduce conflict | Need ↑ team decision making/coordination |
| Need to clarify roles | Need continuous self-correction capability |
| Task interdependence is **low** | Task interdependence is **high** |

## Future Directions

- Virtual/geographically dispersed teams
- Multiteam systems with complex interdependent goals (DeChurch et al. 2011)
- Culturally diverse teams (Connaughton & Shuffler 2007)
- Interactions between multiple simultaneous interventions (building + training combined)
