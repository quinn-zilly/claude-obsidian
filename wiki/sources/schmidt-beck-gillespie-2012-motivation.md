# Motivation — Schmidt, Beck & Gillespie (2012)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Control Theory (Self-Regulation)]] | [[Multiple Goal Self-Regulation]]

Handbook chapter reviewing work motivation research ca. 2002–2012. Organized around goal-based frameworks as integrating structure. Emphasizes contemporary directions: goal striving/self-regulation over goal setting, within-person dynamics, multiple-goal allocation, automaticity/nonconscious processes.

Core thesis: **"Work motivation is the allocation of finite resources to goals over time."**

## Goals and Goal Processes

### Goal Setting (brief)
- GST (Locke & Latham 1990, 2002): specific difficult goals → performance via attention, effort, persistence, strategy
- Moderators: goal commitment, feedback, task complexity (simple tasks benefit most)
- Learning goals superior on complex tasks (Seijts et al. 2004; Kozlowski & Bell 2006)

### Goal Striving / Control Theory
See [[Control Theory (Self-Regulation)]] for full treatment. Key: discrepancy reduction loop; rate of progress (velocity) independently predicts affect + satisfaction beyond magnitude (Chang et al. 2010); external disturbances influence goal progress independent of actor.

### Goal Revision
- Downward revision when discrepancy persists; upward revision after success
- Attributions moderate: internal attribution → stronger upward/downward revision
- Self-efficacy → higher subsequent goals (mediates affect → goal setting)
- Deadline proximity shifts: early = favor most discrepant; late = favor closest to completion

### Goal Hierarchies
Means–ends structure: superordinate goals set standards for subordinates; activate related means; inhibit competing alternatives. Incentives function as superordinate goals (Schmidt & DeShon 2007: sensitivity to rewarded task discrepancies > unrewarded).

### Goal Content
- Intrinsic vs. extrinsic goal circumplex (Grouzet et al. 2005): intrinsic = psychological needs; extrinsic = social reward + money
- SDT: intrinsic goals satisfy competence, autonomy, relatedness; extrinsic do not
- PFP effective for simple/boring tasks; potentially detrimental on complex/interesting tasks (Gagné & Forest 2008)

### Goal Framing
- Approach vs. avoidance; gain vs. loss; promotion vs. prevention
- Prevention: slower, fewer commission errors, higher commitment even at low expectancy, better goal shielding
- Promotion: faster, fewer omission errors, creativity, OCBs
- Implicit theories: incremental (malleable) → resilience; entity (fixed) → withdrawal after failure

### Nonconscious Goals
- Priming activates goals below awareness (cleaner scent → cleanliness behavior)
- Supraliminal primes (achievement words) → performance gains above conscious goals, persist 1 day
- Goal activation → spreading activation to means; inhibition of competing goals
- Goal deactivation after accomplishment; also when probability of success → 0

## Expectancies and Self-Efficacy

**Expectancy theory (Vroom 1964):** utility = E × I × V; within-person tests more compelling than between-person
**Self-efficacy** (Bandura): beliefs in capabilities to mobilize resources; r=0.38 with performance (Stajkovic & Luthans 1998)

### SE Debate
Vancouver et al. (2001, 2002): within-person SE → **negative** performance effect; high SE → perceive fewer resources needed → underallocate. Replication consistent in lab + field.

Schmidt & DeShon moderators:
- **Ambiguity** (2010): negative SE effect when performance feedback ambiguous (SE biases self-appraisal → underallocation)
- **Challenge level** (2009): positive SE when situation is challenging (persistence); negative SE when easy (coast)
- **Goal difficulty** (Beck & Schmidt 2011): positive SE with difficult goal; negative SE with easy goal

Vancouver et al. (2008): high SE → less time per trial → adaptive resource conservation for future trials.

Interpretation: SE effects generally adaptive (efficient allocation), not pathological.

### Related Constructs
- General SE (Chen et al. 2001): broader, mediated by task-specific SE
- Core Self-Evaluations (Judge et al. 2005): global worth/competence; relates to job + life satisfaction via goal self-concordance
- External/means efficacy (Eden 2001): beliefs about tool usefulness → performance
- Collective efficacy → team performance r positive; but early high CE → less constructive conflict → lower performance (Goncalo et al. 2010)

## Affect

**Definition:** umbrella: states (moods, emotions) + traits (PA/NA). Emotion = directed, discrete; mood = diffuse, longer.

### Affect → Behavior
- PA/NA → job performance (Kaplan et al. 2009 meta-analysis); mediated by SE + persistence
- Positive affect → higher goals, more time on goals, higher performance (Seo & Ilies 2009); mediates via SE + expectancy (Erez & Isen 2002)
- PA + positive velocity → creativity; negative affect signals danger → narrow attention
- Risk decisions: positive affect → less loss aversion, more exploration (Friedman & Förster 2010)

### Goal Progress → Affect
- Success → positive affect; failure/slow progress → negative affect
- Velocity (rate of discrepancy reduction) independently predicts satisfaction beyond discrepancy magnitude (Chang et al. 2010)
- Affect → subsequent goal revision: negative feedback → negative affect → lower goals; moderated by learning orientation

### Emotional Labor
See [[Emotional Labor]] for full treatment.
- Surface acting (response-focused): masks emotion; → exhaustion, depersonalization, reduced attention
- Deep acting (antecedent-focused): changes internal state; → sense of accomplishment, but slower to deploy
- Autonomy buffers exhaustion from surface acting
- Display rules as goals: commitment moderates display rule → surface/deep acting relationships (Gosserand & Diefendorff 2005)

## Individual Differences

### SDT / Basic Psychological Needs
Competence, relatedness, autonomy → intrinsic motivation; SDT-need satisfaction mediates P-E fit → commitment + performance (Greguras & Diefendorff 2009)

### Motivated Action Theory (DeShon & Gillespie 2005)
Three self-goals: agency, esteem, affiliation. Achievement goals (learning, performance) set to facilitate self-goals. Optimal functioning = low self-goal discrepancy.

### Core Self-Evaluations
CSE = self-esteem + GSE + locus of control + emotional stability. → job performance; → job/life satisfaction via goal self-concordance (Judge et al. 2005). Favorable environment moderates CSE → performance (Kacmar et al. 2009).

### Five Factor Model
Conscientiousness = most consistent predictor across occupations. Moderators: goal-focused leadership (↑ effect), performance goal after negative feedback (↑ tension, ↓ performance for high-C individuals). Within-person variability: majority of Big Five variance is within-person (extraversion 78%, C 75%).

### BIS/BAS
BAS = appetitive motivation (reward sensitivity); BIS = aversive motivation (punishment sensitivity). Relevant to deviant work behavior and mood → goal revision relationships (Richard & Diefendorff 2011).

### Regulatory Focus
See [[Regulatory Focus Theory]]. Promotion → speed + productivity + OCBs; prevention → accuracy + safety compliance + CWBs (negative). Safety climate → prevention focus (Wallace & Chen 2006).

### Action-State Orientation
Action orientation: flexibly disengage, initiate, persist. State orientation: rumination reduces resources for goal striving. Moderates affect → next-day effort (Wanberg et al. 2010).

### Goal Orientation
See [[Goal Orientation]]. Dispositional stability (moderate test-retest); yet majority of variance is within-person. Mastery → adapts better to new technology long-term; PGO → maintains short-term but never returns to baseline (Ahearne et al. 2010).

## Temporal Dynamics

**Temporal Motivation Theory (Steel & König 2006):** integrates expectancy-value + hyperbolic discounting. Utility = (E × V) / (1 + delay). Near-term rewards dominate; procrastination = hyperbolic discounting.

**Deadlines:** proximal → urgency + commitment. Time pressure → narrowed attention → can impair creativity (Amabile et al. 2002), though absence of pressure also hurts.

**Procrastination** (Steel 2007 meta-analysis): + impulsiveness, boredom, distractibility; − conscientiousness, need for achievement. Mediated by discounting; reduced by "unpacking" tasks (Kruger & Evans 2004).

**Planning fallacy:** underestimated completion times; more severe for open/distributed tasks; unpacking subtasks reduces bias.

## Multiple Goal Self-Regulation

See [[Multiple Goal Self-Regulation]] for full treatment. Key: Schmidt & DeShon (2007) lab paradigm: more time to most discrepant goal early; shift near deadline toward closest-to-completion. Incentive → more sensitivity to rewarded goal discrepancy. Loss framing → stronger pull than gain framing. Feedback frequency → more time to better-feedback task (Northcraft et al. 2011).

Vancouver, Weinhardt & Schmidt (2010) computational model: dynamic expectancy (= discrepancy / pace) × dynamic value (= discrepancy magnitude × consequence) → task selection. Matches Schmidt & DeShon patterns.

### Applied Multiple-Goal Contexts
- Speed vs. accuracy: promotion → speed; prevention → accuracy (Förster et al. 2003)
- Safety vs. efficiency: current research is between-person; within-person moment-to-moment trade-offs needed
- Development vs. performance: mastery goal → short-term loss, long-term gain; PGO → short-term maintenance, no long-term gain
- Ethical decision making: difficult goals → narrow attention + short-term focus → unethical shortcuts (Ordóñez et al. 2009); sleep deprivation → regulatory depletion → unethical behavior
- Work–life conflict: personal goals often higher in hierarchy than work goals; personal = "oughts" → shield work goals

## Future Directions

1. Automaticity/nonconscious: durability of primes in work settings; competing prime reconciliation; individual differences in priming
2. Affect: beyond PA/NA to discrete emotions, sensory affects, bodily homeostatic affects; group-level affect regulation
3. Time: perceptions of available vs. required time; affect ↔ time perception reciprocity
4. Theory integration via computational modeling (necessitates mathematical precision; tests whether theories generate observed patterns)
