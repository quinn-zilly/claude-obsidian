# Dik & Duffy (2009) — Calling and Vocation at Work

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]] | [[Work as a Calling MOC]]

Seminal conceptual paper (one of the two "founding" articles of the modern calling literature, alongside Wrzesniewski et al. 1997) that establishes **calling** and **vocation** as constructs within counseling and vocational psychology. Its goal is to supply detailed, multidimensional, measurable definitions to end the field's definitional vagueness and enable reliable measurement and career-counseling application.

## The two definitions

**Calling:** "a transcendent summons, experienced as originating beyond the self, to approach a particular life role in a manner oriented toward demonstrating or deriving a sense of purpose or meaningfulness and that holds other-oriented values and goals as primary sources of motivation."

**[[Vocation (Dik & Duffy)|Vocation]]:** same definition minus the transcendent-summons clause — an approach to a life role oriented toward purpose/meaning with other-oriented values as primary motivation, but where the impetus is **not** perceived as external to the self.

The distinction: both link work to purpose and other-oriented ends, but **only calling involves a perceived external summons**. Each construct has three dimensions — (1) transcendent summons (calling only), (2) purpose/meaningfulness in the life role, (3) prosocial / other-oriented orientation. The summons source is deliberately left open (God, society's needs, fate/serendipity), and callings can occur in **any life role**, not just work.

## Key claims and framing

Persons are treated as **active agents** (Bandura 2006) but influenced by biological, environmental, and spiritual factors. Calling/vocation is **not a one-time discovery** but an ongoing process of evaluating the purpose and prosocial contribution of one's activities — which makes it compatible with **[[Job Crafting|job crafting]]** (Wrzesniewski & Dutton 2001): even those in low-prestige or "dirty" jobs, or who did not obtain an ideal career, can reframe work into a calling. The authors treat these as ways one **approaches** work, not properties of the job content.

The **[[Presence vs Search for Calling|"presence of" vs "search for"]]** distinction is introduced as critical (later operationalized in the Brief Calling Scale and the Calling and Vocation Questionnaire). The paper argues calling is distinct from work centrality, work commitment, job involvement, personal engagement (Kahn 1990), flow, intrinsic motivation, the protean career, and workplace spirituality — sharing variance but not identical.

## Practice implications

Proposes calling/vocation as a framework for career counseling: assess clients' beliefs about active vs passive career decision-making; help clients articulate a larger framework of meaning; introduce **"social fit"** (fit between abilities and a set of social needs, from Plato's *Republic*) as a companion to traditional person–environment **"personal fit."** Ties the agenda to vocational psychology's social-justice push (Blustein).

## Why it matters for this vault

The most-adopted single definition in the calling literature (used by 25 of the 84 articles in [[Thompson-Bunderson-2019-Work-as-a-Calling|Thompson & Bunderson 2019]]) and, per that review, one of the few definitions to explicitly straddle **both** [[Inner and Outer Requiredness|inner and outer requiredness]] — the transcendent summons (outer) plus purpose/meaning (inner). Anchors the calling/vocation distinction and the presence/search distinction for the vault's meaning-of-work cluster.
