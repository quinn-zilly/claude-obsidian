# Wageman, Hackman & Lehman (2005) — Team Diagnostic Survey

**Source:** *The Journal of Applied Behavioral Science*, 41(4), 373–398.

## Core Argument

Team measurement faces "seemingly contradictory imperatives": research needs psychometrically sound, construct-specific measures; practice needs measures of *manipulable* variables that generalize across team types so norms can be built. Existing tools fail one or the other — consultant instruments have face validity but weak theory; scholar instruments are context-bound and non-comparable. The **Team Diagnostic Survey (TDS)** is designed to serve both, grounded explicitly in [[Enabling Conditions for Team Effectiveness|Hackman's model of team effectiveness]]. See [[Team Diagnostic Survey]].

## Conceptual Basis

The model is **unconventional**: it posits no direct causal input→output links. Teams are **semi-autonomous social systems** that evolve idiosyncratically; five conditions, when present, *raise the likelihood* (but do not guarantee) of effectiveness.

### Team effectiveness — three dimensions
See [[Team Effectiveness Criteria]].
1. **Productive output** meets/exceeds standards of the team's *clients* (not members).
2. **Social processes** enhance members' capability to work together in future.
3. **Group experience** contributes to individual members' learning and well-being.

TDS measures criteria 2 and 3 directly; criterion 1 is assessed *indirectly* via three **process criteria** (a self-report instrument can't judge client acceptability): level of effort, task-appropriateness of performance strategy, and knowledge/skill brought to bear (Hackman & Morris 1975).

### Five enabling conditions
See [[Enabling Conditions for Team Effectiveness]].
1. **Real team** — bounded, interdependent, stable membership.
2. **Compelling direction** — challenging, clear, consequential; ends specified, means left to team.
3. **Enabling structure** — well-designed task (motivating potential), right composition, clear core norms.
4. **Supportive organizational context** — reward, information, and education systems (+ material resources).
5. **Available, expert coaching** — task-focused; helps effort, strategy, knowledge/skill; useless if design badly flawed.

## The Instrument

10 sections; ~15–20 min; mostly 5-point *highly inaccurate → highly accurate*, reverse-scored items included. Team-level composites average across **items and members** (each member = an additional estimate of the team's true score). Group-level reliability via **Spearman-Brown** applied to individual-level alphas (illustration: individual α = .50 → team α = .80 for a 4-person team). Size and diversity scored as **algebraic difference** scores (0 = optimum: neither too small/large, neither too homogeneous/heterogeneous). A separate item set classifies team **authority** (self-governing → manager-led).

## Psychometrics

Validation across a diversity of organizations. Development ran two full revisions of a preliminary instrument.
- Draft 1: 181 teams / 63 orgs (N = 1,272 respondents).
- Revised: 140 teams (76 management, 64 analytic; N = 1,202).
- Abstract's headline sample: **2,474 members of 321 teams**.

All team-level reliabilities satisfactory. **Cautions**: subcomponents *size* and *diversity* (team composition) and *whole task* and *autonomy/judgment* (task design) have low reliability; discriminant validity unsatisfactory for *diversity* and *whole task*. Higher-order constructs (real team, direction, structure, context) deliberately have **no** internal-consistency computed — their elements legitimately vary independently, which is diagnostically meaningful, not a reliability flaw.
