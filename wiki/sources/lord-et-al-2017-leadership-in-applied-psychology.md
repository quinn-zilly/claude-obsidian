# Lord et al. 2017 — Leadership in Applied Psychology: Three Waves of Theory and Research

Centennial review in the *Journal of Applied Psychology* tracing 100 years of leadership research through three major waves of conceptual, empirical, and methodological development. Selective and interpretive — emphasizes 17 seminal JAP articles (Table 1) that represent turning points in the field.

## Core Argument

Leadership research evolved from early trait-focused work (WWII era) through three sustained waves driven by theoretical, methodological, and contextual shifts. Despite apparent discontinuities, many themes (leader–follower interdependence, situational context, cognitive processes) recur across all waves.

## The Three Waves

### Pre-Wave: Trait Paradigm (pre-1948)
Early leadership research in JAP was dominated by WWI-era interest in intelligence and individual differences (Terman 1916; Army Alpha project). Key finding from this era: intelligence moderately predicts leadership but non-intellectual traits matter too (Bingham & Davis 1924). Backlash emerged by the 1940s — Stogdill (1948) argued that situational factors primarily determine who is seen as a leader.

Early books also shaped the field: Bogardus (1934) introduced the key insight that all individuals have both leadership and followership traits; Barnard (1938) emphasized cooperative action and vision.

### First Wave (1948–1961): Behavioral Style and Follower Attitudes
Catalyzed by Ohio State University research group. Key shift: from *who leaders are* to *what leaders do*.

- **Stogdill & Shartle (1948)**: Launched Ohio State program; focus on leadership behavior, not personality
- **Bass (1949)**: Leaderless group discussion technique — forerunner of assessment centers
- **Fleishman (1953a)**: Factor analysis of Supervisory Behavior Description Questionnaire → **Initiating Structure** (clarifying roles, rules) and **Consideration** (friendly, supportive) — dominated research until mid-1980s

Also: Bales (1950) task vs. socioemotional functions; Hollander (1954, 1957) peer nominations; Cleven & Fiedler (1956) → foreshadowed contingency model.

Assessment center methodology emerged from this wave (AT&T Management Progress Study).

### Second Wave (1969–1989): Cognitive, Contingency, and Gender
Psychology's cognitive turn fundamentally challenged behavioral ratings.

**Social-cognitive challenge:**
- **Eden & Leviatan (1975)**: Introduced *Implicit Leadership Theories* (ILTs) — factor structure of leader behavior ratings reflects rater schemas, not necessarily actual behavior
- **Schein (1973)**: "Think manager, think male" — gendered leadership stereotype, launched gender line
- **Lord et al. (1984)**: Leader categorization theory — leadership perceptions as prototype matching; dynamic, context-sensitive prototypes

**Contingency theories:**
- Fiedler (1964): LPC model — task structure × leader–member relations × position power moderate LPC–performance relationship; widely cited but inconsistent replication
- Kerr & Jermier (1978): Leadership substitutes/neutralizers; meta-analytic support weak (Podsakoff et al. 1996)
- **Lord, De Vader, & Alliger (1986)** [Table 1]: First meta-analysis on leadership in JAP; helped revitalize trait perspective by attributing prior inconsistency to sampling error

### Third Wave (1999–2007): Expansion and Pluralism
Broadened unit of analysis (individual → dyad → team → organization), methods (multilevel modeling, meta-analysis), and constructs.

**Meta-analysis impact:**
- Judge et al. (2002): Big Five → leadership; R=.53 emergence, R=.39 effectiveness
- Judge & Piccolo (2004): Definitive transformational/transactional meta-analysis — both relate similarly to performance; augmentation hypothesis partially supported
- Dirks & Ferrin (2002): Trust in leadership meta-analysis — trust → performance, OCB, satisfaction, commitment

**LMX:**
- Gerstner & Day (1997) [Table 1]: First LMX meta-analysis; leader–member LMX agreement only r=.29; "supervisor relationship as lens through which entire work experience is viewed"
- Settoon et al. (1996): POS predicts commitment; LMX predicts citizenship and in-role behavior
- Liden et al. (1993): LMX quality is predictable within 5 days of dyad formation

**Transformational/Charismatic:**
- Burns (1978) → Bass (1985): charisma, inspiration, vision
- Hater & Bass (1988) [Table 1]: First JAP article on transformational leadership
- Howell & Avolio (1993): Transformational → business unit performance
- Bass et al. (2003): Transformational → team potency/cohesion → team performance
- Bono & Judge (2004): Personality explains <5% variance in transformational leadership
- Criticism: van Knippenberg & Sitkin (2013) — conceptualization and measurement problems remain

**Team leadership:**
- Marks et al. (2000): Leader briefing quality → team communication → shared mental models → performance (stronger on novel tasks)
- Zaccaro et al. functional leadership perspective: leaders provide what teams need when needed
- Shared leadership gaining prominence (Wang et al. 2014 meta-analysis: shared leadership → team effectiveness)

**Gender:**
- Eagly & Johnson (1990) meta-analysis: Women more democratic/participative
- Eagly et al. (2003): Women more transformational, especially in building follower relationships
- Role congruity theory (Eagly & Karau 2002): Women face prejudice when leader role conflicts with gender role
- Koenig et al. (2011): Leadership masculinity has ebbed somewhat as interpersonal skill demands increased

## Emerging Directions (Future Research)
Authors identify directions emerging in 2017:
1. **Neuroscience and cognition** — neural bases of leadership decision-making
2. **Technology and virtual leadership** — most research is face-to-face; leadership at distance understudied
3. **Multigenerational workforces** — age-diverse organizations require adapted leadership models
4. **Collective and shared leadership** — locus shifting from individual toward team/organization/crowd
5. **Leadership development** — lagging behind practice; multilevel longitudinal designs needed
6. **Social network approaches** — charisma from network centrality, not vice versa (Balkundi et al. 2011)
7. **Complexity and emergence** — bottom-up emergence vs. hierarchically directed change
8. **Sociopolitical context** — immigration, climate change, demographic shifts will reshape leadership agendas
