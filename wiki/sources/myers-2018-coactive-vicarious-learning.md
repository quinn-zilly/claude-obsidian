# Myers (2018) — Coactive Vicarious Learning

Nav: [[index]] | [[Learning & Development MOC]] | [[Coactive Vicarious Learning (CVL)]]

> [!abstract] One-line
> A conceptual/theory-building *AMR* paper reframing **[[Enactive and Vicarious Learning|vicarious learning]]** from an intrapersonal, one-way process of observation-and-imitation into a **relational, coconstructed process** — **coactive vicarious learning (CVL)** — that occurs through discursive interaction between a learner and a model.

## The problem with the received view

Vicarious learning ("an observer learns from the behavior and consequences experienced by a model rather than from outcomes stemming from his or her own performance attempts"; Gioia & Manz, 1985) has been treated as **intrapersonal and one-way**: the learner attentively observes a model (or a symbolic artifact — a case, video, KM database) and independently retains, reproduces, and imitates (Bandura's social learning theory). Myers labels this **[[Independent Vicarious Learning (IVL)]]**.

IVL presupposes the learner can (a) observe what's relevant and (b) infer the model's underlying goals — i.e., that what is to be learned is **overtly observable or symbolically codifiable**. But modern **knowledge work** is distributed/virtual, laden with **tacit** knowledge, and full of "ambiguity, equivocality, and uncertainty" (Hofmann, Lei & Grant, 2009). Here individuals often *cannot deduce the "lesson"* of another's experience without discussing that person's thoughts and decision process. The one-way assumption breaks down.

## The theory: CVL

Integrating **experiential learning** (Kolb, 1984; Schön — learning as meaning-making by transforming experience) with **symbolic interactionism** (Mead, 1934; Blumer — meaning is cocreated and continually reinterpreted through interpersonal interaction), Myers defines:

> **Coactive vicarious learning (CVL)** — a discursive learning process where individuals (a model and learner) **intentionally share and jointly process a model's work experience(s) in interpersonal interactions to coconstruct an emergent, situated understanding** of the experience(s).

"Coactive" is borrowed from computer science (agents learning by sharing examples/responses). The key shift: the **model is a cocreator**, engaging the learner across the *full* experiential-learning cycle (mutual reflection, shared abstraction, collaborative "trying out" of interpretations) — not merely supplying an observable experience.

**Outcome definition.** Learning = changes in **response repertoires** (Sitkin et al., 1998) — the stock of available responses *and* the capability to recombine them — which need not manifest as performance to count as learning.

## Distinguishing CVL from prior perspectives (Table 1)

| | Learning process | Learning activity | Intentionality | Content |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **CVL** | joint, coconstructed meaning-making | interpersonal interactions | intentional (both) | situated (emerges from discourse) |
| Observational (IVL) | within-person adoption after exposure | observation & imitation | intentional *or* un | defined ex ante (behavior observed) |
| [[Knowledge Transfer]] | within-person, post-exposure | conduits for transferring knowledge | intentional | defined ex ante (existing knowledge) |
| [[Communities of Practice]] | within-person, post-exposure | fuller participation in community | unintentional | situated (from participation) |

CVL shares *some* assumptions with priors (intentionality with knowledge transfer; emergent content with communities of practice) but they are "necessary but insufficient" elements of CVL.

## The three interactional elements (Hernadi's hermeneutic triad)

A CVL interaction is built from three **multiplicative** discursive components (low levels of any one inhibit learning):

- **Experience** — the "raw material": exposure to the model's experience (shadowing, apprenticing, storytelling, in-person simulation like firefighters' "staff rides"). Unlike IVL, learners and models can interact *during/after* exposure; learners may add their own contrasting/complementing experience.
- **Analysis** — probing questions, comments, requests for clarification that let both parties evaluate, reinterpret, and compare their emerging understanding (a learner's question can make the model take the learner's perspective and revise their own story — second-order learning, akin to Giddens' double hermeneutic).
- **Support** — statements/behaviors conveying social-emotional assistance; builds trust, self-efficacy, and relational capacity, and *indirectly* enables fuller sharing of experience and analysis (embedded/friendship ties facilitate tacit-knowledge exchange).

## Antecedent contexts (Propositions 1–3)

- **P1 — Relational context**: higher (a) relationship quality, (b) positive affective tone, (c) familiarity/history of prior interactions → more CVL engagement.
- **P2 — Structural context**: (a) closer proximity, (b) more helping-/learning-focused role structures (e.g., formal mentoring, nurse preceptors), (c) more interdependent or less time-intensive task structures → more CVL.
- **P3 — Individual context**: (a) stronger [[Goal Orientation|learning goal orientation]] & [[Motivation to Learn]], (b) greater existing knowledge stocks (absorptive capacity), (c) more generalist ("T-shaped") knowledge configurations → more CVL.

## Developmental consequences (Propositions 4–6)

- **P4 — Individual knowledge**: CVL increases knowledge for *both* learner and model (mutual codevelopment; multiple perspectives reveal insights unobtainable alone).
- **P5 — Individual capacity for future learning**: increases (a) [[Transactive Memory Systems|transactive memory]] ("who knows what"), (b) perspective-taking, (c) self-efficacy beliefs.
- **P6 — Relational capacity** (dyad-specific): increases (a) [[Shared Mental Models]], (b) trust, (c) affective commitment.

These consequences **feed back** into the antecedent contexts (dashed loops), making CVL cyclical and self-reinforcing — growth in knowledge/capacity alters the individual and relational context of *future* interactions.
