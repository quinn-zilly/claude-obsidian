# Breaugh (2013) — Employee Recruitment

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> The first **Annual Review of Psychology** treatment of **[[Employee Recruitment|employee recruitment]]** — a selective narrative review (emphasis on post-2000 work) organized around recruitment **targeting, methods, message, recruiters, the site visit, the job offer, and timing**, plus recruiting **underrepresented groups**. Core theme: **there is still no general theory of recruitment**; the literature is fragmented, over-reliant on **college-student samples**, and has neglected **prehire** variables (attracting attention, applicant perceptions) in favor of posthire outcomes. A second running theme: basic **persuasion / attitude** research (Hovland; McGuire; **[[Elaboration Likelihood Model|Petty & Cacioppo's ELM]]**; mere exposure; selective exposure; confirmation bias) applies directly to recruitment and deserves more use.

## Definition & criteria

**External recruitment** = an employer's actions intended to (1) bring an opening to potential candidates' attention, (2) influence whether they apply, (3) affect whether they maintain interest until an offer, and (4) influence whether an offer is accepted (Breaugh 2008). Outcomes split into **prehire** (# applicants, applicant quality, offer-acceptance rate) and **posthire** (job performance, turnover). No integrative theory exists — only partial models of specific pieces.

## Applicable psychological theory

Three persuasion models frame recruitment as **attitude formation/change**: Hovland et al. (1953, source credibility), **McGuire (1968)** six-stage information-processing sequence (presentation → attention → comprehension → yielding → retention → behavior), and **[[Elaboration Likelihood Model|Petty & Cacioppo (1986) ELM]]** (central/deliberative vs peripheral/heuristic route). Applicants are usually assumed to use the **central route** given the stakes — but **peripheral processing** is likely early (forming a first impression). Related principles: **mere exposure** (image advertising works even without conscious attention), **direct experience** (internships shape initial attitudes), and — once an attitude forms — **selective exposure**, **confirmation bias**, and biased information processing make initial impressions sticky (→ target people with no/positive prior attitude).

## The applicant's perspective

Application depends on perceived **attractiveness** (job/org attributes, especially **[[Signaling Theory (Recruitment)|reputation as a signal]]** of positive attributes), plus **hiring expectancy** and **alternatives** (people downgrade jobs they're unlikely to get). Decision making is **noncompensatory then compensatory** (Highhouse & Hoffman 2001): threshold attributes must be met (~90% report a minimum requirement, Osborn 1990) before other attributes trade off. Applicants may lack **self-insight** about their abilities (Dunning) — complicating the assumption that accurate info yields good self-selection.

## Targeting

The **most important** decision — whom to recruit — drives method, message, and timing, yet is **understudied**. Former employees, similar-others, and non-relocators look beneficial but evidence is thin/confounded (Taylor & Schmidt 1983 confounded group with method). Similarity-attraction (Devendorf & Highhouse 2008) and relocation aversion (Becker et al. 2010) inform targeting.

## Methods, message, recruiters

- **[[Recruitment Methods|Methods]]** — two explanations for why method matters: the **realism hypothesis** (referrals → accurate expectations → informed self-selection) and the **individual-difference hypothesis** (different methods reach systematically different people). **[[Employee Referrals|Employee referrals]]** are consistently superior: referrals show better credentials, are more likely hired, and perform better (Fernandez & Weinberg 1997; Castilla 2005); **referrals by high performers** beat referrals by low performers (Yakubovich & Lup 2006). Collins and colleagues: early recruitment actions (sponsorship, advertising, WOM endorsements) and general **publicity** raise applicant quantity, quality, and attention.
- **[[Recruitment Message|Message]]** — amount, specificity, and **realism** of information (the **[[Realistic Job Preview|realistic job preview]]** lineage) shape prehire and posthire outcomes; two-sided/credible messages persuade better; web-site design, testimonials, awards, and P-O **fit feedback** (Dineen et al.) improve effectiveness and screen unqualified applicants.
- **Recruiters** — informative/personable/credible recruiters help; recruiter **demographics** matter little. (Consistent with [[uggerslev-et-al-2012-recruiting-through-stages|Uggerslev et al.]] on personableness.)

## Site visit, job offer, timing

- **Site visit** — understudied but high-impact: a long, rich, firsthand interaction; meeting prospective coworkers/high-status hosts and good site logistics raise attraction; ~30% of Rynes et al.'s (1991) interviewees rejected offers *after* a visit. A visit is also read as a **signal** of offer likelihood.
- **Job offer** — offer **flexibility** (start date) raises acceptance; recruits tend to accept the first offer exceeding threshold (uncertainty reduction, Becker et al. 2010).
- **Timing** — **delays hurt**: late-season recruiting yields fewer, lower-quality applicants (Turban & Cable 2003); slow responses lower attractiveness and drive attrition — effects **stronger for high-quality applicants**. Perceived (subjective) timeliness predicts attraction better than objective days elapsed (Carless & Hetherington 2011).

## Recruiting underrepresented groups

Diversity cues (minorities in brochures/sites, especially in supervisory roles) raise attractiveness and recall **for minorities** without hurting majority reactions; identity-conscious/preferential programs risk perceived unfairness and stigma; **gendered wording** in job ads (Gaucher et al. 2011) signals belonging and depresses women's interest in male-typed jobs (not via perceived skill). Reputation for diversity likely matters more than any single message.

## Summary points & future issues

Recruitment actions influence applicant interest, ability, diversity, performance, and retention; **targeting** and **attracting attention** are under-researched; current-employee methods work; message wording matters; over-reliance on students; basic persuasion research (selective exposure, confirmation bias) should inform design. Open problems: build a **general recruitment model**; test whether student findings generalize; test the realism/prescreening mechanisms of referrals; study **passive job seekers**.
