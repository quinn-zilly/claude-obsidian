# Zell & Lesick (2022) — Big Five and Performance: A Synthesis of 50+ Meta-Analyses

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> A **[[Second-Order Meta-Analysis|second-order meta-analysis]]** aggregating **54 meta-analyses (k = 2028, N = 554,778)** — the first direct estimate of the association of Big Five traits with **overall/aggregate performance** and the first direct comparison across performance *categories*. Headline: **Conscientiousness is the strongest and most robust correlate of performance (ρ = 0.19), at least 46% larger than the other four traits** (which are comparable: E .10, A .10, N −.12, O .13). But **associations fluctuate dramatically by domain** — conscientiousness matters more for academic (.28) than job (.20) performance, while extraversion (−.01 vs .14) and neuroticism (−.03 vs −.15) barely predict academic performance. Effects **replicate well across independent meta-analyses**.

## Method: second-order meta-analysis

Follows recommended second-order procedures (Cooper & Koenka 2012; Zell & Krizan 2014): exhaustive search of PsycInfo/Business Source Complete/ERIC → formal inclusion (must report meta-analytic zero-order effects for **all five** traits vs performance; k ≥ 3 or N ≥ 500 per trait) → 54 meta-analyses. Excluded team/group performance and correlates-of-performance (satisfaction, motivation, CWB, leadership style). Population effects estimated via **unweighted averages** of prior meta-analytic effects (shown to perform as well or better than sample-size weighting); sensitivity analyses weighted by N, by k, and inverse-weighted by sample overlap (mean overlap 36%). Effect-size benchmarks (Funder & Ozer 2019; Gignac & Szodorai 2016): .05 very small, .10 small, .20 medium, .30 large, .40 very large.

## Primary results (overall performance, all 54 meta-analyses)

| Trait | ρ (overall) | Size |
|---|---|---|
| Conscientiousness | **0.19** | medium; CI non-overlapping with others |
| Openness | 0.13 | small |
| Neuroticism | −0.12 | small |
| Extraversion | 0.10 | small |
| Agreeableness | 0.10 | small |

Each Big Five trait is a **meaningful correlate** of performance in the predicted direction, but conscientiousness stands apart. Effect variability is large: excluding conscientiousness, each trait's SD ≈ its own effect size (i.e., individual meta-analyses range from near-zero/opposite-signed to very large).

## Results by performance category

- **Job** (m = 32): C .20, E .14, A .11, N −.15, O .11 — all robust (replicates Barrick et al. 2001, who found C .24, E .15, N −.15, A .11, O .07 across 8 job-performance meta-analyses).
- **Academic** (m = 7): **strikingly different pattern** — C **.28 (large)**, O .14, A .07, but E −.01 and N −.03 (≈ zero). Conscientiousness helps students stay on task even when tasks are low-importance.
- **Other** (m = 4+, incl. cognitive/EI tests, creativity, programming, negotiation): O largest (.16), reflecting intellectual dimensions; E .08, A .09, C .12, N −.10.

**Cross-category comparison**: A and O replicate across all three categories; **E and N are significantly weaker for academic than for job performance**; C is large (academic) > medium (job) > small (other), each pairwise difference significant.

## Effects by correction strategy

Meta-analyses correcting for **both unreliability and range restriction** yielded larger effects (|ρ| .12–.22) than those correcting unreliability only (.09–.18) or uncorrected (.05–.13). So the overall estimates here are likely **slight underestimates** of true associations.

## Replicability

Where independent teams meta-analyzed the same personality × performance association in the same population, estimates were **highly consistent** (e.g., five university-GPA meta-analyses agreed closely, esp. C ≈ .23–.27). This is rare evidence on the *replicability of meta-analyses* themselves — the disparate personality literature converges.

## Interpretation

> [!key-insight] Core vs context-specific
> If personality effects were constant across domains, it would imply a **fundamental core** to performance overlapping with personality. Instead, effects **vary by domain** → personality–performance associations are partly **context-specific**, and validity in one domain (academic) should not be assumed in another (vocational).

Builds directly on **Barrick, Mount & Judge (2001)** (8-meta-analysis job synthesis), **Wilmot & Ones (2019)** (conscientiousness across 48 meta-analyses, ρ ≈ .17), and **Wilmot et al. (2019)** (extraversion across 45 meta-analyses, ρ ≈ .10); extends them to *all* five traits, *simultaneously*, across *all* performance domains.

## Links

- Quantifies the qualitative validity patterns reviewed in [[hough-johnson-2013-personality-in-work-settings|Hough & Johnson (2013)]] and [[hough-oswald-ock-2015-beyond-the-big-five|Hough, Oswald & Ock (2015)]].
- The broad-factor focus here is exactly what Hough et al. critique — see the [[#Contradiction / tension|tension note]] below.

> [!contradiction] Broad-factor synthesis vs the "beyond the Big Five" agenda
> Zell & Lesick treat the **broad Big Five factors** as the units of analysis and conclude they have "robust associations with performance." [[hough-oswald-ock-2015-beyond-the-big-five|Hough, Oswald & Ock (2015)]] argue that broad-factor analysis **obscures** the stronger, more interpretable relationships that live at the **facet** level, and that averaging across facets/measures (as second-order meta-analysis necessarily does) is precisely what buries them. The two are not strictly incompatible — Zell & Lesick answer "how well do broad traits predict *overall* performance?" while Hough et al. answer "how do we *maximize and understand* prediction?" — but they point in opposite methodological directions (aggregate vs disaggregate). See [[Facet-Level Personality Measurement]].
