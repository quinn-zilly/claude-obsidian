# Bell et al. (2017) — 100 Years of Training and Development Research

Source: `.raw/Bell et al. - 2017 - 100 years of training and development research What we know and where we should go..pdf`

## Summary

Centennial review of training/development research in *Journal of Applied Psychology* (JAP), 1918–2015. 458 articles coded. Uses citation analysis + expert synthesis to map evolution across four themes and propose future directions.

**Core argument**: T&D research evolved from atheoretical intervention descriptions → theory-driven, learner-centered, multilevel, context-sensitive science. Many advances; persistent gaps in needs analysis, informal learning, multilevel criteria, and technology design.

## Timeline of Research

| Era | Label | Key Shift |
|-----|-------|-----------|
| 1917–1959 | Early years | Describe/evaluate specific programs; military-driven; operator/motor skills |
| 1960s–1970s | Emergence of theory | Move from method comparison → theory; behavioral to cognitive; social learning theory enters |
| 1980s | Trainee is alive | Learner-centered; self-efficacy, transfer, motivation emerge; first meta-analyses |
| 1990s | Training in context | Work environment shapes learning; transfer climate; Kraiger et al. 1993 broadens criteria |
| 2000s–present | Beyond individual & classroom | Team/org levels; informal learning; technology-based; within-person change |

## Theme 1: Training Criteria

**Expanding understanding of learning**
- Behaviorist era: drill, practice, reinforcement → mastery
- Cognitive turn: learning hierarchies (Bloom 1956), conditions of learning (Gagne 1965)
- Kraiger, Ford & Salas (1993): multidimensional classification — cognitive (declarative knowledge, knowledge organization, cognitive strategies), skill-based (compilation, automaticity), affective (motivation, attitudes)

**Learning task evolution**: motor/operator tasks (1919–1972) → cognitive/knowledge tasks → complex decision-making under dynamic conditions

**Broadening criteria**
- 1950s–60s: job performance added (not just immediate learning)
- 1970s–80s: trainee attitudes as outcomes
- 2000s: multilevel — team and org performance (Van Iddekinge et al. 2009; Kim & Ployhart 2014)

**Key finding**: Training → individual learning → team performance → unit/org effectiveness chain now statistically supported via multilevel modeling.

## Theme 2: Trainee Characteristics

**Chronological evolution**
1. Pre-1960s: GMA, aptitude as primary predictors
2. 1960s–70s: personality, interests, attitudes enter; trainability testing distinct from job success
3. 1980s: self-efficacy (Gist et al. 1989), goal orientation (Kanfer & Ackerman 1989 info-processing model)
4. 1990s: motivation to participate; socialization; goal orientation × design interaction
5. 2000s: meta-analyses (Colquitt, LePine & Noe 2000); within-person dynamics; self-efficacy-learning relationship is *negative* in within-person designs (Vancouver & Kendall 2006)

**Key findings**
- GMA important but not sufficient; personality, motivation, self-efficacy all matter
- Trainability ≠ job success: different constructs
- Goal orientation interacts with design: learning orientation + mastery design → best outcomes; performance orientation effects depend on ability and outcome measured
- Self-efficacy→learning: *positive* between-persons, *negative* within-persons (high self-efficacy → fewer planning resources → worse performance)

## Theme 3: Training Design and Delivery

**Design evolution**: description of programs → method comparison → theory-driven → active learning → learner-centered

**Active learning**
- Passive → active: behavioral paradigm replaced by learner-centered
- Key interventions: guided exploration (Debowski et al. 2001), error management training (Gully et al. 2002; Keith & Frese 2005, 2008), mastery goal framing
- Arthur et al. (2003) meta-analysis: method effectiveness depends on skill type (cognitive vs. interpersonal) and criterion used

**Technology-based training**
- Evolution mirrors traditional methods: comparison studies first → design features second
- Clark (1994): media doesn't influence learning — design does
- Learner control: positive for satisfaction, but can harm learning if not scaffolded
- Future: blended learning, mobile, user-generated content largely unresearched

**Team training** (see [[Team Training]] for full detail)
- Task work vs. teamwork skill distinction enables generalizable interventions
- Cross-training → shared mental models (Marks et al. 2002)
- Leader briefings + after-event reviews effective as "mini-trainings"

## Theme 4: Training in Context

**Systems perspective** (Baldwin & Ford 1988): three components — instructional design, trainee characteristics, organizational conditions. Context affects all downstream outcomes.

**Work conditions that influence training**
- Supervisor/peer support → motivation to learn AND transfer
- Transfer climate (Tracey et al. 1995): supervisor + peer encouraging trained skills use → post-training behavior
- Opportunity to practice on job (Ford et al. 1992)
- Organizational culture and policies (training framing: "remedial" vs. "advanced" matters; Quiñones 1995)
- Prior work experiences → motivation and readiness

**Supervisor role**
- Taylor et al. (2005) meta-analysis: behavior change after training far greater when supervisor *also* trained
- Mentoring → perceived organizational support for development

**Developmental experiences**
- Challenge stressors → positive learning motivation; hindrance stressors → negative (LePine et al. 2004)
- Optimal challenge window: some challenge good; overload harmful (DeRue & Wellman 2009)

## Future Research Directions (per authors)

| Area | Gap |
|------|-----|
| Criteria | Needs analysis neglected; rapid needs assessment methods needed; within-person learning trajectories; multilevel designs |
| Trainee char. | Informal learning moderators; proactivity in tech-based training; knowledge sharing characteristics |
| Design | Blended learning; flipped classrooms; social media in training; active learning mechanisms (mental models, not just metacognition) |
| Context | Informal learning conceptualization; optimal formal/informal mix; more intensive transfer interventions |

## Key Conclusions Table (from Table 1)

**Training criteria**: Learning multidimensional; training influences individual/team/org criteria; within-person change provides diagnostic information.

**Trainee characteristics**: GMA important but personality, motivation, self-efficacy must be included; goal orientation × design interactions matter; trainability distinct from job success.

**Training design**: Method effectiveness depends on skill type and criterion; active self-regulated learning → complex skills and adaptive performance; technology = design, not media; team training tools (cross-training, briefings, AERs) effective.

**Training context**: Pre/post conditions shape effectiveness greatly; systems perspective essential; supervisor support, practice opportunity, and culture determine transfer.
