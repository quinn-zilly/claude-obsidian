# Shockley, Shen, DeNunzio, Arvan & Knudsen (2017) — Disentangling Gender and Work–Family Conflict

> [!abstract] One-line
> A *JAP* monograph meta-analysis (>350 samples, N ≈ 250,000) showing that, contrary to the pervasive lay belief, **men and women barely differ in reported work–family conflict** — and then meta-analytic path-testing of which theories explain the (tiny) gender relationship.

Addresses the "popular-press–science paradox": the media (Slaughter's *Why Women Still Can't Have It All*; Sandberg's *Lean In*) treat work–family juggling as a women's problem, yet the academic literature has never found clear support. The paper resolves this with the largest meta-analysis to date and by **pitting competing theories against each other in a single path model**.

---

## Setup

**[[Work–Family Conflict]]** is bidirectional: **WIF** (work-interference-with-family) and **FIW** (family-interference-with-work), per Greenhaus & Beutell (1985). The literature invokes many, often contradictory theories and rarely tests them against one another.

Gender coded so **positive = men higher**.

## Main effects (Research Question 1)

Overall gender differences in WFC are **statistically significant but negligible**, in the direction of women reporting slightly more conflict — small enough to be practically null. This replicates and tightens Byron's (2005) earlier small estimates (WIF r ≈ −.03; FIW r ≈ .06) on a far larger base. See **[[Gender Similarity in Work–Family Conflict]]**.

## Moderators (Research Question 2 + Hyps 1–2)

Four work/family-equating subgroups and two context moderators:

- **Parental status**: **mothers report more FIW than fathers** — the largest gender effect found (still modest), consistent with the rational view (children ↑ family demands, borne disproportionately by women).
- **Dual-earner couples**: wives report more FIW; husbands report more WIF (both small).
- **Same job**: women report slightly more WIF.
- **Type of conflict**: **men actually report more *time-based* WIF** than women.
- **Cultural gender egalitarianism** (GLOBE practices/values, WEF Gender Gap, UN GII): did **not** reliably moderate (Hyp 1 unsupported).
- **Publication date**: did **not** moderate (Hyp 2 unsupported) — despite substantial true heterogeneity.

## Theory-testing via meta-analytic path analysis (Hyps 3–5)

Four theories operationalized through mediators (work/family hours, work/family salience, work/family boundary strength):

| Theory | Mediator | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Rational View (Work–Family)]]** | Work & family **hours** | **Supported** (WIF & FIW) — men work more hours → more WIF; women more family hours → more FIW |
| **[[Sensitization Perspective (Gender Role View)]]** | Work & family **salience** | **Not supported** (WIF or FIW) |
| **[[Asymmetrical Domain Permeability Model|Asymmetrical permeability]]** | **Boundary strength** | **Partial** — supported for WIF; for FIW the significant path runs *opposite* to prediction |
| Male segmentation (rival boundary account) | Boundary strength | **Not supported** |

## Theory-building

For **WIF**, the modeled mediators leave a **significant negative residual** gender path → **competitive mediation**: something still pushes women toward WIF that the existing theories omit. Adding **job autonomy** (post-hoc, from Michel et al. 2011) did not close the gap. For **FIW**, two opposite-signed mediators (family hours ↑ FIW for women; women's *stronger work boundary* ↓ FIW) **cancel out**, sufficiently explaining the near-null overall relationship.

## Why it matters

If gender–WFC is small/null, the **stereotype that women are inherently higher-WFC is empirically unwarranted** — a stereotype that penalizes women's careers (assumed family-orientation; Hoobler et al. 2009) and stigmatizes men who use flexibility (Vandello et al. 2013). Evidence for **similarity over difference**.
