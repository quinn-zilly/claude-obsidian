# Cascio & Aguinis (2011) — Criteria: Concepts, Measurement, and Evaluation

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> The canonical textbook treatment of the **[[Criterion Problem|criterion problem]]**. A criterion is "an operational statement of the goals or desired outcomes of the program under study" — something important or desirable, used to **predict** (before a decision) or **evaluate** (after). Criteria are multidimensional and dynamic. Good operational criteria must be **relevant, reliable, sensitive, and practical**, and must minimize **[[Criterion Relevance, Deficiency & Contamination|deficiency and contamination]]**. Closes with the enduring **[[Composite vs Multiple Criteria|composite-vs-multiple-criteria]]** debate. Explicitly frames itself as a step toward the "comprehensive theory of the behavior of men and women at work" called for by [[viswesvaran-ones-2000-models-of-job-performance|Viswesvaran & Ones (2000)]].

## Definition

A **criterion** is an evaluative standard — something important or desirable — that can measure a person's performance, attitude, or motivation (Astin 1964; Blum & Naylor 1968). The only difference between a predictor and a criterion is **time**: an evaluative standard applied *before* a decision is a predictor; the same standard applied *after* is a criterion (Mullins & Ratliff 1979).

## Job performance as a criterion

- **Performance** = observable things people do that are relevant for organizational goals (Campbell et al. 1990). Distinguish **performance** (behavior) from **effectiveness** (outcomes/results of performance) (Aguinis 2009).
- **[[Ultimate Criterion|Ultimate criterion]]** (Thorndike 1949): the full, conceptual domain of everything that defines success on the job. Strictly conceptual — cannot be measured or observed — but anchors the relevance of any operational measure.

## Dimensionality of criteria (Ghiselli 1956)

Three kinds:

1. **Static** — at a single point in time, performance is multidimensional (Campbell 1990). Includes both facet-independence (high on one facet, low on another) and the **[[Typical vs Maximal Performance|typical–maximum]]** distinction. Two general facets: **[[Task Performance]]** and **[[Contextual Performance]]** (a.k.a. pro-social / OCB), which do not necessarily co-occur. Dark side = **workplace deviance / [[Counterproductive Work Behavior|counterproductive behaviors]]** — distinct from contextual performance, not just its opposite pole (Judge, LePine & Rich 2006).
2. **Dynamic / temporal** — see [[Dynamic Criteria]]. Ultimate criteria are impractical for day-to-day use, so **immediate → intermediate → summary** substitute criteria are used (all partial approximations; Thorndike 1949).
3. **Individual** — two people equally good at the same job may contribute differently, so different criterion dimensions may be needed to evaluate each (Kingsbury 1933).

## Evaluating criteria — three yardsticks

- **[[Criterion Relevance, Deficiency & Contamination|Relevance]]** — the criterion must be logically related to the performance domain (SIOP *Principles* 2003). Objective and subjective measures are **not interchangeable** (r ≈ .39; Bommer et al. 1995) — don't use one as a proxy for the other.
- **Sensitivity / discriminability** — must distinguish effective from ineffective employees. Note: criterion variance and criterion relevance are **not** necessarily associated (a low-variance measure like accident dollar-cost can still be highly relevant).
- **Practicality** — record-keeping must not unduly interfere with operations. "Keep criterion measurement practical!"

## Deficiency and contamination

- **[[Criterion Relevance, Deficiency & Contamination|Deficiency]]** — the operational measure fails to cover part of the criterion domain (e.g., rating a professor on teaching + service but omitting research).
- **[[Criterion Relevance, Deficiency & Contamination|Contamination]]** — operational criterion contains variance unrelated to the ultimate criterion. Two parts: **error** (random, correlates with nothing) and **bias** (systematic, can correlate with predictors). Maier (1988): cleaning contaminated performance-test data raised validities from .09/.17 to .49/.37. Three bias sources: **knowledge of predictor info** (keep predictor data away from raters — Bray & Grant 1966), **group membership**, and **rating bias** (Thorndike 1920).

## Criterion equivalence

Two criteria are equivalent only if they correlate ~perfectly after correcting for unreliability (rare in practice). Less-than-perfect correlation ⇒ not equivalent; must consider time, type, and level of measurement (e.g., training vs job performance; Ghiselli 1966; Hunter & Hunter 1984).

## Composite vs multiple criteria

> [!key-insight] The core debate
> **Composite** advocates (Brogden & Taylor 1950; Thorndike 1949; Toops; Nagle) want a single "value to the organization" index — indispensable for decisions; the **dollar criterion** treats the criterion as an *economic* construct. **Multiple-criteria** advocates (Cattell 1957; Dunnette 1963; Guion 1961; Schmidt & Kaplan 1971) argue that combining demonstrably different, uncorrelated dimensions is "psychologically nonsensical" and destroys understanding; the criterion should be a *behavioral/psychological* construct. Resolution depends on **purpose**: keep dimensions separate for psychological understanding; combine into an economic composite for managerial decisions. Both are legitimate for their own ends — the goal is to enhance *utility and understanding*. See [[Composite vs Multiple Criteria]].

Murphy & Shiarella (1997): **34% of the variance in a selection battery's validity** was explained by *how* task and contextual performance were weighted into the composite — compositing is a consequential strategic choice.

## Research design & criterion theory

Two guiding objectives for applied psychologists: (1) demonstrate **utility** of procedures, and (2) enhance **understanding** of the determinants of job success. Binning & Barrett (1989) supply a more complete inferential model than the simple predictor→composite-criterion picture.
