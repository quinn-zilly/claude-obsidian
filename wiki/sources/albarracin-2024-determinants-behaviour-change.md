# Albarracín et al. 2024 — Determinants of Behaviour

> [!key-insight] Central Finding
> Across all behavioural domains, interventions that **enable people to circumvent obstacles** (access, social support, habits) outperform interventions targeting salient but causally weak factors (knowledge, general attitudes, beliefs). Correlational evidence systematically overestimates intervention efficacy.

## Citation

Albarracín, D., Fayaz-Farkhad, B., & Granados Samayoa, J. A. (2024). Determinants of behaviour and their efficacy as targets of behavioural change interventions. *Nature Reviews Psychology*. https://doi.org/10.1038/s44159-024-00305-0

## Summary

A cross-domain meta-review synthesizing multidisciplinary meta-analyses of individual and social-structural determinants of behaviour. The paper classifies determinants, reports correlational and interventional effect sizes for each, and orders them by intervention efficacy. The key policy-facing result: the most commonly targeted determinants (knowledge, attitudes, beliefs) are among the least effective intervention targets.

## Framework

The paper distinguishes two broad classes of determinants:

**Individual determinants** (internal to the person): knowledge, general skills, general attitudes, beliefs, emotions, behavioural skills, behavioural attitudes, habits.

**Social-structural determinants** (external, social/environmental): legal and administrative sanctions, institutional trustworthiness, injunctive norms, monitors and reminders, descriptive norms, material incentives, social support, access.

## Individual Determinants — Effect Sizes

Ordered from least to most effective as intervention targets (mean odds ratio for intervention studies):

| Determinant | Correlation w/ behaviour | Intervention efficacy | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| Knowledge | Small (r=0.09–0.29) | **Negligible** (OR≈1.11) | Least effective |
| General skills | Small (r≈0.14) | **Negligible** (OR≈1.23) | |
| General attitudes | Medium (r=0.14–0.39) | **Negligible** (OR≈1.35) | Gap vs. correlation |
| Beliefs | Small–medium | **Negligible** (OR≈1.43) | Conspiracy beliefs β=0.09 |
| Emotions | Small–medium (r=0.12–0.24) | **Small** (OR≈1.60) | g=0.31 overall |
| Behavioural skills | Medium (r≈0.35–0.39) | **Small** (OR≈1.99) | More promising |
| Behavioural attitudes | Large (r=0.38–0.58) | **Small** (OR≈2.09) | Lab>>field |
| Habits | Large (r=0.33–0.50) | **Medium** (OR≈2.65) | Best individual target |

> [!gap] Correlation-Intervention Gap
> Correlational effect sizes consistently exceed intervention effect sizes for every individual determinant. Implies that correlational studies should not be used to select intervention targets. A key methodological warning for applied behavioural science.

## Key Findings by Domain

**Health behaviours**: Same ordering as all behaviours. Access, social support, descriptive norms, material incentives are most effective.

**Environmental behaviours**: Similar pattern. Descriptive norms = medium; material incentives = medium. Beliefs show slightly larger effect than for other domains (small, not negligible), meaning climate communication isn't entirely futile but is insufficient alone.

**Cross-domain consistency**: The hierarchy is robust. The paper argues policymakers should shift emphasis from knowledge/attitude campaigns to structural interventions.

## Theoretical Anchors

Three main theoretical frameworks informing the determinant taxonomy:
- [[Reasoned Action Approach]] (Fishbein & Ajzen): beliefs → attitudes → intentions → behaviour; knowledge, attitudes, behavioural skills
- [[Information-Motivation-Behavioural Skills Model]] (Fisher & Fisher): information, motivation, behavioural skills
- [[Social Cognitive Theory]] (Bandura): self-efficacy, modelling, environmental constraints

The paper extends these by giving equal weight to social-structural factors often treated as "external variables."

## Policy Implications

> [!key-insight] Policymakers' Naive Theory Problem
> Policymakers tend to believe knowledge → behaviour change, so they default to information/education campaigns. This review provides evidence-based pushback: knowledge campaigns waste resources. Better targets are access, social support, habits, and descriptive norms.

Specific critique of COVID-19 vaccination campaign in the USA: targeted vaccine confidence (general attitudes), but should have increased access + behavioural skills + social support + material incentives.

Climate change: beliefs have small but non-negligible effect, so communicating about climate change is not entirely misguided — but behavioural skills training and structural access interventions are needed as well.

## Limitations

1. Channel-level variation not examined (individual vs. school vs. community delivery).
2. Synthesizing meta-analyses may over-represent heavily studied areas.
3. Intervention content not examined — only target determinant; implementation matters too (readers directed to primary literature).
4. Possible mechanism overlap (e.g., a descriptive norm intervention may also affect attitudes).
