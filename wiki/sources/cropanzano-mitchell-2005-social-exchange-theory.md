# Cropanzano & Mitchell (2005) — Social Exchange Theory: An Interdisciplinary Review

> [!key-insight]
> The most-cited modern statement of [[Social Exchange Theory]] (SET) in management. Its core move is diagnostic: SET is influential but conceptually *underspecified*. The authors trace the trouble to three foundational ideas that were never cleanly articulated — **rules/norms of exchange, resources exchanged, and the relationships that emerge** — and argue most empirical tests rely on an incompletely specified set of ideas.

## Core Contribution

A conceptual review (not meta-analysis) of SET as used in organizational behavior. SET roots span anthropology (Malinowski, Mauss), social psychology (Homans, Gouldner, Thibaut & Kelley), and sociology (Blau). All variants agree on one essence: **social exchange is a series of interdependent, contingent interactions that generate obligations and can — under certain conditions — produce high-quality relationships.** The paper organizes SET's "explanatory power" into three pillars, each of which it argues has lacked clear definition.

## Pillar 1: Rules and Norms of Exchange

Rules are the "normative definition of the situation" (Emerson 1976) guiding exchange. Management research over-focuses on **reciprocity** and neglects the rest.

Gouldner (1960) distinguished three senses of reciprocity:

1. **Reciprocity as interdependent exchange** — contingent moves; one party acts, the other responds in kind. Distinct from independence (solo outcomes) and dependence (other's outcomes). Interdependence reduces risk and builds cooperation (Molm).
2. **Reciprocity as a folk belief** — the cultural expectation that "everything works out in the end" (cf. Lerner's just-world belief).
3. **Reciprocity as a moral norm** — a standard one *ought* to follow; possibly a human universal (see [[Norm of Reciprocity]]).

Individuals differ in **exchange ideology / orientation** — high-exchange-orientation people "keep score." Exchange ideology moderates the [[Perceived Organizational Support]]→outcome links (Eisenberger et al.). Positive vs. negative reciprocity orientations also differ (quid-pro-quo for good or ill).

**Beyond reciprocity:** Meeker (1971) lists six exchange rules — reciprocity, rationality, altruism, group gain, status consistency, competition. Management has ignored five of six. Also **negotiated rules** (explicit, quid-pro-quo, e.g., pay negotiation); Molm shows reciprocal exchange breeds more trust and commitment than negotiated exchange.

## Pillar 2: Resources of Exchange

[[Foa & Foa Resource Theory]]: six resources (love, status, information, money, goods, services) on two dimensions — **particularism** (does worth depend on the source?) and **concreteness** (tangibility). Organizational research collapses these into **economic vs. socioemotional outcomes**. Concrete/universal resources (money) get exchanged in short-term quid-pro-quo; particularistic/symbolic resources (love, status) in open-ended fashion. Tsui et al. (1997) employee–employer typology: quasi-spot, mutual investment, underinvestment, overinvestment.

## Pillar 3: Social Exchange Relationships

The most-researched pillar in management. Blau (1964): social exchange entails **unspecified obligations**, **diffuse future obligations**, gratitude and trust — unlike purely economic exchange. The social exchange relationship is usually modeled as a **mediator** between fair/advantageous treatment and effective work behavior.

The paper reviews the empirical "multifoci" models that operationalize exchange quality:

- **POS and LMX** — POS = quality of employee↔organization exchange; [[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)]] = employee↔supervisor exchange. Masterson et al. (2000) multifoci finding: POS best predicts OCB-O, commitment, turnover intentions; LMX best predicts OCB-S, satisfaction, performance ratings.
- **Support → commitment** ([[Organizational Commitment]] as repayment for support; Rhoades & Eisenberger meta-analytic POS–commitment r = .60/.67).
- **Team support, supervisory support, and trust** as additional relational mediators.

> [!key-insight] The causal-order ambiguity
> Do exchanges create relationships, or do relationships shape exchanges? The authors resolve it with a **ladder metaphor**: relationship development is iterative, and "the output from a past transaction can be the resource exchanged in a future transaction." Molm's lab work captures *early-stage* relationship formation; Eisenberger/Bishop field work captures *mature* relationships. Cause and effect depend on where in the sequence the researcher snips. So relational benefits (trust, commitment) are **both a result and a resource** — no necessary contradiction.

## The 2×2 Typology (Figure 1)

Separating *type of transaction* (social vs. economic) from *type of relationship* (social vs. economic) yields four cells: two matches (Cell 1 social-in-social, Cell 4 economic-in-economic) and two mismatches (Cell 2 economic transaction in social relationship — risk of betrayal; Cell 3 social transaction in economic relationship — e.g., emotional labor in service work).

## Why It Matters for the Vault

This is the conceptual anchor for the justice cluster. [[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al. (2013)]] explicitly cites this review as the definition of "contemporary social exchange theory" and operationalizes its three pillars (resources = justice, rules = reciprocity, relationships = trust/commitment/POS/LMX) in a meta-analytic test.
