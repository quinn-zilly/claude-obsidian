# Hong, Ford & Jong (2024): Employee Benefits Meta-Analysis

> [!abstract] One-line
> First meta-analysis to separate the three **benefit experiences** — availability, use, and subjective evaluation — and show that how employees *evaluate* a benefit predicts attitudes and well-being far more than whether they merely have or use it, with the relationship partly running through [[Perceived Organizational Support|POS]].

## The problem

Benefits are **~38.1% of US compensation costs** yet the literature was disjointed. Prior reviews blurred three distinct experiences of a benefit. The paper organizes benefits research under **[[Social Exchange Theory]]** (Cropanzano & Mitchell 2005): benefits are a tangible resource that initiates reciprocity and signals organizational care, driving commitment, retention, satisfaction, and well-being — partly via POS, partly directly by fulfilling material/socioemotional needs.

## The key distinction — three benefit experiences

See [[Benefit Experience Framework]]:

- **Availability** — the org offers it and the employee is aware, regardless of use. A *symbolic* signal of concern.
- **Use** — the employee actually takes advantage of it. An *exchange of goods/services*.
- **Subjective evaluation** — the employee's judgment of the benefit's value/quality and satisfaction with it. Captures the *amount and quality* of the resource, which social exchange says is what matters most.

## Method

- Meta-analysis combining literature search (APA PsycInfo, 14 management + 5 public-admin journals, SIOP conference 2010–2023, references of prior family-friendly-policy reviews) with **11 archival datasets** (NSCW, GSS multiple years, MIDUS).
- Search returned 21,141 items. Included only current-employee samples with bivariate effect sizes for availability/use/subjective evaluation.
- Analyzed in Mplus 8.1 + R (psychmeta, metafor). Meta-analytic path model combines own estimates with prior meta-analyses to test POS mediation. **Not preregistered.**
- Benefits categorized by the US National Compensation Survey scheme (financial, health-care, insurance, leave, nonproduction bonuses, quality-of-life, retirement, training, plus general packages).

## Headline findings (ρ = corrected correlation)

### Subjective evaluation ≫ availability ≫ use

- **Subjective evaluation** is the strongest predictor across most outcomes — e.g., evaluation→POS ρ≈**.40**, evaluation→affective commitment ρ≈**.33**. General-benefit evaluation→POS ρ≈.49.
- **Availability** matters but is much weaker — availability→POS ρ≈**.08**, availability→AOC ρ≈**.08**, availability→job satisfaction ρ≈.05, availability→turnover intentions ρ≈**−.07**.
- **Use** contributed **little** beyond availability and subjective evaluation. (Supports **H5**: evaluation > availability/use.)

### Partial POS mediation

POS **partially** mediates benefit-experience → commitment/withdrawal/satisfaction relationships (H7), but benefits also act on outcomes **independently** of POS by directly fulfilling needs and raising interdependence.

### Effects vary by benefit type

- **Training benefits** most strongly related to **job satisfaction** and commitment (training→AOC ρ≈.13).
- **Health-care benefits** most strongly related to **turnover intentions** (health→turnover intent ρ≈**−.22**).
- Leave and quality-of-life benefits show smaller, noisier relationships.

## Moderators explored

Age and national context (US vs. non-US) as moderators — motivated by the theory that older workers value health/retirement more, and that US benefits (health insurance, paid family leave) carry more weight because the US lacks universal provision.

## Practical takeaway

> [!key-insight] Measure evaluation, not just availability
> Many employers make benefits available or track use without ever measuring how employees *evaluate* them — yet evaluation is what predicts POS, commitment, satisfaction, and retention. A widely-available, heavily-used benefit that employees judge poorly (high-copay health plan, inconvenient childcare) can even backfire via negative reciprocity. Spend on benefits employees will value, and *ask* them.

## Why it matters here

Provides the meta-analytic anchor for [[Employee Benefits]] and introduces the [[Benefit Experience Framework]]. Extends the social-exchange cluster ([[Social Exchange Theory]], [[Perceived Organizational Support]], [[Organizational Commitment]]) into compensation. Companion to [[fulmer-li-2022-compensation-benefits-total-rewards|Fulmer & Li (2022)]] — Fulmer & Li flagged benefits research as thin and reaction-dominated; Hong et al. quantify exactly those employee reactions and rank the three experiences.
