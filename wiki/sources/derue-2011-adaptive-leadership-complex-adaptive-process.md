# Adaptive Leadership Theory: Leading and Following as a Complex Adaptive Process

Nav: [[index]] | [[Leadership MOC]]

Theoretical article in *Research in Organizational Behavior* developing [[Adaptive Leadership Theory]]: leadership as a socially complex, adaptive process. (Note: this is DeRue's complex-adaptive-systems theory — distinct from Heifetz's practitioner "adaptive leadership.") The paper foregrounds the *evolutionary value* of a fluid leading–following process over static labels of "leader" and "follower."

## Four Limiting Assumptions of Existing Leadership Theory

DeRue argues dominant theories are inconsistent with the standard definition of leadership (a mutual, reciprocal influence process toward a collective goal) because they embed four assumptions:

1. **Conflating leadership with hierarchical supervision** — 84% of 2003–2008 management leadership research took a supervisor-as-leader stance. But supervisors are often not seen as leaders, and non-supervisors often are.
2. **Portraying leadership as one-directional influence** — definitions specify mutuality, yet research foregrounds one actor steering others. This overlooks that leading and following are interdependent.
3. **Depicting leadership as a personal attribute** — "great man"/trait/behavioral views locate leadership *in the person*. DeRue (echoing Bateson's "stamp out nouns") urges dropping static leader/follower labels for the *acts* of leading and following.
4. **Treating the environment as exogenous** — contingency theories cast the environment as external variation leaders must adapt to; DeRue argues leading–following processes are **endogenous** and can *create* their own environmental conditions.

## Leading–Following as a Complex Adaptive Process

Five core propositions build a multilevel model:

- **P1**: The basic unit is the [[Leading-Following Double Interact]] — actor A leads, B follows, A leads again. Any single leadership act's effect is contingent on others' responses.
- **P2**: Double interacts drive [[Leadership Identity Construction|leader/follower identity construction]] (claiming and granting; DeRue & Ashford 2010), which becomes the basis for relationships and group structures.
- **P3**: Context is endogenous to the process.
- **P4**: The process is fluid — actors shift between leading and following, producing group-level structures that can change over time.
- **P5** (requisite variety, Ashby 1956): a group's adaptive capacity is a function of the **variability** in its leading–following pattern. More dynamic environments reward more fluid patterns.

### Magnitude and Dispersion → Four Patterns

Patterns are characterized mathematically by **magnitude** (density of reciprocated leading–following double interacts) and **dispersion**, where dispersion has two properties: **centralization** (Freeman 1979) and **centrality variance** over time.

| Pattern | Magnitude | Centralization | Centrality variance | Description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Centralized** | Low | High | Low | One actor consistently leads; others follow. |
| **Distributed** | Low | Low | High | Leadership rotates *sequentially* among members (division of labor over time). |
| **Shared** | High | Low | Low | Members lead and follow *concurrently* — shared leadership entails shared followership. |
| **Leadership void** | Low | Low | Low | Few reciprocal leading–following interactions — can reflect a lack of *following*, not just leading. |

> [!key-insight] Distributed ≠ shared
> DeRue distinguishes **distributed** (sequential rotation) from **shared** (concurrent) leadership — a distinction prior work (e.g. Pearce & Sims 2002) collapsed. He also critiques the shared-leadership literature for measuring only *magnitude*, ignoring centralization and centrality variance — so "shared" leadership in prior studies "might not have been shared at all."

## Theoretical Implications

Reconsiders whether leadership requires a collective goal; reframes individual differences (traits shape *who attempts* to lead/follow and others' *zones of acceptance*, not leadership per se); calls for modeling *change* in leading–following patterns and reconciling exchange-based development (LMX) with social-construction accounts; and raises methodological challenges of capturing double interacts and dispersion dynamically.
