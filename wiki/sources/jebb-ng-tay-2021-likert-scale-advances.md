# Jebb, Ng & Tay (2021) — Likert Scale Development Advances: 1995–2019

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]]

## Citation

Jebb, A. T., Ng, V., & Tay, L. (2021). A Review of Key Likert Scale Development Advances: 1995–2019. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 12, 637547. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.637547

**Affiliation:** Purdue University (Jebb, Tay); University of Houston (Ng)

## One-Line Summary

Selective review of 40 advances in Likert scale development from six major measurement journals (1995–2019), with in-depth treatment of five key topics: construct validity, construct definition, item readability, reliability alternatives (omega/IRT), and ant colony optimization for short forms.

## Why It Matters

Classic tutorials (Clark & Watson 1995; Hinkin 1998) are 25+ years old. This paper serves as a companion update — not a full tutorial, but a curated synthesis of what changed. Written for general psychologists, not methodologists.

## Scope & Method

- **Journals surveyed:** Applied Psychological Measurement, Psychological Assessment, Educational and Psychological Measurement, Psychological Methods, AMPPS, Organizational Research Methods
- **Period:** January 1995 – December 2019
- **PRISMA screening:** 40 papers included (7 excluded as off-topic, 23 too narrow, 11 superseded)
- **Scope exclusions:** Response biases, measurement invariance, implicit measures, forced-choice scales

## Five In-Depth Topics

### 1. Construct Validity — See [[Construct Validity]]
Two camps: (a) validity as property of **interpretations** of scores (Messick 1989; Kane 1992) — dominant view endorsed by Clark & Watson and the Standards; (b) validity as property of **tests** — Borsboom et al. (2004), reviving Kelley (1927). Both camps agree: validity is **unitary** — "content validity," "criterion validity," etc. are not separate types; they are all evidence bearing on the central construct validity inference. As of 2013, 122 subtypes had been named, most counterproductively.

### 2. Construct Definition — See [[Construct Definition]]
Three aspects: (a) *consider the construct* (essence, concretes, negation, comparison to similars); (b) *create a formal definition* (simple language, subconcepts, genus/differentia, brevity); (c) *consult alternatives* (dictionaries, cross-disciplinary literature, experts, peers). Also: specify the **latent continuum** — unipolar vs. bipolar, poles, degrees (intensity vs. frequency), operationalization implications for item wording and response format.

### 3. Readability Tests — See [[Readability Tests for Survey Items]]
Average US adult reads at ~8th grade; 20% below 5th grade. University samples hide this. Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level recommended (common, grade-level output, built into MS Word). Target ≤8.0 per item. Items must be tested *individually*, not as a block.

### 4. Alternative Reliability Estimates — See [[Coefficient Omega]] and [[Item Response Theory]]
- **Alpha's flaw:** assumes tau-equivalence (equal factor loadings). Rare for psychological scales. Underestimates true reliability when violated.
- **Omega (McDonald 1999):** uses congeneric factor model; at least as good as alpha when alpha's assumptions hold. McNeish (2018) is the key tutorial (R + Excel).
- **IRT information:** precision assessed at response-category, item, and test level across the latent trait range. Unlike alpha, not a single number — a function. Enables targeted item selection and short-form construction.

### 5. Ant Colony Optimization (ACO) — See [[Ant Colony Optimization (Scale Shortening)]]
Algorithm mimicking ant pathfinding: randomly samples item subsets, evaluates by multiple criteria (reliability, CFA fit, criterion correlations), increases selection probability of high-performing items via "pheromone" weights. Outperforms factor-loading maximization, genetic algorithms, and PURIFY in simulation. Used for proactive personality, supervisor support, situational characteristics scales. Limitation: does not guarantee content coverage — manual review required.

## Authors' Future Concerns
1. Sophistication ≠ accuracy — even ACO has limitations.
2. **Construct proliferation** is a serious problem; new constructs need evidence of both importance AND distinctiveness before scale development begins.
3. Incoherent constructs can have good psychometrics — theory must precede empirics.
