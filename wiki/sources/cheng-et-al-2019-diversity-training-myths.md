# Cheng et al. (2019) — Challenging Diversity Training Myths

**Source:** `.raw/input/Cheng et al. - 2019 - Challenging diversity training myths.pdf`
**Published in:** *Organizational Dynamics* (published online 2018)

## Summary

A myth-centered narrative review of the diversity training literature. The authors extracted 19 myths from public discourse and practitioner claims, then challenged each with empirical research. Myths are organized around three categories: effectiveness/evaluation, content, and design/delivery. The core argument is that the question should shift from "does diversity training work?" to "for whom, how, when, where, and to what end does it work?"

Key meta-analytic anchor throughout: **Bezrukova et al. (2016)** — 40+ years of diversity training research; overall effect size *g* = .38; largest effect on reactions (*g* = .61).

## The 19 Myths

### Effectiveness & Evaluation

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| Diversity training does not work | Works when designed/implemented appropriately; question is *conditions*, not binary |
| There is always backlash | Backlash is possible but reactions are typically positive; moderators (voluntary, % women) matter |
| Diversity training always does more good than harm | Potential negatives: backlash, reinforcing stereotypes, de-legitimizing discrimination claims via false sense of fairness |
| If people didn't like it, it didn't work | Satisfaction ≠ success; utility reactions predict learning better than affective reactions |
| ROI is unfavorable | When distal outcomes included (turnover, litigation, discrimination costs), ROI is likely favorable |

### Content

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| Diversity is just common sense | People have biases they are unaware of; self-enhancement motives create blind spots |
| Only about championing minority groups | Also drives business outcomes, learning outcomes, social justice outcomes |
| Only about "doing the right thing" | Also competitive advantage, reputation, legal protection (EEOC resolved $484M in 2017) |
| One-size-fits-all approach works | Needs analyses are essential; tailor to org |
| Diversity is just race and gender | Surface-level (visible) AND deep-level (attitudes, values, religion, SES, sexual orientation) |
| Training should make people colorblind | Colorblindness is counterproductive; multicultural approach (acknowledgement + appreciation) is better |
| Training blames white men | Broad framing prevents tokenism and majority resentment; diversity training benefits everyone including white men |

### Design & Delivery

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| Diversity training is like any other training | Content-outcome links are unique; design factors (mandatory vs. voluntary) have different effects than in other training types |
| One session is enough | Single sessions can be meaningful, but spaced, longer programs (avg ~24 hrs in meta-analysis) are more effective |
| Should always be mandatory | Mixed evidence; mandatory shows organizational buy-in; voluntary increases motivation; context-dependent |
| Anyone can lead it | Trainers need professional knowledge (group dynamics, evaluation, history) AND interpersonal skills; demographics of trainer matter |
| Leaders don't need to be involved | Leadership support is critical; three levels (strategic, tactical, operational); visible leader engagement drives training effectiveness |
| Marginalized groups don't need to go | Biases and discrimination exist within all groups; all benefit; including marginalized voices is necessary for some outcomes |
| Diversity training is the only initiative needed | Positive outcomes are amplified when paired with organizational support, policies, follow-up sessions, consequences/rewards, resources |

## Key Theoretical Points

- **Pre-training beliefs** (including myths) are barriers to training effectiveness — hence myth-centered approach addresses root causes
- **Nomological network of outcomes**: reactions → learning → behavior → results (Kirkpatrick). Organizations overuse reactions alone.
- **Utility reactions** (perceived job relevance) predict learning better than **affective reactions** (enjoyment)
- **Spaced practice** consistently outperforms massed practice for skill transfer
- **Multicultural > colorblind** framing for interracial interaction improvement
- **Framing matters**: broad diversity framing reduces backlash from both majority and minority participants
- **Ally confrontation**: non-target confrontation of prejudice is more effective than target confrontation (Czopp & Monteith, 2003)
