# Transitions
**Yost & Plunkett (2009)** — Ch. 19 in *Real Time Leadership Development*. Oxford: Wiley-Blackwell.

## Core Argument

Transitions are the **rule**, not the exception (Hall, BU). Stability is increasingly the anomaly. To thrive, leaders need two meta-competencies: **identity** (self-awareness, strengths, passion) and **adaptability** (learn, adapt, build relationships). Leaders who navigate transitions well are also the ones organizations rely on for external change adaptation.

## Typical Leadership Transitions

Two complementary frameworks:

**Dotlich, Noel & Walker (2004) — *Leadership Passages*:** Nine common transitions:
1. Joining a company
2. Moving into a leadership role
3. Accepting a stretch assignment
4. Assuming responsibility for a business
5. Dealing with significant failure you are responsible for
6. Coping with a bad boss and competitive peers
7. Losing your job / being passed over
8. Being part of an acquisition or merger
9. Living in a different culture

**Charan, Drotter & Noel (2001) — *The Leadership Pipeline*:** Six level transitions:
1. Managing yourself (individual contributor)
2. Managing others
3. Managing managers
4. Functional management
5. Business management
6. Group/Enterprise management

Each passage requires **new competencies and giving up old ones** — not gradual skill growth but a series of wrenching nonlinear steps. Classic trap: the functional expert promoted to business unit leader who continues seeing the world through one lens.

## Navigating Transitions Successfully

Five strategies:

1. **Identify what is required in the new role** — brief before you start; talk to boss, team, stakeholders; read analyst reports if changing companies; figure out what to give up

2. **Contract with your boss on what "success" means** — explicit agreement on goals and deliverables; too many leaders pursue the wrong priorities; bosses are often unclear themselves

3. **Get to know key stakeholders** — ask boss, peers, team to identify who matters; prioritize relationships over early wins (relationships enable the right wins); explicitly invite feedback on derailment signs

4. **Build a development plan** — don't miss learning opportunities in the first months; establish development goals with boss early

5. **Look for small wins** — Watkins (*The First 90 Days*): 90-day credibility window; break long-term projects into 30–90 day deliverables; early wins build momentum for later efforts

## Organizational Implications

**Coach new team members through transitions:**
- Establish clear success criteria from day one
- Basics matter (badge, office key, working email)
- Intro to team; walk through transition checklist; connect to key stakeholders
- Help identify early small wins
- Discuss development goals; provide early direct feedback on cultural misalignment

**Build a transition-friendly organization:**
- Rigorous, valid selection systems (structured interviews)
- Culture where managers are accountable for new employee success
- New employee orientation programs
- Performance goals required within first 30 days of any new assignment

## Transition Checklist

Four domains to complete in first 1–2 months:

**My Manager:** Discussed team assessment, success metrics, challenges/barriers, key stakeholders, resources needed, development goals + career aspirations

**My Direct Reports:** Met all to discuss team health, opportunities; met each individually on performance goals, deliverables, career aspirations

**My Network:** Identified and scheduled meetings with key stakeholders across levels and functions; set up ongoing meetings; explicitly asked for early-warning feedback

**My Performance:** Identified top 3 priorities; identified 3 small wins for 30–90 days; documented performance + development goals; started mapping long-term goals
