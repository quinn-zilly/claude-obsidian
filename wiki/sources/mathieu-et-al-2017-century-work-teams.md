# Mathieu, Hollenbeck, van Knippenberg & Ilgen (2017) — A Century of Work Teams in JAP

**Source:** *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 102(3), 452–467. Part of the JAP centennial series.

## Core Argument

Work groups are the vital link between individuals and organizations. This review chronicles a century of team research in JAP — a shift from a focus on **individuals within teams** (or individual-vs-team comparisons) toward the **team itself** and larger **systems of teams (MTS)**. Adopts Kozlowski & Ilgen's (2006) team definition (2+ interdependent members, shared goals, differentiated roles, embedded in an organizational system).

## The Historical Arc

- **Pre-JAP Teams Era (1917–1949):** JAP published *zero* team articles in its first 32 years; groups were the province of sociology and social psychology (Hawthorne work appeared elsewhere). Field dominated by individual differences.
- **Decline & handoff:** Group research waned by the 1960s (Steiner 1974: the group "seemed rather dead"); the torch passed to organizational scholars (Levine & Moreland 1990).
- **The 1990s surge:** Global competitiveness, complex/volatile environments, demise of bureaucratic simplified jobs, military team-decision-making funding (USS *Vincennes*/*Stark*), and the digital age drove team-based work. Article frequency shows a sharp curvilinear rise (R² = .81); JAP became a primary outlet.

### Three early heritages (McGrath)
1. **Lewin → Michigan** — group influence on individual attitudes/behavior; birth of lab experiments.
2. **Bales / Harvard** — groups as intact social systems; Interaction Process Analysis.
3. **McGrath school** — universal group properties → task-contingency; produced the **IPO model** (McGrath 1964; Hackman & Morris 1975).

## Three Unifying Substantive Themes

The review organizes team research around three domains (Figure 3 construct map) that are **dynamic and reciprocal**, not a linear pipeline.

1. **Team tasks and structure**
   - *Task* characterized by **scope** (number of component acts) × **complexity** (component complexity, coordinative complexity, dynamic complexity; Wood 1986).
   - Early individualist view treated interdependence as inefficiency to be *engineered out* (Briggs & Naylor 1965). As tasks grew in scope/complexity, "can individuals beat groups?" became moot.
   - *Structure* = how the team decomposes a task, via **skill differentiation** and **authority differentiation** (Hollenbeck, Beersma & Schouten 2012) — a horizontal/vertical microstructure analogous to an org chart.
2. **Member characteristics and team composition** — who is on the team and how their attributes combine.
3. **Team processes and emergent states** — dynamic interaction ([[Teamwork Processes (Marks et al. Taxonomy)]]) plus [[Emergent States (Teams)]].

## Outcomes

Advocates a **multilevel, multiple-constituencies** effectiveness framework. Two broad criteria classes: **tangible outputs** (productivity, efficiency, quality, retention, creativity) and **members' reactions** (attitudes, turnover intentions, well-being). Under-studied by-products: customer satisfaction, safety, conservation ("green"). There is — and should be — **no standard criterion set**; effectiveness is context-specific.

## Framework Evolution

The classic linear **IPO** model is criticized as a limiting factor stifling dynamic multilevel theory (Ilgen et al. 2005; Mathieu et al. 2008), giving way to **IMOI** (Input–Mediator–Output–Input) with feedback loops. See [[IPO–IMOI Framework]]. Closes with a call for research modeling **dynamic team relationships in context** and teams operating in **complex systems (MTS)**.
