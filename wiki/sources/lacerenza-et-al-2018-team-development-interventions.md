# Lacerenza et al. 2018 — Team Development Interventions

Nav: [[index]] | [[sources/_index]]

## Core Argument

Four distinct evidence-based TDI types exist, each targeting a different aspect of team effectiveness. They are not interchangeable. The two-category framework: **training interventions** (leadership training, team training — can be for ad hoc or intact teams) and **process interventions** (team building, team debriefing — for intact teams only). Match the TDI to the specific deficiency; science-based design is non-negotiable.

## Context

Teams are the #1 global workforce trend (Deloitte, 130 countries). Employees report 50%+ increase in team-related tasks. Yet 36% of graduates have deficient team competencies; only 21% of executives believe their org can design cross-functional teams effectively. Teams produce outcomes greater than the sum of individual contributions — but only with effective **team processes** and **emergent states** (Marks et al. 2001).

Team competencies (KSAs) classified into three domains:
- **Attitude-based**: cohesion, trust, psychological safety, collective efficacy, commitment to teamwork
- **Skill-based**: coordination, communication, conflict resolution, shared leadership, backup behavior
- **Knowledge-based**: situation awareness, transactive memory systems, shared mental models

## Four TDI Types (Figure 2)

### 1. Team Training
*Target*: Team competencies (KSAs). *For*: ad hoc or intact teams.
Formalized, structured learning with preset objectives targeting specific team KSAs. Meta-analytic support: effective across individual, team, and organizational levels (Delise et al. 2010; Salas et al. 2007, 2008; Hughes et al. 2016).

**Real impact**: saved lives — team training in healthcare associated with lower mortality (Hughes et al. 2016).

**Evidence-based recommendations**:
- Set training goals at team level; evaluate team processes (e.g., communication) AND outcomes (e.g., performance)
- Foster **psychological safety** during training — enables error discussion and learning (Edmondson 1999)
- Needs analysis first; identify KSA gaps and org support for transfer
- Multiple delivery methods (information + demonstration + practice + feedback)

### 2. Leadership Training
*Target*: Leader capabilities. *For*: individual leaders (impacts team outcomes indirectly).
Heredity = ~30% of leadership variance; training + experience = ~70% (Arvey et al. 2006). Meta-analytic evidence: leadership training improves learning, transfer, and organizational outcomes by up to **29%** (Lacerenza et al. 2017). Effects cascade: → leader capabilities → team performance + subordinate job satisfaction.

**Stronger for cognitive/skill-based than affective outcomes** (Avolio et al. 2009; Lacerenza et al. 2017).

**Evidence-based recommendations**:
- Target soft skills (interpersonal, intrapersonal) to enhance subordinate teamwork
- Transformational and empowering leadership styles → team cohesion, potency, performance
- Initiating structure skills → team-level effects
- Evaluate cognitive and skill-based content

### 3. Team Building
*Target*: Interpersonal processes. *For*: intact teams only.
Four components (Beer 1976; Dyer 1977): goal setting, interpersonal-relationship management, role clarification, problem solving. Meta-analysis (Klein et al. 2009, 60 ES): significant positive effects on cognitive, affective (trust), and process (coordination) outcomes. **No significant direct effect on team performance** — performance mediated via upstream process improvements.

Goal setting + role clarification = strongest components (build shared mental models).

Key mechanisms:
- **Trust**: enables overcoming uncertainty, accepting vulnerability → better coordination (De Jong, Dirks & Gillespie 2016 meta-analysis)
- **Role clarity ≠ role rigidity**: shared understanding of who does what, including flexible role-switching — reduces confusion, facilitates backup behavior
- **Action plans + follow-up**: essential for accountability; without them, improvements don't persist

**Warning**: "ropes course" and "icebreaker" activities without scientifically grounded components → no durable benefit (Shuffler et al. 2013).

### 4. Team Debriefing
*Target*: Team processes. *For*: intact teams (post-performance episode).
"One of the most promising methods for accelerating learning from experience" (Eddy, Tannenbaum & Mathieu 2013). Team members reflect on a performance episode: discuss what happened, uncover problems, confirm successes, plan future periods.

**Meta-analytic effect**: team debriefs increase performance by **20–25%** on average (Tannenbaum & Cerasoli 2013). Average debrief = ~18 min. Health care application: improved patient outcomes (return of spontaneous circulation; Couper et al. 2013). Also reduced employee burnout over time (Chen et al. 2017).

**Evidence-based recommendations**:
- Psychologically safe climate essential — surface criticism without blame
- Trained facilitator preferred; debrief leader should not dominate but guide
- Structure by performance/teamwork categories — NOT chronological recap
- Discuss both positive and negative behaviors (builds shared mental models)
- Document conclusions; follow up on agreements

## General Training Principles (apply to all training TDIs)

Baldwin & Ford (1988) transfer model: transfer ← training design features + trainee characteristics + work environment.

1. **Needs analysis** — identify KSA gaps, tailor to org context
2. **Multiple delivery methods** — information (lecture), demonstration (video), practice (role play, simulation); best programs mix all three
3. **Diagnostic feedback** — frame as developmental not evaluative (Kluger & DeNisi 1996)
4. **Stakeholder alignment** — ensure expectations match achievable outcomes

## Future Directions

Three emerging team types needing targeted TDIs:
1. **Virtual teams**: communication richness/timeliness challenges; social presence limitations → trust formation difficulty; real-time/wearable-tech-delivered training suggested
2. **Software development teams**: short lifespan → need swift trust; agile/scrum approaches → team debriefs and building tailored to sprint cycles
3. **Shared/non-hierarchical leadership teams**: different competencies needed (team KSAs vs. individual self-efficacy); leadership training must include team-level content
