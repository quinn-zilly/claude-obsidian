# Cerasoli et al. (2018) — Antecedents and Outcomes of Informal Learning Behaviors

Nav: [[index]] | [[Learning & Development MOC]] | [[Informal Learning Behaviors (ILBs)]]

> [!abstract] One-line
> The **first meta-analysis** of **[[Informal Learning Behaviors (ILBs)]]** (k=49, N=55,514): personal *and* situational antecedents predict engagement in ILBs, and ILBs are positively linked to attitudes (ρ=.29), knowledge/skill acquisition (ρ=.41), and performance (ρ=.42).

## Motivation

Consensus estimates hold that **70–90% of workplace learning is informal** (on-the-job, ongoing), yet ~80% of learning research and ~80% of learning *investment* target **formal training**. Cerasoli et al. address this misalignment: hundreds of studies and a dozen meta-analyses cover formal learning, but no prior comprehensive theory or meta-analysis had systematically mapped the antecedents and outcomes of informal learning. Two goals: **(1)** offer an integrative definition of ILBs to bound the construct; **(2)** meta-analyze primary data on ILB correlates.

## Defining ILBs

Building on Tannenbaum et al. (2010), the paper separates *learning* (K/S gains) from the *behaviors* that produce them:

> **Informal learning behaviors (ILBs)** are **non-curricular behaviors and activities pursued in service of knowledge and skill acquisition that take place outside formally-designated learning contexts.** They are **predominantly self-directed, intentional, and field-based** — not syllabus-based, discrete, or linear.

Contrast: **Formal learning behaviors (FLBs)** are curricular, organizationally sanctioned, externally directed, classroom/course-based, with set objectives and a discrete beginning/end.

Key boundary features: ILBs are self-initiated, intrinsically driven, individually controlled, aimed at learner-set goals; the **same behavior (e.g., asking a question) is an ILB only in a non-training context**. **Intentionality** is critical — ILBs require conscious, goal-directed agency, which distinguishes them from **incidental/implicit/accidental learning** (unconscious). ILBs differ from mentoring/coaching (usually agreed-upon criteria + linear progression) and from social/vicarious/network learning (require interaction with others). See [[Informal Learning Behaviors (ILBs)]] for the full construct map.

## Framework (three levels of construct specificity)

| Level 1 | Level 2 | Level 3 examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Personal antecedents** | Individual predispositions | personality/propensity (conscientiousness, curiosity, adaptability); general learning-related motives ([[Goal Orientation|learning goal orientation]], [[Motivation to Learn]], self-efficacy) |
| | Demographics | age, education, sex, income, rank/tenure, experience, marital status |
| **Situational antecedents** | Job/task characteristics | demands, resources, control/autonomy |
| | Support | people support; formal org support (rewards); informal org support (climate, POS, norms) |
| | Opportunities for learning | potential for new learning, lower workload, time |
| **Outcomes** | Attitudes / Knowledge-skill / Performance | job satisfaction, commitment, engagement / proficiency, core skills / effectiveness, salary, promotions |

## Method

Random-effects meta-analysis (Hunter & Schmidt, 2004), corrected for measurement unreliability in predictor and criterion. **376 effect sizes, 43 primary studies, 49 independent samples, N=55,514.** Search through 2015 across psycINFO, ERIC, PubMed, Scopus, Google Scholar, Dissertation Abstracts (published + unpublished). Excluded: experiential learning, debriefing, incidental learning, mentoring, socialization, error-management learning. A cross-classified HLM test found **neither design features nor source quality moderated effect magnitudes** — methodological rigor posed no threat to validity.

## Key results

**Personal antecedents:**

- **Individual predispositions overall** ρ=.27 (k=12, N=4,432). Personality/propensity ρ=.27; learning motives ρ=.33.
- **Demographics overall** ρ=.02 (near-zero). Non-zero but small: education .10, rank/tenure .18, married (vs single) .17; age −.07, income −.05, sex −.03 (male-coded). Age and sex effects are small, consistent with their weak links to attitudes/motivation/performance elsewhere.

**Situational antecedents (largest average effects):**

- **Situational overall** ρ=.22 (k=28).
- **Support** ρ=.32 (k=20) — strongest cluster. **Formal** support ρ=.38 (tangible rewards) > **informal** .30 ≈ **people** .31. Notably formal (tangible) support edged out informal, though CrIs overlap.
- **Job/task characteristics** ρ=.21: control/autonomy .31, resources .30, demands .13.
- **Opportunities for learning** ρ=.04 (equivocal): potential for new learning .12, time available .14, lower workload −.02 (CrI spans zero).

**Outcomes (all positive):**

- **Overall** ρ=.33 (k=23, N=10,975).
- **Attitudes** ρ=.29 · **Knowledge/skill acquisition** ρ=.41 · **Performance** ρ=.42.

## Interpretation & implications

Support is the highest-signal antecedent — consistent with social-psych evidence on the power of social cues/environments. The authors speculate a possible **curvilinear** time/demand relationship (too much slack obviates ILBs; too much pressure precludes them) — untested here. Practically: organizations should invest in **support (especially rewards), autonomy, and learning-rich tasks** to promote ILBs, since ILBs pay off in valued criteria. ILBs and FLBs are **complementary, not incompatible** — an open question is whether formal training can be used to *instigate* ILBs.
