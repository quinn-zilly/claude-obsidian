# Joshi, Son & Roh (2015) — When Can Women Close the Gap?

> [!abstract] One-line
> A meta-analysis showing that women largely **close the performance gap but not the reward gap** — sex differences in rewards (d = .56) were **~14× larger** than in performance evaluations (d = .04) — and that performance differences do **not** explain reward differences.

Meta-analytic test of whether occupation-, industry-, and job-level factors mitigate or exacerbate male–female differences in performance evaluations (**k = 93; n = 95,882**) and rewards (**k = 97; n = 378,850**), spanning ~30 years. Bridges the **micro–macro divide** in gender research (Baron & Pfeffer 1994) by uniting institutional/structural perspectives with sociopsychological perspectives on gender bias.

---

## Headline effect sizes

| Outcome | d | k | 95% CI | Read |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Performance evaluations | **.04** | 93 | −.12 to .20 | includes zero → **no significant sex difference** |
| Rewards (pay + bonus + promotions) | **.56** | 97 | .45 to .65 | men rewarded higher |
| — Pay subgroup | .67 | 67 | .55 to .75 | largest gap |
| — Promotions subgroup | .24 | 29 | .08 to .39 | smaller gap |

Reward gap ≈ **14× the performance gap**. High Q → substantial heterogeneity → moderators matter.

> [!key-insight] Central finding
> **Women often close the gap in performance, but not in rewards**, and performance differences do **not** account for reward differences. Reward allocation (often zero-sum, e.g. promotion to partner) and performance evaluation (not zero-sum, e.g. billable hours) are **distinct** decisions and must be disentangled.

---

## Moderators (meta-regression)

- **Occupational demographic composition** — a higher **proportion of men** enhances the gap in *both* performance and rewards (H1a/b supported). See [[Role Congruity Theory]].
- **Occupational prestige** — in **highly prestigious occupations** women performed **equally** but were **rewarded significantly lower** (H2b supported; H2a not — no performance penalty). Prestige acts as a hierarchy-enhancing context; see [[Social Dominance Theory (Rewards)]].
- **Industry-level female executives** — a higher **proportion of women at executive/senior-manager levels** was the **only** factor that let women *reverse* the gap in *both* performance and rewards (H3a/b supported).
- **Job complexity** — enhances the gap in both outcomes (H4a/b supported): complex jobs entail ambiguous/idiosyncratic criteria, "face-time" demands, and less scrutiny of individual inputs. See [[Occupational Prestige and Job Complexity]].
- **H5 (performance explains rewards): rejected.** A semipartial correlation test showed performance differences did **not** account for reward differences.

---

## Configurational (QCA) analysis

Crisp-set Qualitative Comparative Analysis over four attributes (occ. sex composition, occ. prestige, % female executives, job complexity). Key results (Table 4):

- **Men rewarded higher than women**: no simple main effect; a bundle including **high job complexity** + **absence of women executives** + male-dominated/high-prestige context.
- **Women evaluated equal/higher than men**: both sufficient configurations involved **less complex (routine) jobs** (e.g., bank tellers) — women could gain parity in routine work even in male-dominated occupations/industries.
- **No configuration was sufficient for women receiving equal-or-higher *rewards* than men.** The reward gap is the sticky one.

---

## Mechanisms & implications

Prestige + masculine norms set up **hierarchy-enhancing** reward allocation (social dominance). In prestigious professions (academia, law), performance criteria are objective but reward allocation is **subjective, opaque, adversarial, high-stakes** ("up or out") — the locus of inequity. "High-involvement"/strategic HR practices (peer evaluations tied to rewards) may **exacerbate** rather than reduce inequality (Castilla 2012; Cappelli 1999).
