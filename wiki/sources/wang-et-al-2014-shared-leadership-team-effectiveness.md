# Wang et al. 2014 — A Meta-Analysis of Shared Leadership and Team Effectiveness

First quantitative cumulation of the [[Shared Leadership|shared-leadership]] literature. Where classic leadership research fixed on the single, formal, vertical leader, this *Journal of Applied Psychology* meta-analysis treats leadership as an **emergent team property** — mutual influence and shared responsibility distributed among members — and asks whether that "sharedness" actually predicts team effectiveness, and under what conditions.

## Core Argument

Shared leadership (a.k.a. collective, distributed leadership) is distinct from teamwork: teamwork is cooperative cognition/action converting inputs to outputs; shared leadership is *distributed influence and responsibility* among members. Wang et al. cumulate 42 independent samples (N = 3,439 teams) and probe five questions: overall effect, whether the **content** of what is shared matters, whether effects differ by **criterion type**, whether **work complexity** moderates, whether the **measurement referent** matters, and whether shared leadership adds anything beyond **vertical** leadership.

## Key Findings

> [!quote] Headline
> Overall corrected ρ = **.34** (k = 42) — a moderately strong, generalizable positive relationship (uncorrected r = .29; publication bias minimal).

**Content of what is shared matters most (H2a, H2b supported):**

| Shared leadership content | ρ |
|---|---|
| Traditional (initiating structure, consideration) | **.18** |
| New-genre (charismatic, transformational) | **.34** |
| Cumulative / overall shared leadership | **.35** |

New-genre and cumulative CIs do not overlap traditional's — sharing *change-oriented* leadership functions pays off far more than sharing traditional exchange/maintenance functions.

**Criterion type (H3, cautionary support):** shared leadership relates more strongly to attitudinal outcomes (ρ=.45) and behavioral processes/emergent states (ρ=.44) than to subjective (ρ=.25) or objective (ρ=.18) team performance — though same-source measurement inflates some of the attitudinal/process links.

**Work complexity moderates (H4 supported):** effect grows as team work is more complex (WLS b=.04, p<.05; model R²=.12). Shared leadership matters most where continuous novelty/interdependence demands distributed input.

**Measurement referent (RQ1):** team-, peer-, and self-referents yield statistically indistinguishable effects — referent choice does not bias the relationship.

**Incremental over vertical leadership (RQ2):** in path models using Judge et al. (2004) / Judge & Piccolo (2004) vertical-leadership estimates, shared leadership adds **unique variance to team performance** beyond vertical leadership (shared traditional β=.09; shared new-genre β=.09; each ΔR²≈.01, p<.05). Small but significant — shared and vertical leadership complement rather than merely substitute.

## Significance

Legitimizes shared leadership as more than a substitute for the boss: it is a plural, emergent form that uniquely contributes to team outcomes, especially for complex work and change-oriented functions. Anchors the "third wave" plural-leadership turn described in [[lord-et-al-2017-leadership-in-applied-psychology|Lord et al. (2017)]].
