# Leslie et al. (2020) — Diversity Ideologies Meta-Analysis

*Journal of Applied Psychology, 105*(5), 453–471. [doi:10.1037/apl0000446](https://dx.doi.org/10.1037/apl0000446)

Random-effects meta-analysis (**k = 296** effect sizes; 167 independent samples) of how four [[Diversity Ideologies|diversity ideologies]] relate to four indicators of high-quality intergroup relations. Core argument: mixed prior findings stem from **conflating** (a) distinct identity-blind ideologies and (b) distinct forms of intergroup bias. Each ideology aligns better with some outcomes than others, so effects diverge both *across ideologies on the same outcome* and *across outcomes for the same ideology*.

## The 4×4 Design

**Ideologies** — three identity-blind ("melting pot": minimize differences) plus one identity-conscious ("salad bowl": acknowledge differences):

- **Colorblindness** — ignore differences (cognitive strategy).
- **Meritocracy** — treat groups equitably; prevent discrimination.
- **Assimilation** — nondominant groups adopt dominant-group practices.
- **Multiculturalism** (identity-conscious) — acknowledge, learn about, maintain, or value differences.

**Outcomes** — three forms of intergroup bias (affect/behavior/cognition) plus policy support:

- **Prejudice** (negative affect), **Discrimination** (negative behavior), **Stereotyping** (negative cognition), **Diversity policy support** (e.g., affirmative action).

## Key Findings (meta-analytic ρ)

| Ideology | Prejudice | Discrimination | Stereotyping | Policy support |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Multiculturalism** | −.32 | −.22 | −.17 | **+.57** |
| **Colorblindness** | (neg.) | ≈ .00 (null) | −.19 | −.19 |
| **Meritocracy** | −.15 (null) | **−.48** | .00 (null) | −.45 |
| **Assimilation** | +.17 | (+) | +.17 | (neg.) |

> [!key-insight] Ideologies are not interchangeable
> The **only consistent effect** is that all three identity-blind ideologies are **negatively related to diversity policy support**. Otherwise, colorblindness, meritocracy, and assimilation are distinct constructs with **divergent effects** — treating them as one construct masks this.

Highlights:
- **Multiculturalism** is the most consistently associated with high-quality intergroup relations (reduces all three bias forms, raises policy support). Its policy-support effect is stronger among **dominant** groups (ρ = .48).
- **Meritocracy** has the strongest negative relationship with **discrimination** (−.48) yet is unrelated to prejudice/stereotyping and *negatively* related to policy support.
- **Colorblindness** is the only ideology negatively related to both negative *and neutral* stereotyping, but is null for discrimination.
- **Assimilation** is the most consistently tied to *low*-quality relations (raises prejudice, discrimination, stereotyping); some effects hold for dominant but not nondominant groups.

## Theory

Grounded in **[[Social Identity Theory|social categorization / social identity]]** plus a **consistency** premise: individuals avoid conflicts among beliefs/attitudes/behaviors, so an ideology most affects the outcome most consistent with its content (e.g., colorblindness — a cognitive strategy — most affects the cognitive bias, stereotyping).

## Implications for Practice

Different diversity training may be needed depending on the **dominant ideology** in an organization:
- Colorblindness prevails → target **discrimination** (behavioral strategies), since colorblindness leaves discrimination untouched.
- Meritocracy prevails → target **prejudice and stereotyping** (cognitions/feelings toward outgroups).
- Assimilation prevails → broad training across all bias types + policy support; especially for **dominant-group** members.
- Multiculturalism prevails → still combat **neutral stereotyping**, its one gap.
- Both colorblindness and meritocracy suppress **policy support** → interventions to raise policy support benefit both.

## Limitations

85% of studies were Western; the consistency premise may not generalize to cultures more comfortable with contradiction (e.g., East Asian). Some cells thin (e.g., k = 2 for assimilation–policy support). Cross-sectional — ideologies treated as antecedents.
