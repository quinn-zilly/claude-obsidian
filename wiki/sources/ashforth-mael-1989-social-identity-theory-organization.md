# Ashforth & Mael (1989) — Social Identity Theory and the Organization

> [!key-insight]
> Foundational paper that imported [[Social Identity Theory]] (SIT) into organizational behavior. Core move: redefine **organizational identification as a purely perceptual-cognitive "perception of oneness with a group"** (the answer to "Who am I?"), and surgically separate it from the constructs it had been confused with — [[Organizational Commitment|commitment]], internalization, loyalty (affect), and effort (behavior). Identification is *cognition only*; behavior and affect are merely potential antecedents/consequences.

## Core Argument (the abstract, restated)

The paper makes three claims:

1. **What identification is** — social identification is a perception of oneness with a group of persons.
2. **Where it comes from** — it stems from the categorization of individuals, the **distinctiveness** and **prestige** of the group, the **salience of out-groups**, and the traditional factors associated with group formation.
3. **What it produces** — activities congruent with the identity, support for institutions that embody the identity, stereotypical perceptions of self and others, and outcomes traditionally tied to group formation — and it **reinforces its own antecedents**.

This lens is then applied to three domains: **organizational socialization, role conflict, and intergroup relations.**

## The Construct-Confusion Problem (the paper's wedge)

Prior research conflated identification with related constructs. Key distinctions the paper draws:

| Construct | Self-statement | Nature |
|-----------|----------------|--------|
| **Identification** | "I am" (a member of X) | Perceptual/cognitive; org-specific |
| **Internalization** | "I believe" (X's values) | Incorporating values as one's own guiding principles |
| **Commitment** | — | Bundles internalization + behavioral intentions + affect (per OCQ) |
| **Classical identification** (Kelman) | wanting to *be like* another person | Predicated on appease/emulate, not self-definition |

You can identify with an organization yet disagree with its values ("young Turks," counterculture). You can score high on the [[Organizational Commitment|OCQ]] not from shared destiny but because the org is a convenient vehicle for personal career goals — and could transfer to a more convenient org without psychic loss. The identifier, by contrast, suffers psychic loss on leaving.

> [!quote]
> "This organization's successes are my successes" — sample item from Mael's (1988) 6-item identification measure, whose two-factor CFA against the 15-item OCQ (AGFI .825 vs .780) showed identification and commitment are empirically differentiable.

## Antecedents of Identification

- **Distinctiveness** of the group's values/practices vs comparable groups (separates "figure from ground"). Explains missionary zeal of new/innovative orgs. Even *negatively* valued distinctiveness can anchor identity ("Black is beautiful").
- **Prestige** of the group — via intergroup comparison, identification feeds self-esteem; people identify with winners (bandwagon effect).
- **Salience of out-groups** — awareness of an out-group sharpens in-group awareness and assumed in-group homogeneity (Wilder; Kanter's token-women study). Intergroup competition is a special case.
- **Traditional group-formation factors** — interaction, similarity, liking, proximity, shared goals/threat, common history. Facilitate but are **not necessary** ([[Minimal Group Paradigm]] shows mere categorization suffices).

## Consequences of Identification

1. Choice of identity-**congruent activities** and support for identity-embodying institutions (Mael's alumni donated, recruited offspring, attended functions).
2. Outcomes tied to group formation — cohesion, cooperation, altruism, positive group evaluation, loyalty/pride — but, crucially, **need not be interpersonal** (see [[Psychological Group]]).
3. Internalization of and adherence to group norms; **self-stereotyping** → depersonalization of the self (see [[Self-Categorization and Self-Stereotyping]]).
4. **Reinforces its own antecedents** (distinctiveness, etc.) — a feedback loop.

## Three Applications

### Organizational Socialization
Newcomers build both a *situational definition* and a *self-definition*; social identity is a large part of the latter. Socialization affects internalization partly **indirectly via identification** (socialization → identification → internalization), alongside a direct path. Investiture vs **divestiture** processes (prisons, military, religious orders, professional schools) reconstruct identity by stripping old symbols and imposing new ones. Symbolic management and **charismatic/transformational leadership** ([[Transformational and Charismatic Leadership]]) work by making membership salient; classical identification with a charismatic leader can be generalized to the org via [[Routinization of Charisma]] (AA vs NCA example).

### Role Conflict
Identity is an **amalgam of multiple, loosely coupled identities**; it is not identities that conflict but the values/norms/demands inside them. Nested org roles (workgroup ⊂ dept ⊂ org) constrain but don't eliminate conflict. Conflict is typically **not** resolved by integrating identities but by **ordering, separating, or buffering** them — via a prepotency hierarchy, cognitive decoupling, or sequential compliance (the Morton Thiokol "take off your engineering hat, put on your management hat" Challenger example). Compartmentalization → possible double standards, apparent hypocrisy, and selective forgetting. Conflict is **endemic but mostly latent**, surfacing only when one must "don different hats simultaneously."

### Intergroup Relations
Assuming an *ideographic* organization (subunit-specific identities), SIT yields a **pessimistic** view: much conflict stems from the mere existence of groups. Biased intergroup comparison → out-group negative stereotypes, social distance, self-serving attributions (Perrow; Bates & White hospital authority study). Conflict can arise **without objective causes** (e.g., scarce resources). **Qualifications:** groups compare only with similar/proximal out-groups; can compare on *different* dimensions (mutual appreciation); and ethnocentrism drops when resource distribution is seen as **legitimate/institutionalized**. A strong **organizational** identity unifies (holographic orgs).

## Why It Matters

> [!quote]
> "Social identity is the cognitive mechanism which makes group behaviour possible." (Turner 1982, quoted approvingly)

The paper's enduring contribution: a psychological group is **far more than an extension of interpersonal relationships**. Identification with a collectivity can arise without interpersonal cohesion, similarity, or interaction, yet powerfully drive affect and behavior. This reverses the conventional logic that interpersonal regard "aggregates up" into an individual–organization bond. Mael's identification scale spawned a large measurement literature; the paper is among the most-cited in organizational behavior.
