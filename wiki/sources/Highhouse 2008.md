# Highhouse (2008) — Stubborn Reliance on Intuition and Subjectivity in Employee Selection

> [!note] Stub
> Seed page created to resolve inbound links from [[Mechanical vs Clinical Prediction]] and [[kuncel-et-al-2013-mechanical-vs-clinical]]. Expand on next ingest of the full article.

Highhouse's focal argument: despite decades of evidence that **mechanical/statistical combination of predictor data outperforms holistic clinical judgment**, practitioners stubbornly prefer intuitive, subjective judgment in employee selection. He identifies two persistent beliefs behind the resistance: (1) the conviction that one can achieve near-perfect prediction by getting to know the "whole person," and (2) the belief that prediction can be substantially improved by combining data in the head rather than mechanically.

This is a canonical statement of the **evidence-based-practice gap** in personnel selection — the field keeps re-litigating a question the data settled long ago.

## Why It Matters Here

- Frames the practitioner-side resistance that [[kuncel-et-al-2013-mechanical-vs-clinical|Kuncel et al. (2013)]] later counter with meta-analytic evidence that mechanical combination beats clinical by a substantial margin.
