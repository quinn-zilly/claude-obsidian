# Shippmann et al. (2000) — The Practice of Competency Modeling

> [!abstract] One-line
> The **SIOP Job Analysis & Competency Modeling Task Force (JACMTF)** report — the field's cardinal reference on competency modeling — that defines the trend, contrasts it with job analysis on a **10-dimension Level of Rigor Scale**, and finds job analysis generally more rigorous except on linking to business strategy.

## What it is

A 2-year investigation (commissioned Sept. 1997) by a task force jointly sponsored by SIOP's Professional Practice and Scientific Affairs Committees, chaired by **Jeffery Shippmann** of PepsiCo, with ten authors including [[Juan Sanchez]] and [[Kenneth Pearlman]]. Method: extensive literature search + **37 subject-matter-expert interviews** + task-force members' applied experience. It set out to answer: *What is a competency? What is the difference between competency modeling and job analysis? Why are competencies appealing to business consumers? What is the future of competency modeling?*

## Context

Competition, flattening, self-managed teams, and matrixed structures fueled worry that traditional job analysis "may be unable to continue to play a central role." Competency modeling "exploded" — surveys reported **75–80%** of responding organizations had some competency-driven application in place.

## The definitional problem

A central finding: **consistent definitions of "competency" do not exist**, and even authorities in the field cannot arrive at a clear meaning of the term. This ambiguity is the root of much of the confusion the report set out to dispel, and it propagates downstream — deficiencies in a competency's meaning translate into deficiencies in any selection or HR tool built on it.

## The Level of Rigor comparison

The task force identified variables on which competency modeling and job analysis can be compared, distinguished into **evaluative** (front-end) criteria that shape inference quality and **non-evaluative** criteria (uses/characteristics). See [[Level of Rigor Scale (JACMTF)]] for the full breakdown. Headline pattern (as later tabulated by [[Sackett-Walmsley-Laczo-2013-job-and-work-analysis|Sackett et al.]] in their Table 4.1):

- **Job analysis rated more rigorous** on nearly every *evaluative* criterion — method of investigation, descriptor content, procedures, level of detail, content review, prioritization, **reliability assessment**, retention criteria, and documentation of the research process — *except* establishing a link to business goals and strategies.
- **Competency modeling rated more rigorous** on: linking to business goals, focus on core competencies, organizational fit vs job match, focus on values/personality orientation, face validity, and training/development applications.
- Job analysis also stronger on focus on technical skills and selection/decision applications.

> [!key-insight] Trade, don't replace
> The task force's constructive move: rather than declaring a winner, **leverage the strengths of one camp to shore up the weaknesses of the other**. Competency modeling's strategic alignment and face validity can enrich job analysis; job analysis's methodological rigor and documentation can discipline competency modeling.

## Why it matters downstream

This report became *the* citation for the competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis debate. Both later sources in this cluster lean on it:
- [[Sackett-Walmsley-Laczo-2013-job-and-work-analysis|Sackett, Walmsley & Laczo (2013)]] reproduce its 17-variable comparison and note its conclusions rest partly on a *small number of expert opinions* (a caveat the authors flagged themselves).
- [[Sanchez-Levine-2012-rise-fall-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine (2012)]] cite it for the finding that CM is less rigorous than JA on data collection, detail, reliability, and documentation.

> [!quote]
> The report closes by invoking the proverb "May you live in interesting times" — the pace of business change plus enterprise computing has made how we identify and describe work-related job aspects and worker requirements genuinely interesting.
