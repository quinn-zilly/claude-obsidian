# lee-park-koo-2015-organizational-identification-meta-analysis

Nav: [[index]] | [[Organizational Identification (Outcomes)]] | [[Teams & Org Behavior MOC]]

> [!abstract] Source summary
> A *Psychological Bulletin* meta-analysis testing whether **organizational identification (OI)** has *unique value* in explaining work attitudes and behaviors — not by accumulating correlates, but by positioning OI inside the classic **[[Attitude-Behavior Relations|Fishbein & Ajzen (1975) attitude–behavior model]]**. Three headline findings: (1) OI is significantly associated with key attitudes (job involvement, job satisfaction, affective commitment) and behaviors (in-role, extra-role performance); (2) OI acts as a **basis** from which general attitudes and behaviors are engendered, and has a **direct effect on general behavior above and beyond general attitude**; (3) effects are **moderated by national culture** — stronger in collectivistic than individualistic cultures.

## Theoretical framing

OI is grounded in **[[Social Identity Theory]]** (Tajfel & Turner): individuals define the self by the same attributes they believe define their organization ("I become we"; depersonalization). Distinct from **[[Organizational Commitment|affective commitment]]** — commitment is a general social-exchange-based attitude lacking the **self-defining** nature central to identification (see [[Social Identification]]).

## Key meta-analytic results

| Relation | Corrected ρ | k | N |
|---|---|---|---|
| OI → in-role performance | **.27** | 41 | 13,870 |
| OI → extra-role performance | **.42** | 53 | 14,459 |
| OI → general attitude (β) | **.74** | — | — |
| General attitude → general behavior (β) | **.24** | — | — |

- OI → extra-role > OI → in-role (identification matters more for discretionary behavior like OCB/voice).
- In-role effects were **higher when self-rated** (ρ=.33) than other-rated — a self-serving-bias caution.
- **Structural model**: the attitude–behavior model with OI as the basis fit better than alternatives; OI predicts **both** general attitude and behavior, and retains a **direct path to behavior** beyond attitude.

## Culture moderation

Using Hofstede's dimensions, OI's effects on attitudes/behaviors are **stronger in collectivistic (and long-term-oriented) cultures**, where identity overlap between individual and collective is more valued.

## Key insight

> [!key-insight]
> Organizational identification is not just another correlate of commitment — because it involves the **essential definition of entities** (who the individual and the organization are), it sits *upstream* as a basis that shapes whole sets of attitudes and behaviors, with a behavioral effect that survives controlling for attitude. This is the meta-analytic payoff of the [[Social Identification|Ashforth & Mael]] "identification ≠ commitment" distinction.
