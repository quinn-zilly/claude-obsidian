# Klein & Kozlowski (2000) — From Micro to Meso: Critical Steps in Conceptualizing and Conducting Multilevel Research

> [!abstract] One-line
> The accessible, practitioner-facing companion to the 2000 chapter: it walks a hypothetical researcher — **"Dr. Faust"** — through the four choices of shifting from single-level to multilevel research, condensing the big chapter into a usable roadmap.

Nav: [[index]] | [[Multilevel Theory & Methods MOC]]

## The device: Dr. Faust

Faust studies how individual performance is predicted by self-efficacy, non-work responsibilities, and perceived leader charisma. Asked by "Acme" to also illuminate **team** performance across ~100 proximal and virtual teams, he must convert a single-level model into a multilevel one. His journey structures the article around **four choices**.

## The four choices

1. **Construct & measurement** — Type each higher-level construct as **[[Global, Shared, and Configural Unit Properties|global, shared, or configural]]**. Team location = global; leadership perceptions and team efficacy = (possibly) shared; non-work responsibilities and team performance = configural. Wrong typing (e.g., averaging self-efficacy to get team efficacy) destroys construct validity — *the orchestra whose members are individually skilled yet sound terrible together.*
2. **Model** — [[Cross-Level Models|Single-level, cross-level (direct-effect / moderator / frog-pond), or homologous]].
3. **Sampling** — Enough **between-unit** variability to avoid range restriction; **within-unit** homogeneity where shared constructs require aggregation; varying **patterns** for configural constructs. See [[Multilevel Research Design and Sampling]].
4. **Analysis** — Procedures to justify aggregation ([[Aggregation Justification Indices|$r_{wg}$, η², ICC(1), ICC(2), WABA]]) and to test models (WABA, CLOP, **HLM**).

## Why not just aggregate?

Blindly generalizing across levels commits the **ecological** or **atomistic** [[Levels of Analysis Fallacies|fallacy]]. An individual-level charisma→performance effect can be neutral or negative at the team level; the mean of within-team-divergent charisma ratings "describes none of the team members' views."

Explicitly derived from [[kozlowski-klein-2000-multilevel-approach|Kozlowski & Klein (2000)]] and Klein et al. (2000); the source to reach for first when *doing* a multilevel study rather than theorizing one.
