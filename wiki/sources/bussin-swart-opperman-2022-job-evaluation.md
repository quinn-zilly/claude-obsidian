# Bussin & Swart-Opperman (2022) — Job Evaluation in the New World of Work

> [!abstract] One-line
> A practitioner text on **[[Job Evaluation]]** — the systematic, objective grading of *jobs* (not people) to build a defensible pay hierarchy and achieve equal pay for work of equal value — with an in-depth treatment of the **[[Paterson Job Evaluation|Paterson method]]** and the link to salary structures. (Chapters 3–5 ingested.)

## What job evaluation is

Job evaluation is the systematic, objective process of comparing one job to another within an organisation to arrive at job levels / a hierarchy. "Job sizing" is the outcome — a ruler measuring the size/weight of each job. **It grades the job, not the person**: it ignores individual characteristics, personality, performance, individual effort, and labour-market conditions.

Benefits: a logical graded hierarchy and pay structure, reduced inequality, control over wage/salary administration, easier collective bargaining (common reference point), and compliance with equal-pay-for-work-of-equal-value legislation.

## Two stages

- **Stage 1 (grading)** — grade the *job* in its current state, impersonally, with no incumbent in mind. Inflexible: grading rules must be observed. Produces job descriptions and a grade.
- **Stage 2 (salary structure)** — only then look at individuals and place them on the pay scale; personal qualities, performance, and skill scarcity may be considered. Produces a [[Salary Structure|salary structure]]. Flexible.

## Why systems fail

Not the system itself but: weak implementation, weak top-management support, lack of employee participation, **upgrading jobs without a corresponding change in job content** (the major cause of collapse), poor administration/communication, and stale job descriptions.

## Choice of system

All popular systems produce similar, workable hierarchies — a ~**0.94 correlation** across systems (only ~6% of jobs differ). What matters is defensibility, executive commitment, universal understanding, low admin burden, and flexibility (e.g. broad-banding). There is "no such thing as a scientific method of job evaluation"; all are systematic approaches.

Systems categorised by (a) basis of comparison — job-vs-job or job-vs-scale — and (b) method of analysis — whole job or job elements/factors (e.g. factor comparison like HAY/IPE; points methods like Peromnes/Paterson Points; ranking; classification).

## Paterson

See [[Paterson Job Evaluation]] — bands jobs A–F by the **kind and complexity of decisions** required. Developed by [[Thomas Thomson Paterson]] (Scotland, 1963); close cousin to Elliott Jaques' [[Stratified Systems Theory]]. The book argues Paterson is the most suitable general system (flexible, quick, robust, ILO-recognised, non-discriminatory).
