# Binning & Barrett (1989) — Validity of Personnel Decisions

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]] | [[Binning-Barrett Validity Framework]]

> [!abstract] One-line
> The seminal unification of construct-, content-, and criterion-related validity into **one inferential system**: validation is theory-building, the traditional "trinity" are merely different **evidential bases** for the same overriding inference, and the neglected criterion side needs the same rigor as the predictor side.

## Validation = theory building

Validity is a property of **inferences from** test information, not of the test (Cronbach; Guion; Messick). Following Landy (1986), validating a selection procedure is a **special case of hypothesis testing / scientific theory building**. Constructs are labels for clusters of covarying behaviors; a measure is "nothing more than a procedure for sampling behaviors" within a construct domain.

## The inference frameworks (Figures 1–3)

**Figure 1 (theory building)** — Nunnally's four inferences linking two constructs and their measures:

1. Measures X and Y relate (the *only* directly testable inference).
2. X measures construct A.
3. Constructs A and B are causally related.
4. Y measures construct B.

**Figure 2 (personnel selection)** — remaps to predictor/criterion:

- **Inference 5** — predictor measure ↔ criterion measure (the classic empirical validity coefficient).
- **Inference 6** — predictor measure samples its **predictor construct domain**.
- **Inference 7** — predictor construct domain overlaps the **performance domain** (theoretical, not empirically testable).
- **Inference 8** — criterion measure samples the performance domain.
- **Inference 9** — predictor measure → performance domain: **the single overriding inference** for which validity evidence is ultimately sought.

## The unified ("Unitarian") view

Construct, content, and criterion-related validity are **not three types of validity** — they are three **evidential bases** for justifying Inference 9:

- **Criterion-related** = empirically linking predictor and criterion (Inference 5); a *research strategy*, and properly a special case of **convergent construct evidence** — not inherently superior despite the *Uniform Guidelines* (1978) endorsement.
- **Content-related** = rational demonstration that the predictor samples the domain (Inference 6/9); "content-oriented test construction" (Tenopyr 1977).
- **Construct-related** = justifying Inferences 6 + 7 with convergent/discriminant evidence.

At its best (neither deficient nor contaminated) the criterion-related approach *reduces to* the content-related approach; at its worst it is atheoretical "dust-bowl empiricism."

## The criterion problem, relocated

The framework locates the **[[Criterion Problem|criterion problem]]** in a **truncated nomological network** — the routine neglect of Inferences 7, 8, 10, and 11 (Figure 3 adds Inference 10: job demands → performance domain; Inference 11: behavior-outcome links). Because criterion measures are rarely developed with predictor-level rigor (values shift, criteria are "dynamic," development is costly), Inference 8 is often weak — which *undermines* the meaning of any predictor validity coefficient. **Criteria must be validated analogously to predictors.**

> [!key-insight] Surprising corollary
> When Inference 8 is questionable, empirical evidence for Inference 5 becomes *less* relevant to Inference 9 — so **construct evidence can be superior to criterion-related evidence** for justifying selection decisions. The authors argue the field has "dramatically underestimated" how often this is true.

## Predictor vs performance domains

Predictor construct domains are *discovered* by research psychologists (naturally occurring behavioral covariance); **performance domains are created** by organizations (behavior-outcome units, differentially valued, goal-related). Campbell (1983): "the meaning of performance is not something to be 'discovered'; it should be imposed." This maps onto **[[Composite vs Multiple Criteria|composite vs multiple criterion]]** models (economic total worth vs behavioral dimensions) and job-oriented vs worker-oriented job analysis. See [[Predictor vs Performance Domain]].

## Agenda (Figure 4)

An elaborated nomological net for personnel research — alternative predictor/criterion measures (Inferences 12–18), a social-cognitive view of job analysis and criterion development, and a call to treat criterion development as rigorous theory-building. The behavioral scientist's role shifts from correlation-hunting to construct validation.
