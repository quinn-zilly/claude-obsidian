# Avolio, Avey & Quisenberry (2010) — Estimating Return on Leadership Development Investment

## Summary

Proposes and demonstrates a methodology — **Return on Development Investment (RODI)** — for quantifying the financial return of leadership development interventions. Uses Cascio & Boudreau's (2008) utility analysis framework combined with a meta-analysis of 133 leadership intervention studies (Avolio et al., 2009). The main argument: leadership development should be evaluated like any capital investment, not treated as a discretionary "nice to have."

> [!key-insight] Core Argument
> Only 10–20% of organizations investing in leadership development ever evaluate its effectiveness on performance outcomes. RODI analysis can change this, making development decisions evidence-based business decisions with quantifiable anticipated returns.

---

## RODI Formula

```
RODI = (N × T × d × SDy) − C
```

Where:
- **N** = number of participants
- **T** = time duration of behavioral change (in years; e.g., 8 weeks = 0.167)
- **d** = effect size (average difference between trained and untrained)
- **SDy** = SD of dollar-valued job performance among untrained employees ≈ **40% of annual salary** (conservative estimate)
- **C** = total cost of training

---

## Effect Size Foundation

From Avolio et al. (2009) meta-analysis of 133 studies:
- Average effect size: **d = 0.52** (leaders); **d = 0.25** (followers/indirect effects)
- Range: d = −0.15 (low) to d = 1.19 (high)
- Confidence intervals constrained to 50% of full range to avoid "outrageous" utility estimates (per Latham & Whyte, 1994 critique)
- Intervention lengths in meta-analysis: 1–7 days; authors model **1.5-day and 3-day** interventions

## Cost Structure (sample assumptions)

| Category | On-site | Off-site | Online |
|----------|---------|---------|--------|
| Time in salary + lost production | ~$3,600/person | ~$4,800/person | ~$3,600/person |
| Instructor + support | $6,000 | $6,000 | $6,500 |
| Travel (participants) | $0 | $13,000 (mid) | $0 |
| Total (30 upper + 100 mid leaders) | ~$27,900 | ~$61,900 | ~$21,100 |

---

## RODI Results (Average Effect Size)

**1.5-day intervention:**
- Upper leaders (n=30): 44–61% RODI depending on location
- Mid leaders (n=100): 46–76% RODI
- Followers: $116,900–$835,000 in additional return (indirect effects)

**3-day intervention:**
- Upper leaders: 50–64% RODI
- Mid leaders: 52–87% RODI

**Top performers only:**
- Average RODI jumps to **197–200%** (capped) for 3-day interventions
- Even at low effect sizes, positive RODI likely due to motivation/ability

RODI capped at 200% to balance linear model against dynamic performance realities.

## Individual Moderators of RODI

- **General mental ability (GMA)** — predicts performance; faster knowledge transfer → higher RODI
- **Self-efficacy** — more confident = more effort = higher RODI
- **Motivation to lead** (Chan & Drasgow, 2002) — distinct from training motivation; predicts application of leadership content specifically
- **Developmental readiness** (Maurer, 2002; Avolio & Hannah, 2008) — learning orientation + feedback orientation → better application → higher RODI
- **Span of control / influence potential** — top leaders affect more organizational facets → higher RODI

---

## Contextual Moderators of RODI

- **Transfer climate** — psychological safety for trying new techniques (Rogg et al., 2001)
- **Generalization of training across levels** — if only mid-level trained, upper-level supervisors don't support transfer → reduces RODI
- **Geographic colocation** — peer accountability for transfer increases RODI
- **Temporal proximity** — effects assumed for 8 weeks; "booster" activities could extend

---

## Limitations

1. Longitudinal data sparse in the meta-analysis; sustainability assumptions are speculative
2. Meta-analysis primarily U.S.-based; cross-cultural generalizability untested
3. Effects on followers-of-followers not well documented
4. Assumes linear relationship between intervention length and effect size (2× effect for 2× length)
5. Cost structures derived from large Fortune 500 organizations; small orgs may differ

---

## Strategic Implications

- RODI is best used as a **planning tool**, not a go/no-go trigger; non-financial returns (retention, culture, employer brand) may justify even negative RODI
- Sharing RODI projections with participants increases their engagement and transfer motivation
- Placing RODI in financial framing shifts leadership development from "cost" to "investment" in executive mindset
- Selecting **high performers and developmentally ready individuals** maximizes organizational RODI
