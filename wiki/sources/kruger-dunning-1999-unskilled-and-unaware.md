# kruger-dunning-1999-unskilled-and-unaware

Nav: [[index]] | [[Dunning-Kruger Effect]] | [[Calibration (Judgment)]]

> [!abstract] Source summary
> The paper that established the **[[Dunning-Kruger Effect]]**. Thesis: people who are unskilled in a domain suffer a **dual burden** — not only do they reach erroneous conclusions and make poor choices, but their **incompetence robs them of the metacognitive ability to realize it**. Because the skills that produce correct answers are often the same skills needed to *recognize* correct answers, the incompetent lack insight into their own incompetence. Opens with the McArthur Wheeler bank-robber anecdote (lemon juice as "invisibility") and Darwin's "ignorance more frequently begets confidence than does knowledge."

## Four predictions

1. Incompetent individuals will **dramatically overestimate** their ability and performance relative to objective criteria.
2. They will show **deficient metacognitive skill** — less able to recognize competence (their own or others').
3. They will be **unable to use social comparison** to gain insight (they can't recognize competence in others — see [[Social Comparison]]).
4. They **can** gain insight — but paradoxically, only by becoming **more competent**, which supplies the metacognitive skill to recognize prior poor performance.

## Four studies

Domains where knowledge/savvy matters: **humor** (Study 1), **logical reasoning** (Study 2), **grammar / American Standard Written English** (Study 3), and a training manipulation (Study 4).

| Finding | Result |
|---|---|
| Bottom-quartile self-estimate | ~62nd–66th percentile (perceived) vs. ~12th (actual) |
| Top-quartile pattern | **Underestimate** their percentile (e.g., 89th actual → ~70th estimated) |
| Study 3 Phase 2 ("it takes one to know one") | Bottom quartile shown better peers' work → **fail to recalibrate**; top quartile recalibrate upward (a **false-consensus** correction) |
| Study 4 (train logic) | Improving skill → **improved calibration** (metacognition granted) |

## Two asymmetric errors

> [!key-insight] Different sources of miscalibration
> The bottom quartile's miscalibration stems from an **error about the self** (a metacognitive deficit — they lack the skill to evaluate their own work). The top quartile's mild *under*estimation stems from an **error about others** — a **false-consensus effect**: skilled people wrongly assume tasks easy for them are easy for everyone, so they underrate their relative standing. Both converge on the "above-average effect" but for opposite reasons.
