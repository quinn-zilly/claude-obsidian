# EEOC (1978) — Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures

## What It Is

Joint federal regulation (EEOC, Civil Service Commission, DOL, DOJ) providing a **single uniform framework** for the proper use of tests and other selection procedures under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964, as amended 1972) and Executive Order 11246. Built on court decisions, prior agency guidelines, and psychological-profession standards (the 1974 APA Standards). Still authoritative (codified at 29 CFR 1607 / 41 CFR 60-3). Applies to any procedure used as basis for an **employment decision** (hiring, promotion, demotion, membership, referral, retention, licensing/certification; and training/transfer if they lead to those).

Does **not** apply to recruitment practices, seniority systems (mostly), ADEA age, or Rehabilitation Act handicap.

## Core Logic (§3–4)

**No validity study is required where there is no adverse impact** — but adverse impact triggers the duty to validate or drop the procedure.

- **Adverse impact = discrimination unless justified** (§3A). A procedure with adverse impact on any race/sex/ethnic group is discriminatory unless validated per the Guidelines. See [[Adverse Impact]].
- **Four-Fifths (80%) Rule** (§4D): a selection rate for any group < 4/5 of the highest group's rate is generally *evidence* of adverse impact. Smaller differences may still count if statistically + practically significant; larger differences may not if based on small/non-significant numbers. See [[Four-Fifths Rule]].
- **"Bottom line"** (§4C): if the *total* process shows no adverse impact, enforcement generally won't scrutinize individual components (with exceptions).
- **Suitable alternatives** (§3B): where two substantially equally valid procedures exist, use the one with **less adverse impact**.
- **Recordkeeping** (§4B): by sex and race/ethnic groups per EEO-1 categories.

## Three Validity Strategies (§5, §14)

See [[Three Validity Strategies (Uniform Guidelines)]]. Any validity study must rest on a **review of information about the job** — a **job analysis** (§14A) — except criterion-related may skip full JA per §14B(3).

1. **Criterion-related** (§14B) — empirical data showing procedure predicts / significantly correlates with important elements of job performance. Requires job analysis of criteria, representative sample, statistical significance (p ≤ .05), fairness study where feasible, guards vs capitalization on chance (cross-validation).
2. **Content validity** (§14C) — procedure is a representative sample of the job's content. Requires JA of critical/important work behaviors. **Not** appropriate for measuring traits/constructs (intelligence, aptitude, personality, judgment, leadership) or KSAs learned on the job.
3. **Construct validity** (§14D) — procedure measures an identifiable construct shown important to performance. Most complex/arduous; requires JA naming/defining the construct + criterion-related evidence linking construct to performance.

**No assumption of validity** (§9): reputation, promotional literature, usage frequency, testimonials — none substitute for empirical evidence. Thorough job analysis + professional development *enhances probability* of validity but is not itself proof.

## Other Key Provisions

- **Disparate treatment** (§11) — distinct from validation: a validated procedure still can't be imposed on one group but not others.
- **Transportability / borrowing validity** (§7) — may rely on other users' criterion-related studies if validity, **job similarity** (same major work behaviors, shown by job analyses), and fairness evidence are met.
- **Method of use** (§5G) — evidence sufficient for pass/fail may be insufficient for ranking; ranking with greater adverse impact needs stronger evidence.
- **Cutoff scores** (§5H) — set reasonable, consistent with acceptable workforce proficiency.
- **Fairness / unfairness** (§14B(8)) — differential prediction: lower scores for a group not reflected in job-performance differences = potential unfairness; investigate where feasible.
- **Affirmative action** (§13) — encouraged; validated procedures don't relieve AA obligations.

## Why It Matters (wiki context)

The Uniform Guidelines are the **legal spine of personnel selection**: they make **job analysis** legally mandatory as the foundation of any validity defense, and they operationalize discrimination via **adverse impact** and the **four-fifths rule**. Directly referenced by [[Sanchez-Levine-2009-competency-modeling-vs-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine (2009) — TJA vs CM]] (job-relatedness provisions push orgs to supplement CM with TJA) and by [[Garcia-Izquierdo-et-al-2015-work-analysis-personnel-selection|García-Izquierdo, Díaz Vilela & Moscoso (2015) — Work Analysis for Personnel Selection]] (WA as EEO safeguard). The three validity strategies mirror the psychometric [[Validity Typology]].

> [!note] Enforcement caveat
> The Guidelines have been criticized as scientifically dated relative to modern validity theory (unitary construct validity, validity generalization / meta-analysis — cf. [[Sackett-et-al-2022-validity-overcorrection]], [[binning-barrett-1989-validity-personnel-decisions]]). They remain the operative legal standard regardless.
