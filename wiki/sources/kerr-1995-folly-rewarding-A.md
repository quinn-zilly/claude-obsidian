# Kerr (1995) — On the Folly of Rewarding A, While Hoping for B

> [!key-insight] Core Claim
> Organizations systematically reward behavior A while hoping for behavior B. This is not exceptional — it is pervasive. The reward system determines behavior, not stated intentions.

Originally published 1975 in *Academy of Management Journal* (Vol. 18, 769–783). This is the 1995 AME updated reprint, designated an Academy Classic.

## Central Argument

Organisms (including humans) seek out what is rewarded and do that — often to the exclusion of unrewarded behavior. When formal reward systems misalign with desired behavior, rational actors follow the rewards, not the stated goals. The problem is not motivational; it is structural.

## Domains of Folly

### Politics
Officials want *operative* goals (specific, funded, actionable) but reward *official* goals (vague, inoffensive). Voters punish candor → politicians avoid specificity.

### War
Vietnam vs. WWII contrast. WWII: soldiers went home when war was won → obedience was rational. Vietnam: soldiers went home after tour regardless → disobedience was rational. Reward system caused the behavior gap.

### Medicine
Type 1 error (label well person sick) vs. Type 2 error (label sick person well). Malpractice risk punishes Type 2; Type 1 generates revenue and steady patients. System rewards over-diagnosis. Broward County example: psychiatrists paid $325 for incompetency ruling, $125 for competency → 570/598 proceedings ended in incapacitation.

### Universities
Research rewarded (tenure, raises, transferable CV); teaching not. Professors rationally neglect teaching. Students rewarded for grades not learning → plagiarism, grade-seeking rational.

### Consulting
HR departments avoid rigorous program evaluation because they championed the programs. Consultants collect anecdotes not data. Top management hopes for evaluation; reward system punishes it.

### Sports
Coaches praise teamwork; scouts and contracts reward individual stats. Rational players optimize scoring, not assists or sacrifice plays.

### Government
Cost-plus contracts and next-year budgets tied to this-year spending → rewards spending, not economy.

### Business (Insurance Claims)
Merit increases: 5% (outstanding), 4% (average), 3% (negligent). Tiny differential → indifference to performance. But absences > 3 in 6 months → forfeit raise. Result: attendance rewarded, not performance.

Whistleblower rewards (up to 30% of fraud amount, no cap) → rational to delay reporting and let fraud grow.

## Four Causes

| Cause | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| Fascination with objective criteria | Simple quantifiable metrics cause [[Goal Displacement]] when applied beyond predictable tasks |
| Overemphasis on visible behaviors | Publications > teaching; scoring > feeding teammates; visible errors > invisible ones |
| Hypocrisy | Rewarder actually *wants* A (not B) despite claiming otherwise (e.g., judges funded by defense attorneys) |
| Morality/equity over efficiency | Spreading costs of war, ADA, health reform — inefficiency rewarded in service of other values |

## Prescriptions

1. Audit what behaviors are *actually* being rewarded — managers are usually surprised.
2. Align formal reinforcement with desired behavior; don't rely on intrinsic motivation to overcome the reward system.
3. Note: some individuals will behave as desired despite misaligned rewards — but the rewarder is then merely a "fortunate bystander," not a causal agent.

## Postscript / Meta-Note

Kerr notes the irony: readers claim to enjoy and know the article's insights, yet organizations keep making the same errors. The principles are intuitive in personal life (birthday cake example, separate checks, lawn inspection) but neglected in organizations.

The article was nearly rejected from the 1975 AMJ — reviewers objected to tone, not content. Published only due to editor Jack Miner breaking a tie.
