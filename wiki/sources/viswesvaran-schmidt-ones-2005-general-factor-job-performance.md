# Viswesvaran, Schmidt & Ones (2005) — A General Factor in Job Performance Ratings

> [!abstract] One-line
> Pooling 90 years of intercorrelations among rated performance dimensions, and a measurement model that nets out four error sources, the authors show a **real construct-level [[General Factor in Job Performance|general factor]] (~60% of variance)** survives once [[Halo Error|halo]] is removed.

## The question

Factor analyses of ratings almost always find one big general factor. Two camps: (a) it's just [[Halo Error|halo error]] (Landy et al.); (b) it's partly a true positive manifold — same abilities/traits (g, conscientiousness) drive all dimensions (Feldman; Motowidlo; Hulin). You can't settle it without controlling **all four** rating error sources at once: halo error, rater leniency, random response error, and transient error.

## The method (the clever part)

Halo = a *psychological process*: each rater's idiosyncratic overall impression (the Rater × Ratee interaction) bleeds into every dimension rating. Its effects are **asymmetric**:

- **Inflates** within-rater reliabilities and within-rater interdimension correlations.
- **Deflates** between-rater reliabilities and between-rater interdimension correlations.

So: correct within-rater correlations with within-rater reliabilities (still halo-inflated), correct between-rater correlations with between-rater reliabilities (halo-cleaned). The **difference** isolates halo's inflationary effect at the construct (true-score / ratee-effect) level — apparently done here for the first time.

## Headline numbers

- General factor at construct level (halo-free) = **60% of total variance** → a real general factor exists.
- Halo inflation of construct-level interdimension correlations: **+33% for supervisors, +63% for peers**.
- Anchoring meta-analytic inputs (Viswesvaran et al. 1996): avg interrater reliability ≈ **.52**; avg rate–rerate (within-rater) reliability ≈ **.81** ⇒ random/transient error ≈ 19%; halo + rater effects ≈ 29% of single-rater variance.
- **Peer and supervisory ratings are construct-equivalent** — same dimensions underlie both; they differ only in amount of measurement error. The hypothesized "rater-level construct difference" is *disconfirmed*.

## Caveats the authors stress

- "Ratings-true scores" ≠ "true performance." The shared-across-raters component is *ratings-true* variance by classical test theory, but may correlate <1.0 with actual performance.
- **Shared halo** (an erroneous impression common to raters) could still inflate even between-rater corrected correlations vs the real world — evidence is conflicting; out of scope here.
- "True halo" is a misnomer; better term is *actual correlations*.

> [!key-insight]
> Halo isn't only an inflator. Because the idiosyncratic impression is uncorrelated *across* raters, it simultaneously **inflates** what one rater sees and **deflates** agreement *between* raters. That asymmetry is exactly what lets you subtract it out.
