# Ten Catalysts to Spark On-the-Job Development in Your Organization

**Yost, P. R., & Plunkett, M. M. (2010).** *Industrial and Organizational Psychology, 3*, 20–23.

> [!key-insight] Core Argument
> Most leadership development happens on the job, but organizations systematically under-leverage this. Traditional HR systems can be *drivers* for OJD rather than mere support systems. Gap = what we know vs. what we do.

## Summary

Commentary responding to McCall (2010) "Recasting Leadership Development." Yost & Plunkett argue it's premature to abandon HR systems — instead, reform them to hardwire [[On-the-Job Development]] (OJD) into organizational DNA.

## The Ten Catalysts

### 1. Build a Leadership Map
Expand competency models to four dimensions: **experiences, competencies, key relationships, learning agility/capabilities** (see [[Leadership Map]]). Publish taxonomy org-wide. Use for self-assessment, succession planning, dev planning.

### 2. Publish a List of OJD Experiences
Post on corporate intranet. Link to talent management processes. Existing lists: Lombardo & Eichinger (1988), McCall et al. (1988), McCauley (2006).

### 3. Help Leaders Capture the Lessons
Experiences alone ≠ development. Must identify: what lessons/competencies to take away, and specific actions to maximize learning (stretch assignments, learning goals, feedback-seeking).

### 4. Separate Goal Setting and Development Discussions
Combined perf+dev discussions = dev gets crowded out. Best practice: perf goals at year-start, dev goals at mid-year. Or two separate meetings.

### 5. Hardwire OJD Into Development Plans ([[70-20-10 Rule]])
Form cues: label each activity as OJD (70%), other people (20%), or training (10%). Link form to OJD experience list. Include Reader's Digest self-assessment of plan quality.

### 6. Require Development Plans
Track completion rates + quality. Audit random sample. High-potential plans get extra scrutiny. Development = business imperative in dynamic markets.

### 7. Turn Training into OJD
Challenge: design programs so 90% of development happens *outside* the classroom. Tools: action learning, inter-session modules, peer coaching, focus on transfer (reward systems, manager support).

### 8. Challenge Employees to Create a Personal Advisory Board
5–7 people as advisory network rather than single mentor (Higgins & Kram, 2001). See [[Personal Advisory Board]].

### 9. Focus on Transitions ([[Transition Checklist]])
Transitions = high-openness-to-learning moments. Serve dual purpose: rapid role effectiveness + self-regulatory skill development. Tool: transition checklist covering stakeholder meetings, success metrics, feedback-seeking, skill identification, direct report relationships.

### 10. Reward OJD
Reward systems must reinforce development alongside performance. Promote leaders who develop teams. Compensate those who navigate multiple critical experiences.
