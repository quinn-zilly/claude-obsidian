# Evaluating the Effectiveness of Performance Management: A 30-Year Integrative Conceptual Review

**Schleicher, D. J., Baumann, H. M., Sullivan, D. W., & Yim, J. (2019).** *Journal of Applied Psychology, 104*(7), 851–887.

> [!key-insight] Core Argument
> We cannot say whether PM "works" because the field never mapped **what "effective" even means**. Different traditions privileged different criteria (psychometric quality, employee reactions, firm performance) and never integrated them. Schleicher et al. build a single **comprehensive, multilevel model of PM evaluative criteria** and use it to code 30+ years (1984–2018) of empirical work — revealing the field has clustered on a few proximal criteria and left the **"value chains" linking PM to organizational outcomes** almost entirely untested.

## The model: a criterion chain across levels
See [[PM Effectiveness Value Chains]] for the full treatment. Adapting training-evaluation theory (Kirkpatrick 1987; Alliger et al. 1997; Kraiger, Ford & Salas 1993) and strategic-HR theory (AMO framework; Ployhart & Moliterno's human-capital-resource emergence), the model runs:

**Reactions → Learning → Transfer → [Emergence Enablers] → Unit-level Human Capital Resources → Operational & Financial Outcomes**

Crucially, criteria are tracked at **both** the employee/ratee **and** manager/rater levels:
- **Reactions** — affective, cognitive, utility, satisfaction (of employees *and* managers).
- **Learning** — cognitive, skills-based, attitudinal/motivational. **Rating quality/accuracy is reclassified here** — as a manifestation of *manager* learning (KSAOs from doing PM), not the end goal.
- **Transfer** — effects that carry back to the job: employee performance/attitudes; manager relational quality + quality of decisions about employees + general managerial effectiveness.
- **Emergence enablers** — unit climate/culture/leadership, trust in management, org learning, team cohesion, quality of human-capital decisions. These moderate whether individual gains aggregate upward.
- **Unit HCRs** (skills/abilities/potential + motivational capabilities) → **operational** (productivity, quality, safety, turnover) and **financial** (ROI, sales growth) outcomes.

## What the 488-article coding revealed
768 criterion instances across 544 studies. The literature overwhelmingly measures **proximal** criteria (reactions, rating quality) and almost never the **distal** unit/firm links or the **manager-side** learning/transfer criteria. The "black box" between PM practices and organizational performance is essentially unstudied.

## Three pressing questions + 16 propositions
The value-chain section is organized around three questions:

1. **How do individual-level outcomes of PM emerge into unit-level outcomes?** — via ability-based *and* motivation-based HCRs; emergence enablers (climate, trust) moderate whether they aggregate (Props 1–5).
2. **How essential are positive reactions to PM effectiveness?** — reactions predict learning/transfer but are likely **not a necessary condition** for them. Good feelings aren't the point (Props 6–12).
3. **What is the value of a performance rating?** — the rating's value may lie less in the *number* and more in what **managers learn by doing the rating** (Props 13–16). Eliminating ratings may *strengthen* the manager-learning→outcome link, but may *lower* the quality of human-capital decisions.

> [!key-insight] The reframe on ratings
> Where [[pulakos-2019-evolution-of-pm|Pulakos et al.]] ask "should we keep ratings?", Schleicher et al. ask "what is a rating *for*?" — and locate its latent value in **manager cognition and decision quality**, a criterion the field forgot to measure. This directly answers the rating debate with a research agenda rather than a yes/no.

## Relationship to the other two reviews
Explicitly extends [[denisi-murphy-2017-100-years-appraisal|DeNisi & Murphy (2017)]]'s eight heyday areas (which are mostly *independent variables* / practices) by building the missing **dependent-variable** side. Frames the whole exercise as a modern instance of the [[Criterion Problem]] (Austin & Villanova 1992). Shares Pulakos et al.'s skepticism about a stable cross-rater "true performance."
