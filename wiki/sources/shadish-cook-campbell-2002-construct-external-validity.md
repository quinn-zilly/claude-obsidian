# Shadish, Cook & Campbell (2002) — Construct Validity and External Validity

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]] | [[Open Science MOC]]

Chapter 3 of *Experimental and Quasi-Experimental Designs for Generalized Causal Inference*. Covers the two **generalization** validity types and closes with tradeoffs/priorities among all four.

## [[Construct Validity (Causal Generalization)]]

Validity of inferences from the **sampling particulars** of a study (the units, treatments, observations, settings actually used) to the **higher-order constructs** they represent. Grew out of psychological testing (Cronbach & Meehl 1955), extended by Cook & Campbell (1979) from outcomes to treatments, and here further to **persons and settings**.

> [!note] Two construct-validity traditions
> This is the **causal-generalization** sense — labeling the operations of an experiment. It differs in scope from the **psychometric / test-score-interpretation** sense on [[Construct Validity]] (Messick, Borsboom). Same term, related but distinct problem: naming study operations vs. defending a test-score interpretation.

Fostered by: clear construct explication → selecting matching instances → assessing slippage → revising. Definitional operationalism (a thing = only what its measure captures) is rejected.

### 14 threats to construct validity

1. **Inadequate Explication of Constructs**
2. **Construct Confounding** — operations embody more than the named construct.
3. **Mono-Operation Bias** — single operationalization underrepresents + adds irrelevancies.
4. **Mono-Method Bias** — shared method (e.g. all self-report) becomes part of the construct.
5. **Confounding Constructs with Levels of Constructs** — only some levels studied ("treatment-at-low-level had no effect").
6. **Treatment-Sensitive Factorial Structure** — measure's structure shifts due to treatment.
7. **Reactive Self-Report Changes** — motivation to be in a condition alters self-reports.
8. **Reactivity to the Experimental Situation** — demand characteristics, placebo, evaluation apprehension.
9. **Experimenter Expectancies** — Pygmalion / expectancy effects part of the treatment package.
10. **Novelty and Disruption Effects** — Hawthorne-type reactions to newness/disruption.
11. **Compensatory Equalization** — staff give control group extra services.
12. **Compensatory Rivalry** — "John Henry effect"; control group competes.
13. **Resentful Demoralization** — untreated group gives up, responds worse.
14. **Treatment Diffusion** — participants get the other condition's treatment.

## [[External Validity]]

Validity of inferences about whether the causal relationship **holds over variation** in persons, settings, treatments, and outcomes — both those in the study and those not. SCC side with **Cronbach**: generalization to as-yet-unstudied instances is a legitimate scientific question.

### Five threats to external validity

1. **Interaction of the Causal Relationship with Units** — effect differs across kinds of units.
2. **Interaction with Treatment Variations** — effect differs across treatment variants/combinations/partial doses.
3. **Interaction with Outcomes** — effect on one outcome measure not another.
4. **Interaction with Settings** — effect differs across settings.
5. **Context-Dependent Mediation** — the mediating mechanism differs across contexts.

## Tradeoffs and priorities

Construct/external validity concern [[Causal Generalization]] (representation + extrapolation across all study elements). Design choices trade validity types off against each other; there is no ranking valid for all studies — priority depends on the research question. See [[Threats to Validity]].
