# Leading Diversity: Towards a Theory of Functional Leadership in Diverse Teams

Nav: [[index]] | [[Leadership MOC]] | [[Teams & Org Behavior MOC]]

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> An integrative conceptual review (*JAP* 2020) that solves a puzzle: why does the same leadership behavior help diverse teams in some studies and hurt them in others? The answer is **[[Functional Team Leadership Theory|functional matching]]** — diversity instigates one of two team processes ([[Categorization-Elaboration Model|intergroup bias vs information elaboration]]), each with different leadership *needs*. The **Leading Diversity (LeaD)** model says effective diversity leaders (a) know the processes, (b) master both [[Complementary vs Supplementary Matching|person- and task-focused]] behaviors, and (c) have the [[Diversity-Related Leader Competencies|competencies]] to predict, diagnose, and match behavior to need — proactively and reactively.

## The problem it solves

Leaders are widely treated as diversity managers, but the field lacked a dynamic, comprehensive theory of the diversity × leadership interplay. Homan et al. reviewed **44 empirical papers** (from ~500 hits) crossing many diversity dimensions with **30+ leadership behaviors/characteristics**. Four conclusions:

1. Team diversity rarely has consistent direct effects on processes/outcomes — corroborating the Van Dijk et al. (2012) meta-analysis; effects are moderated, so moderators like leadership matter.
2. A large variety of leadership styles has been studied; **transformational leadership** is the most-studied but its moderating effects are **inconsistent** (positive, negative, and null).
3. Leader *behaviors* and leader *characteristics/abilities* have been studied in isolation, hiding potential synergies.
4. Leaders act both as **proactive shapers** (first-stage moderation: diversity → process) and **reactive managers** (second-stage moderation: process → outcome); reactive leadership is under-studied.

The "idiosyncratic" scatter of one-off combinations can't yield general conclusions. LeaD supplies the guiding framework.

## Setting the stage: two diversity processes

Grounded in the **[[Categorization-Elaboration Model]]** (Van Knippenberg, De Dreu & Homan, 2004), all diversity types can trigger either:

- **Intergroup bias** (negative) — subgroup categorization → ingroup/outgroup favoritism, conflict, distrust, limited communication.
- **Information elaboration** (positive) — exchange, processing, and integration of a richer pool of task-relevant information → creativity, better decisions.

The two are negatively related; **one is typically dominant at a given time**. Diversity is not carved into "surface-level = bad / deep-level = good" — evidence doesn't support that; *any* dimension can go either way depending on moderators (and on members being aware of the difference).

## The LeaD model

Central tenet: emerging/existing diversity-related processes and leadership behaviors **interact** to determine team performance. LeaD has three moving parts.

### 1. Leader behaviors — person- vs task-focused

Following the parsimonious two-category consensus (Burke et al., 2006; Fleishman et al., 1991; [[morgeson-et-al-2010-leadership-in-teams-functional|Morgeson et al., 2010]]; Yukl):

- **Task-focused**: structuring, goal direction, feedback, rewards, accountability (initiating structure, contingent reward, directive, intellectual stimulation).
- **Person-focused**: support, consideration, conflict management, trust/cohesion building, charisma, positive [[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)|LMX]].

These are **distinct but not mutually exclusive** (Judge et al. 2004 meta *r* ≈ .17) — but rarely deployable *simultaneously*.

### 2. The matching principle

See [[Complementary vs Supplementary Matching]]:

| Dominant process | Effective behavior | Match type |
|---|---|---|
| **Intergroup bias** | Person-focused | **Complementary** (supply what the team lacks: relational bonding) — via recategorization, decategorization/individuation, reducing identity threat (**P4**) |
| **Information elaboration** | Task-focused | **Supplementary** (add to what's working: structure task exchange) — via task structure, feedback, accountability/epistemic motivation (**P5**) |

The off-diagonal cells are **mismatches**: task-focus during bias can backfire (people reject info from disliked outgroups); person-focus during elaboration is redundant or fuels groupthink / hidden-profile convergence. This matching logic explains the field's contradictions — e.g., Klein et al. (2011, cooperative nonprofit) vs Homan & Greer (2013, competitive for-profit) found opposite person-focused-leadership effects because the *teams had different dominant processes*.

### 3. Diversity-related competencies

See [[Diversity-Related Leader Competencies]]. To match, leaders must predict or diagnose the process and adapt:

- **Cognitive understanding** → *predict* the future dominant process (proactive). Cues: faultlines vs cross-categorization, reward structure, team history, diversity climate. Antecedents: multicultural experience, cultural intelligence. (**P1**)
- **Social perceptiveness** → *diagnose* the current dominant process (reactive). Cues: seating/body language and heated affect (bias) vs task focus, clarifying questions, explicit task conflict (elaboration). Antecedents: openness to experience, emotional intelligence. (**P2**)
- **Behavioral flexibility** → *match* behavior to need. Antecedents: interpersonal flexibility, openness. (**P3**)

## Proactive, reactive, and temporal dynamics

- **Proactive** diversity leadership shapes *which* process arises (prevent categorization / invite elaboration) — first-stage moderation.
- **Reactive** diversity leadership manages a process already active (categorization can fire automatically) — second-stage moderation.
- **Behavioral dynamism**: needs change, so leaders must shift over time. **P6a** (anticipated events → predict → proactively shift), **P6b** (unanticipated events → diagnose → reactively shift). Even *successful* matching creates a new need (once bias is solved, drop person-focus and add task-focus).

## Propositions (compact)

- **P1–P3**: cognitive understanding → prediction; social perceptiveness → diagnosis; behavioral flexibility → matching.
- **P4**: greater intergroup bias → better performance with relatively more person-focused (fewer task-focused) behaviors.
- **P5**: greater information elaboration → better performance with relatively more task-focused (fewer person-focused) behaviors.
- **P6a / P6b**: prediction (anticipated) / diagnosis (unanticipated) events drive proactive / reactive behavioral shifting.

## Implications

- **Testing**: experiments (manipulate process via subgroup salience/info distribution; manipulate leadership via confederates/video), quasi-experiments (competency → prediction/diagnosis), longitudinal field and diary/coding studies for temporal dynamics; be explicit about diversity as separation/variety/disparity (Harrison & Klein, 2007).
- **Practice**: recruit/select leaders for diversity-related traits (time abroad, diversity education); train the three *competencies* rather than broad, often-ineffective diversity training; 360-feedback to build cognitive understanding. Moves the field from stable "styles" toward **trainable competencies**.
- **Scope**: extends [[Categorization-Elaboration Model|CEM]] by adding second-stage (reactive) moderation; scales up to organization-level diversity management (no one-size-fits-all interventions).

## Connections

- Applies **[[Functional Team Leadership Theory]]** (leadership = meeting team needs; McGrath, Hackman & Walton) to the specific need created by diversity.
- Builds directly on the [[Categorization-Elaboration Model]] and the [[Daan van Knippenberg|van Knippenberg]] diversity tradition.
- The person/task-focused split is the same two-factor behavioral structure underlying the [[Integrated Trait-Behavioral Model of Leadership|DeRue et al. (2011)]] taxonomy and the [[Leader Behavior and Ohio State Studies|Ohio State]] consideration/initiating-structure distinction.
- On **[[Transformational and Charismatic Leadership|transformational leadership]]**: LeaD reframes TL's inconsistent diversity effects as a symptom of unmodeled team needs — a [[Lumped vs Split Conceptualizations of Leading|lumped construct]] hiding heterogeneous mechanisms (cf. [[carton-2022-science-of-leadership|Carton, 2022]]).

> [!note] Gender/diversity contrast with Fletcher
> Homan et al. treat leadership of difference as a **competency/matching** problem — solvable by trained, flexible leaders adjusting behavior to team needs. [[fletcher-2004-paradox-postheroic-leadership|Fletcher (2004)]] argues the relational/collaborative leadership these models valorize is covertly **gendered and power-laden**, so behavioral prescriptions alone can be co-opted. See [[Postheroic Leadership]] for the tension.
