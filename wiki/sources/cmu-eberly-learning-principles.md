# CMU Eberly Center — Learning Principles

> [!key-insight] Core claim
> Seven research-distilled principles govern effective student learning, spanning cognition, motivation, development, and metacognition.

**Source:** [Eberly Center, Carnegie Mellon University](https://www.cmu.edu/teaching/principles/learning.html)
**Type:** Institutional research synthesis
**Audience:** Higher education instructors

## The Seven Principles

| # | Principle | Core concept |

| 1 | Prior knowledge helps or hinders | [[Prior Knowledge]] |
| 2 | Knowledge organization shapes retrieval | [[Knowledge Organization]] |
| 3 | Motivation determines learning behavior | [[Motivation and Learning]] |

| 6 | Developmental level × classroom climate | [[Classroom Climate]] |
| 7 | Self-directed learning requires metacognition | [[Metacognition and Self-Directed Learning]] |

## Key Takeaways

- Prior knowledge is double-edged: robust and accurate → foundation; inert, insufficient, or inaccurate → interference.
- Knowledge structure matters as much as content: random connections impede retrieval.
- Motivation is tripartite: **value** × **expectancy** × **perceived environmental support**.
- Mastery ≠ knowing components; it requires fluency in *integration* and knowing *when* to apply.
- Practice quality > quantity: must target appropriate challenge level + be coupled with explicit, timely feedback.
- Classroom climate is shapeable by instructors and has documented effects on performance; negative climate impedes, positive climate energizes.
- Metacognition is not natural — students must be explicitly taught to monitor and adjust.
