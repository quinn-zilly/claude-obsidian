# LePine et al. 2008 — Teamwork Processes Meta-Analysis

Nav: [[index]] | [[sources/_index]]

## Core Argument

Teamwork processes form a **three-level hierarchical structure**: 10 narrow processes → 3 intermediate dimensions (transition, action, interpersonal) → 1 general teamwork process factor. All processes have positive, broadly similar relationships with team performance and member satisfaction. Relationships are stronger when task interdependence is high and teams are larger.

## Background

Problem: team process research fragmented — some studies use narrow constructs without considering redundancy, others use broad undifferentiated measures. Neither approach has been rigorously justified. Solution: meta-analytically test the Marks et al. (2001) hierarchical taxonomy.

Sample: 138 studies, 1,507 correlations from 147 independent samples. PsycINFO search + reference chains + researcher contacts. Coding ICC = .93.

## The Marks et al. (2001) Taxonomy

**Definition**: teamwork processes = "members' interdependent acts that convert inputs to outcomes through cognitive, verbal, and behavioral activities directed toward organizing taskwork to achieve collective goals" (p. 357).

Organized around **performance episodes** — distinguishable periods of time over which work is performed and evaluated.

### Transition Processes (between episodes)
Reflect and interpret past; prepare for future.
1. **Mission analysis** — identify/evaluate team tasks, challenges, resources
2. **Goal specification** — identify and prioritize team goals
3. **Strategy formulation and planning** — develop courses of action, contingency plans

### Action Processes (during episodes)
Active work toward accomplishment.
4. **Monitoring progress toward goals** — attend to, interpret, communicate progress information
5. **Systems monitoring** — track team resources and environmental factors
6. **Team monitoring and backup behavior** — assist teammates (feedback, direct help, compensation for struggling members); overlaps cooperation, workload sharing, group-level OCB
7. **Coordination** — synchronize activities by sequence and timing

### Interpersonal Processes (ongoing, both during and between episodes)
8. **Conflict management** — proactively/reactively manage conflict; mutual respect, compromise, harmony norms
9. **Motivating and confidence building** — develop/maintain member motivation and collective confidence
10. **Affect management** — foster emotional balance, togetherness, coping with stress

## Structural Findings: CFA

Three-factor model (transition, action, interpersonal) fit best: χ²(32)=462.85, NFI=.96, CFI=.96, SRMR=.043 — superior to one-factor and all two-factor alternatives.

Second-order model: three dimensions load onto single general teamwork process factor with strong, significant factor loadings (.81–1.04). Average true correlation among first-order factors = .83 → supports higher-order factor.

Key implication: **narrow processes show strong inter-correlations** (Table 1, all positive, range ~.41–.87). Common inputs and emergent states drive co-occurrence across process types.

## Relationships with Team Effectiveness (Tables 3–4)

### Narrow processes → Team performance (ρ range):
| Process | ρ perf | ρ sat |
|---|---|---|
| Mission analysis | .27 | .32 |
| Goal specification | .34 | .36 |
| Strategy formulation | .35 | .38 |
| Monitoring progress | .25 | .30 |
| Systems monitoring | .17 | .29 |
| Team monitoring | .30 | .29 |
| Coordination | .29 | .34 |
| Conflict management | .26 | .32 |
| Motivation | .34 | .41 |
| Affect management | .30 | .47 |

### Broad processes → Team performance & satisfaction:
| Process | ρ perf | ρ sat |
|---|---|---|
| Transition | .29 | .45 |
| Action | .29 | .46 |
| Interpersonal | .29 | .37 |
| Overall | .31 | .43 |

**Key finding**: relationships remarkably similar across process types and levels of specificity — no significant differences. 1 SD increase in overall process → >1/3 SD increase in performance, ~1/2 SD increase in satisfaction.

### Emergent States (Table 5)
| Process | ρ cohesion | ρ potency |
|---|---|---|
| Transition | .60 | .63 |
| Action | .61 | .65 |
| Interpersonal | .53 | .70 |

Processes strongly related to cohesion and potency — emergent states are both antecedents and outcomes of processes in the IPO/IMOI framework.

## Moderators of Process–Performance Relationships (Table 6)

- **Task interdependence**: B=.02, p<.05 → stronger process–performance links when interdependence higher
- **Team size**: B=.03, p<.05 → stronger links in larger teams
- Neither moderates process–satisfaction links (insufficient studies)
- Methodological moderators (common source, time lag, lab/field) collectively non-significant for performance; common source inflates effect sizes somewhat (B=.17)

## Key Implications

**For researchers**: match level of process specificity to level of theory. Narrow process → narrow measure; broad process → broad measure (Fishbein & Ajzen 1974 compatibility principle). Time-based designs needed — simultaneous assessment of all processes conflates temporally distinct phenomena and inflates correlations via halo.

**For practitioners**: process assessment provides diagnostic leverage. Knowing *which* dimension fails (transition = planning breakdowns; action = coordination failures; interpersonal = relationship undermining) allows targeted intervention rather than generic team building.

**For intervention science**: directly links to [[Team Development Interventions]] — transition process deficits → [[Team Building]] (role clarification, goal-setting); action/cognitive process deficits → [[Team Training]] (cross-training, coordination training, CRM).

## Limitations

- Meta-analytic estimates inherit primary research problems
- Few studies on within-process-category inter-relationships → cannot fully distinguish narrow facets
- Moderate-to-wide credibility intervals on some estimates → substantial cross-study variability
- Interdependence/team size coded as study-level averages — within-study variability ignored
