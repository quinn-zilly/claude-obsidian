# Campbell & Wiernik (2015) — The Modeling and Assessment of Work Performance

> [!abstract] One-line
> The canonical Annual Review synthesis of individual work performance: it argues the field's many performance models converge on a single near-consensus latent structure ([[Campbell Model of Job Performance|Campbell's eight factors]]), then works through performance dynamics, adaptability, and assessment.

## Framing: What Performance Is / Is Not

Individual job performance = **things people actually do — actions they take — that contribute to organizational goals**, scaled by proficiency. Deliberately distinguished from:

- **Determinants** (traits, knowledge, skill, motivation, situation) — these affect performance only through three real-time direct determinants: role-specific knowledge, skill, and choice behavior (direction/intensity/duration of effort). Knowledge and skill are *not* performance.
- **Outcomes / results** (sales, stock price, ROI, salary) — these are contaminated by factors outside the individual's control. An outcome is only a performance measure if it is almost entirely under individual control.
- Also ≠ efficiency/productivity (input–output ratios), development, attrition, or promotion.

This "actions, not results" stance is the article's methodological spine and drives its assessment recommendations.

## The Near-Consensus Latent Structure

Since the 1980s (Project A being the milestone empirical effort), performance-modeling streams have converged. Once the catalog is purged of (a) content-free labels (problem solving, creativity), (b) metrics not behavior (quantity, quality, productivity), and (c) determinants (KSAOs), the models agree. Campbell (2012) codifies this as **eight factors** — see [[Campbell Model of Job Performance]]. The structure is asserted **invariant across organizational levels, functional specialties, industries, and cultures** (a "universal competency model").

Building blocks folded into the consensus:
- **Project A** — 5-factor (enlisted) / 6-factor (NCO) solutions, robust across occupations and cohorts.
- **Core task vs. [[Contextual Performance]]** (Borman & Motowidlo 1993/97).
- **[[Organizational Citizenship Behavior]]** (Organ 1988) — ~complete overlap with contextual performance subfactors.
- **[[Counterproductive Work Behavior]]** (CWB) — distinct dimension, not the negative pole of OCB.
- Griffin et al. (2007) proficiency×adaptivity×proactivity 3×3; competency models (Great Eight, etc.).

## General Factor & Compound Domains

- A **[[General Factor in Job Performance|general factor]]** appears in virtually all performance covariance matrices, but it is **formative, not reflective** — a weighted sum of components, not a single latent variable. Differential validity across subfactors persists beneath it.
- **Compound domains** (e.g., environmental/"green" performance, IT performance) cut across several of the eight factors around a shared goal; useful to study together but not separate from the latent structure.

## Performance Dynamics & Adaptability

Performance changes over time — from changing requirements, from individuals changing, or simply with the passage of time (a surrogate for practice, aging, affect). Growth curves can be linear/nonlinear, occupation-characteristic or individual-differential (Dalal et al. 2014 on within-person variability). **Adaptability** is contested: either a component of performance (Pulakos et al. 2000, eight adaptability factors) or a property of the adaptive individual (Ployhart & Bliese 2006). Campbell & Wiernik lean toward **dimension-specific** dynamics: "performance is not one thing, and neither is adaptability."

## Assessment

Assessment is hard *because* performance is defined as behavior — ruling out contaminated outcome criteria. Five distinct **purposes** each shape method: research (construct validity paramount), legal defense (reliability paramount), high-stakes appraisal (fairness/transparency), feedback & development (concrete, specific, avoid global evaluations), self-managed improvement. Any comprehensive measure must cover all eight factors at some level of specificity. Typical vs. maximum performance is a key general measurement issue.

## My Notes

> [!key-insight] The "actions not outcomes" rule
> This is the most portable idea in the paper: hold individuals accountable only for behaviors under their control, never for outcome variance driven by other factors. It quietly indicts using sales/ROI as performance criteria and reframes much of the appraisal literature.

> [!note] Bridge to CORE
> [[marshall-aguinis-beltran-2024-theories-of-performance|Marshall et al. (2024)]] cite Campbell & Wiernik as the individual-level exemplar of the performance literature. Campbell's behavioral content model nests inside the individual-level slice of the [[CORE Model of Performance]]; both reviews independently argue for cross-level *invariance* of performance's latent structure.
