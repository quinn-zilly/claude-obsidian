# Micelotta et al. 2017 — Pathways of Institutional Change

## Summary

An integrative review of 119 empirical articles on institutional change (1990–2015), followed by a novel typology that organizes this fragmented literature. Reviews three chronological research streams — exogenous shock approaches, institutional entrepreneurship, and practice-based/microfoundations approaches — then develops a 2×2 framework using **scope** (transformational vs. developmental) × **pace** (revolutionary vs. evolutionary) to define four distinct pathways. Leverages the institutional logics perspective as a meta-theory throughout.

## Core Contribution: The 2×2 Typology

The framework classifies institutional change along two dimensions:

- **Scope (x-axis):** Transformational (fundamental shift in shared understandings, logics overturned) vs. Developmental (stretching existing arrangements without discarding them — contraction, assimilation, or elaboration of logics)
- **Pace (y-axis):** Revolutionary (fast, disruptive, driven by shocks or mobilization) vs. Evolutionary (slow, cumulative, persuasive embedding)

This yields four pathways — see [[Institutional Change Pathways]] for full detail.

## Three Research Streams Reviewed

### 1. Exogenous Changes in Institutional Environments (pre-agentic turn)
Institutions as external, durable structures. Change triggered by "jolts" — political regime shifts, competitive shocks, regulatory upheaval, technological disruption. Three sub-streams:
- Population ecology predictions: organizations can't adapt fast enough → selection → disappearance of forms
- Adaptive organizations: metamorphosis of strategies/structures under disruption
- Logic shifts: Thornton & Ocasio (1999) — replacement of one dominant logic by another

### 2. Institutional Entrepreneurship (the "agentic turn," 1990s–2000s)
The "paradox of embedded agency" (Holm 1995): how can actors embedded in taken-for-granted arrangements challenge them? Focus on:
- *Who* are entrepreneurs: structural position (periphery vs. core of field; Leblebici et al. 1991; Greenwood & Suddaby 2006), "institutional voids" (Mair & Marti 2009)
- *How* change is introduced: cultural entrepreneurship (Lounsbury & Glynn 2001); bricolage, robust design, frames, theorization (nondisruptive); framing contests, protests, boycotts (disruptive)
- Key blind spot: change conflated with *successful* change; the institutionalization step understudied; "heroic actor" problem

### 3. Practice-Based / Microfoundations (post-2008)
Bottom-up change from mundane day-to-day practice improvisations. Smets et al. (2012) canonical example: new practices introduced unobtrusively within a firm "moved up" to field level and shifted logics — no exogenous shock, no heroic entrepreneur. Key challenge: linking micro-level improvisations to field-level change.

## Key Critiques of Prior Literature

1. **Linear causality assumption:** displacement/alignment dominate because scholars assume exogenous shock → transformation and evolutionary process → modest change. Nonlinear pathways (accommodation, accretion) are understudied.
2. **Neglect of temporality:** pace, sequence, duration rarely examined
3. **Neglect of institutional pluralism:** most theory developed for monistic environments; fields are actually pluralistic (multiple coexisting logics)
4. **Embedded agency paradox unresolved:** purposeful vs. pragmatic agency remains theoretically murky
5. **Missing developmental scope:** almost all change assumed to be transformational; developmental variants ignored

## Research Agenda (per quadrant)

- **Q1 (Displacement):** How does field-level logic multiplicity moderate organizational responses to revolutionary change? How does institutional complexity at org level shape interpretation?
- **Q2 (Alignment):** How does pluralism facilitate/hinder innovation embedding? How do change agents craft legitimation strategies for heterogeneous audiences?
- **Q3 (Accommodation):** How is logic competition/coexistence constructed and negotiated on the ground? What forms do settlements take in pluralistic environments?
- **Q4 (Accretion):** Under what conditions do micro-level improvisations stimulate field-level transformation? Which organizational responses to complexity feed back to the field vs. remain local?

## Methodological Calls

- ~70% of reviewed studies used qualitative methods; strong historical field studies and embedded case studies
- Calls for variance theory / quantitative approaches to re-enter this domain
- Field-level ethnography (Lounsbury & Kaghan 2001; Zilber 2015) as promising bridge method
- More systematic comparative research across organizations in same field
