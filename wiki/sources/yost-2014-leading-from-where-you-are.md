# Yost (2014) — Leading from Where You Are

**Source:** Ch. 6 in *Using Experience to Develop Leadership Talent*, McCauley & McCall (Eds.), Jossey-Bass, 2014.

## Core Argument

HR and I-O professionals rarely control the whole organization. They must lead from the middle — launching OJD initiatives inside a single HR process, geography, or employee group. The secret is to **design those initiatives as catalysts** that can spread virally through the talent management system, rather than waiting for a top-down mandate that may never come.

> "Leading from where you are means building on-the-job development initiatives in a way that can 'go viral' and 'infect' the talent management processes and systems throughout the organization."

## Five Design Characteristics for Viral OJD

See [[Five Design Characteristics (OJD)]] for full treatment.

| Characteristic | Core Question |
|---|---|
| **Strategic** | Does it align with and support the business strategy? |
| **Simple** | Is it easy to use? Does it avoid HR-speak? |
| **Sneeze-able** | Do people want to use AND pass it on? |
| **Systemic** | Can it plug into other HR processes? |
| **Sustainable** | Will it outlast you? Is it self-adapting? |

## Illustrative Example: Executive On-Boarding Checklist

A transition checklist (Exhibit 6.1) demonstrates all five characteristics:
- **Strategic**: frames OJD as driving results, not HR paperwork
- **Simple**: one page; business language ("how will your success be measured?" not "set performance goals")
- **Sneeze-able**: standalone, first-person, items leaders would be "stupid to ignore"
- **Systemic**: language broad enough to work at multiple levels and in new-employee orientation, manager training, etc.
- **Sustainable**: pilot in one business unit → advocate network → self-propagating

## Three Talent Management Integration Points

### 1. Succession Management

Natural signal for valued experiences. Key moves:
- Include developmental experience ratings in succession candidate profiles (start-ups, turnarounds, global, cross-functional)
- Define experiences broadly so they cascade to lower levels
- Create an Experience × Lessons Matrix (Figure 6.1) — post on intranet (simple, sneeze-able)
- Succession discussion covers future strategy + needed experiences (strategic)

### 2. Performance Management

Development plans are the broadest-reach process but are often the most maligned. Remedies:
- Add specific lists of on-the-job development ideas by challenge type (Exhibit 6.3) — standalone PDF link in the form itself (systemic, simple, sneeze-able)
- Reference the **70–20–10 Rule** in instructions
- Define experiences by proficiency level (limited/moderate/deep) so leaders self-match (Exhibit 6.4)
- Separate conversations: one for performance goals, one for development goals (even if same meeting)
- Note: 70–20–10 percentages may not hold for women and minorities, for whom "other people" plays a larger developmental role (Douglas 2003; Van Velsor & Hughes-James 1990)

### 3. People, Networks, and Communities of Practice

Informal learning is more effective than formal mentoring (Chao et al. 1992; Ragins & Cotton 1999). Design formal systems that mimic informal characteristics:
- Mentor pools (mentee chooses)
- Peer coaching embedded in training programs
- Affinity groups for underrepresented employees
- **Communities of Practice** (Wenger & Snyder 2000): "groups of people informally bound together by shared expertise and passion for joint enterprise"

CoP sustainability conditions (McDermott & Archibald 2010; Kirkman et al. 2011):
- Senior leader sponsorship (strategic)
- Pilot groups as models (simple, sneeze-able, systemic)
- Self-sustaining structures with shared leadership (sustainable)
- Easy on-boarding for outsiders (sneeze-able)

## Theoretical Anchoring

- **Small wins** (Weick 1984): optimal small wins = discrete, self-contained, high P(success) → build on each other iteratively
- **Minority influence** (Hogg 2010; Sharp 2010): confident, consistent, sustained minority voices produce change
- **Sustained interventions** (Yost et al. 2011): 16 senior HR interviews; interventions lasting 10+ years; proactive middle-out leadership > passive top-down wait

## Key Tensions

> [!key-insight] Bottom-up vs. top-down
> Top-down approaches underestimate middle-org actors AND depend on stable leadership (CEO tenure has dropped; ~40% leave within 1.8 years). A viral, catalyst-designed initiative survives leadership turnover; a top-down mandate does not.

> [!key-insight] Sneeze-ability paradox
> The most comprehensive tools are often the least sneeze-able. A 10-page on-boarding guide won't spread. A one-page checklist that leaders "would be stupid to ignore" will.
