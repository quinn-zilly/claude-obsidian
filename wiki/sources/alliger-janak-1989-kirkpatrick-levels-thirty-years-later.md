# Alliger & Janak 1989 — Kirkpatrick's Levels, Thirty Years Later

Nav: [[index]] | [[sources/_index|Sources]]

## One-Line Summary

The first systematic critique and empirical test of the three **implicit assumptions** underlying [[Kirkpatrick Four-Level Model]] — showing, from a small literature-derived correlation set, that the levels are *not* neatly hierarchical and that reactions are largely independent of the other levels.

## The Three Problematic Assumptions

Kirkpatrick (1959a–1960b) proposed four "steps" of evaluation criteria: **Reactions, Learning, Behavior, Results**. Over 30 years these hardened (often implicitly) into a "hierarchical model" carrying three assumptions the authors argue Kirkpatrick himself likely never intended:

1. **Ascending information value** — each higher level is more informative than the last (leading many to treat Level 4 / the "dollar criterion" as invariably "best"). But some training legitimately targets only reactions (morale, company pride) or only learning (knowledge of history/philosophy).
2. **Causal linkage** — reactions → learning → behavior → results (Hamblin 1974). The authors challenge the reactions→learning link on temporal and empirical grounds: reactions and learning are often measured at the same time, and liking a program need not cause learning (humorous lectures are liked more but do not teach more; challenging/unpleasant training may raise learning while lowering reactions → possible *negative* correlation).
3. **Positive manifold** — all levels positively intercorrelated. Studies routinely report different training effects at different levels, indirect evidence against strong positive intercorrelation.

## Proposed Alternative Causal Model (Figure 1)

Rather than a strict ascending chain, the authors propose: **Level 1 (Reactions) is essentially unrelated** to the others; **Level 2 (Learning)** partly drives Levels 3 and 4; and **Levels 3 (Behavior) and 4 (Results) are causally interdependent** (results feed back to sustain behavior).

## Method — Literature-Based Correlations

Citation search on Kirkpatrick (1969–present via SSCI) yielded 55 articles; only 8 evaluated a program and none reported inter-level correlations. A broader journal review (AMJ, JABS, JAP, PP, Personnel, TDJ) found **203 training-evaluation articles** — most reporting only a *single* level. After following reference trails, the final usable set was just **12 articles / 26 correlations**. Most studies that *could* have reported inter-level correlations did not — suggesting the hierarchical model was simply *assumed* correct.

## Key Results (sample-size-weighted mean r)

| Pair | Weighted mean r |
|---|---|
| Reactions–Learning (1–2) | **.07** |
| Reactions–Behavior (1–3) | **.05** |
| Reactions–Results (1–4) | .48 (single correlation) |
| Learning–Behavior (2–3) | **.13** |
| Learning–Results (2–4) | .40 |
| Behavior–Results (3–4) | .19 |

Reactions correlate only slightly with other levels; Levels 2–4 are somewhat larger but still modest. Sampling error accounts for <15% of the variance among all correlations, so the spread is not merely artifactual. Reduced variance of reaction measures may deflate some correlations, but not uniformly.

> [!key-insight]
> Liking training tells you almost nothing about whether people learned, changed behavior, or produced results. The pervasive practitioner habit of evaluating only reactions rests on an assumed hierarchy the data do not support.

## Caveats the Authors Raise

- Very small number of correlations → generalize cautiously.
- Same-level criteria may disagree (e.g., behavior rated by superiors vs peers vs subordinates).
- Kirkpatrick's model may "never have been meant to be more than a first, global heuristic" — and as such "has done well."

## Significance / Lineage

This is the **direct predecessor** to [[alliger-et-al-1997-training-criteria-meta-analysis]], which expanded the correlation set (34 studies / 115 correlations) and introduced the [[Augmented Training Criteria Framework]] splitting reactions into affective vs utility and learning into sub-types. See [[Kirkpatrick Level Intercorrelation Critique]] for the consolidated critique across both papers.
