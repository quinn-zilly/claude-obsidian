# Munafò et al. (2017) — A Manifesto for Reproducible Science

> [!key-insight] Core Claim
> Reproducibility failures are systemic and cultural, not merely technical. Five categories of reform — methods, reporting, reproducibility infrastructure, evaluation, and incentives — each targeting specific mechanisms, are collectively necessary and individually feasible.

## Bibliographic Details

Marcus R. Munafò et al. "A Manifesto for Reproducible Science." *Nature Human Behaviour* 1 (2017): 0021. Open Access (CC BY 4.0).

10 authors from 9 institutions across UK, USA, and Netherlands. Published in the inaugural volume of *Nature Human Behaviour*.

## Summary

### Diagnosis: Five Threats to Reproducibility

The paper identifies five major threats, mapped onto the hypothetico-deductive research cycle (Fig. 1):

| Stage | Threat |
|-------|--------|
| Hypothesis generation | HARKing (hypothesizing after results are known) |
| Study design | Low statistical power; failure to control for bias |
| Data collection | Poor quality control |
| Analysis | P-hacking; analytic flexibility |
| Dissemination | Publication bias; lack of data sharing |

Together these threats inflate false-positive rates, overestimate effect sizes, and create a literature that accumulates misleading evidence.

### Five Reform Categories

#### 1. Methods
- **Protect against cognitive biases**: blinding, pre-registration as bias-reduction tool
- **Improve methodological training**: statistics education for all career stages; interactive tools (P-hacker simulator, EDA for animal studies)
- **Independent methodological support**: trial steering committees; CHDI Foundation ISSC model (Box 1)
- **Team science / collaboration**: Many Labs model; distributed student projects (Box 2); increases statistical power and generalizability

#### 2. Reporting and Dissemination
- **Pre-registration**: OSF, AsPredicted, Registered Reports format (Box 3); separates confirmatory from exploratory research
- **Quality of reporting**: TOP guidelines (757 journal signatories as of 2016); CONSORT; PRISMA; Equator Network
- **Conflicts of interest**: disclosure; exclusion; independent oversight

#### 3. Reproducibility (Open Science)
- **Open data** (Box 5): public repositories; NIH and NSF data sharing mandates
- **Open materials and code**: analysis pipelines, stimuli, instruments
- **Disclosure** (Box 6): 21-word statement; explicit reporting of exclusions, all measures

#### 4. Evaluation
- **Diversifying peer review**: preprints (arXiv, bioRxiv, PsyArXiv); post-publication review (PubPeer, PubMed Commons); results-free/results-blind review

#### 5. Incentives
- **Badges for open practices**: COS badges adopted by *Psychological Science* → 10× increase in open data (Fig. 2)
- **Registered Reports**: publication guaranteed before results known; >40 journals as of Nov 2016
- **TOP guidelines**: institutional and journal adoption
- **Funding replication studies**: NWO, NSF SBE replication funding programs
- **Open science in hiring/promotion**: nicebread.de guidelines

### The Feynman Principle

The paper closes with Feynman's dictum: "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself – and you are the easiest person to fool." Reproducibility reform is fundamentally about institutional design to counter scientists' natural cognitive biases.

### Adoption Status (2016 estimates in Table 1)

Most measures had <5% adoption at time of writing. Pre-registration >60% only for clinical trials. The paper is a prospective program, not a description of current state.
