# The Paradox of Postheroic Leadership

Nav: [[index]] | [[Leadership MOC]] | [[Gender & Careers MOC]]

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> A conceptual essay (*Leadership Quarterly* 2004) arguing that "new" **[[Postheroic Leadership|postheroic]]** models — leadership as shared, relational, social, and learning-oriented — are presented as gender- and power-neutral but are nothing of the sort. The relational skills they require are culturally coded **feminine**, and the "power with" stance is misread as **powerlessness**. Because work interactions are occasions to **[[Doing Gender, Doing Power, Doing Leadership|"do gender" and "do power"]]**, enacting postheroic leadership becomes "doing femininity/powerlessness" — displays not associated with leaders. This drives three paradoxes: heroic individualism stays resilient, the **female advantage never materializes**, and the model's transformational potential risks being **co-opted**.

## What postheroic leadership is (Fletcher's three characteristics)

1. **Leadership as practice — shared and distributed.** Not a set of traits held by those at the top, but shared practices enacted at all levels. Positional "heroes" are mere tips of icebergs, sustained by countless invisible acts of enabling and supporting. Blurs the leadership/[[Followership Theory|followership]] distinction.
2. **Leadership as social process — interactions.** Dynamic, multidirectional, emergent; occurs *in and through* relationships and networks of influence ("power up," "leading up," servant, quiet, connective). Requires a shift in the notion of **self**, from independent entity to **self-in-relation**.
3. **Leadership as learning — outcomes.** The interactions count as leadership by virtue of producing mutual learning, collective understanding, and positive action — reenvisioning power from **"power over" to "power with."**

## The gender/power argument

- **The traits are gendered.** Heroic traits (individualism, control, assertiveness, domination) are coded masculine; postheroic traits (empathy, community, vulnerability, inquiry, collaboration) are coded feminine — regardless of who displays them.
- **Separate spheres / "logic of effectiveness."** The masculine sphere carries knowledge of how to "produce things" (skilled, valued); the feminine/domestic sphere carries knowledge of how to "grow people" (assumed innate, devalued). The spheres are socially constructed as separate, sex-linked, and unequally valued. Postheroic leadership imports the *devalued* "growing people" wisdom — so adopting it is subconsciously associated with femininity.
- **Power distortion.** In systems of unequal power, the less powerful must adopt a distorted, non-mutual self-in-relation stance (anticipating the powerful's needs without reciprocity). This wrongly links the relational stance to **powerlessness**.

## The three paradoxes

1. **Resiliency of heroic individualism.** Despite two decades of rhetoric, everyday leadership narratives stay stuck in heroic images (leaders reconstruct their success as individual action; followers "romanticize" leadership — Meindl et al. 1985). A gender/power lens adds: relational practices are invisible partly because they read as "doing femininity" and "doing powerlessness," which people are pressured to hide.
2. **The "female advantage" that doesn't advantage women.** If postheroic leadership aligns with the feminine, women "should" benefit — yet few are its architects. Reason: **gender schema** filter behavior. When women enact relational leadership it is **conflated with mothering / selfless giving** — which is by definition non-mutual and erases the reciprocity postheroic leadership requires. Women are then relied on to do relational work *invisibly and without expecting anything back*, exposing them to exploitation, while men enacting the same behaviors can carry an expectation of reciprocity and be seen as "doing something new."
3. **Co-optation of transformational potential.** The model's radical challenge is to individualistic meritocracy, hierarchical power, and the myth of individual achievement. If it is imported as merely "add relational skills to the individual toolkit," the underlying belief system (the alternative logic of effectiveness) is silenced — its transformation is "castrated." The result: **"postheroic heroes"** who lead quietly but keep the same hierarchical power and reverence for individualism — old model, new language.

## Conclusion

A gender/power lens highlights three things: (1) the move to postheroic leadership is *not* gender-neutral — it engages charged gender- and power-linked identity; (2) it is not sex-neutral in practice — **the body matters**, gender schema make the experience different for men and women; (3) the transformational promise risks co-optation unless theory explicitly **names the radical challenge** rather than prescribing individual-level skills alone.

## Connections

- Extends **[[Gender and Leadership]]** beyond the "do men and women lead differently?" question into how relational leadership is *perceived* and *rewarded*.
- A critical counterpoint to celebratory accounts of [[Shared Leadership]], [[Co-Production of Leadership|co-produced]], and [[Transformational and Charismatic Leadership|transformational]] leadership — the same relational models this wiki tracks in the third wave. See [[Postheroic Leadership]].
- Draws on West & Zimmerman's "doing gender," Miller's relational-cultural theory, and Meindl's romance of leadership. Fletcher's own *Disappearing Acts* (1999) supplies the "relational practice made invisible" mechanism.
