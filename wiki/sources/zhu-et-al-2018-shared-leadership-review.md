# Shared Leadership: A State-of-the-Art Review and Future Research Agenda

Nav: [[index]] | [[Leadership MOC]] | [[Teams & Org Behavior MOC]]

Qualitative review in *Journal of Organizational Behavior* synthesizing the fragmented shared-leadership literature. Where meta-analyses (e.g. [[wang-et-al-2014-shared-leadership-team-effectiveness|Wang et al. 2014]]; D'Innocenzo et al. 2016; Nicolaides et al. 2014) are limited to variables studied across many samples, this review captures the full breadth of antecedents, consequences, mediators, and boundary conditions.

## Defining Shared Leadership

The authors define [[Shared Leadership]] as **an emergent team phenomenon whereby leadership roles and influence are distributed among team members** (following Carson et al. 2007; D'Innocenzo et al. 2016). Across 20+ representative definitions, three key characteristics recur:

1. **Lateral influence among peers** — the *source* of influence is horizontal team members, not a vertical designated leader.
2. **An emergent team-level phenomenon** — the *unit of analysis* is the collective, not the individual.
3. **Dispersed leadership roles/influence** — leadership is "broadly distributed" across the many, not centralized around the few.

Shared leadership is **not a substitute for [[Vertical vs Shared Leadership|vertical leadership]]**; both can operate in tandem and should be studied together (Ensley et al. 2006 found both predicted new-venture performance).

## Disentangling from Overlapping Constructs

The three characteristics let the authors distinguish shared leadership from six neighbors:

| Construct | How it differs from shared leadership |
|---|---|
| **Collective leadership** | Very close; emphasizes "fit"/"contextual" distribution of leadership as the situation demands. Often used interchangeably. |
| **Emergent leadership** | Individual-level; one or two members emerge as informal leaders — does not capture whole-group distribution. |
| **Self-leadership** | Sphere of influence is the self only; individual-level; a possible *antecedent* of shared leadership. |
| **Empowering leadership** | Vertical influence from the formal leader enhancing member autonomy; not lateral peer influence. |
| **Participative leadership** | Formal leader solicits input but retains final decision authority. |
| **Team leadership** | Broader superordinate; includes both horizontal (members) and vertical (formal leader) influence, and does not require decentralization. Shared leadership is one *form* of team leadership. |

## Operationalization

Two measurement traditions:

- **Aggregation / referent-shift approach** (Chan 1998): take an existing leadership scale, shift the referent from "my supervisor" to "my team members," aggregate to team level. Useful for studying sharing of a *specific* style (e.g. shared transformational), but assumes convergence and takes decentralization for granted.
- **Social-network approach**: model dyadic leading–following ties, then index the network as **density** (amount of leadership shared; Carson et al. 2007) or **decentralization** (distribution pattern; Mehra et al. 2006). Density is most common; each index alone is limited, so **best practice uses both simultaneously** (DeRue et al. 2015).

> [!key-insight] Content vs. configuration
> Network measures capture the *configuration* of leadership but overlook its *content* (which leadership function is performed by whom). Zhu et al. call for integrating shared leadership with **functional leadership theory** (Morgeson et al. 2010) so future measures capture both.

## Integrative Framework

Antecedents → shared leadership → proximal outcomes (team processes/emergent states) → distal outcomes, moderated by boundary conditions.

- **Antecedents**: formal-leader factors (empowering, servant, transformational leadership; leader humility; supportive coaching; high LMX — but LMX *differentiation* harms it), team characteristics (shared purpose, social support, voice, task cohesion, functional diversity under cooperative conflict management), and shared member characteristics (core self-evaluations, integrity, intrateam trust, collectivism, self-leadership).
- **Proximal outcomes**: team confidence/potency (mediator per Nicolaides et al. 2014), cohesion, trust, psychological safety, team learning, shared mental models, positive affective tone.
- **Distal outcomes**: team task performance, creativity/innovation, OCB, new-venture performance, member satisfaction.
- **Boundary conditions**: content of shared leadership (new-genre > traditional), task complexity/interdependence (strengthen effects), team tenure (stronger in newer teams), virtuality, operationalization (network indices show stronger effects than aggregation), demographic faultlines (informational + / social-category −).

## Future Research Agenda

Refine the construct contextually; develop **composite density + decentralization** and **function-based** measures; build a **unified, contextually-grounded, group-level theory** of leadership emergence (drawing on [[Adaptive Leadership Theory]] (DeRue 2011; DeRue & Ashford 2010), social information processing, social learning, relational theory (Uhl-Bien 2006)); study **temporal dynamics** (transition vs. action phases; shifting between vertical and shared leadership under crisis); investigate structure/HR antecedents; and — notably — probe the **potential dark side** (slower consensus, free-riding/social loafing, groupthink, threat to formal leaders' self-efficacy).
