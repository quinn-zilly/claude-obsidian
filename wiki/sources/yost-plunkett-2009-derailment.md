# When Leaders Derail
**Yost & Plunkett (2009)** — Ch. 9 in *Real Time Leadership Development*. Oxford: Wiley-Blackwell.

## Core Argument

Derailment (involuntary cessation of an advancing career) is most likely during **transitions** — new boss, new job, or major environmental shift. Past strengths become weaknesses in new conditions. The secret to both prevention and recovery is **staying open to learning**.

> [!key-insight] Derailment isn't always bad
> It can be the most significant developmental experience of a leader's career — forcing self-reflection, values clarification, and confronting unwanted truths.

## The 10 Deadly Sins of Derailment

1. **Poor performance** — unclear success criteria; bad person–job fit; focusing on next job instead of current one
2. **Failure to adapt** — doesn't adjust when environment changes (new boss, market shift, reorg)
3. **Arrogance** — fine line between self-confidence and arrogance; ignoring feedback; ethics drift
4. **Blind spots** — overreliance on strengths as much as weaknesses; e.g., marketing leader who sees everything as a marketing problem
5. **Failure to build a strong team** — staffing weak talent or copies of self; not creating conditions for team success
6. **Failure to build a network** — career tied too closely to one senior sponsor; invisible in succession discussions
7. **Poor working relationships** — drive for results becomes abrasive; or trying to please everyone
8. **Avoiding stretch assignments** — reduces future promotion chances; leaders must prove themselves "in the fire"
9. **Unethical behavior** — gray-area choices; front-page test
10. **Bad luck** — wrong place, wrong time; key is not blaming everyone else

## Recovery Process

Three stages of recovery work:
1. **Find your part in the story** — neither over-blame nor under-own responsibility
2. **Get outside perspectives** — from trusted people AND people who are not your biggest fans
3. **Come to terms** — accept what was unfair, own your role, decide how to live going forward

Leaders most at peace are those who did the hard work to think through past situations rather than remaining driven by past failures and injustices.

## Organizational Implications

**Early warning systems:**
- Structured onboarding for new leadership roles
- Rigorous performance management / 360-degree feedback
- Pre-move interviews (peers, seniors, directs) — Microsoft, IBM models
- Clear success criteria defined jointly with manager

**Derailment-resistant culture:**
- Define success broadly, not just by management promotions
- Create technical career paths so management is reserved for people who want to lead
- Examine individual derailment cases for systemic factors — "is it the person or the system?"

## Key Diagnostic Questions

- "Given that I eventually want to _____, what is it about me that will get in the way?"
- "What truth am I blurring?"
- "Would I feel comfortable if this action was published on the front page of the paper tomorrow morning?"
- (From the future): "If a younger leader were going through this experience, what advice would you give him or her?"
