# Ones, Dilchert, Viswesvaran & Judge (2007) — In Support of Personality Assessment in Organizational Settings

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> A point-by-point rebuttal to the [[Morgeson et al. (2007) Panel Critique|Morgeson et al. (2007)]] panel that argued personality tests should be reconsidered for selection on grounds of low validity and faking. Marshalling **hundreds of primary studies and dozens of meta-analyses since the mid-1980s**, the authors defend four claims: (1) the **[[Five-Factor Model|Big Five]] as a set** predicts work behavior with operational multiple correlations in the **.30s–.40s**; (2) **[[Conscientiousness]]** is the best generalizable predictor, with different trait combinations optimal for different jobs; (3) **[[Compound Traits (Personality)|compound measures]]** — especially **[[Integrity Tests|integrity tests]]** (~.40 for overall JP) — are the strongest personality predictors and give **[[Incremental Validity Over Cognitive Ability|incremental validity over cognitive ability]]**; and (4) **[[Directed Faking vs Applicant Response Distortion|response distortion among real applicants]]** does not destroy validity. "Writing off the whole domain of individual differences in personality... is counterproductive for the science and practice of I-O psychology."

## Context: the debate

*Personnel Psychology* published enhanced transcripts of a 2004 SIOP panel (Campion, Dipboye, Hollenbeck, Murphy, Schmitt — the "[[Morgeson et al. (2007) Panel Critique|Morgeson et al. (2007)]]" article), which questioned personality measures in selection on two grounds: **purportedly low validity** and **response distortion (faking)**. This article is the rebuttal. A central methodological disagreement: Morgeson et al. relied on **vote-counting / qualitative review** (as Guion & Gottier 1965 did); Ones et al. insist on **[[psychometric meta-analysis|meta-analytic]] quantitative summaries** and on **[[Operational Validity (Selection)|operational validities]]** (corrected for criterion unreliability and range restriction only — *not* predictor unreliability), the conservative correction Morgeson et al. themselves endorse.

## 1. Validities of the Big Five as a set

Operationally-weighted multiple correlations, computed from meta-analytic intercorrelations (Big Five intercorrelations from Ones 1993; predictor reliabilities from [[viswesvaran-ones-2000-models-of-job-performance|Viswesvaran & Ones 2000]]):

| Criterion | Operational R (optimal) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Overall job performance | .27 | Barrick, Mount & Judge (2001) |
| Objective performance | .23 | Barrick, Mount & Judge (2001) |
| Individual teamwork | .37 | Barrick, Mount & Judge (2001) |
| CWB: interpersonal deviance (avoiding) | .44 | Berry, Ones & Sackett (2007) |
| CWB: organizational deviance (avoiding) | .45 | Berry, Ones & Sackett (2007) |
| Contextual: citizenship | .31 | Borman et al. (2001) |
| Leadership (overall) | .45 | Judge, Bono, Ilies & Gerhardt (2002) |
| Leader emergence | .49 | Judge et al. (2002) |
| Training performance | .40 | Barrick, Mount & Judge (2001) |
| Team performance | .60 | Peeters et al. (2006) |
| Job satisfaction | .36 | Judge, Heller & Mount (2002) |

Observed (uncorrected) Rs average only **.08** points below operational Rs (SD .04) — so the picture is not "produced by magical or fanciful corrections." Unit-weighted composites tell the same story (Rs .11–.49).

## 2. Conscientiousness and its facets

[[Conscientiousness]] is "the single best, generalizable Big Five predictor of job performance." Operational bivariate validities (ρ) range from .04 (altruism/OCB) to .38 (avoiding organizational deviance): overall JP ρ = .23, objective .19, task .15. Facets matter and differ by criterion — **achievement** predicts task performance (.22) and job dedication (.34); **dependability** predicts job dedication (.40) and avoiding CWB (.30). These validities are on par with assessment centers (.36), structured interviews (.44), biodata (.35), and SJTs (.26–.34).

**Different jobs, different traits** (Table 3): apart from Conscientiousness, no single trait predicts overall JP consistently across jobs. For professionals only C predicts; for police C + Emotional Stability + Agreeableness; for customer service *all* Big Five; for managers Extraversion facets (dominance, energy) + C facets (achievement, dependability).

## 3. Compound traits and integrity tests

Compound scales assess more than one Big Five dimension: **[[Integrity Tests|integrity tests]]**, customer-service, stress-tolerance, violence, managerial-potential scales. They are the **highest-validity personality predictors of overall JP** — only general mental ability is superior. Careful, predictive, applicant-based validities for integrity tests (Ones et al. 1993): **.41** for supervisory ratings of overall JP; **.39** (overt) and **.29** (personality-based) for externally-detected CWB; **.28** for production records. Other compound scales: .19–.42.

Also flagged as useful beyond the Big Five: **core self-evaluations** (Judge, Erez et al.) and **proactivity** (Crant), each incremental over the full Big Five set.

## 4. Incremental validity over cognitive ability

See [[Incremental Validity Over Cognitive Ability]]. Integrity tests add **~.14** validity points over GMA for overall JP; multiple Rs (GMA + integrity) of **.64–.71** across job-complexity levels (Ones et al. 1993). Other compound scales add **.07–.16**. "There is incremental validity to be gained... even when cognitive variables are already included."

## 5. Faking is overrated

See [[Directed Faking vs Applicant Response Distortion]]. The core move is to **separate lab directed-faking studies from studies of naturally-occurring applicant distortion**. Lab directed-faking (students told to fake-good) documents only *maximum limits* on outright lying under artificial demand — a different phenomenon from real applicants. Among actual applicants:

- **Criterion-related validity holds** in predictive studies (Hough 1998b; the integrity-test validities above are all applicant-based).
- **Construct validity / factor structure holds up** in applicant samples (NEO-PI-R, HPI; Bradley & Hauenstein 2006 meta-analysis: distortion has "a small, practically unimportant" influence on Big Five intercorrelations).
- **Social-desirability scales don't predict** job performance (Ones, Viswesvaran & Reiss 1996) — so IM/SD is not a useful trait.

## 6. Evaluation of alternatives

Each proposed replacement for traditional self-report is judged **not clearly superior**:

- **Faking-index score corrections** — fail to improve prediction (Schmitt & Oswald 2006: "minimal effects").
- **Conditional reasoning** (L. James) — validity drastically overestimated; ~.14 for JP, .15–.24 for CWB (Berry, Sackett & Tobares 2007); costly to build.
- **Forced-choice / ipsative** — ipsative scores break normative between-subjects comparison, the whole point of selection; not certain to improve validity.
- **Homegrown scales** (Schmitt's suggestion) — no evidence they beat professionally-developed, validated off-the-shelf inventories; likely worse.
- **Others' (peer/supervisor) ratings** — promising; somewhat higher validity + incremental over self-reports; worth further research.
- **Enhancement strategies** — nonlinear effects, dark-side traits, profile analysis, trait × trait interactions, and **using multiple measures of the same trait** (up to +50% validity for C; Connelly & Ones 2007b).

## Contradictions / tensions

> [!contradiction] Pre-recalibration validity magnitudes vs [[Sackett-et-al-2022-validity-overcorrection|Sackett et al. (2022)]]
> This paper reports operational validities under the **Schmidt–Hunter correction regime** (range-restriction + criterion-unreliability corrections). [[Sackett-et-al-2022-validity-overcorrection|Sackett, Zhang, Berry & Lievens (2022)]] argue that **range-restriction corrections were systematically over-applied** in exactly this selection literature, so many headline operational validities here run **higher than the recalibrated 2022 estimates**. Ones et al. are careful to note personality meta-analyses used *more conservative* corrections than most predictors — but the direction of the 2022 critique still bears on these magnitudes. Treat the *relative* rankings as robust; treat the absolute values as pre-recalibration.
