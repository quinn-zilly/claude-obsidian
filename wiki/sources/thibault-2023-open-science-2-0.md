# Thibault et al. (2023) — Open Science 2.0

> [!key-insight] Core Claim
> Openness is necessary but not sufficient. The first wave of open science (call it **Open Science 1.0**) achieved widespread *sharing* of research outputs. The next target — **Open Science 2.0** — is a research ecosystem where transparency permeates the *entire process* and where scientists *directly and regularly interact* with each other's open scholarship to increase rigor and impact. Getting there requires sustained public funding, not reliance on volunteers and philanthropy.

## Bibliographic Details

Thibault RT, Amaral OB, Argolo F, Bandrowski AE, Davidson AR, Drude NI. "Open Science 2.0: Towards a truly collaborative research ecosystem." *PLOS Biology* 21(10): e3002362 (2023). Part of the *PLOS Biology* 20th Anniversary Collection. Open Access (CC BY).

An **Essay**, not an empirical study — a landscape review of two decades of open science plus a vision and a 7-point roadmap.

## The 1.0 → 2.0 Distinction

The authors borrow the **Web 1.0 → Web 2.0** analogy. Web 2.0 turned passive consumers into active participants; Open Science 2.0 should turn researchers from passive sharers into active collaborators on each other's outputs.

Two criteria define the 2.0 state:

1. The vast majority of research products *and processes* are openly available.
2. Scientific actors directly and regularly interact with that open scholarship to increase impact and rigor.

**Open Science 1.0** is offered as a retronym: widespread openness only (criterion 1). The authors stress we are not yet close to even 1.0's modest goals — data sharing remains uncommon.

## The Landscape — Four Topics

The Essay tours four fronts (its Table 1):

| Topic | Past/ongoing | Example initiatives | Future direction |
|-------|--------------|---------------------|------------------|
| **Methods transparency** | Reporting checklists; persistent reagent IDs | CONSORT, ARRIVE, PRISMA, [[Research Resource Identifier (RRID)\|RRIDs]] | Standards throughout the cycle; ongoing quality control |
| **Scholarly communication** | Preprints, preregistration, data sharing | OSF, ClinicalTrials.gov, arXiv | All scholarship shared regardless of "success"; linked outputs |
| **Team science** | Contributorship statements; shared databases | [[CRediT]], UK Biobank | Specialized roles; teams large and small |
| **Research culture** | Discussion + bottom-up coordination | [[Reproducibility Networks]], NASA TOPS, DORA | Merge bottom-up + top-down; open science as default |

### Selected empirical facts

- **RRIDs**: ~half of antibody references were trackable by 2020, up from 15% in the 1990s. Over 1,000 journals now request them.
- **Data sharing gap**: a meta-analysis of >2M publications found 5–11% *declared* open data but only 1–3% *actually had* it. Code sharing <0.5%. Only *declarations* rose over time.
- **Preregistration**: likely ≤10% in psychology, far lower in social sciences; one preclinical registry had just 161 registrations as of Sept 2023. Two reviewers agreed on the number of hypotheses in only 14% of preregistrations; ~⅓ of trials changed a primary outcome without disclosure.
- **Open access** (the "simple" case): after 20+ years of effort, OA tops out around ~50%, and cost shifted from subscriptions to APCs — a cautionary tale for the harder reforms.

## Vision: What Open Science 2.0 Requires

Four ingredients of an ideal ecosystem-wide implementation:

1. **A modular and dynamic research record** — share components (hypotheses, protocols, data, code, manuscripts, reviews) when each is ready, not at the end. Linked via persistent identifiers, with version control and forking. A "record of versions" rather than a static "version of record." GitHub/open-source is the working model; protocols.io, Figshare, Dryad, Octopus, ResearchEquals are partial instances.
2. **Standardization and interoperability** — agreed data structures, vocabularies, metadata so humans *and machines* can integrate datasets. GenBank/UniProt/AlphaFold show the payoff. Custom spreadsheets are the anti-pattern.
3. **Ongoing quality control** — distributed and partly automated, throughout the cycle (like manufacturing QC), not just at the end. AI could flag missing info, retracted references, bad RRIDs, protocol inconsistencies.
4. **Reorganization of scientific labor** — task specialization across larger teams (Data Manager, Systematic Reviewer, Statistician), rewarded as such. Distributed collaborations like [[Big Team Science\|Psychological Science Accelerator]] and ManyBabies as models.

## The 7-Point Roadmap

Cross-stakeholder recommendations (funders, institutions, journals, regulators):

1. **Monitor progress and policy compliance** — trackers (FDAAA, EU Trials Tracker) plus advocacy; QUEST public dashboard.
2. **Fund open science infrastructure, training, and support** — stop relying on volunteers/philanthropy. NIH at 0.1% of budget ≈ $47M/yr; Research England gave UKRN £4.5M; NASA TOPS = $40M over 5 yr; SciELO as diamond-OA infrastructure.
3. **Invite innovation** — let new models emerge (Peer Community In; *eLife* dropping accept/reject; OpenSAFELY).
4. **Fund meta-research** — research-on-research to test whether interventions actually work (the open-data-badge cycle as exemplar).
5. **Align incentives across stakeholders** — e.g., Registered Report Funding Partnerships (funder–journal).
6. **Promote teams and specialization** — change the *level* of assessment (UK REF moving to discipline-level; Netherlands Recognition & Rewards).
7. **Treat open science as a means, not an end** — "Open Science: Just Science Done Right" is too modest; openness without rigor and reuse is a box-ticking exercise.

## Caveats the Authors Raise

- Open Science 2.0 could **amplify inequalities** — large open datasets skew Global North; higher upfront rigor costs could exclude the Global South.
- A flood of modular outputs needs **curation and synthesis** or it just adds noise.
- **Credit allocation** gets harder as units of output diversify and collaboration deepens.
