# APA Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct (2017)

The **canonical ethics code for U.S. psychologists**. Adopted 2002, effective 2003, with 2010 amendments (Standards 1.02/1.03) and a 2016 amendment to Standard 3.04 (effective Jan 1, 2017). Published in *American Psychologist*. Maintained by the [[American Psychological Association]].

## Structure

Three layers (see [[Aspirational vs Enforceable Standards]]):

1. **Introduction + Preamble** — intent, scope, applicability. Not enforceable.
2. **Five General Principles (A–E)** — aspirational ideals. Guide and inspire; *not* a basis for sanctions. See [[Five General Principles (APA)]].
3. **Ethical Standards** — enforceable rules, organized into 10 substantive sections.

The Code applies only to psychologists' **scientific, educational, or professional roles** — not purely private conduct. Lack of awareness of a standard is not a defense.

## The Five General Principles

- **A — Beneficence and Nonmaleficence**: benefit those you work with; do no harm; guard against misuse of influence.
- **B — Fidelity and Responsibility**: relationships of trust; responsibility to society and community; manage conflicts of interest; contribute pro bono.
- **C — Integrity**: accuracy, honesty, truthfulness; no fraud or misrepresentation.
- **D — Justice**: fairness entitles *all* persons to access and benefit from psychology; guard against bias and boundaries of competence.
- **E — Respect for People's Rights and Dignity**: dignity, privacy, confidentiality, self-determination; special safeguards for the vulnerable.

> [!quote] Preamble
> Psychologists are committed to ... the use of such knowledge "to improve the condition of individuals, organizations, and society."

This **"individuals, organizations, **and** society"** clause is the hook [[Joel Lefkowitz]] (2008) uses to argue I/O psychology under-serves its societal mandate. See [[apa-2017-ethical-principles-code-of-conduct#Connection to Lefkowitz 2008|below]].

## The 10 Ethical Standards (sections)

1. Resolving Ethical Issues (incl. 1.02 conflicts with law, 1.03 conflicts with organizational demands — "under no circumstances may this standard be used to justify ... violating human rights")
2. Competence (boundaries; cultural competence; personal problems)
3. Human Relations (unfair discrimination, harassment, [[Avoiding Harm (Standard 3.04)|avoiding harm]], multiple relationships, conflict of interest, [[Informed Consent]])
4. Privacy and Confidentiality
5. Advertising and Other Public Statements
6. Record Keeping and Fees
7. Education and Training
8. Research and Publication (informed consent, deception, debriefing, data sharing, [[Publication Credit]], plagiarism)
9. Assessment
10. Therapy

## Notable standards for applied / I-O work

- **1.03 Conflicts Between Ethics and Organizational Demands** — when an employer's demands conflict with the Code, the psychologist clarifies the conflict, affirms commitment to the Code, and resolves it consistent with the Principles. Directly relevant to [[Joel Lefkowitz|Lefkowitz's]] [[Pro-Management Bias]] critique.
- **3.04 Avoiding Harm** — the 2016 amendment added 3.04(b) barring participation in torture or cruel/inhuman/degrading treatment.
- **8.05 / 8.14** — research without consent allowed for low-risk organizational-effectiveness studies; data-sharing for verification (ties to [[Open Science MOC]]).

## Connection to Lefkowitz 2008

[[lefkowitz-2008-values-organizational-psychology|Lefkowitz (2008)]] treats this Code as evidence that psychology *formally* commits to a humanist, society-serving ideal — then argues organizational psychology's lived **values** (the [[Scientist-Practitioner Model]]) fail to live up to it. The Code is the normative yardstick; the [[Scientist-Practitioner-Humanist (S-P-H) Model|S-P-H model]] is his proposed correction.
