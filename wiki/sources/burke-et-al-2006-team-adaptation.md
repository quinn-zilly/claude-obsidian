# Burke, Stagl, Salas, Pierce & Kendall (2006) — Understanding Team Adaptation

**Source:** *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 91(6), 1189–1207. Lead article of a JAP special section on team adaptation.

## Core Argument

Adaptation lies at the heart of team effectiveness: interdependence means members must serve as **compensatory systems** for one another. Yet team adaptation was under-theorized. The paper offers a **multidisciplinary, multilevel, multiphasic** conceptualization rooted in cognitive, human-factors, and I-O psychology, and advances a testable **cross-level mixed-determinants** input–throughput–output model (Figure 1).

## Key Definitions

> [!abstract] Team adaptation
> A change in team performance, in response to a salient cue or cue stream, that leads to a functional outcome for the entire team. Manifested in innovation of new — or modification of existing — structures, capacities, and/or behavioral or cognitive goal-directed actions.

**Adaptive team performance** = the emergent phenomenon that *compiles* over time from a recursive cycle whereby members use resources to functionally change cognitive/behavioral action to meet expected or unexpected demands. It is the proximal antecedent of team adaptation. See [[Adaptive Team Performance]].

Distinguished from related constructs: **team learning** (necessary but insufficient — a latent cognitive repertoire that may never manifest); **team innovation** (a process, may not yield functional outcome; innovation precedes adaptation); **problem management** (teams can solve problems without adapting).

## The Adaptive Cycle — Four Phases

The model's core is a four-phase cycle at the heart of adaptive team performance. See [[Team Adaptation (Burke Cross-Level Model)]] for full treatment.

1. **Situation assessment** — cue scanning, cue recognition, meaning ascription. Habitual routines work *against* cue detection. Outputs shared mental models + team situation awareness. (Propositions 1–2)
2. **Plan formulation** — deciding course of action, roles, priorities. Fed by [[Team Situation Awareness]] and [[Psychological Safety]]. (Propositions 3–4); plan formulation revises shared cognition (Proposition 5).
3. **Plan execution** — enacted via individual + team behaviors: mutual performance monitoring, backup behavior, communication, leadership, and team-level coordination. See [[Adaptive Coordination Behaviors]]. (Propositions through 8)
4. **Team learning** — feedback; competencies acquired as a *result* of adapting feed back into the cycle.

## Emergent Cognitive States

Three emergent states recur across phases as both inputs and outputs (compilation vs composition emergence, per Kozlowski & Klein 2000):

- **[[Shared Mental Models]]** — compatible knowledge of task, team, equipment, roles.
- **[[Team Situation Awareness]]** — shared understanding of the current situation; three ascending levels (perception → comprehension → projection; Endsley 1995).
- **[[Psychological Safety]]** — shared belief the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking (Edmondson 1999); enables speaking up during plan formulation.

## Triggers to Adapt

Cues that prompt adaptation (Louis & Sutton 1991): situation experienced as unusual/novel; a discrepancy, disruption, or unexpected failure; deliberate initiative raising conscious attention. Project **midpoint** is a common trigger (Gersick 1989; Waller et al. 2002).

## Scope Conditions

Most illustrative for teams with **moderate-to-high task/outcome interdependence**; generalizes to lower interdependence, but the weighting of adaptive-cycle components varies. Deliberately models **bottom-up emergence only** — top-down and multiteam/organizational levels excluded at this theory-building stage.

## Why It Matters (wiki context)

This is the **classic predecessor** to the [[Team Adaptation (Stimulus-Based Model)|Pearsall, Christian & Croitoru (2025) stimulus-based model]]. Pearsall et al. critique exactly this lineage — IPO-framed, behavior/response-focused adaptation that collapses across stimulus types and under-theorizes the detection/assessment front end.

> [!contradiction] Superseded framing vs [[Team Adaptation (Stimulus-Based Model)]]
> Burke et al. (2006) frame adaptation as an **input–throughput–output** cycle beginning at situation assessment, treating team attributes as inputs/antecedents. Pearsall et al. (2025) argue this is response-focused and stimulus-blind: they recenter the model on the *adaptive stimulus* itself and treat team attributes as **phase-specific moderators**. Both live in the wiki; Burke is the seminal roots, Pearsall the modern revision.
