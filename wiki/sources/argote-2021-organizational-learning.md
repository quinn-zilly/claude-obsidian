# Argote, Lee & Park (2021) — Organizational Learning Processes

Navigation: [[sources/_index|Sources]] | [[Organizational Learning]] | [[Learning Curves]]

## Core Claim

As organizations gain experience, performance improves at a decreasing rate (the learning curve). But organizations vary widely in learning rates. Understanding this variation requires decomposing organizational learning into four distinct processes — search, knowledge creation, knowledge retention, and knowledge transfer — and examining how experience dimensions and organizational context affect each.

## Purpose

Comprehensive review of empirical organizational learning research published since 1990, with a focus on Management Science publications. Identifies major findings, current themes, and future research directions including the impact of machine learning and business platforms on organizational learning.

## Key Contributions

### The Four Organizational Learning Processes

**1. Search** — how organizations explore their environment for new knowledge
- Local vs. distant search (March, 1991 exploration/exploitation)
- Search breadth and depth affect innovation outcomes
- Organizational structure shapes search scope

**2. Knowledge Creation** — generating new knowledge from experience
- Direct experience vs. vicarious learning (learning from others)
- Experiential learning vs. experimental learning
- Interpersonal and team-level knowledge creation
- Individual learning is necessary but not sufficient for organizational learning

**3. Knowledge Retention** — storing knowledge so it can be used later
- Repositories: individual members, tools/technology, routines/practices, transactive memory systems
- Knowledge depreciation — knowledge can be lost when members leave, routines atrophy, or production slows (the Lockheed L-1011 case)
- Organizational memory is distributed, not centralized

**4. Knowledge Transfer** — moving knowledge across people, units, or organizations
- Causal ambiguity impedes transfer
- Relational factors (trust, shared identity) facilitate transfer
- Codification aids explicit knowledge transfer; socialization aids tacit transfer
- Interorganizational transfer through alliances, acquisitions, geographic proximity

### The Learning Curve — Core Phenomenon

Wright (1936) first documented that labor required to produce an aircraft decreases at a decreasing rate as cumulative output grows. The pattern is characterized by a **progress ratio** — e.g., an 80% progress ratio means costs fall to 80% of their prior level with each doubling of cumulative output.

Learning curves found across: manufacturing (aircraft, semiconductors, chemicals), services (hospitals, software), and knowledge-intensive work (biotech, design).

> [!key-insight] Knowledge is not always cumulative
> The Lockheed L-1011 case showed that when production slowed, costs *rose back above* previously achieved levels — suggesting acquired knowledge depreciates or is lost when not actively practiced.

### Dimensions of Experience

Learning rate varies with:
- **Depth vs. breadth** — specialized vs. diverse experience
- **Direct vs. vicarious** — own vs. others' experience
- **Individual, team, and organizational levels** — each contributes independently (Reagans et al., 2005, surgical teams)
- **Recency vs. staleness** — recent experience matters more than older

### Future Directions

- Machine learning and AI as new forms of organizational "learning"
- Business platforms as knowledge ecosystems
- Interorganizational learning in the gig economy
- How remote work changes knowledge transfer and retention
