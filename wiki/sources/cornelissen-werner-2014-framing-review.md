# Cornelissen & Werner (2014) — Putting Framing in Perspective

## One-Line Summary

A comprehensive multi-level review of framing research across management and organizational theory, arguing that the construct spans micro cognition, meso meaning-construction, and macro institutionalization — and that the field suffers from reification, static tendencies, and siloed levels of analysis.

## Core Argument

Framing is one of the most ubiquitous yet theoretically fragmented constructs in the social sciences. Cornelissen and Werner show that "frame" means fundamentally different things at three levels of analysis — and that failure to distinguish *cognitive frames* (stable knowledge schemas) from *acts of framing* (dynamic meaning construction in language/interaction) has created persistent problems: reification, static bias, and an inability to connect micro, meso, and macro processes.

Their two formative contributions:
1. **The priming/framing distinction** — priming = activating a pre-existing cognitive frame; framing = actively constructing new meaning in social interaction. These are reciprocally connected but analytically distinct.
2. **Multi-level integration** — frames at different levels (cognitive → interactive → field → institutional) are dynamically linked; future research needs to trace these connections empirically.

## Structure: Three Levels of Analysis

### Micro Level — Cognition in Action

**Cognitive frames / frames of reference:** Knowledge structures (schemas) that direct perception, inference, and behavior. Originated in the Carnegie tradition (March & Simon 1958). Frames are "theory-driven" (top-down): a familiar frame is activated by cues, then creates expectations that guide subsequent perception.

Key dynamics:
- **Sensemaking** (Weick 1995): "cue + relation + frame" = basic unit of meaning. Frames are past socialization; cues are present experience.
- **Sensebreaking:** when cues violate frame expectations, meaning collapses; individuals may resist reframing due to prior socialization.
- **Cognitive change:** analogy and blending are primary mechanisms. Benner & Tripsas (2012) — incumbent manufacturers extend prior-industry frames to novel markets (digital cameras). Gavetti et al. (2005) — analogical projection across industries. Cornelissen & Clarke (2010) — conceptual blending of input frames into hybrid frames with emergent properties.

**Framing effects / frames of reference (behavioral decision-making):**
- Draws on prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky 1979): same scenario framed as gains vs. losses → different risk preferences.
- **Equivalency frames:** logically identical information presented positively/negatively (e.g., "95% employed" vs. "5% unemployed").
- **Emphasis/issue frames:** highlight qualitatively different considerations (legitimacy vs. efficiency; threat vs. opportunity).
- Dual-process model: gain/loss framing primes System 1; cognitive mapping interventions can activate System 2.

**Research frontier:** Better distinguish equivalency frames (utility, gains/losses) from emphasis/issue frames (social legitimacy, trust); explore varieties of analogical reasoning across strategic contexts.

### Meso Level — Moving Others

Operates from a symbolic interactionist (Goffman, Blumer) rather than cognitive-science tradition. Framing is **bottom-up** meaning construction through language and interaction, not just priming of internal schemas.

**Strategic frames:** Jointly constructed cognitive representations of industry competitive assumptions. Bind organizations to capabilities; blind them to alternatives (Nadkarni & Narayanan 2007). Kaplan (2008) — framing contests model: actors use frames strategically to win political support in organizations; "skilled actors" find frames that make others think a proposal is in their interests.

**Strategic framing (change literature):** Purposeful communication to reshape interpretive frames of stakeholders (Fiss & Zajac 2006; Gioia & Chittipeddi 1991; Kellogg 2009). Tools: metaphors, catchphrases, slogans, stories, contrast, spin. Limitation: tends toward asymmetric, one-way model (speaker → listener), neglecting employees as active co-constructors.

**Technological frames** (Orlikowski & Gash 1994): Collectively constructed assumptions about a technology's nature, role, and appropriate use. Frame incongruence → different innovation paths (Leonardi 2011). Ambiguity in frames can actually enable coordination (Donnellon et al. 1986; Van Burg et al. 2013) — overly tight congruence not always optimal.

**Collective action frames** (Benford & Snow 2000): Joint accounts that define an injustice and mobilize action. Three framing tasks: diagnostic (define problem/blame), prognostic (solutions/tactics), motivational (call to action). Four alignment processes: bridging, amplification, extension, transformation. Key critique (Benford 1997): descriptive bias, static tendency, reification; field moved from studying *framing processes* to cataloguing *frames*.

**Common limitation across meso streams:** Focus on frames as outputs (products, stable cognitive representations) rather than framing as ongoing process; neglect of interactive negotiation.

### Macro Level — Field Frames and Institutional Change

**Frames and institutions:** In neo-institutional theory, frames bridge micro sensemaking and macro institutionalization. Institutional logics are increasingly theorized as "frames of reference" (Thornton et al. 2012). Beckert (2010): institutions ≈ inter-subjectively shared cognitive frames.

**Field frames** (Lounsbury et al. 2003): Durable, endogenous interpretive conventions within an institutional field — have the stickiness of institutional logics but are subject to actor agency. Resolves the embedded agency paradox.

**Framing struggles and settlements:** Framing contests (Ryan 1991) arise when fields undergo upheaval. Cultural entrepreneurs (Fligstein 2001) — skilled social actors who frame "stories" inducing cooperation by appealing to group identity while opposing opponents. Discursive opportunity structures (Koopmans & Statham 1999) — culturally resonant discourses provide windows for new frames to gain traction (Kellogg 2011 — hospital reform: same framing arguments resonated only in early 2000s, not 1970s or 1990s).

**From field frame to institutional frame:** Powell & Colyvas (2008) — "career of metaphor" trajectory: novel framing → conventionalized metaphorical category → cognitively embedded schema → naturalized/taken-for-granted institutional frame (the metaphor is "dead"). Example: French chefs framed as "creators" (Rao et al. 2003) — initially active comparison, eventually automatic categorization.

**Common ground** (Clark 1996): Personal common ground (built in interaction) scales into communal common ground (field frames) through repeated exposure and convention propagation. Mechanism linking interactive frames to field frames.

## Discussion: Integration Across Levels

Cornelissen & Werner propose a synthesis around Fillmore's (1975, 1982) frame semantics: frames are structured semantic representations invoked by words, rooted in background cultural knowledge, which constrain and are reshaped by actors' language use. This implies:

1. **Institutional frames constrain individual framing** — words invoke culturally embedded schemas that limit available meanings.
2. **Actors can laminate/edit** (Weber & Glynn 2006; Diehl & McFarland 2010) — reframing builds on but extends existing institutional frames.
3. **Interactive frames** (Tannen 1985) — in real-time social interaction, participants negotiate common footing (Goffman 1981) that aligns their individual schemas.
4. **Practice grounding** — frames gain credibility and "naturalness" when anchored in habitual practices (Reay et al. 2013; Glaser et al. 2011).

Key linkages:
- Cognitive frame ↔ Interactive frame: individual schemas are enacted and modified in interaction.
- Interactive frame ↔ Field frame: personal common ground propagates into communal common ground via repeated interactions.
- Field frame ↔ Institutional frame: conventionalization + naturalization + forgetting the metaphorical origin.

## Methodological Implications

**Current dominant method:** Thematic content analysis — keyword/phrase occurrence in texts → infer frames. Problems: assumes stable signifiers map directly to cognitive frames; risks tautology (frames inferred from discourse that produced them).

**Recommended expansions:**
1. **Ethnographic / micro-level methods** — conversation analysis, interaction analysis, ethnomethodology; study framing *in real time*; avoid reification.
2. **Semantic network analysis** — grammatical/associational relationships between concepts; captures relational structure of frames (roles, causes, consequences) rather than keyword frequency.
3. **Multi-dimensional scaling / correspondence analysis** — map actors' relative positions via frame similarities/differences (Meyer & Höllerer 2010).

## Research Gaps Identified

1. Varieties of analogical reasoning and their applicability across strategic contexts
2. Distinction between equivalency frames and emphasis/issue frames in organizational contexts
3. More processual, ethnographic research on framing-in-interaction (rather than frame outputs)
4. How common ground is built in repeated interactions (mechanism linking interactive → field frames)
5. Connections between frames and categories in institutional theory
6. Semantic network analysis methods for frame analysis
7. Experiential/practice grounding of frames in institutional change
