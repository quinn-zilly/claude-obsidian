# Fulmer & Li (2022) — Compensation, Benefits, and Total Rewards: A Bird's-Eye (Re)View

> [!abstract] One-line
> A selective, high-level ("bird's-eye") review of ~15 years of research on **compensation, benefits, and their combination as [[Total Rewards|total rewards]]**, arguing the fields have been studied separately despite practitioners and employees viewing pay+benefits holistically.

## Scope & framing

Focuses on **core total rewards** = compensation + employer-provided **retirement, health, and work-life** benefits, in the US context. First broad management/applied-psych review of compensation since Dulebohn & Werling (2007) and of benefits since Dulebohn et al. (2009). Benefits are ~31% of total employer compensation costs (up to ~38% in state/local government), so "fringe" is a misnomer.

## Compensation findings

- **Internal equity / pay structure**: less attention now to job evaluation and formal pay grades; more to the *residual* [[Pay Dispersion|pay dispersion]] that accumulates from many past pay decisions.
- **Pay dispersion**: mixed effects reconciled by contingencies — work interdependence, **explained vs unexplained** dispersion (explained = performance-based is positive/curvilinear for teams; unexplained is null), and vertical (between-job, positive) vs horizontal (within-job, less positive) dispersion.
- **External market**: research still scarce for non-executives; most external-comparison work is on executive pay.
- **Individual PFP**: [[Pay-for-Performance]] is an effective motivator *contingent on design/fit*; apparent negative effects often reflect poor implementation (Gerhart, Shaw & Gupta). Self-determination framing: PFP as challenge → autonomous motivation; as hindrance → controlled motivation. Distal effects studied: mental-health medication use, injury, turnover spillovers.
- **Individualization**: compensation-activation theory (Fulmer & Shaw 2018); financial [[Idiosyncratic Deals (i-deals)|i-deals]].
- **Pay communication / transparency**: renewed attention; outcome vs process transparency; links to inequality.

## Benefits findings

- **Retirement**: research remains thin; some strategic/sorting work (e.g. trucking firms offering supplemental plans → safer drivers as a sorting proxy).
- **Health & well-being**: mindfulness/meditation interventions (mixed on burnout); Pfeffer et al. (2020) estimated the cost of employees' time on insurance admin at tens of billions.
- **Work-life benefits**: dominated by employee-reaction studies; work-family supports (WFS); flexibility availability vs use; mixed organizational outcomes.

## Total rewards & future directions

Little research combines pay + benefits. Applicant-attraction meta-analysis (Uggerslev et al. 2012): benefits (r=.31) predict attraction *more strongly* than pay (r=.23); combined r=.29. Five future themes:

1. **Implementation & administration** — control for/measure communication and implementation, not just design.
2. **Sorting + total rewards** — study [[Sorting Effect (Compensation)|sorting]] (recruitment/turnover), not only [[Incentive Effect (Compensation)|incentive]] effects.
3. **Dispersion of total rewards** — benefits dispersion + i-deals → possible gender/race **total rewards gaps**; hard because benefit value is hard to quantify financially.
4. **Individualized total rewards** — customized pay + benefits to fit a heterogeneous workforce; extend compensation-activation logic to benefits.
5. **Contingent & gig work** — these workers self-assemble their "total rewards"; less health/retirement coverage; calls for de-coupling benefits from traditional employment.
