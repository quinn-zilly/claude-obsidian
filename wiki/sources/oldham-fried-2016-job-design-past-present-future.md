# Job Design Research and Theory: Past, Present and Future — Oldham & Fried (2016)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]] | [[Job Characteristics Model]] | [[Teams & Org Behavior MOC]]

A narrative review of ~50 years of job-design research by **Greg Oldham** (co-author of JCT) and **Yitzhak Fried** (co-author of the 1987 meta-analysis). Structured as *past* (early theory), *present* (contemporary streams), and *future* (eight new directions). Complements the quantitative [[humphrey-nahrgang-morgeson-2007-work-design-meta-analysis|Humphrey, Nahrgang & Morgeson (2007)]] with the field's senior-author retrospective.

## Past: From Simplification to Enrichment

- **Scientific management** (Taylor 1911; Babbage/Smith): standardized, simplified jobs for efficiency — but produced tardiness, output restriction, dissatisfaction (Walker & Guest 1952).
- **Motivation-Hygiene Theory** (Herzberg 1966): enrich rather than simplify; add motivators (responsibility, achievement, growth). Limits: no measurement technology, still top-down, ignored individual differences.
- **Turner & Lawrence (1965):** six Requisite Task Attributes; RTA index related to satisfaction differently across subcultures.
- **Hackman & Lawler (1971):** four characteristics → internal work motivation, moderated by growth need strength (GNS).
- **Job Characteristics Theory (Hackman & Oldham 1976, 1980):** five characteristics → three psychological states → outcomes, moderated by GNS, knowledge/skill, and context satisfaction; measured by the Job Diagnostic Survey (JDS).

> [!key-insight] Verdict on JCT after hundreds of studies
> Strong support for positive characteristic→attitude relations and for **experienced meaningfulness as mediator**. Only **modest** characteristic→behavior (performance, attendance) relations. **Weak** support for experienced responsibility and knowledge of results. **Mixed** support for GNS and context-satisfaction moderators; the knowledge/skill moderator was never directly tested.

## Present: Streams That Refined and Expanded JCT

- **Measurement of job characteristics:** JDS factor-structure problems; negatively-worded-item fixes didn't improve prediction; employee ratings are *generally accurate* (moderate self–observer convergence), shaped somewhat by dispositions and social information. Field moved to self-reports via JDS, JCI, or WDQ.
- **New moderators:** personality (esp. neuroticism), career stage, physical context, others' jobs, uncertainty (job control matters more under high uncertainty), and the **JD-R framework** (Demerouti et al. 2001) — see [[Job Demands–Control–Support Model]].
- **Effects of organizational context on job design:** structure (formalization, centralization), technology (Thompson 1967 categories), and physical space shape the characteristics jobs actually have.
- **Interdisciplinary perspective** (Campion) → see [[Interdisciplinary Model of Job Design]].
- **Alternative job characteristics:** social dimensions (Grant & Parker 2009) and Humphrey et al.'s (2007) motivational/contextual/social groupings; service/knowledge economy raised their salience.
- **Alternative outcomes:** creativity, organizational citizenship behaviors (OCBs), and **employee health/well-being** — including curvilinear effects (Fried et al. 2013: moderately complex jobs → lowest obesity rates).
- **New mediators:** beyond meaningfulness — self-efficacy, psychological ownership, empowerment, perceived social impact/worth (Grant), and **work engagement** (mediates characteristics → performance and change-oriented behaviors).
- **Design of jobs for teams:** Hackman's (1987) model; autonomous teams help when the task is interdependent, members have low individual autonomy, teams aren't isolated, and existing reward/feedback systems are weak — but can hurt **inter-team** coordination (high team ownership).

## Future: Eight New Research Directions

1. **Job crafting** — employee-initiated customization (the largest section); see [[Job Crafting]]. Mixed effects on the crafter's own outcomes; under-studied effects on coworkers and on the organization.
2. **Effects of jobs on the organizational context** — whether bottom-up redesign/crafting actually loosens formal structural constraints.
3. **Generational differences** — Gen Y may respond less positively to motivational characteristics and value work-life balance over intrinsic rewards (interpret cross-sectional data cautiously — confounds age and cohort).
4. **Transformational leadership and job design** — leaders shape perceived characteristics (intellectual stimulation → variety/autonomy; idealized influence → significance); job characteristics also mediate and can substitute for or strengthen leadership effects. See [[Transformational and Charismatic Leadership]].
5. **Job design for temporary employees** — "blended" workforces (~12% of US workforce); permanents may disengage when threatened by temporaries.
6. **Job design across cultures** — autonomy is positive in low-power-distance cultures (USA) but can be negative in high-power-distance ones (India; Robert et al. 2000). See [[Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions]].
7. **New work arrangements** — telecommuting/hubs change autonomy, feedback sources, and crafting opportunities; risk of feedback loss or cognitive overload from computing technologies.
8. **Effects of jobs on jobholders** — reverse causality: enriched work over time increases intellectual flexibility (Kohn & Schooler 1982), self-esteem, proactive personality, and Big Five traits → argues for **dynamic** (vs. static) models of job design and continuous crafting/redesign.

## Why This Source Matters

- Authoritative synthesis from JCT's own author; frames where the field has been and should go.
- Establishes **job crafting** and **dynamic person–job fit** as the contemporary frontier, balancing the manager-imposed-design tradition.
- Pairs with Humphrey et al. (2007) for the qualitative + quantitative picture of the same literature.
