# Ng, Eby, Sorensen & Feldman (2005) — Predictors of Objective and Subjective Career Success

> [!abstract] One-line
> The foundational meta-analysis of career-success predictors: four predictor families (human capital, organizational sponsorship, sociodemographic status, stable individual differences) tested against **objective** success (salary, promotions) and **subjective** success (career satisfaction), organized by the [[Contest-Mobility vs Sponsored-Mobility|contest- vs. sponsored-mobility]] lens.

## Design

- **Career success** = accumulated positive work and psychological outcomes from one's work experience (Seibert & Kraimer 2001).
- **Objective** (extrinsic): salary, number of promotions. **Subjective** (intrinsic): career satisfaction.
- Theoretical guides: Turner's (1960) two mobility systems — **contest mobility** (get ahead by merit/performance) and **sponsored mobility** (elites select and sponsor high-potentials). Human-capital predictors ↔ contest lens; organizational-sponsorship + sociodemographic predictors ↔ sponsored lens.
- Moderators: gender, time (study date), common-method bias.

## Headline Pattern

Human-capital and sociodemographic predictors relate more strongly to **objective** success; organizational-sponsorship and stable-individual-difference predictors relate more strongly to **subjective** success. Objective and subjective success are only modestly correlated (salary–career-satisfaction ρ ≈ .30; promotion–career-satisfaction ρ ≈ .22; salary–promotion ρ ≈ .18), confirming they are distinct constructs.

## Selected Corrected Correlations (ρ)

| Predictor | → Salary | → Promotion | → Career satisfaction |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Hours worked | .24 | .13 | .13 |
| Organization tenure | .20 | .03 | .02 |
| Education level | **.29** | .05 | .03 |
| Career sponsorship | .22 | .12 | **.44** |
| Supervisor support | .05 | .02 | **.46** |
| Conscientiousness | .07 | .06 | .14 |
| Proactivity | .11 | .16 | **.38** |

The contrast is the story: **education** pays off in salary but barely in satisfaction; **sponsorship, supervisor support, and proactivity** drive satisfaction far more than pay. Objective success is bought largely with human capital; subjective success with relational sponsorship and disposition.

## Why It Matters

- Establishes the **objective/subjective distinction** as empirically real, not just conceptual — different predictor profiles.
- Gives the field its default predictor taxonomy for career-success research (still the reference point in later reviews).
- Shows sponsorship/relational factors, not just merit, shape careers — nuancing pure contest-mobility (meritocratic) assumptions.

## My Notes

> [!note] Lineage into the current vault
> This 2005 meta-analysis is the empirical ancestor of the career-success pages already here.
