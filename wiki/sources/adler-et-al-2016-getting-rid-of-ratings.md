# Adler et al. (2016) — Getting Rid of Performance Ratings: Genius or Folly?

> [!abstract] TL;DR
> A structured **debate** (grown out of a standing-room-only 2015 SIOP panel) on whether organizations should eliminate formal performance ratings. **Anti-ratings side** (Colquitt, [[Kevin Murphy|Murphy]], Ollander-Krane): rating is a **failed experiment** after ~a century of research — disappointing interventions, rater disagreement (interrater r ≈ .50s), weak/arbitrary criteria for evaluating ratings, politically motivated inflation, conflicting purposes, inconsistent feedback effects, and a weak research–practice link. **Pro-ratings side** ([[Seymour Adler|Adler]], [[Michael Campion|Campion]], Grubb): performance is *always* evaluated in some form; "too hard" is no excuse for I-O psychology; ratings have at least modest reliability/validity and enable **[[Differentiated Evaluations|differentiated rewards]]** that attract and retain top performers. [[Elaine Pulakos|Pulakos]] moderates: the keep/drop question is the **wrong first question** — start from the business outcomes you want.

## The setup (Pulakos, background)

Dissatisfaction with performance management (PM) is at an all-time high: >90% of managers, employees, and HR heads feel PM fails to deliver. Managers/employees spend ~210/40 hrs per year on PM; a 10,000-person firm can spend **>$35M/year**. Within ~2 years, many firms (Eli Lilly, Adobe, GAP, Accenture, Deloitte, Microsoft) began abandoning or curtailing annual ratings. Key reframe (Pulakos & O'Leary 2011): formal-system changes have shown **no discernible impact** on PM behavior or performance over 25+ years — because the *mechanics* of formal systems conflict with providing frequent, credible, useful feedback.

## Anti-ratings case (Colquitt, Murphy, Ollander-Krane)

Performance rating is a **failed experiment**. Seven problem headings:

1. **Disappointing interventions.** Decades of work on rating scales ([[Halo Error|BARS]], BOS) and rater training (error training, [[Frame-of-Reference Training|frame-of-reference]]) produced only marginal gains. Landy & Farr (1980) called a **moratorium** on rating-scale research. The interventions assumed raters *lack ability*; the real problem is rater **motivation and goals**.
2. **Disagreement among raters.** Ratings from two sets of raters correlate only in the **.50s** ([[viswesvaran-schmidt-ones-2005-general-factor-job-performance|Viswesvaran et al.]] 1996) — far below what a good measure should show. Pooling across raters (360) mixes roles/perspectives → systematic, not resolvable, disagreement.
3. **Weak criteria for evaluating ratings.** [[Rater Error Measures|Rater-error measures]] (halo, leniency) rest on **arbitrary** distributional assumptions and are essentially **useless as accuracy indices**; accuracy can only be measured in tightly-controlled lab settings, not the field.
4. **Contextual / political effects.** Ratings are driven by nonperformance factors, most studied being the **[[Political Use of Performance Ratings|political use of appraisal]]** — raters pursue task, interpersonal, strategic, and internalized goals (Murphy & Cleveland 1995), pushing toward a **"Lake Wobegon"** inflation where nearly everyone is above average.
5. **Conflicting purposes.** Ratings serve rewards, development, validation, and legal documentation simultaneously — uses that differentiate *between* people conflict with uses that highlight differences *within* a person (Meyer, Kay & French 1965).
6. **Feedback not accepted/acted on.** The core PM assumption (feedback → motivation → improvement) fails: feedback effects are inconsistent (Kluger & DeNisi 1996); self-ratings exceed others' (fundamental attribution error); Barankay (2011) — 75% *say* they want ratings but most *choose* the un-rated job.
7. **Weak research–practice link.** Banks & Murphy (1985): a mismatch between researcher questions (cognitive processes) and practitioner problems; institutional theory explains why bad practices (e.g., GE/Welch forced ranking) persist.

Caveat from this side: **tread carefully** — reward/promotion/termination decisions currently ride on ratings; abandoning them requires solving those downstream problems.

## Pro-ratings case (Adler, Campion, Grubb)

1. **Ratings ≠ performance management.** The debate has fixated on the *least* meaningful (but most I-O-comfortable) element — measurement. Don't conflate ratings with PM as a whole.
2. **Performance is always evaluated.** Whether called ratings, standards, or judgments, evaluative decisions about people are made continuously and drive reward/role decisions. Even "development cultures" make where-you-are-now judgments — i.e., ratings. Many firms that "eliminated" ratings still enter numbers for merit/bonus, kept the scale but dropped the forced distribution, or just renamed dimensions ("overall impact"). Deloitte's 3-item scale is *still* rating.
3. **"Too hard" is no excuse for I-O psychology.** Differential psychology *is* the core of the field; measurement challenges don't justify abandoning it. For many roles ratings are **legally mandated** (civil service, teacher evaluations).
4. **Merits of ratings.** Meta-analytic evidence shows **at least modest reliability** (Rothstein 1990; Viswesvaran et al. 1996); pooled across raters, reliable. Ratings are the workhorse **criterion** in thousands of selection-validation studies. Subjective supervisor rating is often the *only feasible* method — objective measures are deficient, informal feedback hard to validate.
5. **[[Differentiated Evaluations|Value of differentiated evaluations.]]** Four converging lines: strong performers are attracted to firms that recognize individual contribution; strong performers **leave** firms that don't differentiate; weak performers leave firms that strongly reward high performance; weak performers **stay** where PFP differentiation is weak. CEB (2014): Millennials are *more* competitive and want relative differentiation.
6. **Artificial tradeoffs** drive abandonment: quantification-vs-humanization, bad-PM-vs-no-ratings, ratings-vs-conversations, feedback-vs-motivation — all **false dichotomies**. Organizations can have both.
7. **Alternatives are usually worse.** Without ratings, measurement is *less* precise (narrative only), merit management harder, lawsuits harder to defend, compensation/promotion decisions weaker.

## The path forward (both sides + Pulakos close)

Improve rather than abandon: **calibration** meetings, **competency modeling**, **[[360-Degree Feedback|360 feedback]]**, simpler forms/fewer dimensions, review panels. But the deeper point (Pulakos): **"Should we have ratings?" is the wrong first question.** Start from *what business problem are we solving* and *what do we want to evaluate and reward.* There is **no universal right answer** — "it depends" on the org's goals, maturity, trust, and management philosophy. Closing questions organizations find useful for driving PM reform are listed (business problem? what to reward? individual vs team? how much do we rely on ratings? readiness?).

## Where it sits in the vault

- Anchors the **keep-vs-drop** debate underlying [[Rating-less Reviews]]; provides the strongest *pro-rating* rationale that page already cites.
- The anti-side's seven problems overlap with — and pre-date the framing of — the **[[Three Assumptions of Performance Rating]]** (Pulakos et al. 2019).
- Extends [[Performance Appraisal]] and [[Rater Error Measures]] with the **political / motivational** diagnosis (rater goals, not rater ability).

> [!contradiction] Same debate, two verdicts
> The anti-ratings side treats interrater r ≈ .50 and rater-error problems as proof rating "does not measure up"; the pro-ratings side treats the *same* modest reliability as *good enough* (poolable, the only feasible criterion, workhorse of selection validation). The disagreement is over the **standard** ratings must meet, not the numbers. Both agree PM practice needs improvement and that day-to-day manager behavior matters most.
