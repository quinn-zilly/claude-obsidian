# Ellis & Davidi (2005) — After-Event Reviews: Drawing Lessons From Successful and Failed Experience

> [!key-insight] Core Claim
> Reviewing both successes and failures (FSAER) produces richer mental models and greater performance improvement than reviewing failures only (FAER). Before intervention, failure mental models are richer than success mental models — but systematic AER of successes closes this gap.

## Overview

Quasi-field experiment with 98 IDF soldiers in a ground navigation course. Two-company design: one company used traditional failure-only AER (FAER), the other used failure + success AER (FSAER). Three-stage study with pre-treatment and two post-treatment measurements of both performance and cognitive cause maps.

## Key Definitions

**After-Event Review (AER):** Organizational learning procedure giving learners opportunity to systematically analyze their behavior and evaluate component contributions to performance outcomes. Three functions:
1. **Self-explanation** — generate causal explanations for actions; builds if–then rules; integrates new with existing knowledge
2. **Data verification** — cross-validate information, counter confirmation bias and hindsight bias
3. **Feedback** — process-focused, not just outcome-focused; cognitive/task/functional validity feedback types

**FAER:** Failure-focused after-event review (traditional)
**FSAER:** Failure- and success-focused after-event review (innovative)

## Theoretical Background

### Why Failures Motivate More Than Successes
- Failed expectations trigger "tweaking" (Schank 1986 failure-driven learning)
- Unexpected/negative outcomes evoke attributional search (Weiner 1985, 2000)
- Failures create fear of invalidity → epistemic activity → mental model revision
- Successes: confirm prior expectations → closure → reduced search, complacency, risk aversion (Sitkin 1992)

### Why Successes Also Need Reviewing
1. When success may be due to luck (not ability) — learners need to understand causal structure
2. When cost of errors is extremely high (nuclear, pharma, military) — must understand *why* routines still work and under what conditions they don't
3. Propositions can be refuted by errors but there's no accepted analysis of how successes enrich mental models — successes just increase confidence in old ones

### Mental Models as Learning Indicators
- Richness (# constructs + # causal links) = depth/breadth of knowledge
- Experts hold more detailed mental models (Murphy & Wright 1984)
- Successful groups use more concepts / larger cognitive maps (Carley 1997)
- Complex knowledge structures → better performance (Neisser 1976 navigators)
- Learning = process of formulating and updating mental models (hypothesis generation + validation)

### Need for Closure (Kruglanski 1989)
- High need for closure → intensive knowledge acquisition
- Failures → fear of invalidity → raise need for closure
- AER of successes = artificial mechanism to raise need for closure without requiring failure

## Method

**Participants:** 98 male IDF soldiers, age 18–20, elite unit navigation course

**Design:** 3 stages
- Stage 1 (Week 1): Both companies, baseline navigation (3 exercises) + pretest cognitive maps
- Stage 2 (Week 2): Company A = FAER; Company B = FSAER (4 exercises)
- Stage 3 (Week 3): Both = FSAER (2 exercises) — cross-validation

**FSAER procedure:** Identify positive/negative aspects → debrief each route segment for success/failure decisions → note 3 aspects to improve or retain

**FAER procedure:** Focus on problems → ask about failures along route → note 3 improvement points (no success debrief)

**Time per AER:** ~15 min per soldier, individual (one-on-one), no significant time difference between groups

**Cognitive cause maps:** Structured interviews (10 soldiers/group × 3 time points = 120 possible). Nodes = constructs/causes; arrows = causal links. 16 content categories (planning, studying, feelings, weather, experience, knowledge, ability, etc.). Interrater reliability = 0.96.

**Performance measures:** (1) points accurately reached (45%), (2) navigation pace (45%), (3) map consultations (10%)

## Results

### Hypothesis 1: Failure maps richer than success maps ✓
Pre-manipulation: both groups showed significantly more nodes and links in failure maps vs. success maps (FSAER: p < .01, W = 1.00; FAER: p < .05, W = .60)

### Hypothesis 2: FSAER produces richer mental models ✓
FSAER group: significant increase in success map complexity M1→M2 (nodes and links, p < .01) and M2→M3 (p < .05). By M3: FSAER success maps *exceeded* failure maps (Fr(1) = 4.5, p < .034).
FAER group: no significant M1→M2 change; after exposure to FSAER in Stage 3, links increased significantly.

### Hypothesis 3: FSAER → greater performance improvement ✓
Significant linear Training × Group interaction F(1,74) = 3.795, p < .05, η² = .049. FSAER group showed steeper linear improvement despite starting lower and facing increasing task difficulty.

### Qualitative: Content shift
FSAER success maps shifted from situational causes (performance, feeling) toward root causes (planning, experience, studying) — reflects deeper causal reasoning rather than post-hoc attribution.

## Limitations

1. No "no AER" control group (army constraint)
2. No "success only" condition
3. Results may not generalize beyond definitive-feedback tasks
4. Could not statistically test mental model richness as mediator (small n per cell)
5. Observer (Davidi) knew hypotheses → possible bias

## Discussion / Implications

- AER is a mechanism to artificially raise need for closure about successes
- Encouraging success contemplation → more hypotheses generated about performance structure
- Metacognitive shift: learners start looking for internal root causes (planning, knowledge) vs. external situational factors
- Org implication: training programs focused only on failure-analysis miss substantial learning value
- Connects to Sitkin (1992): complements "strategy of small losses" for high-reliability orgs — reviewing successes may be safer alternative than engineered failures
