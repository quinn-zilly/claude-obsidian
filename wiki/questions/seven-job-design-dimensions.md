# Seven Job-Design Dimensions That Predict Performance and Engagement

Nav: [[index]] | [[Job Characteristics Model]] | [[Relational Job Design]]

> [!abstract] Answer
> The cleanest "7 dimensions" reading combines the [[Job Characteristics Model|JCM]]'s **five core characteristics** (Hackman & Oldham 1975/76) with **two social/relational characteristics** that the definitive meta-analysis showed add the most incremental variance ([[humphrey-nahrgang-morgeson-2007-work-design-meta-analysis|Humphrey, Nahrgang & Morgeson 2007]]; [[Relational Job Design|Grant 2007]]). The five motivational features work through three psychological states; the social features add up to **+40% incremental variance** for outcomes like organizational commitment beyond the motivational set.

## The Seven Dimensions

1. **Skill variety** — the job uses a range of different abilities → experienced *meaningfulness*.
2. **Task identity** — doing a whole, identifiable piece of work start-to-finish → *meaningfulness*.
3. **Task significance** — the work visibly affects other people's lives or work → *meaningfulness* (the strongest mediator overall).
4. **Autonomy** — discretion over how/when/what work gets done → experienced *responsibility for outcomes*.
5. **Feedback from the job** — the work itself tells you how well you're doing → *knowledge of results*.
6. **Social support** — help, advice, and backing from supervisor and coworkers ([[Work Design Questionnaire|WDQ]] social characteristic; incrementally predicts satisfaction at near-zero training cost).
7. **Beneficiary contact / relational architecture** — structured connection to the people the work helps ([[Relational Job Design|Grant's relational job design]]), driving prosocial motivation and effort.

> [!tip] The near-free-lunch finding
> Per [[morgeson-humphrey-2006-work-design-questionnaire|Morgeson & Humphrey 2006]], the **task** characteristics (1–5) raise satisfaction *without* raising training or compensation costs — only "knowledge" characteristics drive those up. **Social support** also incrementally predicts satisfaction and is *negatively* tied to training cost. The moderator to watch is **growth-need strength**, though even low-GNS workers show small gains.

## Worked Example: Hospital Dietary Aide

A bare version of this job is low-scope — fetch tray, deliver, collect. Building in each dimension:

1. **Skill variety** — train them to read modified-diet orders, check tray accuracy against allergy/texture restrictions, and note intake. Now it draws on perception, judgment, and interpersonal skill, not just carrying.
2. **Task identity** — make them own the *full* meal cycle for a unit: menu confirmation → tray assembly check → delivery → pickup → intake logging, rather than one isolated step on a line.
3. **Task significance** — reinforce that correct trays prevent aspiration, allergic reactions, and malnutrition in recovery. Significance is real here; the design job is making it *salient*.
4. **Autonomy** — let them set their own delivery route and sequencing, decide when to follow up on low-intake patients, and flag diet concerns to nursing directly.
5. **Feedback from the job** — build in self-recorded intake tracking and an accuracy check at pickup, so they directly see whether trays were right and whether patients ate.
6. **Social support** — pair new aides with an experienced mentor, hold brief shift huddles with the nursing team, and make a dietitian reachable. (Lifts satisfaction with essentially no training-cost penalty.)
7. **Beneficiary contact** — assign the *same* patients consistently so relationships form, and surface impact (a recovered patient's thanks; unit intake data). Even brief, real beneficiary contact measurably raises effort and persistence.

> [!note] Integration point
> Dimensions 3, 6, and 7 reinforce each other — significance becomes vivid precisely *through* beneficiary contact and a supportive team that talks about why the work matters.

## Caveat on Framing

"7 dimensions" is not a single canonical model — it is the JCM's 5 plus 2 social characteristics. Other valid groupings exist (e.g., the full [[Work Design Questionnaire|WDQ]] structure, or the [[Interdisciplinary Model of Job Design|Campion interdisciplinary model]]'s four approaches). [[oldham-fried-2016-job-design-past-present-future|Oldham & Fried 2016]] note the JCM is strong on attitudes/meaningfulness, modest on behavior, with mixed moderator support — so treat the performance link as real but weaker than the satisfaction link.
