# Knowledge Management in Academic Science: Problems and Proposed Solutions

> [!abstract] Core Claim
> The failures of knowledge management in academic science are **systemic and cultural, not technical**. They are fixable only by changing what science *rewards* (incentives) and how it *communicates, organizes, and shares* knowledge (infrastructure and labor). The technology to fix them already exists; the barriers are social.

Nosek's three-part **Scientific Utopia** series sharpens one diagnosis along three axes — *communication*, *incentives*, and *organization* — and proposes complementary structural fixes. [[munafo-2017-manifesto]] operationalizes that agenda into concrete reform categories, and [[thibault-2023-open-science-2-0]] argues a decade later that the reforms so far are necessary but not sufficient.

## Problems

### 1. The communication infrastructure is broken

(Source: [[nosek-bar-anan-2012-scientific-utopia-i]]) Scientific communication is inefficient, slow, incomplete, inaccurate, and unmodifiable: negative/null results go unpublished (the [[File-Drawer Problem]]); publication takes ~677 days on average; page limits force omission of methods and data; errors are undetectable in the polished final product; and corrections rarely happen post-publication. Journals **bundle four separable functions** — registration, dissemination, peer review, and archiving — and do each one suboptimally. The barriers to reform are *social* (inertia, vested prestige interests), not technical or financial.

### 2. The incentive structure rewards publishability over truth

(Source: [[nosek-2012-scientific-utopia-ii]]) A structural [[Conflict of Interest in Science|conflict of interest]]: the professional incentive (publish novel, positive results) directly competes with the accuracy incentive (get it right). This drives [[P-hacking]], [[HARKing]], selective reporting, optional stopping, and avoidance of replication. The evidence is stark — 94% of editors say replications aren't encouraged, >90% of published psychology findings are positive, and industry reproduction rates ran as low as 11% (Amgen) to 25% (Bayer).

> [!quote]
> "Openness is not needed because we are untrustworthy; it is needed because we are human."

Education campaigns and the "myth of self-correction" don't work, because they don't touch incentives.

### 3. The organizational model doesn't scale or distribute fairly

(Source: [[uhlmann-2019-crowdsourcing-science]]) The standard [[Vertical Integration (Science)|vertically integrated]] small-team model (one lab does everything) can't run studies that exceed single-lab capacity, and the [[Matthew Effect (Science)|Matthew effect]] compounds early advantages so resources concentrate in elite institutions. Analytic conclusions also turn out to be fragile: Silberzahn's 29 analyst teams given the *same* dataset produced effect sizes from negative to large positive (69% significant, 31% not) — conclusions are contingent on defensible analytic choices.

### 4. Openness on paper hasn't become openness in practice

(Source: [[thibault-2023-open-science-2-0]]) Even where sharing norms exist, the knowledge base is thin: a meta-analysis of >2M publications found 5–11% *declared* open data but only 1–3% *actually had* it; code sharing under 0.5%. Preregistration is ≤10% in psychology, and open access stalled around ~50% after 20+ years with costs merely shifting from subscriptions to APCs. Knowledge sits in custom spreadsheets and a static "version of record" rather than interoperable, machine-readable form. **Openness is necessary but not sufficient** — research can be fully open and still bad science.

## Proposed Solutions

### Utopia I — open the communication channel

Full digital publishing (no page limits; data/code/multimedia standard); [[Open Access]] (est. ~$900M/yr savings); disentangle publication from evaluation (arXiv-style deposit-first, filter-later); a grading system so journals become curated reading lists rather than gatekeepers; published peer reviews as citable products; and open continuous post-publication review, turning static papers into living documents. The principle is to **unbundle** the four functions journals conflate.

### Utopia II — restructure incentives to make truth competitive with publishability

Short-term bottom-up fixes (author/reviewer/editor disclosure checklists modeled on CONSORT, [[Paradigm-Driven Research]], replication-value metrics, crowdsourced replication); medium-term systemic shifts (soundness-not-importance journals like PLoS ONE, post-publication review making publication trivial); and the long-term core solution — **[[Open Science]] across three dimensions: open data, open methods/tools, and [[Open Workflow|open workflow]]** ([[Preregistration]] via registries that separate confirmatory from exploratory work).

### Utopia III — crowdsource science as a complementary organizational model

Distribute research *horizontally* across many contributors under a coordination team, alongside (not replacing) the vertical model. Three benefits: enabling big science beyond single-lab capacity; democratizing participation against the Matthew effect; and assessing robustness through multilab designs (aggregating 6 replication initiatives, crowd teams replicated 47% of 190 effects, with failures consistent across labs). Structural reforms: distribute grant funding more evenly and tie it to open practices, adopt [[CRediT]] contribution statements, reward replication in hiring/promotion, integrate crowd projects into teaching (CREP), and require independent replication for top outlets.

### Munafò manifesto — five concrete reform categories

(Source: [[munafo-2017-manifesto]]) Operationalizes the above into: **methods** (blinding, preregistration, stats training, team science); **reporting** (TOP/CONSORT/PRISMA standards, disclosure, conflict-of-interest rules); **reproducibility** (open data, materials, code; the 21-word disclosure statement); **evaluation** (preprints, post-publication review); and **incentives** (open-practice badges — which drove a 10× increase in open data at *Psychological Science* — Registered Reports, replication funding, open science in hiring/promotion).

### Open Science 2.0 — finish the job with funded infrastructure

(Source: [[thibault-2023-open-science-2-0]]) Four ingredients: a *modular, dynamic research record* (share components as ready, version-controlled, "record of versions" not static "version of record"); *standardization and interoperability* ([[FAIR Principles|FAIR]] data; GenBank/UniProt/AlphaFold as proof); *ongoing distributed quality control* throughout the cycle, partly AI-automated; and *reorganization of scientific labor* into specialized, credited roles. Its 7-point roadmap stresses **funding** infrastructure and training (not volunteers), aligning incentives across funders/journals, funding meta-research to test what works, and treating open science as a means, not an end. Caveats: could amplify Global North/South inequality; the flood of modular outputs needs curation; credit allocation gets harder.

## How the sources fit together

[[open-science-collaboration-2015]] is the empirical shock (36% replication) that validated the worry; the three **Utopia** papers are the prospective program (communication → incentives → organization); [[munafo-2017-manifesto]] operationalizes them into five reform categories; and [[thibault-2023-open-science-2-0]] argues those reforms are necessary-but-insufficient and sketches the next target. The through-line across all five: the failures are systemic and cultural, fixable only by changing what science rewards and how it organizes and shares knowledge.
