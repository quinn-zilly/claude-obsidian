# Knowledge Transfer

## Definition

Knowledge transfer is the process by which knowledge moves from one person, group, unit, or organization to another such that the recipient's knowledge or performance changes as a result. Transfer is distinct from mere communication — the knowledge must actually be absorbed and usable.

## Barriers to Transfer

**Causal ambiguity** (Szulanski, 1996) — when cause-effect relationships are poorly understood, recipients cannot tell which elements of a practice to replicate, even when exposed to the source. The "stickiness" of knowledge.

**Tacitness** — tacit knowledge (experiential, difficult to articulate) cannot be transferred through documents or instructions; it requires socialization, observation, or shared practice. See: [[Tacit vs Explicit Knowledge]]

**Relational factors** — lack of trust, weak ties, low motivation to share, or absence of shared identity all impede transfer (Levin & Cross, 2004; Kane et al., 2005).

**Absorptive capacity** — recipients must have sufficient prior related knowledge to receive and use new knowledge (Cohen & Levinthal, 1990). Relates to [[Knowledge Integration]] at the macro level.

## Mechanisms of Transfer

| Mechanism | Best for |
|---|---|
| Documentation, codification | Explicit, codifiable knowledge |
| Training and instruction | Structured procedural knowledge |
| Socialization, mentorship | Tacit knowledge and know-how |
| Personnel movement | Embodied expertise |
| Communities of practice | Context-specific tacit knowledge |
| IT-mediated systems (KMS) | Codified explicit knowledge at scale |

## Levels of Transfer

Transfer occurs within and across levels:
- **Individual to individual** — mentorship, conversation
- **Individual to group** — embedding in routines or transactive memory
- **Unit to unit** — knowledge spillovers, best-practice transfer
- **Interorganizational** — alliances, acquisitions, geographic proximity, industry associations

## Vicarious Learning

Organizations can learn not just from their own experience but from observing others — **vicarious learning** or **learning from peers**. Chan et al. (2014) showed that learning from high-ability peers at the same counter (cosmetics department) was more important than learning from adjacent peers or one's own experience.
