# Identifiability of Individual Inputs

[[shaw-2014-pay-dispersion|Shaw's (2014)]] proposed replacement for **work interdependence** as the key contingency governing reactions to **[[Pay Dispersion|pay dispersion]]**.

> [!key-insight] Beyond interdependence
> Nearly all work is interdependent, so interdependence is a poor discriminator (and its empirical support is weak). The real question is whether **individual contributions can be reliably observed and accurately assessed**.

- **High identifiability** (reliable markers exist — e.g., goals, assists, billable output): performance-based dispersion can be used effectively *even under high interdependence* (Trevor et al. 2012 hockey findings).
- **Low identifiability** (no established, accurate metric of contribution): dispersion based on individual performance may not be viable; employees are likely to react negatively to performance-differentiated pay.

Open agenda item — Shaw calls for research that measures identifiability directly and tests reactions to dispersed vs. compressed pay under varying identifiability. Conceptually adjacent to job-complexity mechanisms in [[joshi-son-roh-2015-women-close-the-gap|Joshi et al. (2015)]], where ambiguous/idiosyncratic complex jobs make individual inputs harder to scrutinize.
