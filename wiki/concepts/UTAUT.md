# UTAUT

The **Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology**, proposed by [[Viswanath Venkatesh|Venkatesh]] et al. (2003), synthesizes eight prior models of IS/IT adoption (TRA, [[Technology Acceptance Model|TAM]], TPB, C-TAM-TPB, the Motivational Model, MPCU, SCT, and IDT) into one model of why people accept and use technology.

## Four core constructs

- **Performance Expectancy (PE)** — belief that using the system improves job performance.
- **Effort Expectancy (EE)** — perceived ease of use of the system.
- **Social Influence (SI)** — perception that important others think one should use the system.
- **[[Facilitating Conditions]] (FC)** — belief that organizational/technical infrastructure supports use.

PE, EE, and SI predict **behavioural intention (BI)**; BI and FC predict **usage behaviour (UB)**.

## Four moderators

Gender, age, experience, and voluntariness — UTAUT's distinguishing feature versus TAM/TPB, intended to sharpen predictive power.

## Performance

In Venkatesh et al. (2003), UTAUT explained ~**70% of variance in behavioural intention** and ~50% in usage, outperforming all eight source models (which ranged 17–53%).

## Known limitations (per [[dwivedi-2019-utaut-revision|Dwivedi et al. (2019)]])

1. **Moderators rarely apply.** Most empirical studies drop them; few model all four. Voluntariness is meaningless under mandated adoption.
2. **A missing path.** UTAUT excludes FC → BI on the assumption that EE absorbs it, but empirical studies find FC predicts BI even with EE present.
3. **The individual is missing.** UTAUT's four constructs cover technology attributes (PE, EE) and contextual factors (FC, SI) but omit individual characteristics — most notably **attitude**. See [[Attitude as Mediator]] and [[Revised UTAUT Model]].

## Lineage

UTAUT2 (Venkatesh et al. 2012) extended the model for consumer contexts by adding hedonic motivation, price value, and habit.
