# Pay Dispersion

**Definition (Fulmer et al., 2023):** "Variation/differentiation in pay or unequal pay among members of a group."

---

## Types

- **Horizontal dispersion:** Variation among individuals doing similar jobs at the same level
- **Vertical dispersion:** Variation across hierarchical levels (pay-grade spreads, executive vs. employee gaps)

---

## What Matters — Source of Dispersion

The key finding from the empirical literature: *how dispersion arises matters as much as its magnitude*.

**Performance-based dispersion** (caused by [[Pay-for-Performance]]):
- Perceived as legitimate under equity theory (Adams, 1963)
- Associated with better performance, less turnover, enhanced attraction of high performers
- Signals that pay reflects contribution → reinforces PFP effectiveness

**Politically-based dispersion** (caused by favoritism, seniority, negotiation):
- Perceived as illegitimate
- Associated with worse performance and higher turnover
- Undermines trust in the compensation system

> Shaw (2014), Trevor, Reilly & Gerhart (2012), Kepes, Delery & Gupta (2009) all find that dispersion effects are largely driven by whether dispersion is performance-based.

---

## Equity ≠ Equality

A common assumption in dispersion research — that lower dispersion promotes cooperation and satisfaction — conflates equity with equality. Under equity theory (Adams, 1963):

- **Equal pay** for unequal performance creates **perceived under-reward inequity** for high performers
- This inequity → dissatisfaction, reduced effort, or exit
- True equity requires *proportional* pay, not equal pay

Shaw (2014) explicitly criticizes the oversimplification that less dispersion is always better.

---

## Connection to Sorting

[[Sorting Effect (Compensation)]]: PFP-driven dispersion attracts high performers (who expect to earn in the upper portion of the distribution) and encourages low performers to leave. This is a key mechanism by which pay dispersion promotes organizational performance — not just through motivation of current employees, but through workforce composition change.

---

## Pay Transparency × Pay Dispersion Interaction

Pay transparency may enhance social comparison — making dispersion more visible and salient. This creates a tension:
- Transparency + PFP-based dispersion → positive: stronger performance-pay instrumentality, legitimate inequality
- Transparency + politically-based dispersion → negative: illegitimacy becomes visible

Obloj & Zenger (2017): easy social comparisons may offset some positive incentive effects of performance-based pay differences.

---

## Research Gap

Fulmer et al. (2023) recommend future dispersion research clarify what it adds *beyond* studying PFP dispersion directly, since the evidence shows dispersion effects are largely tied to their performance-basis. Studying total dispersion without decomposing its sources conflates very different phenomena.
