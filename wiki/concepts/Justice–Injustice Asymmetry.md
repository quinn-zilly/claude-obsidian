# Justice–Injustice Asymmetry

The question of whether **justice** and **injustice** are simply opposite ends of one continuum, or **distinct constructs** with different antecedents and consequences (Colquitt et al. 2015; Hillebrandt & Barclay 2013; Mayer 2012).

## The default assumption (and its challenge)

- Most empirical work treats injustice as the **opposite of justice on the same continuum**, measuring *adherence* to justice norms and neglecting norm **violations**.
- Prominent justice theories (self-interest, group engagement, [[Fairness Heuristic Theory|fairness heuristic]], uncertainty management, [[Deontic Justice Perspective|deontic]]) **do not** distinguish justice from injustice.

## Evidence for distinctness

Colquitt et al. (2015), interview + survey studies:

- **Injustice evokes more nuanced, differentiated reactions**; justice reactions are vaguer, expectation-based. Injustice more strongly drives focus of attention, (dis)trust, and [[Counterproductive Work Behavior|counterproductive behaviors]].
- But **justice is sometimes more salient** — predicting self-esteem-related behavior, reciprocation, OCB, task performance. Trust is influenced roughly equally by both.
- Interviews produced ~29,000 injustice-related words vs. ~21,000 justice-related — hinting at a **negativity bias** ("bad is stronger than good," Baumeister et al. 2001), though Colquitt et al. did **not** cleanly confirm injustice-salience dominance.

## Open problems

- **Construct validity of injustice** vs. adjacent constructs: incivility, microaggression, bullying, abusive supervision, CWB. Needs sharper definition.
- Whether the distinction matters for the [[Deontic Justice Perspective|deontic]]/moral account and for conflict (injustice may relate to conflict more strongly than justice does).
