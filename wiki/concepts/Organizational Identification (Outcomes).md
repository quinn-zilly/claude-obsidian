# Organizational Identification (Outcomes)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Social Identification]]

> [!note] Companion page
> The **definition** of organizational identification (a perception of oneness with the organization; "who am I?") lives on **[[Social Identification]]** (Ashforth & Mael 1989). This page covers its **meta-analytic outcomes** and its structural position, per [[lee-park-koo-2015-organizational-identification-meta-analysis|Lee, Park & Koo (2015)]].

## OI as a basis for attitudes and behaviors

Rather than one more correlate of commitment, OI sits **upstream** as a basis from which whole sets of attitudes and behaviors are engendered. In the [[Attitude-Behavior Relations|Fishbein & Ajzen model]], OI strongly predicts **general attitude** (β=.74), and retains a **direct effect on general behavior beyond attitude** — because identification involves the self-definition of the individual *and* the organization.

## Meta-analytic outcome estimates (Lee et al. 2015)

| Outcome | Corrected ρ | k | N |
|---|---|---|---|
| Job involvement, job satisfaction, affective commitment | positive (key attitudes) | — | — |
| **In-role performance** | .27 | 41 | 13,870 |
| **Extra-role performance** (OCB, voice) | .42 | 53 | 14,459 |

Identification predicts **discretionary/extra-role** behavior more strongly than in-role — strong identifiers treat the organization's goals as their own. In-role effects inflate under self-rating (ρ=.33 self vs. lower for other-rated).

## Culture moderation

Effects are **stronger in collectivistic (and long-term-oriented) cultures** (Hofstede), where identity overlap between person and collective is more valued.

## Distinction from commitment

> [!contradiction] Identification vs. commitment
> Affective [[Organizational Commitment]] and OI both capture psychological attachment, but commitment is a general **social-exchange-based** attitude that **lacks the self-defining nature** central to identification. Treating OI as merely a facet of commitment understates its unique, upstream causal role. See [[Social Identification]] for the Ashforth & Mael construct separation.

Source: [[lee-park-koo-2015-organizational-identification-meta-analysis|Lee, Park & Koo (2015)]].
