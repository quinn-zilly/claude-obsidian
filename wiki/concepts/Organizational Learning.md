# Organizational Learning

## Core Definition

**Organizational learning is a change in the organization's knowledge that occurs as a function of experience** (Fiol & Lyles, 1985; Argote & Miron-Spektor, 2011). The knowledge can manifest in changed cognitions or behaviors and includes both explicit and tacit components.

The core cycle (Argote & Miron-Spektor, 2011):

> Experience × Context → Knowledge → (modifies) Context → shapes future Experience

## The Four Learning Processes (Argote et al., 2021)

| Process | What it covers |
|---|---|
| **Search** | Exploring for new knowledge; local vs. distant search |
| **Knowledge Creation** | Generating new knowledge from direct or vicarious experience |
| **Knowledge Retention** | Embedding knowledge in repositories so it persists |
| **Knowledge Transfer** | Moving knowledge across people, units, or organizations |

Organizations vary in learning rate because each process is affected differently by experience dimensions and organizational context.

## The Active and Latent Context (Argote & Miron-Spektor, 2011)

**Active context** — members, tools, and tasks and their networks — the elements that actually act and through which learning occurs.

**Latent context** — culture, structure, identity, incentives, strategy — shapes who the members are, what tools they have, which tasks they perform, but cannot act directly.

Knowledge is embedded in both layers. Latent context (e.g., trust, psychological safety, shared identity) enables or inhibits the active context's ability to learn.

## Learning Curves

The canonical empirical phenomenon: as organizations accumulate experience, performance improves at a decreasing rate. First documented by Wright (1936) for aircraft production. Characterized by a **progress ratio** (e.g., 80% = costs fall to 80% with each doubling of cumulative output).

Key complication: **knowledge depreciation**. If production slows, knowledge can be lost and costs rise back above previously achieved levels (Lockheed L-1011 case). Learning is not simply cumulative.

## Levels of Analysis

Organizational learning occurs at individual, group, organizational, and interorganizational levels — each contributing independently. Individual learning is necessary but not sufficient: for group or organizational learning, knowledge must be embedded in supraindividual repositories (routines, databases, transactive memory).

## Relation to Knowledge Integration

[[Knowledge Integration]] is one framing of what organizations do when they learn — combining specialist knowledge across levels and boundaries. Organizational learning is the temporal process; knowledge integration is the combinatorial act.
