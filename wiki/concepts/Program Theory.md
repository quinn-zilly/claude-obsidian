# Program Theory

A **program theory** is an explicit model — usually a graphical diagram, sometimes tabular or narrative — specifying the presumed cause-and-effect relationships among programmatic actions, outcomes, and contextual factors. It is the crux of [[Theory-Driven Evaluation]].

## Linear model (program logic)

**Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Initial outcomes → Intermediate outcomes → Long-term outcomes**

- **Program process theory** = inputs + activities + outputs (resources, actions like training/service delivery, immediate results).
- **Program impact theory** = the chain of outcomes: initial (knowledge/skills/attitudes) → intermediate (behavioral change) → long-term (distal impacts, e.g. reduced HIV incidence).

Favored historically by Weiss and Wholey.

## Nonlinear / ecological models

Chen (2005) and Rogers (2008) advocate more contextualized, systems-oriented models with an **action model** (implementing organizations, partners, protocols, implementers, ecological context) and a **change model** (intervention → determinants → outcomes). Critics (Patton) question how well any fixed model represents complex, adaptive, open systems.

## Sources of program theory (Donaldson)

Four potential sources: (1) prior theory and research, (2) implicit theories of those close to the program, (3) observation of the program in operation, (4) exploratory research testing critical assumptions. Patton favors deductive, inductive, or user-oriented approaches; Chen favors a stakeholder-oriented approach with the evaluator as facilitator.

## Quality criterion

A program theory should be **plausible** (credible on its face) and stipulate the cause-and-effect sequence through which actions produce long-term outcomes.
