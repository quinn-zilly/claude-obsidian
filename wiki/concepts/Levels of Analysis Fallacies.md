# Levels of Analysis Fallacies

Nav: [[index]] | [[Multilevel Theory & Methods MOC]]

**Findings do not generalize neatly across levels of analysis** except under very restrictive conditions (Firebaugh 1979). Relationships at one level may be stronger, weaker, or even **reversed** at another (Ostroff 1993). Blindly generalizing across levels commits a fallacy.

## The two mirror-image fallacies

- **Ecological fallacy** — generalizing an **aggregate**-level relationship **down** to individuals. Ecological correlations based on aggregated data are generally **inflated** estimates of lower-level relationships (Robinson 1950; Thorndike 1939). Plagues macro-oriented policy disciplines (sociology, economics, epidemiology, education).
- **Atomistic fallacy** — generalizing an **individual**-level relationship **up** to higher levels. Common in I/O psychology when scholars propose team- or organization-level interventions from individual-level data (e.g., "higher aggregate cognitive ability from selection will raise organizational performance").

> [!example]
> An individual-level charisma→performance relationship may vanish or reverse at the team level: perceptions may vary within but not between teams (no between-team variance to predict), or charismatic leaders may be **assigned** to low-performing teams (negative team-level relationship).

## The deeper problem: misalignment
Most multilevel controversies stem from **misalignment** among the level of a construct, its measurement, and its analytic representation. Symptoms: blind aggregation of individual data to represent unit constructs, use of unit-level data to infer lower-level relations (aggregation bias / ecological correlation), and use of informants who lack unique knowledge. Misalignment degrades **construct validity** and generalizability.

The remedy is not statistical but conceptual: type each construct ([[Global, Shared, and Configural Unit Properties]]), justify aggregation against the composition assumption ([[Aggregation Justification Indices]]), and sample so every relevant level has variance.
