# Asymmetrical Domain Permeability Model

A **boundary-theory** account of gendered **[[Work–Family Conflict]]** (Pleck 1977; Ashforth, Kreiner & Fugate 2000). Boundaries around roles vary in permeability/flexibility; stronger, less-permeable boundaries → more segmentation → less cross-role conflict.

## Prediction (asymmetrical version)
Through socialization and traditional division of labor, **women build stronger boundaries around family** and **men around work**. Therefore women allow fewer intrusions into family (less WIF) and men fewer into work (less FIW).

## Rival: "male segmentation"
A competing boundary account (Rothbard 2001; Crosby 1991) argues women have **more permeable boundaries in general** — so women segment less and experience **more** conflict in *both* directions. Shockley et al. test these as competing hypotheses in one model.

## Verdict in Shockley et al. (2017)
- **Partial support** for asymmetrical permeability — **WIF only**: stronger family boundaries → less WIF (supported).
- For **FIW**, the gender→boundary path is significant but runs **opposite** to prediction (women form *stronger* work boundaries, which *reduces* their FIW).
- **Male segmentation: not supported** (WIF or FIW).

The opposite-signed FIW paths (family hours ↑ FIW; women's stronger work boundary ↓ FIW) **cancel out**, which is why the overall gender→FIW relationship is near-null.
