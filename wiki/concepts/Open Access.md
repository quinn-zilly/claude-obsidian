# Open Access

Scientific literature made freely available to readers without subscription paywalls or access fees. A core pillar of the [[Open Science]] reform movement and one of six proposed changes in [[nosek-bar-anan-2012-scientific-utopia-i|Nosek & Bar-Anan (2012)]].

## Two Main Models

**Gold OA** — Publisher makes the article immediately and permanently free at point of publication. Author or institution typically pays an Article Processing Charge (APC). Example: *PLOS ONE*, *PLOS Biology*.

**Green OA** — Author self-archives a version (preprint or accepted manuscript) in an institutional or subject repository. The published version may remain behind a paywall; the deposited version is free. Example: PsyArXiv, arXiv, PubMed Central.

## Economic Argument (Nosek & Bar-Anan 2012)

- Estimated $900M annual savings if the scientific community shifted from subscription-based to open-access publishing
- The cost argument: subscription model requires institutions (often publicly funded) to *pay again* to access research that was itself often publicly funded
- Core moral claim: taxpayer-funded research should be publicly accessible

## Scientific Communication Rationale

Open Access alone does not solve the [[Replication Crisis]], but it is a precondition for:
- Large-scale systematic review and meta-analysis (can't replicate what you can't read)
- Rapid dissemination of corrections and updates
- Machine-readable science (text mining, AI-assisted synthesis)
- Global equity: researchers in low-resource institutions can access the literature

## Relationship to Preprint Infrastructure

Nosek & Bar-Anan argue for disentangling *publication* from *evaluation* — depositing in preprint repositories (arXiv model) achieves immediate OA regardless of peer review status. This separates "does it exist" (always yes, immediately) from "is it good" (evaluated separately).

## Cross-Domain Connections

- **[[Linked Open Data]]**: Both OA and LOD rest on the same principle — knowledge should be freely accessible and machine-readable as a structural good. DBpedia is, in effect, open access for encyclopedic knowledge.
- **[[Knowledge Transfer]] (KM)**: Subscription paywalls introduce friction into the knowledge transfer channel; OA removes that friction, making tacit-to-explicit and explicit-to-explicit transfer faster.
