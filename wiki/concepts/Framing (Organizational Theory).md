# Framing (Organizational Theory)

## Core Distinction

> [!key-insight] The Central Problem
> The construct of "frame" means fundamentally different things at different levels of analysis. Cornelissen & Werner (2014) argue the field must distinguish **cognitive frames** (stable knowledge schemas, accessed via priming) from **acts of framing** (dynamic, socially situated meaning construction in language and interaction). Conflating these produces reification, static bias, and false paradigm unity.

**Priming** = activating a pre-existing cognitive frame based on observable cues; largely automatic; top-down.  
**Framing** = actively constructing new meaning in context through language, symbols, and interaction; bottom-up; social.

Both are real and reciprocally connected: an act of framing in language may prime a cognitive frame; the activated cognitive frame shapes the available framings.

## Genealogy

The construct was formulated by Burke (1937) and Bateson (1955/1972), popularized by Goffman (1974). It then diffused across linguistics, sociology, cognitive psychology, journalism, and organizational theory — each tradition pulling the concept in a different direction.

## Three Levels of Frame Analysis

### Micro — Cognition in Action

Cognitive frames are knowledge structures (schemas) that organize perception and inference. Originating in the Carnegie tradition (March & Simon 1958), they are:
- **Top-down:** an activated frame creates expectations that guide subsequent perception
- **Retrospective:** abstracted from prior experience
- **Binding:** reinforced through in-group communication and socialization

The basic unit of sensemaking (Weick 1995): *cue + relation + frame = meaning*.

When cues violate frame expectations → sensebreaking; recovery requires reframing via analogy or conceptual blending.

Frames of reference in **behavioral decision-making** draw on prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky 1979):
- **Equivalency frames:** same information, positive vs. negative framing → different risk preferences
- **Emphasis/issue frames:** highlight qualitatively different considerations (legitimacy vs. efficiency)

See: [[Cognitive Frame]], [[Framing Effects]], [[Priming vs Framing]]

### Meso — Meaning Construction in Organizations and Movements

At the meso level, framing is studied as social construction and negotiation of meaning within organized groups. Drawing on symbolic interactionism (Goffman, Blumer), it is bottom-up and interactive.

Four major sub-literatures:
1. **Strategic frames** — collectively constructed competitive assumptions within an industry (Nadkarni & Narayanan 2007; Kaplan 2008)
2. **Strategic framing** — purposeful communication by leaders/managers to reshape others' interpretive frames (Fiss & Zajac 2006; Gioia & Chittipeddi 1991)
3. **Technological frames** — shared assumptions about technology's nature and appropriate use (Orlikowski & Gash 1994)
4. **Collective action frames** — joint accounts mobilizing activism; diagnostic/prognostic/motivational tasks (Benford & Snow 2000)

Common limitation: focus on *frames as outputs* rather than *framing as process*, leading to static, reified accounts.

See: [[Strategic Framing]], [[Technological Frame]], [[Collective Action Frame]]

### Macro — Field-Level Frames and Institutional Change

At the macro level, frames are cultural-cognitive templates that structure expectations and scripts within institutional fields. They become institutionalized through a process of conventionalization and naturalization.

Key constructs:
- **Field frame** (Lounsbury et al. 2003): endogenous, durable but changeable interpretive convention
- **Institutional frame** (Weber & Glynn 2006; Borum 2004): taken-for-granted; naturalized; actors no longer recognize it as a frame
- **Framing contests** (Ryan 1991): struggles over meaning in periods of field upheaval
- **Discursive opportunity structures** (Koopmans & Statham 1999): culturally salient discourses that make certain frames resonant at particular moments

Trajectory (Powell & Colyvas 2008): novel framing → conventionalized metaphor → cognitively embedded field frame → naturalized institutional frame ("dead metaphor").

See: [[Field Frame]], [[Institutional Frame]]

## Multi-Level Integration (Cornelissen & Werner 2014)

Fillmore's (1975, 1982) frame semantics provides the integrating anchor: frames are structured semantic representations invoked by words, rooted in background cultural knowledge.

Key cross-level mechanisms:
- **Lamination / editing** (Diehl & McFarland 2010; Weber & Glynn 2006): actors reframe by building on but extending institutional frames
- **Interactive frames** (Tannen 1985; Goffman 1981 "footing"): real-time negotiation of common interpretive ground
- **Common ground propagation** (Clark 1996): personal common ground (interaction) → communal common ground (field frames)
- **Practice grounding** (Reay et al. 2013): frames stabilize when anchored in habitual enactment
