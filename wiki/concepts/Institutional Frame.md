# Institutional Frame

## Definition

An **institutional frame** is a fully naturalized, taken-for-granted cognitive frame that structures expectations and scripts behavior across an institutional field. Actors no longer recognize it as a frame — it is simply "how things are." The original metaphorical or analogical source from which the frame was constructed has been forgotten.

Borum (2004); Weber & Glynn (2006): institutional frames are the "content" of institutions — latent meaning structures organizing social and cultural experience, stabilizing power arrangements, and cueing role enactments.

Scott (2003, p. 880): the cultural-cognitive aspect of institutions involves "the creation of shared conceptions that constitute the nature of social reality and the frames through which meaning is made."

## Trajectory from Framing to Institutionalization

Following Powell & Colyvas (2008) — "career of metaphor":

1. **Novel framing** — actors introduce an analogy/metaphor to make sense of a new phenomenon (e.g., chefs as "creators"; Rao et al. 2003)
2. **Active comparison** — interactants align target and source domains; import inferences (what does it mean for a chef to be a creator?)
3. **Conventionalization** → [[Field Frame]]: the comparison becomes a conventional category; automatic rather than deliberate
4. **Naturalization** → **Institutional Frame**: widely diffused and repeatedly instantiated; actors "forget" the metaphorical origin; the frame is simply "true"

"Making metaphor dead" = institutionalization (Powell & Colyvas 2008, p. 294).

## Properties

**Taken-for-grantedness:** Actors are not aware they are using a frame — it is the invisible background against which action occurs. Berger & Luckmann (1966); Douglas (1986).

**Structures of expectation** (Tannen 1979, 1985): primes individuals to elaborate on typical roles and behavioral scripts; high inertia.

**Durability:** Survives without actors actively maintaining or endorsing it; simply is.

**Invisibility:** Unlike [[Field Frame]]s (which are visible as contested conventions), institutional frames are only visible when violated or from outside the field.

## Relation to Institutional Logics

Thornton, Ocasio & Lounsbury (2012) increasingly define institutional logics as "frames of reference that condition actors' choices for sensemaking, the vocabulary they use to motivate action and their sense of self and identity" — converging on the institutional frame concept.

Beckert (2010, p. 607): neo-institutional theory "emphasizes the role of cognitive frames and meaning structures as decisive for the explanation of economic outcomes by broadening the notion of institution; institutions are defined as inter-subjectively shared meanings and thereby become almost indistinguishable from cognitive frames."

## Agency Within Institutional Frames

Despite their taken-for-grantedness, actors can "laminate" (Diehl & McFarland 2010) or "edit" (Weber & Glynn 2006) institutional frames — building on the prevailing frame while introducing deviations. Creative writing builds on established genres; analogously, reframing builds on institutional frames rather than creating entirely new systems of meaning.

**Lefsrud & Meyer (2012):** oil industry engineers contested the institutionalized frame of human-caused global warming using personal biographical narratives of expertise — demonstrating how institutional frames can be challenged via "defensive institutional work."

## Methodological Challenge

Institutional frames are, by definition, invisible to participants — they cannot be directly reported or easily observed. Researchers must infer them from:
- Patterns of emphasis in discourse (what goes unsaid)
- Violations and breakdowns (when the frame becomes visible)
- Comparative historical analysis (before naturalization vs. after)
- Ethnographic immersion that gives access to tacit assumptions
