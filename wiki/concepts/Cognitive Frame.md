# Cognitive Frame

## Definition

A **cognitive frame** (also: frame of reference, knowledge schema) is a structured knowledge representation in memory that organizes and directs the perception, inference, and behavior of individuals. When activated by environmental cues, a cognitive frame creates expectations about a situation and guides subsequent interpretation.

Carnegie tradition definition (March & Simon 1958): individuals in organizations "cognitively detect regularities in environments and compress them into much less detailed cognitive frames of reference" that then guide perception, inference, and behavior.

Starbuck & Milliken (1988): frames enable individuals to "comprehend, understand, explain, attribute, extrapolate, and predict."

## Core Properties

**Top-down / theory-driven:** An activated frame filters incoming cues through prior expectation, not the other way around. Gavetti & Levinthal (2000): frame-based cognition dominates in all but the most novel situations.

**Inferential:** Frames are not merely storage bins — they generate inferences. Goffman (1974, p. 38): "we can hardly glance at anything without applying a framework, thereby forming conjectures as to what occurred before and expectations as to what is likely to happen now."

**Relational structure:** Frames encode causal and contiguous links — roles ↔ actions, causes ↔ effects, actions ↔ consequences — not just feature lists. This distinguishes them from categories (feature-based).

**Reference-dependent:** An activated frame sets a reference point relative to which subsequent information is evaluated (prospect theory application; George et al. 2006).

**Socially reinforced:** Frames stabilize through "in-group communication" with others who share the same frame (March & Simon 1958). This creates institutional stickiness.

## Sensemaking as Frame Activation

Weick (1995) — basic unit of meaning: **cue + relation + frame**. Frames are past socialization; cues are present experience. Sensemaking = constructing a relational connection between them.

Classic example (Mann Gulch fire, Weick 1993): the label "10 o'clock fire" activated a frame that created expectations about outcomes, roles, and tactics — blinding firefighters to an alternative interpretation until collapse.

**Sensebreaking** occurs when cues violate activated-frame expectations severely enough to trigger a meaning void. Recovery requires frame-shifting, which may be resisted when collective commitment to the frame is strong.

## Cognitive Change: Analogy and Blending

Two main mechanisms for generating new cognitive frames:

**Analogical reasoning** (Gavetti et al. 2005; Benner & Tripsas 2012): project a familiar source frame onto a novel target domain to generate new inferences. Risk: superficial similarity may lead to misleading projections (Lovallo et al. 2012). Offset by reflexivity and heterogeneous comparisons.

**Conceptual blending** (Cornelissen & Clarke 2010; Fauconnier & Turner 2002): align and integrate elements from two or more input frames into a blended mental space with emergent structure not present in either input. Examples: "micro-credit + finance" (Battilana & Dorado 2010); "environmental auditing + financial reporting" (Etzion & Ferraro 2010).

## Rigidity and Failure

Cognitive frames are "limited and limiting by their very nature" (Cornelissen & Werner 2014) — their inferential capacity is bounded by what is already represented in the frame. In novel or unprecedented circumstances, this creates:
- Perceptual blindness to disconfirming cues
- Commitment escalation (Weick's Bhopal and Mann Gulch cases)
- Resistance to reframing despite evidence

## Relation to Framing

> [!key-insight] Priming vs. Framing
> **Priming** = accessing/activating a pre-existing cognitive frame (top-down, largely automatic).  
> **Framing** = actively constructing new meaning in social interaction (bottom-up, dynamic).  
> Most micro-level organizational cognition research studies priming effects, not framing as meaning construction. The distinction matters for research design and theoretical claims.
