# Horizontal Distribution (Science)

## Definition

The complementary model to vertical integration in which research ownership, resources, and expertise are distributed across many contributors. Rather than one team controlling the full pipeline, numerous researchers each carry out specific components under the direction of a core coordination team.

Also called: **crowdsourcing science**, **distributed science**, **large-scale collaboration**.

## Key Features

- Ownership distributed: many contributors, not one team
- Resources pooled: combined capacity exceeds any single lab
- Communication variable: from largely independent (curated contributions) to highly collaborative (crowd collaboration)
- Inclusiveness variable: from select experts to open participation
- Credit via contribution statements (CRediT taxonomy) rather than authorship position

## Why Horizontal Distribution Helps

**Breaks the vertical incentive trap:** when many analysts analyze the same data, no individual's career depends on a particular outcome → reduces [[Motivated Reasoning]] and analytic flexibility pressures.

**Enables scale impossible locally:** hundreds of labs, thousands of participants, multiple populations and stimulus sets → tests generalizability rather than assuming it.

**Exposes analytic variability:** Silberzahn et al. (2018) showed 29 teams produced 29 distinct specifications and effect-size estimates ranging from negative to large positive for the same question — a fact only visible via horizontal distribution.

## Vertical vs. Horizontal — Not Binary

The two models are a continuum. Team size has grown from 1.9 (1960s) to 3.5 (2005) authors on average. Team-authored papers are more cited. Horizontal distribution is the next step, not a replacement.
