# Work Analysis (Job Analysis)

Systematic process for gathering information about work **activities** (tasks, duties, responsibilities) and the **personal attributes** (KSAOs) an employee needs to accomplish org objectives for a job — via analysis, synthesis, and inference. "Work analysis" (WA) is the broad umbrella term (Sánchez & Levine 2001); "job analysis" is the historically dominant narrower term. WA encompasses tasks, abilities, roles, and the evolving nature of work.

## Two Outputs
- **Job description** — activities, workflow, conditions, methods
- **Job specification** — inferred worker traits / KSAOs for successful performance

Three information categories: job identification, job description, job specification. WA feeds both **predictor** and **criterion** development and their alignment — the cornerstone of HRM decisions (selection, promotion, training).

## Two Orientations
See [[Task-Oriented vs Worker-Oriented Analysis]].

## Methods (Pearlman & Sánchez 2010)
Four applications: predictor dev, criterion dev, validity-evidence dev, validity-evidence extension. Qualitative (observation, interview) + quantitative (job-task inventories, F-JAS, PPRF, PIC, PAQ, O*NET databases). Should be **iterative**.

## Reliability Warning
**Consistency ≠ agreement** (Ployhart et al. 2006). High interrater correlations can hide divergent rater means; a cut-off based on the mean may represent no rater. Morgeson & Campion (1997): 16 sources of inaccuracy (social + cognitive).

## Legal Anchor
WA is legally mandatory as the foundation of any validity defense under the [[EEOC-1978-uniform-guidelines-employee-selection|Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection (1978)]]; it documents the KSAO–task **job-relatedness** ([[Job-Relatedness]]) that EEO requires.
