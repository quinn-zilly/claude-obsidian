# Dynamic Criteria

Criteria are **temporally dimensional** — a criterion must be taken at some point in time, and results differ by when (Weitz 1961). "Dynamic" has three distinct meanings (Barrett, Caldwell & Alexander 1985):

1. **Changes over time in average group performance** — e.g., Ghiselli & Haire (1960) saw 650% productivity growth over 10 years, but only among survivors; broader evidence is weak (Barrett et al. 1985).
2. **Changes over time in validity coefficients** — the *changing-task model* (importance of criteria shifts) vs the *changing-subjects model* (individuals' ability levels shift) (Henry & Hulin 1987). Neither has unqualified support, but longitudinal studies show validity **decrements** more often than increments.
3. **Changes over time in rank ordering** on the criterion — the most-studied form. Evidence (Deadrick & Madigan 1990; Hulin et al. 1990; Hoffmann et al. 1993) shows a **simplex pattern**: adjacent-time correlations are high, distant-time correlations low, so prediction deteriorates with lead time.

> [!note] Substitute criteria along the timeline
> Because the [[Ultimate Criterion|ultimate criterion]] is impractical, criteria are taken as **immediate** (near-term, e.g., final-day training scores) → **intermediate** (~6 months, e.g., supervisory ratings) → **summary** (longer-term averages/totals that balance out short-term noise). All are partial (Thorndike 1949).

Viswesvaran & Ones (2000) note the related debate on whether **rank order or correlations** actually change, citing Barrett et al.'s (1989) finding of scant evidence, alongside the typical/maximal distinction ([[Typical vs Maximal Performance]]).
