# Construct Validity (Causal Generalization)

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]]

The validity of inferences from the **sampling particulars** of a study — the specific units, treatments, observations, and settings used — to the **higher-order constructs** they are meant to represent. Third component of the [[Validity Typology]]; a form of [[Causal Generalization]] (representation).

> [!note] Two senses of "construct validity"
> This page covers the **experimental / causal-generalization** sense from Shadish, Cook & Campbell (2002): correctly *labeling the operations* of an experiment. The existing [[Construct Validity]] page covers the **psychometric** sense (Cronbach & Meehl → Messick, Borsboom): defending a *test-score interpretation*. Related roots (both trace to Cronbach & Meehl 1955), distinct problems. Not a contradiction — different scope.

## Why construct inference is hard

Constructs have fuzzy, prototype-based boundaries; the "prototypical" features depend on context and language community. Social-science constructs (aggression, incentive, intention) lack the natural "joints" of physical kinds. So a one-to-one operation↔construct mapping is impossible — **definitional operationalism is rejected**.

## Fostering it

Clear explication → select matching instances → multiple operations *and* multiple methods per construct → assess slippage → revise. Cook & Campbell (1979) extended construct validity from outcomes to treatments; SCC (2002) further to **persons and settings**.

## 14 threats

Inadequate Explication · Construct Confounding · Mono-Operation Bias · Mono-Method Bias · Confounding Constructs with Levels · Treatment-Sensitive Factorial Structure · Reactive Self-Report Changes · Reactivity to the Experimental Situation · Experimenter Expectancies · Novelty & Disruption · Compensatory Equalization · Compensatory Rivalry (John Henry effect) · Resentful Demoralization · Treatment Diffusion.
