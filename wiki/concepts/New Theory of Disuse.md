# New Theory of Disuse

The New Theory of Disuse (Bjork & Bjork, 1992) proposes that memories have two distinct strengths: **storage strength** (how well consolidated a memory is) and **retrieval strength** (how readily accessible a memory is right now). These two strengths are dissociable and follow opposite dynamics.

## Core Distinction

- **Storage strength**: accumulates with learning; does not decrease with time or disuse; only goes up
- **Retrieval strength**: reflects current accessibility; decays with time and disuse; highly context-dependent

## Key Implication

High current retrieval strength (i.e., something is easy to remember right now) actually *slows* the growth of storage strength when the item is restudied. This is why desirable difficulties work: when retrieval strength is low (information has been "forgotten"), successfully retrieving it produces a larger gain in storage strength than restudying when retrieval strength is still high.

## Relationship to Desirable Difficulties

The New Theory of Disuse is the theoretical backbone of [[Desirable Difficulties]]. It explains why:
- Spacing is effective (retrieval strength decays between sessions, amplifying the benefit of re-encoding)
- The [[Testing Effect]] is effective (low-retrieval-strength retrieval boosts storage strength)
- Massed practice feels effective but isn't (keeps retrieval strength high, limiting storage gains)
