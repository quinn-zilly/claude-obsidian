# Basic Psychological Needs Theory (BPNT)

Fourth mini-theory of [[Self-Determination Theory]]. Formally articulates the three basic psychological needs — [[Autonomy (SDT)]], [[Competence (SDT)]], [[Relatedness (SDT)]] — and specifies their role in psychological growth, integrity, and well-being.

## Core Propositions

1. The three needs are universal — found across cultures, ages, and domains
2. Need *satisfaction* → well-being, vitality, integrated functioning
3. Need *frustration* → ill-being, psychopathology, defensive functioning
4. Satisfaction and frustration are **distinct constructs** — not simply opposite ends of one dimension

## Satisfaction vs. Frustration

Critical distinction established in 2000s–2010s literature: need frustration (active thwarting) has effects beyond the absence of need satisfaction. Frustration predicts ill-being uniquely and powerfully; satisfaction predicts well-being. Measuring only satisfaction misses the frustration dimension.

See: [[Need Frustration (SDT)]]

## Meta-Analytic Evidence (Ryan et al. 2022)

- **Competence satisfaction → positive affect**: r = .45 (Stanley et al. 2021)
- **Autonomy → subjective well-being**: r = .46 cross-cultural (Yu et al. 2018)
- **Relatedness → well-being**: consistent cross-cultural
- **Need frustration**: NOT yet meta-analyzed — major gap

## Universality

Cross-cultural studies (Ryan et al. 2022, Yu et al. 2018) confirm need satisfaction → well-being across collectivist and individualist cultures. Autonomy need ≠ independence or individualism — even in collectivist contexts, autonomy (volition, not external control) predicts well-being.

## Distinction from Maslow

BPNT needs are not hierarchical and not deficiency needs. All three operate continuously; none is satisfied once and transcended. They are nutriments for ongoing psychological health, not a developmental ladder.
