# Institutional Alignment

One of four change pathways in the [[Institutional Change Pathways]] typology. Characterized by **evolutionary pace** and **developmental scope**: gradual change that stretches and elaborates existing institutional arrangements without overturning them.

## Characteristics

- Change agents persuade constituencies and legitimize innovations through cultural entrepreneurship — framing, storytelling, theorization, robust design — that present innovations as non-threatening
- Macro-environmental evolutions (slow societal shifts) drive gradual, piecemeal institutional transitions
- Outcome: maintenance of institutional integrity with incremental developmental change (contraction, assimilation, elaboration of logics)
- Second most studied pathway (46 of 119 studies in Micelotta et al. review)

## Mechanisms

- Cultural entrepreneurship (Lounsbury & Glynn 2001): legitimation through narrative
- Persuasive embedding and "pragmatic collaborations" (Reay & Hinings 2009)
- Gradual logic coexistence / balance shifts (Wright & Zammuto 2013)
- Actors in different social positions pursuing self-interest while collectively shifting logic emphasis

## Gaps (per Micelotta et al.)

Like displacement studies, alignment research typically treats institutional pluralism as a *transitory enabling condition* for entrepreneurial reflexivity, not a permanent structural feature. Key open questions:
- How does pluralism facilitate or hinder the embedding of innovation (c1)?
- How do change agents craft legitimation strategies that resonate with culturally heterogeneous audiences (c2)?
- How does the balance among logics evolve in pluralistic fields (d1)?
- How do actors experiencing complexity negotiate logics and maintain balance among them (d2)?

## Connections

- [[Institutional Entrepreneurship]]: alignment is the primary pathway for nondisruptive cultural entrepreneurship
- [[Organizational Learning]]: alignment resembles incremental organizational learning — gradual absorption of new practices into existing routines
- [[Knowledge Transfer]]: legitimation strategies function as knowledge transfer mechanisms, making new practices legible and acceptable to field audiences
