# Synthetic Validity

**Synthetic validation** is a logical process of *inferring* the validity of a predictor battery for a job from the established relationships between **job components** (clusters of similar tasks/behaviors) and tests of the attributes needed to perform them (Mossholder & Arvey 1984). Validity is "synthesized" for a job that may never have had its own local validation study.

## How it works

1. Accumulate a large store of **specific personality × criterion-construct validities** (from primary studies and meta-analyses), filterable by computer.
2. Identify the **job components** most relevant to a target job (via personality-based and criterion-based **job analysis**).
3. **Tailor** a predictor equation / battery for that job (or a future job) by matching narrow predictors to the relevant components — computing an estimated validity coefficient.

Because multiple jobs share components, relationships can be estimated across jobs with far larger samples than any single job affords.

## Why it matters here

- Resolves the **[[Bandwidth-Fidelity Dilemma]]**: narrow, high-fidelity predictors are matched to criteria and assembled into batteries, raising validity while cutting testing time (drop non-contributing constructs).
- Treats **validity as multifaceted** (Scherbaum 2005) — the natural partner of generalizability theory (reliability as multifaceted) in operationalizing **[[Nomological-Web Clustering]]**.
- More **flexible than traditional meta-analysis**: its constant-validity-across-jobs assumption can be *relaxed* by putting moderators (job complexity, climate for autonomy, other [[Situation Strength|situational-strength]] variables) in the database — weak situations let personality predict more strongly (Beaty et al. 2001).

## Advocacy

[[Jeff W. Johnson]] and colleagues (2010): "**Validation is like motor oil: synthetic is better.**" They and [[Leaetta M. Hough]] (Hough 2001) call for building a shared predictor/criterion database, requiring **agreed-upon taxonomies** on both sides. The metaBUS project (Steel, Bosco, Uggerslev) is cited as the infrastructure that could make a large synthetic-validity database real.
