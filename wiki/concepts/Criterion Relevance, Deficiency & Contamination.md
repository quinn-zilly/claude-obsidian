# Criterion Relevance, Deficiency & Contamination

The three-way relationship between what an **operational** criterion measures and the **[[Ultimate Criterion|ultimate (conceptual) criterion]]**. Think of two overlapping circles — the conceptual criterion and the actual measure:

- **Relevance** — the *overlap*. The operational measure must be logically related to the performance domain (SIOP *Principles* 2003). The most relevant measure is often not the cheapest. Objective and subjective measures are **not interchangeable** (r ≈ .39; Bommer et al. 1995), so one should not proxy for the other.
- **Deficiency** — the part of the conceptual criterion the measure **fails to cover**. E.g., evaluating a professor on teaching + service while omitting research. Deficiency causes training-utility estimates to understate value when the criterion samples only a subset of learned tasks (Morrow, Jarrett & Rupinski 1997).
- **Contamination** — variance in the operational measure **unrelated to** the ultimate criterion. Splits into:
  - **Error** — random variation (nonstandardized procedures, mood fluctuations); correlates with nothing except by chance.
  - **Bias** — systematic contamination; *can* correlate with predictors, so it distorts validity coefficients (Brogden & Taylor 1950).

> [!example] Contamination is not academic
> Maier (1988): hands-on job-performance tests for Marine radio-repairers and auto-mechanics were collected carelessly, yielding validities of .09 and .17. After statistically cleaning the contaminated data, validities rose to **.49 and .37** — reversing the conclusion about how valid the aptitude tests were.

## Three common sources of bias

1. **Knowledge of predictor information** — a rater aware of a subordinate's assessment-center prediction may rate accordingly, making the predictor look better (or worse) than it is. Rule: *keep predictor information away from those who provide criterion data* (Bray & Grant 1966).
2. **Group membership** — hiring/promotion policies tied to biographical group traits contaminate subsequent success studies.
3. **Rating bias** — subjective ratings add spotty observation, unequal opportunity to perform, personal prejudice, and inability to distinguish dimensions (Thorndike 1920).
