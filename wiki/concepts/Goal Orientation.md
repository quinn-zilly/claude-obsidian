# Goal Orientation

Nav: [[index]] | [[Goal-Setting Theory]] | [[Learning Goals vs Performance Goals]]

Dispositional tendency to pursue particular achievement goals across situations over time (DeShon & Gillespie 2005). Three primary orientations (2×2 approach: Payne et al. 2007):
- **Mastery/Learning** (approach): develop competence, learn
- **Performance-approach**: demonstrate competence to others
- **Performance-avoid**: avoid demonstrating incompetence
- *(Mastery-avoid: avoid failing to learn; less studied)*

Independent dimensions — can be high on multiple simultaneously.

## Stability and Within-Person Variance

Payne et al. (2007) meta-analysis: moderate test-retest reliability — dispositionally stable. Yet majority of variance is within-person (state goal orientation fluctuates). Implications: dispositional GO sets central tendency; situational cues shift state GO.

## Antecedents

- Conscientiousness → + mastery, − performance-avoid (no effect on performance-approach)
- Implicit theories of ability: incremental → mastery; entity → performance
- Self-esteem

## Consequences

**Proximal:** state goal orientation, anxiety, feedback seeking
**Distal:** learning + performance

## Key Applied Findings

**Mastery vs. performance GO on complex tasks:**
- Kozlowski et al. (2001): learning goals → better knowledge structures → performance on challenging version
- Seijts et al. (2004): difficult specific learning goals > difficult specific performance goals on complex task
- Kozlowski & Bell (2006): distal learning goal + mastery frame → best self-regulatory behavior + highest end performance

**Technology adoption (Ahearne et al. 2010):**
- High mastery GO → initial sales drop (spending time learning), then exceeds baseline
- High performance-prove GO → less initial drop, never returns to baseline
- Long-term: mastery GO > performance GO for complex adaptive tasks

**Negative feedback:**
- High learning orientation → resilient (keeps goals high after negative feedback)
- High performance GO → negative emotions → goal revision downward (Cron et al. 2005)

**High conscientiousness × performance goal × negative feedback → tension + low performance** (Cianci et al. 2010)

## Integration with GST

GST's recommendation for complex tasks: assign learning goals (find strategies) not performance goals (Winters & Latham 1996; Locke & Latham 2002). GO provides the dispositional basis for why some individuals benefit more from learning goal framing. See [[Learning Goals vs Performance Goals]].
