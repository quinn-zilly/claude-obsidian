# Objective Career Success

**Objective career success (OCS)** = career outcomes that are quantitative and independently verifiable — income, occupational status, number/speed of promotions, professional title or hierarchical rank. Because peers or incumbents can use these to compare across people, OCS implicitly reflects an intersubjective, social-comparison standard (Gattiker & Larwood 1986). Contrast with [[Subjective Career Success]], which is self-referential.

## The critique of OCS

Since the 1990s, career (especially OP) scholars argued OCS indicators are **contaminated** (affected by factors outside the individual, e.g. occupational wage structures) and **deficient** (ignore satisfaction and meaning), and that in the contemporary/boundaryless era employees will/should care less about OCS and more about SCS.

## Seibert et al.'s (2024) reply — revaluing OCS

[[seibert-akkermans-liu-2024-contemporary-career-success|Seibert, Akkermans & Liu (2024)]] argue these critiques are overstated:

- **Objectivity is a virtue** — circumvents common-method variance in self-report designs.
- Salary is a **direct measure of a career goal**, not a deficient proxy for career *performance* (Campbell & Wiernik 2015).
- The de-emphasis may be a **rhetorical strategy** normalizing the loss of stable organizational careers (Inkson et al. 2012).
- Meaning-focus may be a **WEIRD-sample luxury**; financial achievement and security still matter globally.
- OCS is central to **equity** — for marginalized groups and non-traditional workers, objective markers are exactly what's at stake.

**Prescription:** include *both* OCS and SCS unless there is a well-articulated theoretical reason to focus on one.

## Weak predictability from disposition

Dispositional/orientation constructs predict OCS only weakly (\|ρ\| < .20; Ng et al. 2005; [[wiernik-kostal-2019-pbco-meta-analysis|Wiernik & Kostal 2019]]). **Human capital and sponsorship** antecedents predict salary and promotions more strongly than personality or career orientations — a pointer for where OCS research should look.
