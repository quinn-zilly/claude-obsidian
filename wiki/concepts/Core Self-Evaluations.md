# Core Self-Evaluations (CSEs)

**Origin:** Judge et al. (1997) — proposed as a higher-order dispositional predictor of job satisfaction.

**Definition:** Fundamental evaluative beliefs individuals hold about themselves, their functioning, and the world. A hierarchical trait with four specific indicators.

## Four Core Traits

| Trait | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Self-esteem** | Global evaluation of one's worth as a person |
| **Generalized self-efficacy** | Belief in one's ability to perform across situations |
| **Locus of control** | Extent to which one believes outcomes are self-determined (internal) vs. external |
| **Emotional stability** (low neuroticism) | Tendency toward positive vs. negative emotional states |

These four are selected because they are evaluative (positive/negative self-assessments), fundamental (broad and deep), and subconscious (foundational assumptions, not surface attitudes).

## Relationship to Job Satisfaction

**Meta-analysis (Judge & Bono 2001):** 169 independent correlations → overall CSE composite correlates rc = .37 with job satisfaction.

**Comparison with other taxonomies (Judge et al. 2008):** Three frameworks (Big Five, PA/NA, CSEs) together explain 36% of variance in self-reported satisfaction and 18% via significant-other reports. Reduced to three factors: (a) CSEs/neuroticism — **only consistent predictor across studies**, (b) extraversion, (c) conscientiousness.

## Mechanisms

**Job selection:** CSEs influence not just perceptions of jobs but actual job complexity obtained. Judge et al. (2000): both subjective and objective job complexity (coded from job titles) mediate CSE → job satisfaction in childhood and early adulthood.

**Perceived organizational constraint:** High-CSE employees less likely to perceive organizational obstacles → lower burnout → higher satisfaction (Best et al. 2005).

**Self-concordance:** Judge et al. (2005) — high-CSE individuals pursue self-concordant goals (for intrinsic reasons), which increases life satisfaction and goal attainment. "CSEs serve more like a trigger than an anchor. People with positive CSEs strive for 'the right reasons,' and therefore 'get the right results.'" (p. 266)

## Integration with Situational Factors

CSEs operate through an **interactionist** lens: dispositions express themselves through perception of and selection into environments. High-CSE individuals perceive the same environment more favorably *and* actually obtain better environments.
