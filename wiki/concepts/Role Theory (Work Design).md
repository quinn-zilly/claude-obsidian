# Role Theory (Work Design)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]] | [[Job Crafting]]

The work design perspective centered on **roles** — broader than jobs, including emergent, social, and self-initiated elements. One of five historical clusters in [[parker-morgeson-johns-2017-100-years-work-design|Parker et al. (2017)]] (the yellow cluster, ~92 terms).

## Roles vs Jobs

Whereas a job = "an aggregation of tasks assigned to a worker" (established, objective task elements), **roles are broader** and include emergent and self-initiated elements (Ilgen & Hollenbeck, 1991). This distinction echoes Turner & Lawrence's (1965) "prescribed" vs "discretionary" task elements.

## Two Strands

**1. Role demands (stress tradition).** Kahn et al. (1964) introduced **role conflict** and **role ambiguity** as stressful role dysfunctions; **role overload** followed. Much evidence links these to turnover, lowered commitment, and strain (Rizzo, House & Lirtzman, 1970; Fisher & Gitelson, 1983; Jackson & Schuler, 1985). In JD-R terms these are hindrance demands. Extends to work-family role interference (Frone et al., 1992).

**2. Role expansion / construction (active tradition).** Rather than treating roles as a fixed demand, this strand asks **when, how, and why people expand or change their roles**:

- **[[Job Crafting]]** (Wrzesniewski & Dutton, 2001): individuals cognitively/behaviorally reshape roles to enhance meaning.
- **Role orientation** (Parker, Wall & Jackson, 1997): autonomous work designs promote flexible role definitions that predict performance.
- **Role breadth self-efficacy** (Parker, 1998) and **personal initiative** (Frese, Garst & Fay, 2007): job autonomy promotes proactive, broad role-taking.
- Job autonomy also promotes role breadth (Morgeson et al., 2005) and proactive behavior (Parker et al., 2006), each enhancing performance (Grant & Parker, 2009).

## Why a Separate Cluster

Role conflict/ambiguity/overload are technically demands within the JD-C/JD-R models, but their distinct sociological/social-psychological history (roles arising from social structure) gives them a separate cluster in the scientific map. The cluster also captures socialization, newcomer adjustment, role innovation, and outcomes like citizenship and organizational commitment.
