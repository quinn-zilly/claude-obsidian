# Decision-Making Competence (DMC)

A measurable, **stable individual difference** in skill at judgment, preference, and choice tasks. Developed by Fischhoff, Parker, Bruine de Bruin et al.

## Measures

- **Y-DMC** (Youth): age-18 assessment; tasks drawn from experimental literature; both accuracy and consistency standards; varied tasks to reduce shared-method variance
- **A-DMC** (Adult): parallel adult version; similar factor structure

## Validation Evidence (CEDAR Longitudinal Study)

CEDAR = Center for Drug and Alcohol Research, tracked participants age 10–30.

**Y-DMC (age 18) correlated with**:
- Lower antisocial disorders, delinquency, marijuana use, multiple sexual partners (poor decision behaviors)
- Higher socioeconomic status, social support, positive peer environments (good environments)
- Fixed and fluid intelligence (but DMC patterns hold controlling for IQ)

**Y-DMC → A-DMC correlation at age 30 = 0.50** — substantial stability across 12 years

**Strongest predictor**: **neighborhood disadvantage at age 10** — persists after controlling for intelligence → social structure shapes decision competence from early childhood

## Implications

DMC is not just a cognitive variable — it reflects developmental opportunity, resource constraints (Mullainathan & Shafir 2013 scarcity argument), and social environment.

Research gap: how do perceived discrimination and opportunity structures affect acquisition of DMC skills?
