# Illusory Correlation

## Definition

The perception of a statistical relationship between two variables that does not exist (or is weaker than perceived), arising because the variables are **strongly associated in memory** or expectation.

## Origin (Chapman & Chapman 1967/1969)

Described by L.J. Chapman and J.P. Chapman. Judges evaluated hypothetical mental patient data (clinical diagnosis + draw-a-person test features). They markedly overestimated the frequency of co-occurrence of *naturally associated* pairs (suspiciousness → peculiar eyes) even when the actual correlation was zero or negative. They "rediscovered" common but unfounded clinical lore.

**Critical finding:** The illusory correlation was highly resistant to contradictory data and prevented judges from detecting *real* correlations that were present in the data.

## Mechanism (Tversky & Kahneman 1974)

The [[Availability Heuristic]] provides the account: co-occurrence frequency is judged by the strength of the **associative bond** between the two events. When the association is strong, one infers frequent co-occurrence. Suspiciousness is more readily associated with eyes than with other body parts → illusory correlation between suspiciousness and peculiar eyes.

## Implications

**Clinical practice:** Illusory correlations sustain unfounded clinical lore. Practitioners "see" expected patterns in patient data even when those patterns don't exist — and fail to notice unexpected (real) patterns.

**Research methodology:** Observer-rated data in studies of clinical or social phenomena may be systematically biased by illusory correlations between conceptually related constructs, even when raters are trained.

**Intergroup perception:** Illusory correlations between rare groups and rare behaviors (double distinctiveness) produce and sustain stereotypes — a key mechanism studied in social psychology after Tversky & Kahneman's original work.

## Connection to Other Concepts

- **[[Publication Bias]]**: The literature may contain illusory correlations — patterns that reached publication because they matched theoretical expectations, not because they were reliably observed.
- **[[Aesthetic Standards in Science]]** ([[Giner-Sorolla 2012]]): Findings that "look like" expected patterns are more publishable, structurally amplifying illusory correlations at the field level.
