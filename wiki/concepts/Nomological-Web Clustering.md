# Nomological-Web Clustering

A **non-hierarchical** approach to discovering personality structure, originated by [[Leaetta M. Hough]] (Hough & Ones 2001; Oswald & Hough 2011; Hough & Johnson 2013). Its historical roots are in **Project A**.

**Core idea**: define personality *taxons* not by factor analysis of intercorrelations, but by **similarity in patterns of relationships with variables outside the personality domain** — job-performance criteria and other correlates. Traits that belong together should share the *same profile* of relationships. This is a direct application of convergent/discriminant construct validity (Campbell & Fiske 1959).

A construct sits at the **center of a web**; threads to other constructs run through n-dimensional space, with distance indicating direction and strength of relationship (positive correlations close, zero far, negative farther still).

Traits in the same cluster should ideally share:

- similar psychobiological bases;
- similar correlations with other individual-difference variables and the environment;
- similar correlations with various criteria;
- similar moderation/mediation patterns;
- similar patterns **across demographic subgroups** (no differential mean differences or differential validity);
- similar malleability over time.

Illustration ([[Trait Activation Theory]]): if a context activates the *sociability* facet of Extraversion but not the *dominance* facet, that signals sociability and dominance belong in **separate** nomological-web clusters — even though both are "Extraversion."

Advantages over the [[Five-Factor Model|FFM]]/[[HEXACO Model|HEXACO]]:

- an **open network**, continuously revisable as new clustering criteria are added (types of nonlinearity, longitudinal processes, measurement invariance);
- does not preclude hierarchy — "explore depth first so that any resulting breadth is more meaningful";
- operationalized via **generalizability theory** (reliability as multifaceted) and **[[Synthetic Validity|synthetic validity]]** (validity as multifaceted).

Supported by evidence that trait structures are **more lattice-like than strictly hierarchical** (Loehlin & Goldberg 2014).
