# Boundaryless Career

The **boundaryless career** (Arthur & Rousseau 1996; DeFillippi & Arthur 1994) refers to career paths in which individuals respond to reduced organizational resources and job security by seeking resources and opportunities *across* organizational boundaries — changing employers, building external professional networks, or moving between occupations and locations — rather than advancing within a single firm.

## Two components of boundaryless career orientation (BCO)

- **Psychological mobility** — desire for variety in work environments and confidence in one's ability to transition between them (Sullivan & Arthur 2006).
- **Physical (organizational) mobility preference** — preference to actually move frequently between employers (or occupations/locations).

## What the meta-analysis revised

[[wiernik-kostal-2019-pbco-meta-analysis|Wiernik & Kostal (2019)]] showed these two components behave very differently:

- **Psychological mobility** is empirically inseparable from [[Protean Career Orientation|protean]] self-direction and values-driven components — all three load on one factor, [[Proactive Career Orientation]].
- **Physical mobility preference** is weakly related to the others, with a divergent nomological net — a genuinely distinct construct.

Their conclusion: grouping psychological and physical mobility under one "boundaryless" label **obscures two different constructs** (echoing Feldman & Ng 2007), and should be studied separately.

## Relation to protean

Related but independent from [[Protean Career Orientation|PCO]] (Briscoe et al. 2006). Boundaryless is about *mobility*; protean about *values and self-direction*. A person can be high-protean yet low-mobility when their organization fits their values ([[hall-yip-doiron-2018-protean-careers-at-work|Hall et al. 2018]]).
