# Categorization-Elaboration Model

The dominant integrative account of **why team diversity has inconsistent effects**, from **Van Knippenberg, De Dreu & Homan (2004)**. Diversity per se is neither good nor bad; it instigates two opposing processes, and which one dominates determines the outcome.

## The two processes

- **Subgroup categorization → intergroup bias** (the negative path). Perceived differences split the team into ingroups/outgroups (cf. [[Social Identity Theory]]); ingroup favoritism produces conflict, distrust, disliking, and limited cross-subgroup communication → impaired performance.
- **Information elaboration** (the positive path). "The degree to which information, ideas, or cognitive processes are shared and are being shared among group members" — the exchange, processing, and integration of a diverse pool of perspectives → creativity, better decision quality.

The processes are **negatively related**; at any moment one is typically dominant and predicts performance better than the other.

## Key implications

- **Reject the surface/deep dichotomy.** It is *not* true that surface-level diversity (e.g., gender) is uniformly harmful and deep-level (e.g., personality) uniformly helpful. Evidence is inconsistent; **any** dimension can trigger either process depending on moderators — and only when members are aware of the difference.
- **Moderators are everything.** Salience-reducing conditions curb intergroup bias; openness/pro-diversity conditions promote elaboration. Faultlines (aligned differences) push toward bias; cross-categorization pushes toward elaboration.

## Relation to LeaD

The [[Leading Diversity (LeaD) Model|LeaD model]] adopts CEM's two-process backbone and adds a leadership layer: leaders can **proactively shape** which process arises (first-stage moderation, as CEM emphasized) *and* **reactively manage** a process already active (second-stage moderation — LeaD's extension).
