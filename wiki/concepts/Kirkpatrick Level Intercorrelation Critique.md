# Kirkpatrick Level Intercorrelation Critique

The empirical case that the four levels of the [[Kirkpatrick Four-Level Model]] are **not** a tight positive hierarchy — that reactions in particular are largely independent of learning, behavior, and results.

## The Three Implicit Assumptions Challenged

Originating in [[alliger-janak-1989-kirkpatrick-levels-thirty-years-later]], the critique targets three assumptions that accreted onto Kirkpatrick's "steps":

1. **Ascending information value** — higher = more informative (and Level 4 = "best").
2. **Causal linkage** — Reactions → Learning → Behavior → Results.
3. **Positive manifold** — all inter-level correlations positive.

Each is logically and empirically questionable. Notably, liking a program need not cause learning; challenging training can raise learning while lowering reactions.

## Empirical Evidence

**Alliger & Janak (1989)** — 12 studies, 26 correlations, sample-size-weighted means:

| Pair | r |
|---|---|
| Reactions–Learning | .07 |
| Reactions–Behavior | .05 |
| Learning–Behavior | .13 |
| Learning–Results | .40 |
| Behavior–Results | .19 |

**[[alliger-et-al-1997-training-criteria-meta-analysis|Alliger et al. (1997)]]** — expanded to 34 studies / 115 correlations and split reactions into *affective* vs *utility*. Affective reactions correlate ≈ 0 with learning; **utility reactions** predict transfer better than learning measures do.

## Proposed Alternative Structure

Reactions form a category largely **independent** of the others; learning partly drives behavior and results; behavior and results are **mutually reinforcing** (results feed back to sustain behavior). This motivated the [[Augmented Training Criteria Framework]].

## Practical Implication

> [!key-insight]
> Reaction ("smile sheet") data is a poor proxy for whether training worked. Utility-judgment reactions ("was this useful for my job?") carry more predictive signal than affective liking.
