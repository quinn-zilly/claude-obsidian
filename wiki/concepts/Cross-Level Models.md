# Cross-Level Models

Nav: [[index]] | [[Multilevel Theory & Methods MOC]]

Types of multilevel model, distinguished by the **levels of the constructs they unite** and the **links proposed** among them. Model type dictates sampling and analysis.

## Single-level models
Relate constructs at **one** level. Individual-level models are familiar but ignore context (biasing standard errors when context matters). Unit-level models can be deceptively complex — a model of two shared constructs embeds a compositional multilevel model in each construct's operationalization.

## Cross-level models (top-down, most common)

1. **Direct-effect** — a higher-level construct directly affects a lower-level construct (e.g., unit technology → individual job characteristics). Variants: *mixed-determinant* (predictors at multiple levels → one lower outcome) and *mixed-effect* (one predictor → outcomes at multiple levels).
2. **Moderator** — a higher-level construct **moderates** the relationship between two lower-level constructs (e.g., unit technology moderates the ability→performance link). Equivalent to a lower-level variable moderating a cross-level relationship.
3. **Frog-pond** (heterogeneous / parts / individual-within-group) — effects of a lower-level entity's **relative standing** within its unit ($X_i -$ group mean). A college-educated person has much influence in a low-education group, little in a high-education group. The "group average" here is **not** a shared property; some scholars treat frog-pond as its own distinct level.

> [!note] What cross-level top-down effects can and can't explain
> A purely between-unit predictor (e.g., team location) can only explain **between-unit** variance in a lower-level outcome; it cannot explain within-unit variability.

## Homologous multilevel models
A relationship **generalizes across levels** (holds for individuals, groups, and organizations alike). Examples: Staw, Sandelands & Dutton's threat-rigidity; Lindsley, Brass & Thomas's efficacy-performance spirals; dependence→power.

> [!key-insight]
> Homologous models are, at best, **powerful and parsimonious** — and at worst **trite**. The search for constructs that hold at every level can abstract the phenomenon until it's useless. There are almost **no good empirical tests** of fully homologous models in the organizational literature.
