# Action Regulation Theory

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Motivation Meta-Framework (Kanfer)]]

German-origin theory of work psychology (Frese & Zapf 1994; Hacker 1985) that integrates goal choice and goal striving by highlighting the central role of **plans** in bridging goals and action.

## Core Ideas

**Plans = mental simulations of actions** that determine how goals can be achieved. Plans are necessary for all actions; the time lag from plan to action varies from milliseconds to years.

**Planning aids performance when it:**
- Prompts deeper thinking about intended actions
- Leads to development of alternative plans ("Plan B and C")
- Operates in unstable, uncertain environments (Frese, Krauss et al. 2007 — African entrepreneurs)

**Plans as mediators:** Plans mediate the goal setting–performance relation (Earley, Wojnaroski & Prest 1987).

## Hierarchical Action Structure

Actions organized hierarchically: superordinate goals constrain subordinate goals and plans. Lower-level plans are more automatized; higher-level plans require conscious regulation.

**Rubicon metaphor** (Heckhausen 1991; compatible with ART): deliberation (goal choice) → commitment (crossing Rubicon) → implementation (goal striving). Planning = the bridge. Failure to decompose distal goals into proximal plans = common error in dynamic environments (Dorner 1991).

## Personal Initiative

Frese & Fay (2001) extend ART to *personal initiative* — a self-starting, proactive, persistent work behavior pattern. Initiative requires: (a) setting self-concordant goals, (b) proactive planning, (c) overcoming barriers. Central to proactive engagement concept.

## Goal Internalization

Frese & Zapf (1994); Zacher & Frese (2015): assigned goals are redefined as one's own via an internalization process dependent on task clarity and absence of goal conflict. Parallels SDT's OIT continuum.
