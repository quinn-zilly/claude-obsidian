# Technology-Based Training

## Definition

Training delivered via computer, web, mobile, simulation, game, or other technology platform. Includes e-learning, computer-assisted instruction (CAI), serious games, simulations, mobile learning.

## Evolution in Research (JAP lens)

1. **Comparison era** (1960s–1990s): CAI vs. classroom. Finding: technology reduces training *time* but outcomes roughly equivalent to traditional methods.
2. **Design era** (1990s–present): What features drive effectiveness? Media irrelevance hypothesis (Clark 1994): *media don't influence learning — instructional design does.*
3. **Learner control era**: Who benefits from control? How much? With what support?

## Key Empirical Findings

**Learner control**
- Positive for satisfaction
- Neutral or negative for learning unless scaffolded (Orvis et al. 2009; Sitzmann & Ely 2010)
- High-ability, mastery-oriented learners benefit most
- Sitzmann & Johnson (2012): planning ability critical — learners must decide when/where/how long

**Individual differences interactions**
- Cognitive ability + Openness → benefit more from error training conditions
- Gist et al. (1989): modeling > tutorial approach for computer software training; interaction with pre-training computer self-efficacy
- Watson et al. (2013): monitoring trainee behavior during tech-based training improves outcomes

**Attention and engagement**
- Off-task attention a key mediator
- Performance orientation unrelated to off-task attention; learner control positive for satisfaction but not attention (Orvis et al. 2009)

## Design Principles (evidence-based)

1. Match technology features to learning objectives — not media type
2. Support learner control with scaffolding, prompts, self-regulatory support
3. Provide guidance on goal setting, strategy, time management within platform
4. Error framing matters in simulation/game contexts
5. Monitor engagement; build in checkpoints

## Open Gaps (Bell et al. 2017)

- **Blended learning**: optimal mix of tech vs. in-person; sequencing effects
- **Flipped classroom**: research nearly absent
- **Social media in training**: credibility, engagement, design principles unknown
- **Mobile learning**: unique constraints (interruption, work-life spillover)
- **User-generated content**: credibility perceptions, learning impact
- **Work-life conflict**: job demands + after-hours tech training → attrition (Sitzmann et al. 2010)
