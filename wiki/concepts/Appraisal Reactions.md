# Appraisal Reactions

**Individual-level attitudinal evaluations of and responses to the performance appraisal process** (Pichler 2012, working definition — no standard operational definition exists). Emerged as an important appraisal *criterion* after critiques (Ilgen, Barnes-Farrell & McKellin 1993) pushed the field beyond the psychometric focus on rating errors and accuracy.

## Constructs measured

Fairness perceptions, appraisal accuracy perceptions, appraisal utility, satisfaction with the appraisal, and motivation to improve performance. These are distinct constructs (Keeping & Levy 2000) but relate similarly to the same predictors, so meta-analyses often collapse them into a **composite reactions** variable for cumulating findings (Cawley et al. 1998; [[pichler-2012-social-context-appraisal|Pichler 2012]]).

## Why they matter

- Some scholars argue reactions are the *most important* appraisal criteria, because managers care about them more than psychometric properties (Balzer & Sulsky 1990).
- Related to job attitudes (Pettijohn et al. 2001) and to changes in job performance (Jawahar 2006; Nathan et al. 1991).
- Downstream: fairness/discrimination perceptions, grievances, and discrimination-claim risk (Werner & Bolino 1997).

## Predictors (Pichler 2012 meta-analysis)

Strongest: **[[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)|rater–ratee relationship quality]]** (composite Mr ≈ .66). Then appraisal participation (Mr ≈ .48) and rating favorability (Mr ≈ .39). See [[Relational Model of Appraisal Reactions]].
