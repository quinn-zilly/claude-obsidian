# Heuristics and Biases Program

Research program initiated by [[Amos Tversky]] and [[Daniel Kahneman]] in the early 1970s. The foundational document is [[tversky-kahneman-1974-heuristics-biases]] (*Science*, 1974).

## Core Claim

People assess probabilities and make predictions using a small number of **cognitive heuristics** — simplified mental operations that reduce complex tasks to simpler judgments. These heuristics are adaptive (usually effective) but produce **systematic, predictable errors** (biases) when they misfire.

## The Three Heuristics (1974)

| Heuristic | Used for | Key biases |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| [[Representativeness Heuristic]] | Judging class membership / process origin | [[Base Rate Neglect]], insensitivity to sample size, [[Gambler's Fallacy]], [[Illusion of Validity]], [[Regression to the Mean]] |
| [[Availability Heuristic]] | Estimating frequency or probability | Retrievability bias, search set bias, imaginability bias, [[Illusory Correlation]] |
| [[Anchoring and Adjustment]] | Numerical estimation | Insufficient adjustment, conjunctive overestimation, disjunctive underestimation, overconfident probability distributions |

## Why Biases Persist

1. **Heuristics are generally useful** — they're retained because they work most of the time.
2. **No appropriate coding** — statistical regularities (regression, sample variance) are experienced but not encoded in ways that would allow learning; people don't group predictions by assigned probability to check calibration.
3. **Biases affect experts** — experienced researchers show the same intuitive biases as naive subjects; statistical training prevents elementary errors but not structural ones in complex problems.
4. **Not motivational** — biases persist with financial incentives for accuracy; they are cognitive, not emotional.

## Scope

The program has expanded substantially beyond the 1974 paper. Key later additions include prospect theory (loss aversion, reference dependence), the conjunction fallacy (Linda problem), the planning fallacy, scope insensitivity, and the affect heuristic.
