# Women's Career Equality

The organizing construct of **[[kossek-su-wu-2017-opting-out-pushed-out|Kossek, Su & Wu (2017)]]**.

## Definition
The degree to which women, **compared to men**, (a) have **equal access to and participation** in career opportunities, and (b) experience **equal work and nonwork outcomes** — both **intrinsic** (job, life, family satisfaction) and **extrinsic** (pay, promotions).

## Key properties
- **Multilevel & multidisciplinary**: measurable at individual and collective (societal, occupational, organizational, group) levels, which are interrelated through social-contextual effects (when women are better represented, individuals *and* organizations benefit).
- **Organizational indicators**: structure and culture, especially **[[Vertical and Horizontal Gender Segregation|vertical and horizontal gender integration]]**.
- **Individual indicators**: equal access to the same opportunities (e.g., expatriate/high-potential roles; the **[[Glass Cliff]]**) and equality in intrinsic/extrinsic outcomes over the **career life course** (esp. midcareer, when women lose ambition/confidence per Bain 2014).
- Deliberately links **career, family, and gender-bias** concepts, which are usually studied separately.

## Why it matters
Reframes the debate away from "do women lean in?" toward a jointly individual-and-organizational, cross-level phenomenon — setting up the **[[Climate for Gender Inclusion]]** as the mechanism and the **[[Opting Out vs Pushed Out]]** integration as the theoretical payoff.

Empirically grounded in **[[Gender Gap in Rewards vs Performance|Joshi, Son & Roh (2015)]]**: women with equal performance receive less pay/promotion, especially in prestigious occupations.
