# Logic of Confidence

The **logic of confidence and good faith** is the mechanism that keeps a [[Decoupling|decoupled]] organization orderly despite the deliberate absence of tight inspection and control. Named by [[John W. Meyer]] and [[Brian Rowan]] in [[meyer-rowan-1977-institutionalized-organizations]] (Proposition 5).

## The Problem It Solves

If a [[Decoupling|decoupled]] organization doesn't really inspect, measure, or coordinate its technical work, why doesn't it fall into chaos? Because internal participants and external constituents extend each other **confidence and good faith** — they assume things are as they seem and that everyone is performing their role properly. That shared assumption is what lets daily routines run on a decoupled structure.

## Goffman's Three Practices

Drawing on Goffman (1967), confidence in structural elements is sustained through:

- **Avoidance** — staying clear of situations that would force a damaging inspection.
- **Discretion** — not probing where probing would reveal inconsistency.
- **Overlooking** — noticing anomalies but declining to act on them.

Decoupling autonomous subunits encourages avoidance and discretion; overlooking is pervasive. Both insiders and outsiders cooperate in these face-saving practices.

## Ceremonial Displays

Organizations whose structure derives heavily from [[Rationalized Myth|rationalized myths]] actively *dramatize* their commitment to those structures: public displays of morale and satisfaction, ritual affirmations, strategic leniency, professionalization. These are not empty — they bind supervisors and subordinates alike to act in good faith and to do the informal backstage coordination that keeps activity running smoothly while avoiding public embarrassment.

## Significance

Together with [[Decoupling]], the logic of confidence completes Meyer & Rowan's answer to how organizations can be simultaneously *legitimate* (conforming to institutional myth) and *functional* (getting real work done) when the two pull in different directions.
