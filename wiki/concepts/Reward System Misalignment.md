# Reward System Misalignment

The condition in which an organization's formal reward system reinforces behavior A while the organization's stated goal is behavior B. Identified and systematized by [[Steven Kerr]] (1975/1995).

## Core Dynamic

Organisms (humans included) identify what is rewarded and do that — often to the exclusion of unrewarded activities. When formal rewards diverge from stated intentions, rational actors follow the rewards. The result is not dysfunction or moral failure; it is rational adaptation to the incentive structure actually in place.

> "The rewarder is not causing the behavior desired but is only a fortunate bystander." — Kerr (1995)

## Four Structural Causes (Kerr)

1. **Objective-criteria fixation** — Simple quantifiable metrics displace harder-to-measure goals ([[Goal Displacement]])
2. **Visibility bias** — Visible behaviors (publications, scoring stats, compliance) crowd out invisible ones (teaching quality, teamwork, sound judgment)
3. **Hypocrisy** — Rewarder actually prefers A despite claiming to prefer B
4. **Morality/equity override** — Efficiency sacrificed for fairness or political acceptability

## Examples Across Domains

| Domain | Rewarded (A) | Hoped for (B) |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Politics | Official/vague goals | Operative/specific goals |
| War (Vietnam) | Survival independent of outcome | Obedience and combat effectiveness |
| Medicine | Over-diagnosis (Type 1 errors) | Diagnostic accuracy |
| Universities | Research output | Teaching quality |
| Business | Attendance | Performance |
| Sports | Individual stats | Team play |
| Government | Spending | Economy |

## Diagnosis

Kerr recommends managers audit what behaviors are *actually* being reinforced before diagnosing motivation problems. Apparent laziness or resistance often traces to misaligned rewards, not character.
