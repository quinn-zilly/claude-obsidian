# Calibration (Judgment)

The degree to which **confidence in beliefs matches their accuracy**. Perfect calibration: correct x% of the time when claiming x% probability of being correct.

## Core Properties

- **Overconfidence** on hard tasks; **underconfidence** on easy tasks — robust finding across domains
- **Global vs. local confidence** must be assessed separately: people may believe they got fewer right overall than their item-by-item probabilities imply
- Calibration is distinct from **resolution** (ability to discriminate high vs. low confidence levels) and **knowledge** (overall accuracy) — Good Judgment Project feedback system separated all three

## Improving Calibration

**Scoring rules**: create incentives for candid probability reports. US National Weather Service uses these; forecasters need extensive feedback to master them (Murphy & Winkler 1974).

**Conditions for learning** (Fischhoff):
1. Prompt, unambiguous feedback
2. Proper incentives  
3. Instruction in unintuitive processes (e.g., how risks accumulate)

Weather forecasters and financial auditors who have these conditions show reasonable calibration. Most everyday experience lacks all three.

**Warning people about overconfidence** has limited effect — people lack cognitive structures or consider themselves immune.

## Good Judgment Project Results

- Brief intense training + scoring-rule feedback → **sustained calibration improvement**
- Remote team interaction helps somewhat
- Self-selected participants already better calibrated than typical lab participants
- Identifies **superforecasters**: stable individual difference, ~2-SD above mean

## Measurement Issues

- Calibration improves with **concentrated feedback** (Lichtenstein & Fischhoff 1980): 200 judgments/round with personal discussion → improvement generalized beyond training tasks
- Very strong or weak confidence (small probabilities) hardest to calibrate — requires rare events
