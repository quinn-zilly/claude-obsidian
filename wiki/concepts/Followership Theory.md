# Followership Theory

Nav: [[index]] | [[Leadership MOC]]

**Followership theory is the study of the nature and impact of followers and following in the leadership process** ([[uhl-bien-et-al-2014-followership-theory-review|Uhl-Bien, Riggio, Lowe & Carsten 2014]]). Its premise: since "there is no leadership without followers," *following behaviors* are a co-constitutive component of leadership rather than a residual of leader action.

## Two Approaches

- **Role-based** ("reversing the lens," Shamir 2007): studies followership as a rank/role (subordinate), with follower characteristics and behaviors as the *independent* variables shaping leader outcomes. Includes follower typologies, follower role orientations (Carsten et al. 2010), [[Implicit Followership Theories]], and following behaviors (obedience, resistance, proactivity, upward influence).
- **Constructionist**: followership and leadership are *co-constructed* in relational interaction and need not track formal hierarchy. Anchored by [[Leadership Identity Construction|claiming–granting]] (DeRue & Ashford 2010), Shamir's [[Co-Production of Leadership|co-production]], and discursive approaches.

## Key Distinctions

- **Follower ≠ employee/subordinate.** A construct counts as followership only if operationalized in relation to leaders or the leadership process, or where individuals hold follower identities.
- **Following is a behavior, not just a position.** Managers also defer to subordinates; "following" includes deferring, obeying, voicing, resisting, advising, and negotiating.
- **Boundary tension with shared leadership.** Shamir's condition that leadership requires *disproportionate influence* sits in productive tension with fully [[Shared Leadership|shared leadership]] — if influence is fully shared, "the term loses any added value."

## Construct Classes (Uhl-Bien et al. formal theory)

Followership **characteristics** (role orientations, motivations, affect), followership **behaviors** (obeying, deferring, voicing, resisting), and followership **outcomes** (individual, relational, and leadership-process outcomes).
