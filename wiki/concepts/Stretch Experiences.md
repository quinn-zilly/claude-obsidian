# Stretch Experiences

Also called **developmental assignments**, **job challenges**, or **challenging assignments**. An experience is stretching to the degree that effective performance requires knowledge, skills, and perspectives *beyond* what the individual currently has — placing them at the edge of their comfort zone.

## Why They Matter

Across multiple studies, managers cite challenging assignments as the primary driver of their own development (McCall, Lombardo & Morrison, 1988). Job incumbents reporting stretch challenges are rated higher on leadership skill development, managerial competencies, and promotability.

Stretch experiences are the mechanism through which [[Learning Agility]] is both *expressed* and *built*.

## Five Challenge Types
(Ohlott, 2004; from lessons-of-experience research)

1. **Unfamiliar responsibilities** — doing something never done before
2. **Creating change** — fixing problems, leading turnarounds, innovation
3. **High levels of responsibility** — high stakes, high visibility
4. **Managing across boundaries** — cross-functional, cross-cultural, influencing without authority
5. **Dealing with diversity** — working across differences in teams or stakeholder groups

## Sizes of Stretch

| Major | Daily/Incremental |
|---|---|
| First supervisory job | Giving difficult feedback for the first time |
| New strategic initiative | Organizing an event outside one's expertise |
| Expatriate assignment | Presenting to a senior stakeholder |
| Business turnaround | Delegating a task that stretches a direct report |

## Learning Agility × Stretch: A Three-Way Relationship
([[mccauley-yost-2021-comfort-zone]])

- **Antecedent**: learning-agile individuals *seek* stretch (change readiness, curiosity, learning goal orientation)
- **Moderator**: learning agility buffers negative consequences (emotional regulation prevents stretch → turnover; self-efficacy prevents stretch → laissez-faire leadership) and amplifies positive ones
- **Outcome**: stretch experiences *build* learning agility (self-insight, resilience, flexibility, feedback seeking)

## Equity Issue

Women and African American managers receive fewer challenging assignments than white male counterparts (King et al., 2012; Douglas, 2003). Organizational systems must actively monitor and equalize access.

## Avoiding Stretch as a Derailment Risk

Avoiding stretch assignments is one of the **10 Deadly Sins** of derailment ([[Derailment]]). Leaders need stretch to prove themselves "in the fire," but stretch also carries real risk — poor performance, career damage, personal stress. The key is calibrated risk management, not avoidance.

## Organizational Levers

1. Build a stretch framework — list stretch experiences alongside courses in development plans
2. Equip managers to assign stretch with deliberate person–job misfit
3. Staff for development via succession, rotational programs, project marketplaces
4. Design new stretch where gaps exist (e.g., IBM Corporate Service Corps)
5. Support with learning resources: goals, coaching, reflection, training
