# Geographic Bias in Indexing

Selective bibliometric databases under-represent journals from non-English-speaking and developing countries. Because metrics computed on them inherit that bias, the global South looks smaller — and less open — than it is.

## The mechanism

[[Web of Science]] descends from a citation-selective index with a documented Western / English-language skew (Mongeon & Paul-Hus 2016). Its journals are mostly also in [[Dimensions]], so the shared core agrees. The journals Dimensions *adds* — smaller national journals, Southern OA platforms (AJOL, AmeliCA, SciELO) — are disproportionately **open access**.

Result ([[basson-et-al-2022-oa-dimensions-wos]]): the OA gap between databases is near-zero for North America and Europe but large for the South — South Asia +57.9%, LAC +36.6%, MENA +33.5%, Sub-Saharan Africa +12.4% (Dimensions over WoS).

## Why it interacts with OA type

[[OA Type Classification|OA route]] also tracks geography:
- **Green-only** is consistently higher in WoS — the West's self-archiving culture (mandates, repositories like PubMed Central) shows up in the well-indexed core.
- **Bronze** is higher in Dimensions everywhere — the added non-DOAJ journals are free-to-read but license-unclear.

So Western OA reads as repository-driven (green); Southern OA reads as journal-driven (gold), much of it invisible to WoS.

## Equity stakes

Measuring OA on a Western-biased database systematically understates OA uptake in the global South, distorting the very policy debates (mandates, funding, APC waivers) that OA equity depends on. The corrective: prefer inclusive databases when measuring OA beyond North America and Europe.

> [!caution] Confound
> Part of the gap may stem from Dimensions including document types WoS excludes (meeting abstracts, editorials) rather than purely from broader journal coverage. Dimensions' lack of document-type disaggregation prevents fully ruling this out.
