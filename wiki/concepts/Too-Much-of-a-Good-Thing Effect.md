# Too-Much-of-a-Good-Thing Effect

The **too-much-of-a-good-thing (TMGT) effect** ([[pierce-aguinis-2013-tmgt-effect|Pierce & Aguinis 2013]]) is a formalized [[Meta-Theory (Management)|meta-theory]]: **ordinarily beneficial antecedents reach a context-specific inflection point after which their relation with a desired outcome ceases to be linear and positive**, turning asymptotic or negative — an overall pattern of **curvilinearity**.

Exceeding the inflection point $I$ is always undesirable: at best it wastes resources (no added benefit), at worst it harms the outcome.

## The claim

*All* seemingly monotonic positive antecedent→outcome relations are subject to it. It reconciles the paradox that three mutually exclusive theses (more $X$ → more / no change / less $Y$) all seem individually tenable: they hold at **different levels of $X$** — Thesis A below $I$, B near $I$, C above $I$.

## Shape: inverted-U vs asymptotic

- **Inverted-U** when Thesis C confirms (relation turns negative past $I$).
- **Asymptotic** when it merely flattens.

Following Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*: **actions** and **passions** are subject to the mean → inverted-U; **things good in themselves** (e.g., general mental ability, wisdom) → asymptotic, no downturn.

## Formal model

TMGT is a special case of moderation where $X$ moderates its own relation with $Y$:

$$ Y = b_0 + b_1 X + b_2 X^2 + e, \qquad I = \frac{-b_1}{2b_2} $$

## Scope

Documented across levels and subfields: leadership, conscientiousness, job enrichment, selection experience, venture planning, firm growth, diversification, organizational slack — plus OCB, self-efficacy, board size, and more. Grounded in the [[Doctrine of the Mean]].
