# Training Method Effectiveness

## Core Principle

No single training method is universally superior. Effectiveness depends on the **match** between method and skill/task type being trained. This is a product of systematic [[Training Needs Analysis]].

## Skill/Task Typology

Three broad categories (Farina & Wheaton, 1973; Fleishman & Quaintance, 1984):

| Type | Definition | Example Content |
|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive** | Thinking, problem solving, knowledge, idea generation | Technical knowledge, decision rules, procedures |
| **Interpersonal** | Interacting with others, leadership, communication, conflict management | Team skills, customer service, leadership |
| **Psychomotor** | Physical/manual activities, fine-to-gross motor coordination | Equipment operation, surgical skills, athletics |

## Method × Skill Fit (Arthur et al., 2003)

| Skill Type | Higher-effectiveness methods |
|---|---|
| Cognitive | Lecture + discussion, programmed instruction, computer-based training |
| Interpersonal | Behavioral role modeling, case studies, simulations, role play |
| Psychomotor | Hands-on practice, simulators, on-the-job training |

Multi-method programs combining cognitive + interpersonal content showed some of the largest behavioral criterion effect sizes (d ≈ 2.08 for behavioral criteria in Arthur et al.).

## Implications

1. **Default to lecture** is a design failure when content is interpersonal or psychomotor
2. Method selection should be driven by needs assessment output — specifically task analysis
3. Multi-method may outperform single-method for complex, multi-skill training targets
