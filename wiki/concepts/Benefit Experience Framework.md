# Benefit Experience Framework

The **benefit experience framework** decomposes an employee's relationship to any given benefit into three distinct experiences (Hong, Ford & Jong 2024, building on Allen et al. 2013; Butts et al. 2013; Dulebohn et al. 2009):

1. **Availability** — the organization offers the benefit and the employee is aware of it, *regardless of use*. Functions as a **symbolic resource** (Foa & Foa) signalling the org's concern.
2. **Use** — whether the employee actually takes advantage of the benefit. An **exchange of goods/services** that fulfills material needs.
3. **Subjective evaluation** — the employee's judgment of the benefit's **value/quality** and their satisfaction with it. Captures the *amount and quality* of the resource exchanged.

## Why the distinction matters

[[Social Exchange Theory]] predicts the value/quality of a resource is what drives exchange quality — so **subjective evaluation should dominate**. It does:

| Experience | Relative predictive strength | Illustrative ρ |
|---|---|---|
| Subjective evaluation | Strongest across outcomes | →POS ≈ .40; →affective commitment ≈ .33 |
| Availability | Weak but nonzero | →POS ≈ .08; →turnover intent ≈ −.07 |
| Use | Adds little beyond the other two | small/near-zero |

([[hong-ford-jong-2024-employee-benefits-meta-analysis|Hong et al. 2024]] meta-analytic corrected correlations.)

## Mediation and independence

Relationships to affective commitment, withdrawal, and job satisfaction are **partially mediated by [[Perceived Organizational Support|POS]]**, and partially direct (benefits fulfill needs and raise employee–organization interdependence regardless of POS).

> [!key-insight] Measurement implication
> Tracking availability or use without measuring evaluation misses the strongest signal. A benefit that is available and used but poorly evaluated (high copay, inconvenient) can trigger **negative reciprocity**. Organizations should measure how employees *value* benefits, not just whether they offer or log them.

## Connection to total rewards

Sits inside [[Employee Benefits]], one half of [[Total Rewards]] (benefits + pay). Complements [[Pay Transparency]] on the pay side: both concern how the *communicated/perceived* quality of a reward — not just its existence — shapes attitudes.
