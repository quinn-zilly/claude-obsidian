# Aesthetic Standards in Science

Aesthetic standards in science are evaluative criteria based on the *appearance* of results — their visual or narrative elegance — rather than their epistemic quality. [[Roger Giner-Sorolla|Giner-Sorolla (2012)]] identifies three aesthetic criteria that, under the [[Publication Bottleneck]], distort scientific judgment:

## The Three Criteria

### 1. Statistical Perfection

The p < .05 threshold has become a **ritual hallmark** (Gigerenzer, 2004): not a considered inferential choice but a binary signal of quality. A result that clears .05 is "clean" and "beautiful"; one that doesn't is "messy" and "ugly."

Why this is aesthetic rather than scientific: a three-study paper with joint p = .0074 is objectively stronger evidence than a two-study paper with joint p = .012, yet the two-study paper *looks* better because both individual studies clear the threshold. Fluency in processing (Reber, Schwarz & Winkielman, 2004) underlies aesthetic preference — a clean, significant result is easy to read and trust.

Practices creating the appearance of perfection (Simmons et al. 2011):
- Dropping non-significant measures/studies
- Trying multiple analyses; reporting only the working ones
- Running more participants post-hoc to reach significance
- Selective one-tailed tests

### 2. Narrative Conformity

Scientific papers must conform to an "easily processed, self-promoting narrative format." The key distortion: [[HARKing]] — presenting post-hoc hypotheses as a priori predictions. The pressure is presentational, not just incentive-based. Admitting a midcourse correction "muddies the story" aesthetically, even when it would be scientifically honest and informative.

### 3. Novelty

Novelty has an inverted-U effect on aesthetic preference (Sluckin et al. 1983): too familiar is boring, too unfamiliar is off-putting. Journals need novelty to distinguish themselves. This structurally excludes **direct replications** (the least novel paper type), creating a moral hazard: the most important check on validity — independent replication across labs — is effectively unpublishable.

## Why Aesthetic Criteria Are Not Inherently Bad

Giner-Sorolla notes that aesthetic standards in science are not intrinsically wrong:
- Clear presentation improves communication
- Strong, consistent results *are* genuinely informative
- Novelty drives exploration and discovery

The problem is the **bottleneck context**: when aesthetic criteria become *gatekeeping* criteria (due to space constraints in competitive markets), they suppress valid but imperfect science. The analogy: Dirac said a theory's beauty should encourage persistence in testing — not that beauty should override disconfirming data.

## The Anna Karenina Effect

Under high rejection rates, editors use **conjunction rules** (Bornmann & Marx, 2012): a paper with one aesthetic flaw is disqualified even if it is scientifically superior overall. This is the Anna Karenina principle: happy (publishable) families are all alike; unhappy ones each fail in their own way.
