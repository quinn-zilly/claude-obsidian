# Sunk Cost Effect

Tendency for previously incurred, unrecoverable costs to influence subsequent decisions — despite normative prescription to ignore them. Also called *escalation of commitment*.

## Normative Position

Sunk costs are irrelevant to future decisions. Only incremental costs and benefits going forward matter. Finishing a bad meal to "get your money's worth" is irrational by standard decision theory.

## Evidence

**Theater ticket study** (Arkes & Blumer 1985): season ticket buyers randomly assigned to full price, modest discount, or substantial discount. Full-price buyers attended significantly more performances. Effect faded later in season.

**Bank loan officers** (Staw, Barsade & Koput 1997): more likely to continue funding problem loans when they initiated the original loan than when they inherited responsibility.

**Foot-in-door** (Freedman & Fraser 1966): prior small compliance → easier large compliance.

**NBA draft picks** (Staw & Hoang 1995): early (expensive) draftees played more than performance warranted. (Contested: may reflect model limitations.)

## Theoretical Explanations (Multiple, Competing)

| Account | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| [[Prospect Theory]] | Initial cost = loss; continuation = risky bet with hope of gain; abandonment = certain loss → risk seeking in loss domain |
| Waste aversion (Arkes 1996) | General aversion to waste, mistakenly extended to sunk costs |
| Self-justification (Staw 1976) | Preserve self-image of original decision |
| Behavioral commitment (Kiesler 1971) | Prior behavior increases commitment |
| Regret-based (Ku 2008) | Abandonment triggers regret for original investment |
| Prevention/promotion (Higgins 2002) | Prevention-focused → stick with original plan |

> "The sunk-cost effect and its relatives seem obviously worrying, possibly widespread, and open to a broad range of theoretical accounts." — Connolly et al. 2012

## Complications

- Degree of completion (not dollars spent) may drive escalation (Conlon & Garland 1993; Garland 1990)
- Dollar expenditure and completion operate independently (Moon 2001)
- Effects change over project lifecycle (He & Mittal 2007)
- Possibly lumping together several distinct phenomena
