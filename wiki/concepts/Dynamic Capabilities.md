# Dynamic Capabilities

## Core Definition

A **dynamic capability** is "the capacity of an organization to purposefully create, extend, or modify its resource base" (Helfat et al., 2007, p. 4). It is distinguished from a **substantive (ordinary) capability** — a high-level routine conferring decision options for producing outputs of a particular type (Winter, 2003) — by *what it acts on*: a dynamic capability reconfigures existing substantive capabilities rather than producing outputs directly.

## The Distinction That Matters

> [!key-insight]
> The qualifier *dynamic* separates one type of ability (e.g., the substantive ability to develop new services) from another (e.g., the ability to **reform the way** the firm develops new services). Substantive capabilities *do*; dynamic capabilities *change how the firm does*.

## Relation to Absorptive Capacity

[[Absorptive Capacity]] can be conceptualized at three levels of "stickiness" (Lane et al., 2006):

| Conceptualization | Nature |
|---|---|
| Asset | static stock of prior knowledge |
| Substantive capability | routines to identify/assimilate/transform/apply knowledge |
| Dynamic capability | ability to reconfigure those substantive capabilities |

When treated as a dynamic capability, absorptive capacity affects the firm's ability to reconfigure existing substantive capabilities. In IS work, [[roberts-2012-absorptive-capacity-is-review|Roberts et al. (2012)]] found only two studies explicitly framing absorptive capacity as a dynamic capability (Malhotra et al., 2005; Pavlou & El Sawy, 2006) — and note that dynamic capability is **inherently organizational-level**, so framing individual-level absorptive capacity as dynamic is inappropriate.

## Lineage

- **Teece, Pisano & Shuen (1997)** — origin of the dynamic-capabilities view.
- **Winter (2003)** — substantive vs. dynamic distinction; "understanding dynamic capabilities."
- **Helfat et al. (2007)** — the canonical definition used above.
- **Pavlou & El Sawy (2006)** — IT/NPD operationalization.
