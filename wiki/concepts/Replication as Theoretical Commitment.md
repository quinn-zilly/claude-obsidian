# Replication as Theoretical Commitment

Framework from Nosek & Errington (2020a) for understanding what a replication *is*.

## The Core Problem

No exact replication exists. Every study differs from prior studies in sample units, settings, treatments, and outcomes. This creates a tension: if studies can never truly be "the same," how can replication be meaningful?

## The Resolution

> "A study is a replication when the innumerable differences from the original study are **believed to be irrelevant** for obtaining the evidence about the same finding."

Replication is a **theoretical claim** — the researcher asserts that the contextual differences don't matter for the phenomenon. The replication then tests that claim. Evidence updates confidence in both the finding and our understanding of the conditions necessary for it.

## Key Implications

**Every replication is a generalizability test, but not vice versa.**
A generalizability test becomes a replication only if *all outcomes* (positive and negative) alter confidence in the original finding. If negative outcomes merely identify a "boundary condition" without reducing confidence in the original claim — it's a generalizability test, not a replication.

**"Conceptual replication" is often not replication.**
Conceptual replications are typically interpreted as supporting findings but rarely as disconfirming them → they function as generalizability tests, not replications under this framework.

**Direct vs. conceptual replication distinction → counterproductive.**
The framework dissolves this dichotomy: what matters is whether the theoretical commitment is shared, not surface similarity.

## Context Sensitivity Appeals

Post-hoc appeals to context sensitivity to explain replication failures are common but weak. When tested directly, presumed context sensitivity frequently fails to account for failures (Ebersole et al. 2016a, 2020; Klein et al. 2014, 2018). Claiming context sensitivity requires *evidence*, not assertion.

## Practical Upshot

Before conducting a replication: specify the theoretical commitment explicitly. This prevents post-hoc goalpost-moving (see [[Registered Reports]] — adversarial collaboration built into study design). "The best time to argue about what a replication means is before you do it." (Nosek & Errington 2020b)
