# Matthew Effect (Science)

## Definition

The Matthew effect in science refers to the cumulative advantage phenomenon: those who already have resources, recognition, and network connections accumulate further resources and recognition at a faster rate than those who do not — regardless of merit alone. "For unto every one that hath shall be given, and he shall have abundance" (Matthew 25:29).

Coined by Robert Merton (1968) in the context of scientific recognition and credit allocation.

## Mechanisms in Science

- **Grant funding:** elite institutions receive disproportionate share; returns on investment would be higher if distributed more evenly (Wahls, 2018)
- **Doctoral institution rank:** early credential from high-ranked institution opens doors to collaborations and opportunities that compound over time
- **Network effects:** professional connections from elite graduate training create ongoing access to collaborations, data, and visibility
- **Citation accumulation:** highly cited authors get cited more; early citation advantages compound via self-reinforcing visibility

## Empirical Evidence

- Early advantages in doctoral institution rank predict long-term career outcomes beyond individual merit (Clauset et al., 2015)
- Grant funding overallocated to elite universities; marginal returns would be higher from more equitable distribution (Wahls, 2018)
- Team-authored articles more cited than solo-authored; this citation gap has grown over time (Wuchty et al., 2007)

## Relevance to Crowdsourcing

Uhlmann et al. (2019) argue crowdsourcing is a structural democratization mechanism: globally distributed projects can recruit researchers with advanced training and much to offer but insufficient resources to compete in the vertically integrated model. Crowd participation provides middle authorship on high-profile papers — accessible to researchers outside elite institutions.

## Relationship to Institutional Isomorphism

The Matthew effect in science is the micro-level career manifestation of [[Institutional Isomorphism]] at the field level: normative isomorphism (shared hiring criteria, credential filters) and mimetic isomorphism (hiring from the same top doctoral programs) reproduce structural advantage across organizations. The result is that the organizational field of science reproduces elite-institution dominance via normal institutional processes, not explicit discrimination.

## Relationship to Career Success / DEI

The Matthew effect intersects with [[Complex Visibility]] and [[Outsider-Within Status]]: minority researchers face both the general Matthew effect disadvantages and the additional resource drain of managed visibility. The compound effect produces the career gaps documented by [[beigi-2025-career-success-minority|Beigi et al. (2025)]].
