# Procedural Justice

Perceived fairness of the **process** used to make a decision (vs [[Distributive Justice|distributive justice]] = fairness of the outcome). Central premise: participation / having a say in a decision process produces more favorable reactions to that process — the [[Voice Effect|voice effect]] (Thibaut & Walker 1975; Greenberg 1990).

## Two theories of voice

Procedural justice offers two competing explanations for *why* voice matters — a distinction Pichler (2012) maps onto competing appraisal models:

**Instrumental (self-interest) model.** Voice is valued because process control lets individuals influence the eventual decision/outcome (Thibaut & Walker 1975; Tyler 1994). "Individuals posit no value for fair procedures independent of their association with fair outcomes" (Konovsky 2000). → underpins the **instrumental** appraisal model.

**Non-instrumental / relational (group-value) model.** Voice is valuable *in and of itself*, regardless of outcome influence, because being heard signals valued group-member status (Lind & Tyler 1988). Especially potent when voice is granted by an authority figure (Tyler & Lind 1992). Relationship quality with decision-makers predicts fairness/attitudes/behavior *more strongly* than instrumental control (Tyler 1989). → underpins the **[[Relational Model of Appraisal Reactions|relational]]** appraisal model.

## Process control vs decision control

- **Process control (voice)** — having a say in the process (Folger 1977). Valued even without decision control.
- **Decision control** — influence over the actual decision. Individuals will relinquish decision control if guaranteed process control (Thibaut & Walker 1975).

## In performance appraisal

Appraisal participation is a form of voice / indirect decision control. Voice effects are attenuated when outcomes are favorable and when [[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)|relationship quality]] with the authority is low (Lind & Tyler 1988; Tyler & Lind 1992). Process-fairness judgments matter most when trustworthiness of the decision-maker is uncertain (van den Bos et al. 1997, 1998). [[pichler-2012-social-context-appraisal|Pichler (2012)]]'s best-fitting model integrates both instrumental and relational propositions.
