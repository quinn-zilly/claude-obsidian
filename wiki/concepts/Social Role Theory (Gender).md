# Social Role Theory (Gender)

The theoretical base of the **gender-bias / "pushed-out"** perspective in **[[kossek-su-wu-2017-opting-out-pushed-out|Kossek, Su & Wu (2017)]]** (Eagly & Steffen 1986; Eagly & Karau 2002; Heilman 2012).

## Core claim
Because men and women have historically occupied different social roles, society forms **role expectations**: men are expected to be **agentic** (assertive, dominant, competitive); women **communal** (helpful, kind, sympathetic). These gender stereotypes generate expectations about how women *should* behave.

## Two forms of prejudice (Eagly & Karau)
- **Descriptive prejudice** — descriptive beliefs about women's qualities are dissimilar to qualities expected in leaders → women judged as having less leadership potential ("think manager, think male"; Schein 1973).
- **Prescriptive prejudice** — prescriptions about how women *ought* to behave (warm, nurturing) → **backlash** when women display agentic/role-incongruent behavior. Formalized as **[[Role Congruity Theory]]** (Eagly & Karau 2002).

## Consequences (individual → organizational)
- Internalized stereotypes → self-directed bias in women's fit assessments; women decline male-typed jobs even when qualified; **[[Stereotype Threat]]** consumes cognitive resources.
- Systemic bias: promotion potential favors men despite equal/higher performance ratings (Roth et al. 2012); **[[Implicit Bias]]** persists after overt bias declines; devalued network brokerage; women's negative emotion attributed internally (Brescoll & Uhlmann 2008).

## Interventions
Reduce explicit and implicit bias in selection/appraisal; cluster hiring to reduce tokenism; reduce ambiguity in evaluation (bias is worse when competence is ambiguous — Koch, D'Mello & Sackett 2015).
