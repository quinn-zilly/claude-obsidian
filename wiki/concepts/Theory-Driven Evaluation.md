# Theory-Driven Evaluation

**Theory-Driven Evaluation (TDE)** — also called theory-based, theory-guided, program-theory-driven, or realist evaluation — is any evaluation strategy that explicitly integrates and uses a [[Program Theory|program theory]] (drawn from stakeholder, social-science, or other theories) in conceptualizing, designing, conducting, interpreting, and applying an evaluation.

Rogers et al. (2000): premised on "an explicit theory or model of how the program causes the intended or observed outcomes and an evaluation that is at least partly guided by this model." It explicitly *excludes* evaluations that merely explicate a program's theory without using it to guide the evaluation.

## Two vital components

1. **Conceptual** — explicate a program theory/model.
2. **Empirical** — investigate how the program causes intended/observed outcomes.

## Value proposition

Goes beyond [[Causal Description vs Causal Explanation|causal description]] (*that* A causes B) to **causal explanation** (*how* A causes B) — the hallmark that distinguishes TDE from black-box, method-driven, or product/consumer-oriented evaluation. When a program fails, TDE aims to separate **implementation failure** from **theory failure** from **unsuitable context**.

## Variants (Chen)

Four variants depending on which part of the program theory is the focus: theory-driven process evaluation, intervening-mechanism evaluation, moderating-mechanism evaluation, and integrative process/outcome evaluation. These allow **tailored** (part of the theory) vs **comprehensive** (whole theory) evaluations.

## Method-neutral

Donaldson and Chen stress TDE is methodologically pluralistic — equally suited to quantitative, qualitative, or mixed methods; it does not require SEM or any particular technique.

## Lineage

Traced to Tyler (1930s); resurfaced 1960s–70s (Suchman, Weiss) and 1980s (Chen, Bickman); consolidated by [[Huey-Tsyh Chen|Chen]]'s 1990 book *Theory-Driven Evaluations*. Widely adopted by the Kellogg Foundation, United Way, CDC, and international development bodies (3ie, UNEG, World Bank IEG).

See [[coryn-et-al-2011-theory-driven-evaluation-review|Coryn et al. (2011)]] for the systematic review of TDE practice and the advocate–critic debate.
