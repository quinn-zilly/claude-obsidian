# Prior Knowledge

## Definition

The knowledge, beliefs, and attitudes students bring to a learning context from prior courses and daily life experience. It shapes how new information is filtered and interpreted.

## Double-Edged Nature

**When helpful:** Prior knowledge that is *robust, accurate, and activated at the appropriate time* provides a strong foundation for building new knowledge.

**When harmful:** Prior knowledge can impede learning when it is:
- **Inert** — not spontaneously activated when relevant
- **Insufficient** — too thin to support the task
- **Inappropriately activated** — triggered in the wrong context
- **Inaccurate** — misconceptions or outdated beliefs (see DiSessa 1982 on Aristotelian physics misconceptions; Clement 1982 on mechanics preconceptions)

## Instructional Implications

- Diagnose prior knowledge at course start (pre-assessments, concept inventories)
- Explicitly activate relevant prior knowledge before new material
- Surface and address misconceptions directly — don't assume inaccurate knowledge will self-correct
- Use analogies and bridging concepts to connect new content to what students already know accurately
