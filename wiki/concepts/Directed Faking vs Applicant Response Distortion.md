# Directed Faking vs Applicant Response Distortion

A distinction at the heart of the faking debate: the two empirical paradigms used to study response distortion on self-report personality measures produce **different phenomena** and must not be conflated.

## The two paradigms

- **Lab directed faking** — participants (usually students) are *instructed* to "fake good" or answer as if applying for a job. Creates strong experimental demand. Documents only the **maximum limits** of outright lying under an artificial "that doesn't count" instruction (Ruch & Ruch 1967; Smith & Ellingson 2002 note it *exacerbates* distortion beyond realistic settings).
- **Applicant response distortion** — the *naturally-occurring* distortion of actual job applicants who have real stakes. This is the phenomenon that matters for selection.

## Why the distinction matters

Much confusion in the literature (and, per [[ones-et-al-2007-personality-assessment-support|Ones et al. 2007]], in the [[Morgeson et al. (2007) Panel Critique|Morgeson et al.]] Table 1) comes from **combining directed-faking and applicant studies** — worsened by vote-counting that weights tiny lab samples equally with large-scale meta-analyses. When the focus is confined to **actual applicant data**:

- **Criterion-related validity holds** — predictive, applicant-based studies (Hough 1998b; the [[Integrity Tests|integrity-test]] validities of .29–.41) show useful validity when the stakes are real.
- **Construct validity / factor structure holds up** — Big Five factor structures replicate in applicant samples (NEO-PI-R, HPI; Bradley & Hauenstein 2006: distortion has "a small, practically unimportant" influence).
- **Correcting for faking doesn't help** — social-desirability corrections produce "minimal effects" (Schmitt & Oswald 2006); SD scales themselves don't predict performance.

## Bottom line

Directed-faking lab results are frequently cited as evidence that faking "ruins" personality tests. They instead bound a worst case that applicants rarely enact. On applicant data, the impact of response distortion is **overrated**. See [[Intentional Distortion (Faking)]] for the broader treatment.
