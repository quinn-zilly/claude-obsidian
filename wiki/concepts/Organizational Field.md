# Organizational Field

The unit of analysis introduced by [[Paul DiMaggio]] and [[Walter Powell]] in [[dimaggio-powell-1983-institutional-isomorphism]] (1983) to explain organizational homogeneity. An organizational field comprises all organizations that together constitute a recognized area of institutional life: key suppliers, resource and product consumers, regulatory agencies, and other organizations producing similar services or products.

## Why This Unit of Analysis

The organizational field concept is broader than:
- **Population ecology** (Hannan & Freeman 1977): which focuses only on competing firms
- **Interorganizational network** approaches: which focus only on organizations that actually interact

The field encompasses the *totality* of relevant actors, capturing both connectedness (direct transactions) and structural equivalence (similar positions even without direct ties).

## Field Structuration

Fields only exist to the extent they are institutionally defined. The process of field structuration involves four parts:
1. Increased interaction among organizations in the field
2. Emergence of sharply defined interorganizational structures of domination and coalition
3. Increased information load that organizations in the field must manage
4. Development of mutual awareness that participants are involved in a common enterprise

Once a field becomes well-established, forces emerge that push toward homogenization — even as individual organizations try to differentiate themselves.

## Life Cycle Pattern

Fields typically show initial diversity (varied approaches, experimentation) followed by increasing homogeneity as the field matures. Early adoption of innovations is driven by genuine performance concerns; later adoption provides legitimacy rather than competitive advantage.
