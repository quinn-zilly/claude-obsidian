# Work Design Influences Framework

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]] | [[Teams & Org Behavior MOC]]

An integrative multilevel framework, from [[parker-vandenbroeck-holman-2017-work-design-influences|Parker, Van den Broeck & Holman (2017)]], for understanding **where work designs come from** — i.e., work design treated as a **dependent variable** rather than the usual independent-variable framing in which work design causes outcomes.

## The two organizing problems

All organizations must solve two "universal problems of organizing" (Puranam, Alexy & Reitzig, 2014): **dividing labor** (task division + allocation) and **integrating effort** (cooperation + coordination). The choices made create individual [[Work Design (Concept)|work designs]].

## Two pathways

> [!info] Formal vs. informal
> **Formal decision-making** — managers/CEOs/team leaders deciding the division of labor and integration of effort.
> **Informal, emergent & social processes** — employee-led [[Job Crafting|crafting]], i-deals (per [[Social Exchange Theory]]), role-making, group norms.

Both pathways run through the [[Ability-Motivation-Opportunity Model|AMO model]]: the relevant actor's **motivation**, **KSAs** (knowledge, skills, abilities), and **opportunity** (including power).

## Four levels of influence

Contextual influences sit at three levels (plus individual factors), each affecting work design through three mechanisms — directly, indirectly via managers' AMO, and indirectly via employees' AMO:

1. **Higher-level external** — global/international (globalization, market liberalization, supply chains), national (economy, culture, institutions, [[Institutional Regimes & Employment Regime Theory|institutional regimes]], [[Institutional Isomorphism|isomorphism]]), and occupational factors.
2. **Organizational** — [[Strategic HRM|strategy/SHRM]], high-involvement HR practices, operational uncertainty, technology (SBTC/RBTC), organizational design (bureaucracy, restructuring).
3. **Local context (work group)** — composition/heterogeneity, interdependence, group autonomy, leadership.
4. **Individual** — demographics, competence/learning, personality/motivation, crafting.

## Three mechanisms (the heart of the framework)

For contextual factors:

1. **Direct** — e.g., national working-time regulation changes work hours; occupational standards demarcate tasks.
2. **Indirect via formal decision-making** — context shapes managers' motivation/KSAs/opportunity, which shapes their work-design choices (e.g., low unemployment motivates managers to enrich jobs to retain staff).
3. **Indirect via informal processes** — context shapes employees' motivation/KSAs/opportunity, which shapes crafting/i-deals (e.g., strong trade unions raise employee power to win enriched designs).

Individual factors mirror this: direct (appraisal, job selection), via managers (signaling/discrimination), and via employees' own agentic action.

## Key claims

- Most higher-level effects are **mediated** by managerial and employee choices, not deterministic.
- **Top-down** effects dominate; **bottom-up** effects (crafting, i-deals → HR policy) are real but slow and weaker.
- Evidence is uneven: strongest for organizational factors and national institutions/regimes; weakest for global, national-economy/culture, and occupational factors.
