# Frame-of-Reference Training

**Frame-of-reference (FOR) training** is the rater-training method that became dominant after error-avoidance training was shown to fail (see [[Rater Error Measures]]). Rather than teaching raters what *not* to do, FOR training installs a **shared theory of work performance**.

## Procedure (Bernardin & Buckley 1981; Woehr 1994)
1. Define the performance dimensions.
2. Provide behavioral incidents exemplifying each dimension.
3. Indicate the performance level each incident represents.
4. Practice rating (e.g., videotapes), then discuss the "true score" for each candidate and *why*.

Foreshadowed by Borman (1979); formally named by Bernardin & Buckley (1981).

> [!info] Why it works
> FOR training gives raters a **common set of categories** for observing, organizing, and recalling performance information — tying the cognitive-process research back to practice. It is most effective when the rater's prior theory of performance is already close to the one FOR training instills (Uggerslev & Sulsky 2008). Consensus (DeNisi & Murphy 2017): training raters what *good vs. poor* performance looks like is beneficial; training them to avoid errors is not.
