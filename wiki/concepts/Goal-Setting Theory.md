# Goal-Setting Theory

Nav: [[index]] | [[wiki/sources/locke-latham-2002-goal-setting-theory|Locke & Latham 2002]]

Inductive theory of work motivation built over 35+ years (Locke & Latham). Core premise from Ryan (1970): **conscious goals are the most proximal determinants of action**. A goal = object or aim of an action; typically a specific proficiency standard within a time limit.

## Core Propositions

1. **Difficulty → performance:** linear positive function. Hardest goals → highest effort + performance. d = .52–.82 across meta-analyses.
2. **Specificity + difficulty > "do your best":** SDG consistently superior (d = .42–.80). Vague goals = idiosyncratic standards = wide performance variance.
3. **Specificity alone** does not guarantee high performance (goals vary in difficulty); specificity reduces variance by reducing ambiguity.

## Four Mechanisms

Goals operate via: (1) **Direction** — focus on goal-relevant activity; (2) **Effort** — high goals energize; (3) **Persistence** — hard goals prolong effort; (4) **Strategy arousal** — goals trigger discovery/use of task-relevant knowledge.

## Key Moderators

| Moderator | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| **Goal commitment** | Strongest moderator for difficult goals; facilitated by importance + self-efficacy |
| **Feedback** | Goals + feedback > goals alone; necessary for adjustment |
| **Task complexity** | Attenuates goal effects (d .48 complex vs .67 simple); performance goals can backfire on complex tasks → use [[Learning Goals vs Performance Goals|learning goals]] |
| **Self-efficacy** | Amplifies goal → performance path; mediates personality and incentive effects |

## Motivation Hub

Personal goals + goal commitment + self-efficacy = **motivation hub** (Locke 1991) — proximal conscious causes of action. Mediates effects of assigned goals, monetary incentives, participative decision-making, and leadership.

## Satisfaction Paradox

Goals = reference standard. Exceeding goal → satisfaction; failing → dissatisfaction. High-goal people are least satisfied per trial (bar is high) but most motivated. **High-Performance Cycle**: high goals → performance → rewards → satisfaction + self-efficacy → higher goals. Satisfaction = result, not cause, of high performance.

## Task Complexity Caveat

On novel/complex tasks, specific *performance* goals can impair strategy discovery (anxiety → unsystematic search). Fix: **learning goals** (discover N strategies). Proximal goals added to distal goals also help on dynamic tasks.

## Scope

100+ task types, 40,000+ participants, 8+ countries, 1-minute to 25-year time spans. Individual, group (O'Leary-Kelly et al. 1994), org-unit (Rogers & Hunter 1991), and whole-org levels (Baum, Locke & Smith 2001). Lab, simulation, and field settings.

## Relation to Other Theories

- [[Self-Determination Theory]]: SDT explains *why* people adopt goals (autonomy, competence needs); GST explains *how* goals work once adopted
- [[Social Cognitive Theory]]: fully compatible; both feature goals + self-efficacy; SCT adds modeling and societal-level mechanisms
- [[Pay-for-Performance]]: PFP effects mediated by personal goals + self-efficacy (T. Lee et al. 1997); incentive type × goal difficulty interactions matter
- Prospect Theory: both use reference points; GST adds aspiration level (distal goal)
- Control Theory: GST rejects thermostat model — motivation is discrepancy-*creating*, not just discrepancy-reducing
