# Learning from Experience

Nav: [[index]] | [[Learning & Development MOC]]

Drawing performance-relevant lessons from prior events. The dominant mechanism of leadership and managerial development: *"to the extent leadership is learned, it is learned through experience"* ([[mccall-2010-recasting-leadership-development|McCall, 2010]]).

## Not automatic

The central problem is that learning from experience is **not automatic**: not everyone learns from experience, people learn different things from the same experience, and prior experience shapes what can be learned from current experience (McCall, 2010). Powerful experiences are powerful because of their **challenges** ([[Developmental Experiences]]); but realizing the lesson depends on the learner's readiness, attention, and — above all — **reflection**, which managers are notoriously poor at (Mintzberg). "Much of development is a matter of attention." Simple interventions (e.g., biweekly "what did you do / what did you learn" prompts) measurably enrich experience.

## Three routes into experience

- **Own direct experience** — enactive learning; the Kolb experiential-learning cycle of concrete experience → reflective observation → abstract conceptualization → application.
- **Others' experience, observed** — [[Enactive and Vicarious Learning|vicarious/observational learning]] (Bandura); one-way.
- **Others' experience, jointly processed** — [[Coactive Vicarious Learning (CVL)]] (Myers, 2018): meaning coconstructed through discursive interaction, not just observed.

## Behavioral micro-level

[[Informal Learning Behaviors (ILBs)]] are the self-directed, intentional, field-based behaviors through which experience becomes learning; meta-analytically they predict attitudes, knowledge/skill, and performance ([[cerasoli-et-al-2018-informal-learning-behaviors-meta-analysis|Cerasoli et al., 2018]]).
