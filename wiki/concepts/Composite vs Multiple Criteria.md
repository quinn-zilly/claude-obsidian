# Composite vs Multiple Criteria

Job performance is multidimensional — so should the several criterion measures be **combined into one composite** or **kept separate**? A long-running debate (Cascio & Aguinis 2011).

## Composite criterion

Advocates (Brogden & Taylor 1950; Thorndike 1949; Toops 1944; Nagle 1953): the criterion should give a single yardstick of "value to the organization." A single index is indispensable for decisions and comparisons. The **dollar criterion** treats the criterion as an **economic** construct — overall contribution measured in dollars via cost accounting; latent factors like skill "do not satisfy the logical requirement of an adequate criterion" because their effect is realized only in the end product.

## Multiple criteria

Advocates (Cattell 1957; Dunnette 1963; Guion 1961; Pulakos et al. 1988): demonstrably different variables should **not** be combined — "Ten men and two bottles of beer cannot be added to give the same total as two men and ten bottles of beer" (Cattell). When dimensions are uncorrelated (e.g., recruiters' selling / human-relations / organizing skills were all valid yet mutually unrelated; Pulakos et al. 1988), a composite is "ambiguous and psychologically nonsensical." The criterion should be a **behavioral/psychological** construct.

> [!contradiction] Composite implies a general factor
> Guion (1961): "The fallacy of the single criterion lies in its assumption... that there is a general factor in all criteria accounting for virtually all of the important variance." Schmidt & Kaplan (1971) refined this: compositing *does* imply a single underlying dimension, but that dimension may be **economic**, not behavioral/psychological. Compare with [[General Factor in Job Performance]], where a *substantive behavioral* general factor is argued to exist — the two debates are related but not identical.

## Resolution — depends on purpose

- For **psychological understanding** of predictor–criterion relationships → keep dimensions **separate**.
- For **managerial decisions** (promotion, budgeting, program evaluation) → **weight into an economic composite**, regardless of intercorrelations.

Both are legitimate for their own ends; theoretical-relevance measures should **supplement, not replace** practical ones. Weighting is consequential: Murphy & Shiarella (1997) found **34% of the variance in a selection battery's validity** came from *how* task and contextual performance were weighted into the composite.

## Domain-construction reading (Binning & Barrett 1989)

[[binning-barrett-1989-validity-personnel-decisions|Binning & Barrett (1989)]] recast the debate as a difference in **how the performance domain is conceptualized** (see [[Predictor vs Performance Domain]]): the **composite** model implies a unitary, often **economic** conception of total worth (job-oriented, "what is accomplished"); the **multiple** model treats performance as a behavioral domain of differentially valued dimensions (worker-oriented, "what is done"). Which job-analysis data justify the criterion-measure inference depends on which model is chosen.
