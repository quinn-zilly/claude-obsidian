# Fairness Heuristic Theory

An **uncertainty-based** explanation for why employees care about [[Organizational Justice|justice]] (Lind 2001; Van den Bos 2001). Justice matters because it helps people manage **uncertainty** — especially social uncertainty about whether an authority can be trusted not to exploit them.

## Mechanism

- People face a **fundamental social dilemma**: cooperating with an authority yields benefits (information, mentoring) but risks exploitation. To decide, they need to judge the authority's **trustworthiness**.
- Information about outcomes of similar others is often unavailable, so people use **[[Procedural Justice|procedural-justice]] information as a substitute** to evaluate the fairness of outcomes and of the authority (Van den Bos & Lind 2001).
- **Primacy effect**: the *first* available justice information forms a **heuristic** that guides future justice judgments. The heuristic is quick, **stable**, and only revised by strong contradictory information (Lind, Kray & Thompson 2001).

## Uncertainty management theory

An extension (Lind & Van den Bos 2002): broadens beyond authority-trust to **general workplace uncertainty** (new job, layoffs, mergers, change, market turmoil). Uncertainty is aversive, so people seek justice information to cope. Justice effects are **stronger under high uncertainty**.

## Why it matters

- Explains the **event-vs-entity** finding ([[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al. 2013]]): global justice judgments form quickly from whatever information is available, so discrete "events" and global "entities" yield similar relationships.
- One of several complementary lenses (instrumental, relational, exchange, deontic, uncertainty) that [[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic (2023)]] argues **complement** rather than compete.
