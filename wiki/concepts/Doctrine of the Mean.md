# Doctrine of the Mean

The **doctrine of the mean** (also the **Golden Mean**) is the cross-cultural philosophical tenet that virtue and good outcomes lie between **deficiency and excess** — proportionality over extremity. "Everything in moderation; nothing in excess."

Traced independently to **Aristotle** (*Nicomachean Ethics*) in the West and **Confucius** in the East ("Perfect is the virtue which is according to the Mean!"), it was for its proponents a moral *and* practical imperative.

## Relevance to management

[[pierce-aguinis-2013-tmgt-effect|Pierce & Aguinis (2013)]] make it the philosophical root of the [[Too-Much-of-a-Good-Thing Effect]]. They note management scholarship has historically discussed the mean rarely, defaulting instead to a "more is better" linear assumption — the deficiency the TMGT meta-theory corrects.

Aristotle's qualification matters for prediction: only **actions** and **passions** are subject to the mean (→ inverted-U shapes); **things good in themselves** are not (→ asymptotic shapes).
