# Testing Effect

The testing effect (also called retrieval practice effect) is the finding that actively retrieving information from memory produces stronger, more durable learning than restudying the same material for the same amount of time.

## Core Finding

Testing — even without feedback — enhances long-term retention more than additional study. This is a [[Desirable Difficulties|desirable difficulty]]: retrieval feels harder in the moment but produces superior long-term learning.

## Mechanism

Retrieval practice strengthens memory traces and improves the accessibility of information. The effort of retrieval — especially when retrieval is difficult — is itself what produces the benefit (see [[New Theory of Disuse]]).

## Implications

- Quizzing, flashcards, and self-testing are more effective than re-reading
- Initial retrieval failure (with subsequent feedback) can be beneficial
- Applies to learning facts, concepts, and procedural knowledge
