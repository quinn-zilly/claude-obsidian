# Registered Reports

Registered Reports (RRs) are a publishing format in which peer review and editorial acceptance occur in two stages: first the design is reviewed and in-principle accepted *before* results are known; then the completed study is published regardless of outcome.

## How It Works

**Stage 1 — Design Review:**
- Authors submit introduction, hypotheses, and detailed methods/analysis plan
- Reviewers assess: Is the question worthwhile? Is the design adequate to test the hypotheses?
- If accepted: **in-principle acceptance (IPA)** — journal commits to publish if protocol is followed

**Stage 2 — Completion Review:**
- Authors submit completed study with results and discussion
- Reviewers verify: Was the registered protocol followed? Are conclusions evidence-bound?
- Article published regardless of whether results are significant or null

## What Registered Reports Eliminate

| Bias | How RRs Address It |
|------|-------------------|
| **[[Publication Bias]]** | Commitment to publish *before* results; null results see print |
| **[[P-hacking]]** | Analysis plan pre-specified; deviations must be disclosed |
| **[[HARKing]]** | Hypotheses registered before data; cannot be reformulated post-hoc |
| **CARKing** (Critiquing After Results are Known) | Reviewers assess design on merits, not outcome |

## Adoption

- Introduced by Christopher Chambers in *Cortex* (2013)
- **>100 journals adopted** as of 2018; >200 by the early 2020s
- *Nature Human Behaviour* — adopted in its inaugural volume (same issue as [[munafo-2017-manifesto]])
- Journals span psychology, neuroscience, medicine, ecology, economics

## Relationship to Preregistration

Registered Reports are the strongest form of preregistration because the journal's commitment is binding. A standard preregistration on OSF documents your plan but doesn't guarantee publication; an RR IPA means the journal has agreed to publish before you collect data.

## Permitted Exploratory Analyses

RRs do not prohibit exploratory analyses. Authors may report any unregistered explorations if clearly labeled as *post hoc exploratory*. The requirement is only that they are not presented as confirmatory tests.

## Source

The format was invented by Christopher Chambers (Cardiff University) and advanced through COS. Nosek and Lakens (2014) described the format theoretically; [[munafo-2017-manifesto]] Box 3 provides a detailed summary.
