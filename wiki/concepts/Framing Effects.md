# Framing Effects

Phenomenon where logically equivalent descriptions of a choice produce different preferences. Arises from [[Prospect Theory]]'s reference-point value function: same outcome framed as gain vs. loss sits on differently shaped parts of the value function.

## Risky Choice Framing

Hogarth (1987): MBA subjects chose between riskless option A and risky option B (equal EV):
- Framed as *money saved* → prefer A (certain, risk averse)
- Framed as *losses* → prefer B (risky, risk seeking)

**Mechanism**: gains domain = concave value function (risk averse); losses domain = convex (risk seeking). Same objective choice, different reference point → different risk preference.

Classic: McNeil, Pauker & Tversky (1988) — mortality rate vs. survival rate framing shifts treatment preferences for both patients and physicians.

## Attribute Framing (Riskless)

Levin & Gaeth (1988): "75% lean" vs. "25% fat" ground beef — lean framing more favorable, even after tasting.

Credit card industry insisted on "cash discount" rather than "credit card surcharge" for gas station pricing (Thaler 1980).

Taxonomy: Levin, Schneider & Gaeth (1998) — three framing types: risky choice, attribute, goal.

## Mechanism Refinements

Kuvaas & Selart (2004): negative framing → more thorough information processing (not just valence shift) → larger processing effort may account for some preference shifts.

## Organizational Implications

- Performance appraisals: same performance described as "met target" vs. "did not exceed" → different evaluations
- Negotiation: gains framing → risk averse → more likely to accept offer; losses framing → risk seeking → more likely to reject and gamble
- Benefit design: opt-in vs. opt-out enrollment (loss-frame default = keep default → very different enrollment rates)
