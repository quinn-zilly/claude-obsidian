# Four Justice Dimensions

The dominant structure of [[Organizational Justice]]: four dimensions, each a bundle of **justice rules** (criteria of appropriateness). Crystallized by **Colquitt (2001)**, who split the older "interactional justice" into interpersonal and informational facets, producing the four-factor model now standard in the field.

## The Dimensions and Their Rules (Colquitt & Rodell 2015, Table 8.1)

### Procedural justice — fairness of decision *processes*
Rules (Thibaut & Walker 1975; Leventhal 1980):

- **Process control** — procedures provide voice
- **Decision control** — influence over outcomes
- **Consistency** — across persons and time
- **Bias suppression** — neutral and unbiased
- **Accuracy** — based on accurate information
- **Correctability** — opportunities for appeal
- **Representativeness** — account for subgroup concerns
- **Ethicality** — uphold moral standards

### Distributive justice — fairness of *outcomes*
Rules (Adams 1965; Leventhal 1976):

- **Equity** — outcomes allocated by contribution
- **Equality** — outcomes allocated equally
- **Need** — outcomes allocated by need

### Interpersonal justice — fairness of *enactment*
Rules (Bies & Moag 1986; Greenberg 1993):

- **Respect** — sincere, polite treatment
- **Propriety** — refrain from improper remarks

### Informational justice — fairness of *explanations*
Rules (Bies & Moag 1986; Greenberg 1993):

- **Truthfulness** — explanations are honest
- **Justification** — explanations are thorough

## Lineage of the Structure

1. **Bies & Moag (1986)** introduce "interactional justice" (respect, propriety, truthfulness, justification).
2. **Moorman (1991)** builds a widely used scale but with cross-pollinated formal/interactional dimensions.
3. **Colquitt (2001)** introduces a clean four-factor scale (the modern standard; supervisor- and event-convertible — Box 8.1 in [[colquitt-rodell-2015-measuring-justice-fairness|Colquitt & Rodell 2015]]) and confirms interpersonal/informational distinctiveness via measurement-model comparison.

## High Intercorrelation

The four dimensions correlate **.37–.64 (avg .49)** ([[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al. 2013]]). This creates **multicollinearity** in faceted models and motivates **latent justice** modeling (second-order factor) — see [[Justice Measurement Taxonomy]].

## Structural vs. Social Split (Greenberg)

Procedural and distributive justice are more **structural** → more stable over time, especially organization-focused. Interpersonal and informational are more **social** → more variable day-to-day, especially supervisor-focused. Relevant for within-person ([[Experience-Sampling Methodology|ESM]]) designs.
