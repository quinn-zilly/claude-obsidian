# Ability-Motivation-Opportunity Model

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design Influences Framework]]

The **AMO model** (Appelbaum, Bailey, Berg & Kalleberg, 2000; Blumberg & Pringle, 1982) holds that behavior/performance is a function of three proximal ingredients: **Ability** (knowledge, skills, abilities — [[KSA Framework|KSAs]]), **Motivation**, and **Opportunity**.

In [[parker-vandenbroeck-holman-2017-work-design-influences|Parker, Van den Broeck & Holman (2017)]], AMO is the **proximal engine** of the [[Work Design Influences Framework]]: contextual and individual factors shape work design largely by shaping the AMO of the two sets of actors who make work-design choices.

## The three components

- **Motivation** — for managers, both *autonomous* motivation (e.g., desire to retain staff, personal commitment to good jobs) and *controlled* motivation (e.g., cost pressure, market pressure to copy competitors). For employees, e.g., wanting work–home balance, fit to KSAs, or to exploit new technology.
- **KSAs / Ability** — e.g., managers' knowledge of work-design options and skill in participative decision-making; employees' competence to craft. Parker et al. combine motivation and KSAs into one category for simplicity, since both are individual attributes shaped by similar forces.
- **Opportunity** — "the particular configuration of the field of forces surrounding a person and his or her task that enables or constrains task performance and that are beyond the person's direct control" (Blumberg & Pringle, 1982: 565). Crucially **includes power**: without power to mobilize resources or influence others, even a motivated, skilled manager (or employee) cannot redesign work.

## Two actors

The model applies symmetrically to:

1. **Managers** (those in formal authority) → formal work-design decisions.
2. **Employees** → informal/emergent work design via [[Job Crafting|crafting]], i-deals, role-making.

## Why it matters

AMO is what makes the [[Work Design Influences Framework|influences framework]] non-deterministic: context rarely dictates work design directly. Instead it shifts actors' motivation, ability, and opportunity — which is why the same contextual force (e.g., new technology, a trade union, operational uncertainty) can yield enriched *or* impoverished jobs depending on the actors and their power.
