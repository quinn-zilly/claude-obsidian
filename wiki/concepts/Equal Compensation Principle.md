# Equal Compensation Principle

**Source:** Milgrom & Roberts (1992) — *Economics, Organization and Management*

**Definition:** When an employee is responsible for multiple performance goals or tasks, and those goals are not incentivized equally, the employee will focus their effort on the most heavily incentivized metrics and neglect the rest. To avoid distorted effort allocation, compensation across all valued tasks must be equalized — either by incentivizing all dimensions or by reducing incentive intensity on the measured ones.

Also known as the **multitasking problem** in agency theory.

---

## Mechanism

PFP systems that measure and reward only a *subset* of performance dimensions create misaligned incentives:

- Results-based PFP tied only to quantity → neglect of quality
- Sales incentives tied only to volume → neglect of customer relationships, compliance, appropriate product-fit
- Short-term bonuses without long-term measures → excessive risk-taking, horizon problem

---

## Classic Example

**Novartis pharmaceutical sales incentives (pre-reform):** Individual bonuses tied solely to prescription volume. Result: improper incentives to physicians to overprescribe Novartis drugs → $678M settlement with the U.S. Department of Justice.

**Fix:** Replace results-only PFP with a combined formula incorporating: (1) volume results, AND (2) ratings of whether employees exhibited appropriate values and behaviors in achieving those results. Also reduced [[PFP Intensity]] by capping bonus at 35% of compensation.

---

## Implications for PFP Design

1. **Use multiple performance measures** — combine results-based AND behavior-based measures to ensure all valued dimensions are incentivized
2. **Monitor for substitution effects** — when intensity increases on one metric, check for deterioration on non-incentivized dimensions
3. **Reduce intensity on narrow metrics** — if comprehensive measurement is impossible, reduce the PFP intensity on the measurable subset

---

## Connection to Endogeneity and Research Design

Most PFP field research focuses on easily measurable outcome metrics. The equal compensation principle implies that PFP effects on *unmeasured* dimensions (safety, quality, customer relationships) may be negative even when effects on measured dimensions are positive. Studies that measure only the incentivized metric will overestimate net PFP effectiveness.
