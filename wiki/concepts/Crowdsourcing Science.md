# Crowdsourcing Science

## Definition

Crowdsourcing science refers to the horizontal distribution of research ownership, resources, and expertise across many contributors — in contrast to the standard **vertically integrated** model in which a small team controls the full research pipeline. Coined in this sense by Uhlmann et al. (2019), *Scientific Utopia III*.

Crowdsourcing is explicitly proposed as a **complement**, not replacement, for standard small-team science.

## Two Models

| Dimension | Standard Model (Vertical) | Crowdsourcing (Horizontal) |
|---|---|---|
| Team size | 1–5 | Dozens to thousands |
| Scope | Full pipeline in one lab | Distributed components |
| Credit | Authorship on one paper | CRediT taxonomy; contribution statements |
| Power | Limited by local resources | Scales to any sample size |
| Generalizability | Single population, single design | Cross-cultural, multi-design |
| Risk of bias | Researcher degrees of freedom; one team's priors | Diluted; diverse analytic approaches; no one team's career depends on result |

## 2×2 Typology

Crowd projects vary on two axes — (1) communication between members (curated independent contributions → crowd collaboration) and (2) inclusiveness (select experts → open to anyone). See [[uhlmann-2019-crowdsourcing-science]] for full typology and examples.

## Three Primary Benefits

1. **Big science** — enables research designs impossible for single labs (high-powered, cross-cultural, multi-stimuli)
2. **Democratization** — opens participation to researchers from less-resourced institutions; counters [[Matthew Effect (Science)]]
3. **Robustness assessment** — uniquely suited to evaluate generalizability; failure to replicate is consistent across labs (not explained by moderators)

## Key Empirical Demonstrations

- **Silberzahn et al. (2018):** 29 analyst teams, same dataset, 29 distinct analytic specifications → effect-size estimates from slightly negative to large positive. Shows analytic flexibility is structural, not individual error.
- **Landy et al. (2018):** same hypothesis given to multiple researcher teams to design independently → 4/5 hypotheses had significant effects in *opposite directions* across designs.
- **Many Labs (Klein et al., 2014, 2018):** 36–125 labs; consistent finding: non-replicable effects fail consistently across populations (see [[Many Labs]])
- **Prediction markets (Dreber et al., 2015):** aggregated scientist predictions accurately anticipate replication outcomes — "wisdom of crowds" works for forecasting science

## Crowdsourcing Lifecycle

Crowd approaches apply at every stage: ideation (Polymath), assembling resources (StudySwap), study design, data collection (citizen science, PSA 300+ labs), data analysis (many analysts), pre-publication replication, writing, peer review, and forecasting future directions.

## Risks and Limits

- **Transaction costs:** organizing global collectives creates bureaucracy; opportunity cost vs. many small independent projects
- **Credit ambiguity:** hundreds of authors creates attribution problems → requires CRediT taxonomy reform
- **Conformity risk:** high-communication crowd designs risk herding; wisdom of crowds requires independent agents (Lorenz et al., 2011)
- **Diminishing returns:** not all questions need crowd treatment; small teams remain best for initial evidence generation

## Structural Reforms Required

Crowdsourcing won't scale without changing: grant allocation, authorship credit (CRediT), promotion criteria (reward replication), publication criteria (require independent verification at top journals), and pedagogy (integrate CREP/Pipeline into methods classes).
