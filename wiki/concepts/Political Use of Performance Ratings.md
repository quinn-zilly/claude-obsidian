# Political Use of Performance Ratings

The most-studied *nonperformance* determinant of ratings: supervisors **deliberately distort** ratings to serve goals other than accurate measurement (Cleveland & Murphy 1992; Longenecker, Sims & Gioia 1987; Murphy & Cleveland 1995). Rating is treated as **goal-directed behavior**, not a measurement act.

## Four rater goals (Murphy & Cleveland 1995)

1. **Task performance** — use ratings to influence the ratee's future performance.
2. **Interpersonal** — preserve/improve the supervisor–subordinate relationship.
3. **Strategic** — raise the supervisor's or workgroup's standing in the org.
4. **Internalized** — the rater's own beliefs about how they *should* rate (e.g., the "hard grader" self-image).

## Consequences

These goals mostly push toward the **"Lake Wobegon effect"** — inflating nearly everyone — because high ratings maximize rewards, protect relationships, and make the supervisor look good, while orgs offer **few rewards for accuracy and few sanctions for inflation**. Common cited reasons to **overrate** (get more money, encourage, avoid confrontation, look good) and to **underrate** (make an example, build a termination case, send a "leave" message).

> [!key-insight] Motivation, not ability
> The political account reframes appraisal's failure: raters largely **can** rate accurately but **won't** — the problem is motivation/goals, undercutting decades of ability-focused interventions (rating scales, error training). This is the anti-ratings side's central diagnosis in [[adler-et-al-2016-getting-rid-of-ratings|Adler et al. (2016)]] and feeds the "raters *will*" leg of the [[Three Assumptions of Performance Rating]].
