# Information-Motivation-Behavioural Skills Model

Developed by Fisher and Fisher (1992, 2006), originally for HIV risk behaviour. Proposes three independent but interacting determinants of health behaviour: information, motivation, and behavioural skills.

## Core Structure

**Information** (knowledge about the behaviour) × **Motivation** (attitudes, norms, intentions) × **Behavioural skills** (routines + perceived self-efficacy) → **Behaviour**

The three components interact: high motivation without skills still fails; skills without motivation may not be deployed.

## Components

1. **Information**: equivalent to [[Behaviour Change Determinants|knowledge]] — factual understanding of the behaviour and its consequences. Necessary but insufficient.

2. **Motivation**: encompasses attitudes (particularly [[Behaviour Change Determinants|behavioural attitudes]]) and social norms (injunctive, descriptive). The evaluative and social pull toward a behaviour.

3. **Behavioural skills**: the specific routines, self-efficacy, and perceived behavioural control needed to enact the behaviour. Most unique contribution of IMB — distinguishes *knowing* from *being able to do*.

## Empirical Status (Albarracín et al. 2024)

The model has strong meta-analytic support in the HIV domain. In the broader cross-domain review, the three components map onto the individual determinants hierarchy:
- Information/knowledge: **negligible** intervention effect
- Motivation (attitudes/norms): **negligible to small** effects
- Behavioural skills: **small** but most effective of the three — validates IMB's emphasis on skills over knowledge

The practical implication: IMB-based interventions should foreground skills training over information provision.

## Extensions

- **Situated IMB** (Amico 2011): adds situational context for chronic care engagement
- Applied to antiretroviral adherence, cancer screening, diabetes management, sexual risk behaviour
