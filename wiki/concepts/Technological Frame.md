# Technological Frame

## Definition

A **technological frame** (Orlikowski & Gash 1994) is a collectively constructed set of assumptions, knowledge, and expectations held by a group of individuals regarding a technology and its uses and applications in a specific organizational context.

Orlikowski & Gash (1994, p. 178): technological frames mark instances in which individuals in organizations make sense "not only of the nature and role of the technology itself, but the specific conditions, applications and consequences of that technology in particular contexts."

## Origin and Development

The concept draws on cognitive frame theory (Minsky 1975; Tannen 1985) and extends it with a sociological focus: technological frames are not just individual cognition but **socially shared** within and across user groups, shaping patterns of technology implementation and use.

Initial stages of framing a technology are formative — they establish interpretive trajectories that constrain subsequent understanding and use (Davidson 2006; Kaplan & Tripsas 2008).

## Frame Congruence and Incongruence

**Incongruent frames** across user groups → different innovation paths and problem definitions. Leonardi (2011) — CrashLab simulation technology: engineers from different departments framed the technology differently, leading to different searches for solutions while "forgetting" their fundamental framing disagreement.

**Congruent frames** → aligned behavior patterns, reduced ambiguity, coordinated use. But: overly tight congruence not always optimal.

**Challenge to congruence assumption:**
- Mazmanian (2013) — mobile email in two occupational groups: shared broad assumptions + room for different uses = effective heterogeneous adaptation
- Vaccaro et al. (2011) — "granularization": decomposing a frame into sub-components enables knowledge exchange across specializing groups
- Van Burg et al. (2013); Donnellon et al. (1986) — ambiguity in technological frames can foster coordination by leaving space for diverse motivations and actions

## "Truce Frame" and Political Contests

Competing technological frames within an organization → political contests. Azad & Faraj (2008) — process of negotiating frames into an aligned "truce frame" reduces ambiguity, fosters joint understanding, and directs patterns of use.

## Key Critique (Davidson 2006)

A typical technological frame study: identifies stakeholder groups → analyzes frames' influence on sensemaking → investigates incongruence → assesses consequences. **Problem:** point-in-time snapshots; static view of frames; neglects dynamic processes of frame construction and evolution.

Needed: more processual research on *how* technological frames are constructed and evolve over time, and how they in turn shape behaviors.
