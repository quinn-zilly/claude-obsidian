# Decision Justification Theory (DJT)

Connolly & Zeelenberg (2002). Two-component model of decision-related regret. Explains when regret is felt strongly and how it shapes decision behavior.

## Two Components

### 1. Outcome Regret
Comparison of actual outcome to a counterfactual reference point:
- Outcome of unchosen alternative
- Status quo
- Expected outcome
- Outcome received by another person

Driven by: how much worse did I do than I could have?

### 2. Process Regret (Self-Blame)
Assessment of whether the decision *itself* was justified, independent of outcome.

Driven by: was this decision defensible? Did I follow a careful, thoughtful process?

> A "vigilant" decision process (Janis & Mann 1977) = regret-reducing justification across most contexts.

## Key Predictions

**Action effect**: commission produces more regret than omission (Kahneman & Tversky 1982 stock-switching study: 95% rated switcher more regretful). But this reverses:
- Over time: short-run regrets action, long-run regrets inaction (Gilovich & Medvec 1994)
- For role-appropriate actions: entrepreneurial investors' actions = justified → less process regret (Seta et al. 2001)
- For contextually justified switches: brand-switching justified by poor prior brand experience → less regret (Inman & Zeelenberg 2002)
- For contextualized soccer coaches: changing a losing team = justified → less regret; changing a winning team = unjustified → more regret (Zeelenberg et al. 2002)

**Vaccination**: mothers' decisions producing bad outcomes → less regret if based on careful process (Reb & Connolly 2010).

## Regret Priming Effects

Regret priming → more careful decisions (Reb 2008: more info, more effort, better outcomes).

**Outcome regret priming**: leads to *myopic regret avoidance* — reject feedback on unchosen alternatives → impede learning → lower earnings (Reb & Connolly 2009).

**Process regret priming**: accept feedback → learn → better performance. Process regret priming > outcome regret priming for decision quality.

## Reason-Based Choice Effects

DJT explains:
- **Decoy effect**: adding dominated option shifts choice toward "justifiable" option
- **Accept/reject asymmetry**: accept task focuses on good attributes; reject focuses on bad → different choices despite identical options
- **Most-important-attribute effect**: choosing based on best attribute = "justifiable" shortcut

Regret salience *reduces* these nonnormative effects by prompting more scrupulous examination of decision process (Connolly, Reb & Kausel 2010).

> DJT regret-salience manipulations = rare example of theoretically grounded debiasing technique.
