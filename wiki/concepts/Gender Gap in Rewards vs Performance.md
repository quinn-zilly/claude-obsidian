# Gender Gap in Rewards vs Performance

The core empirical pattern from [[joshi-son-roh-2015-women-close-the-gap|Joshi, Son & Roh (2015)]]: **the male–female gap in organizational rewards is far larger than the gap in performance evaluations, and the two are decoupled.**

- Rewards gap **d = .56** vs. performance gap **d = .04** → rewards gap ≈ **14×** larger.
- Performance gap's CI includes zero → women essentially **close the performance gap**.
- **Performance differences do NOT explain reward differences** (H5 rejected via semipartial correlation).

> [!key-insight] Why this matters
> Reward-allocation and performance-evaluation are **distinct decisions**. Rewards (promotions, bonuses) are often **zero-sum** choices among qualified candidates; performance metrics (billable hours, sales) are not. Treating "productivity differences" as the explanation for pay gaps (the common human-capital assumption) is empirically unsupported once performance is measured directly.

## Where the gap widens vs. closes
- **Widens:** male-dominated occupations, high occupational prestige (reward-only penalty), high job complexity.
- **Closes / reverses:** only when the **industry has a high proportion of women at executive/senior-manager levels** (the sole reversal condition); women also reach performance parity in **routine/low-complexity jobs** even in male-dominated settings.

Connects the DEI/diversity cluster to the compensation cluster ([[Pay Dispersion]], reward allocation). The complex-job mechanism mirrors [[Identifiability of Individual Inputs|Shaw's identifiability]] problem — low-scrutiny inputs let bias enter reward decisions.
