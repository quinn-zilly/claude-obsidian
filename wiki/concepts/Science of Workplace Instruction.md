# Science of Workplace Instruction

Proposed by [[Kurt Kraiger]] and [[J. Kevin Ford]] ([[kraiger-ford-2021-workplace-instruction|Kraiger & Ford 2021]]) to bridge the **science of training** (organizational effectiveness perspective) and the **science of learning** (cognitive mechanisms perspective).

## Definition

> The application of evidence-based principles that have been found to help individuals learn knowledge, skills, and attitudes that impact job performance and organizational effectiveness.

Key move: directs attention from *whether* training works to *how* people learn — the learning events that mediate between instructional events and outcomes.

## Two Frameworks

### Training Systems Framework (Figure 1)

```
Instructional events          Instructional outcomes
(Principles, Methods, Media)  (Transfer, Performance, Effectiveness)
        |                              ^
        v                              |
Learning events ——→ Learning outcomes
(Encoding, Organizing,     (Affective, Knowledge, Skills)
 Retrieving)
```

**Gap identified:** I-O training research focuses on corners (events → outcomes); the *mediating path* (learning events, learning outcomes) is understudied.

### Core Elements (Figure 2)

Three foundational elements with meaningful intersections:

- **Learner** — individual variability, developmental stage
- **Instructional Principles** — empirically supported propositions (see [[Instructional Principles]])
- **Training Delivery** — methods (behavioral modeling, error management) + media (VR, online, classroom)

Key claim: **the medium is not the message**. Methods should be invariant over media. Design quality (principles) > channel choice.

## Learning Definition

> Learning = engagement in mental processes (encoding, organizing, retrieving) that result in acquisition and retention of knowledge, skills, and/or affect over time, along with the capacity to identify conditions of performance and respond appropriately.

Three learning events:
- **Encoding** — selecting content into working memory
- **Organizing** — building meaningful representations (task sequences, if-then schemas)
- **Retrieving** — recalling and applying; strengthens memory traces

## Implications

1. Instructional principles generalize across learners — no need for ATIs by learning style or age
2. Well-designed training works for all; heterogeneity in meta-analyses reflects design quality variance
3. Self-directed learning fails without built-in structure (see [[Self-Directed Learning]])
4. Technology (VR, games) only helps when instructional principles are embedded (see [[Synthetic Learning Environments]])

## Relations

- [[On-the-Job Development]] — informal learning domain where same principles apply but are rarely engineered in
- [[Organizational Learning]] — individual learning events aggregate to org-level knowledge
- [[70-20-10 Rule]] — the 70% experience bucket is often unsupported by principles; this framework shows why that matters
