# Critical–Ideological Paradigm

A paradigm of **emancipation and transformation** in which the researcher's proactive values are central to the purpose and methods of research. Critical theory exists to disrupt and challenge the status quo (Kincheloe & McLaren). Ponterotto (2005) labels it "critical–ideological" and folds **feminist, critical race, and queer theory** under the same rubric; it often anchors qualitative multicultural research.

## Lineage

Origins at the **Frankfurt School** (Institute of Social Research, Univ. of Frankfurt, 1920s) — Horkheimer, Adorno, Marcuse, drawing on Marx, Kant, Hegel, Weber, who held that "injustice and subjugation shape the lived world." After fleeing Nazi Germany, Marcuse stayed in the US and influenced the 1960s student movement, spreading critical theory through academia.

There is **no single critical theory** (Kincheloe & McLaren), but criticalists share assumptions: all thought is mediated by socially/historically constituted power relations; facts can't be separated from values or ideology; language is central to subjectivity; some groups are privileged over others; oppression has many interconnected faces; and mainstream research often reproduces class, race, and gender oppression. The criticalist uses research as a form of social criticism. Compare [[Constructivism–Interpretivism]]: both treat reality as socially constructed, but criticalists frame it within power relations and aim to **emancipate** oppressed groups.

## Stance on the five [[Philosophy of Science Parameters|parameters]]

- **Ontology**: reality shaped by ethnic, cultural, gender, social, political values and mediated by power.
- **Epistemology**: transactional, subjectivist, and **dialectic** — interaction aims to empower participants toward egalitarian change.
- **Axiology**: values go furthest of all paradigms — the researcher *hopes and expects* their value biases to drive the outcome (emancipation). Linked to liberation thinkers (Freire).
- **Rhetoric**: first-person, value-laden; participants may be co-investigators and coauthors.
- **Methodology**: interviews used dialogically (deep insight) **and** dialectically (transformation); participatory action research. Idiographic and emic.
