# Affective Events Theory (AET)

**Origin:** Weiss & Cropanzano (1996) — proposed to address organizational psychology's neglect of affect in job attitudes research.

**Core model:** Work features (stable job characteristics, organizational policies) → distribution of work events → job affect → affect-driven behaviors AND/OR cognitive evaluation → judgment-driven behaviors.

> [!key-insight] Key Distinction
> AET separates two attitude–behavior pathways: (1) **affect → affect-driven behaviors** (OCB, work withdrawal — short-term, statelike); (2) **cognitive evaluation → judgment-driven behaviors** (turnover — longer-term, reasoned). Dispositions moderate the events → affect link.

## Three Differentiating Features (Judge et al. 2011)

1. **Job features vs. job events** — features (policies, physical conditions) shape the *distribution* of events but are not the proximal cause of affect; specific episodic events are
2. **Centrality of affect** — affect is not a residual but a core feature of job attitudes
3. **Independent pathways** — affect-driven and cognition-driven behaviors are distinct streams

## Empirical Support

AET has become a dominant framework for ESM/within-person research:
- Nearly every study of moods/emotions at work or within-individual attitude variation invokes AET
- ESM research shows within-person variability in job attitudes nearly as large as in moods/emotions
- Negative events → negative moods; positive events → positive moods (Miner et al. 2005)
- Interpersonal conflicts with customers trigger more negative attitudes (Grandey et al. 2002)
- Work emotions mediate work features → job satisfaction (Wegge et al. 2006)
- Transformational leadership → more positive employee emotions; buffers emotion regulation–dissatisfaction link (Bono et al. 2007)

## Relationship to ESM Research

AET motivates the use of [[Experience-Sampling Methodology]] because affective reactions are fleeting and episodic — one-shot designs cannot capture them. ESM allows ecological momentary assessment of both job attitudes and behaviors.

Multilevel ESM studies show:
- Within-person job attitude variation predicts within-person OCB variation (Ilies et al. 2006)
- Within-person attitude variation predicts workplace deviance (Judge et al. 2006)

## The Affect Lens on Justice

AET grounds the **affect-based lens** on [[Organizational Justice|justice]] (co-author [[Russell Cropanzano]]). [[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al. (2013)]] found justice→performance is mediated by **state positive and negative affect** — a complement to the social-exchange lens. Justice reduces state negative affect; affect may be the missing mediator for justice→CWB, which exchange quality fails to explain.
