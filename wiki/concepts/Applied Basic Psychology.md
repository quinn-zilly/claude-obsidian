# Applied Basic Psychology

Two-part framework (Baddeley 1979) for integrating science and practice:

1. **Applied basic psychology**: testing theories in real-world settings — seeing how well lab-derived theories fare when applied
2. **Basic applied psychology**: domesticating phenomena observed in real-world settings for basic research — bringing field problems back into the lab

## Fischhoff's Use

These two bridging activities explain how decision science has expanded into climate change, intelligence analysis, risk management, and health policy.

- Real-world engagement tests whether lab results replicate in naturalistic settings (applied basic)
- Real-world problems reveal phenomena (e.g., superforecasters, ED physician decision failures) that become new research objects (basic applied)

## Three Roles for Decision Scientists in Applied Settings

1. **Representing all of psychology** — not just own specialty; may be only psychologist in room
2. **Translation** — making psychological findings interpretable to analytically-oriented colleagues (natural scientists, policy analysts, engineers)
3. **Sustained relationships** with decision makers — learn their concerns, earn trust, benefit from domain expertise

## Significance

The two-way translation between basic and applied research is what made decision science productive and policy-relevant. Without it, behavioral research stays abstract; without basic research, applied work lacks theory.
