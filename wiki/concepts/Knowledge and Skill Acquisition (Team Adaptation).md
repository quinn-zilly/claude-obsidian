# Knowledge and Skill Acquisition (Team Adaptation)

**Phase 6** of the [[Team Adaptation (Stimulus-Based Model)|Pearsall et al. (2025) stimulus-based model]].

## Definition

The outcome of each adaptive performance episode can result in "a relatively permanent change in the team's collective level of knowledge and skill produced by the shared experience of the team members" (Ellis et al., 2003, p. 822).

This phase is **post-episode** — broader than reflexive learning (Phase 5) and focused on building the team's *future adaptability*.

## What Gets Improved

Three components of improved adaptability:
1. **Adaptive strategies** — better-matched response repertoires
2. **Collective efficacy** — confidence built through shared adaptive experiences (Carmeli et al., 2013)
3. **Mental models** — transactive memory and team mental models emerge through interaction and learning (Lewis et al., 2007; Pearsall et al., 2010)

These loop back to Phase 1, enhancing detection, urgency/duration assessment, and response quality for future stimuli.

## Learning from Failure

Failure is "a natural, instructive part of the learning process" (Unger-Aviram & Erez, 2016). Setbacks may present opportunities to grow if teams engage in collective sensemaking. However, negative outcomes tend to cause efficacy loss and withdrawal — making beneficial sensemaking after failure difficult.

Ameliorating strategies:
- **Reflexive narratives** — help teams sidestep the outcome itself (Schippers et al., 2014)
- **Concept maps** — help relearn after change (Santos et al., 2021)
- **Vicarious learning** — from others' failures (Bresman, 2010; Kim & Miner, 2007)

## Link to Team Resilience

Through learning, teams may develop resilience as a *team attribute* — not just trait-level individual resilience, but a collective capacity to manage challenges. This emerges from successful (and even unsuccessful) adaptive episodes where teams feel they put forth their best effort.

## Intersection with Continuous Improvement

Although distinguished from ongoing kaizen-type improvement processes, Phase 6 learning and continuous improvement may run *in parallel* or intersect. They may enhance each other (codified knowledge smooths future adaptive response) or interfere (structural changes disrupt efficient routines). Future work should examine synergy vs. conflict between these streams.
