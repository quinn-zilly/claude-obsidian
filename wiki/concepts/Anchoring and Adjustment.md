# Anchoring and Adjustment

One of the three core heuristics identified by [[tversky-kahneman-1974-heuristics-biases]].

## Definition

Estimates are made by starting from an **initial value (anchor)** and adjusting toward the final answer. Adjustments are typically **insufficient** — the final estimate stays too close to the anchor. Different anchors for the same quantity produce systematically different estimates.

**Canonical use:** Numerical prediction when a relevant value is available — either given externally or derived from partial computation.

## Key Demonstrations

**Wheel-of-fortune experiment:** Subjects watched a random number (0–100) generated by a rigged wheel, then estimated quantities (e.g., % African countries in the UN). The random anchor markedly affected estimates: anchor = 10 → median estimate 25; anchor = 65 → median estimate 45. Payoffs for accuracy did not reduce the effect.

**Factorial estimation:** High school students estimated 8×7×6×5×4×3×2×1 vs. 1×2×3×4×5×6×7×8 in 5 seconds. Partial computation in descending order starts with 8×7=56 (large); ascending starts with 1×2=2 (small). Medians: 2,250 (descending) vs. 512 (ascending). Correct: 40,320. Both groups underestimate, but descending anchors higher.

## Biases in Conjunctive/Disjunctive Events

| Structure | Direction of bias | Reason | Domain |
|-----------|------------------|--------|--------|
| Conjunctive (all N events must occur) | **Overestimation** | Anchor on P(single event); adjust downward insufficiently | Project planning, product development |
| Disjunctive (any of N events must occur) | **Underestimation** | Anchor on P(single event); adjust upward insufficiently | Risk assessment, system reliability |

**Planning fallacy:** Complex projects require all steps to succeed — conjunctive structure. Even 12 steps each at 90% probability → P(success) ≈ .28. Anchoring on step-level probability produces overconfident plans.

**System reliability:** Nuclear reactors, human bodies malfunction if *any* essential component fails — disjunctive structure. Anchoring on component reliability produces underestimated overall failure probability.

## Overconfidence in Probability Distributions

Experts elicited for probability distributions (e.g., Dow-Jones average) produce **overly narrow confidence intervals**: actual values fall outside stated X₀₁–X₉₉ range ~30% of the time (expected 2%). Mechanism: anchor on best estimate, adjust outward insufficiently in both directions. Procedure of elicitation matters — different anchoring procedures produce systematically different (and differently miscalibrated) distributions.

## Connection to Other Concepts

- **[[Overfitting]]** ([[Yarkoni & Westfall 2017]]): Insufficient adjustment from initial model fit is structurally related to anchoring on training data; both represent failure to account for uncertainty in estimation.
- **[[Preregistration]]**: Addresses anchoring by committing to analysis plan before data are seen — prevents post-hoc anchoring on observed outcomes.
- **[[Procedural Overfitting]]**: p-hacking can be framed as analyst anchoring on preliminary results and insufficiently adjusting for capitalization on chance.
