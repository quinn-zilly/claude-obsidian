# IT Culture (IT values)

## Definition

**The values a group attributes to information technology itself** — the least-studied of the IT-culture themes in Leidner & Kayworth (2006). The premise is that IT is **not value-neutral**: it is symbolic and values-laden (Coombs et al. 1992; Feldman & March 1981; Robey & Markus 1984; Scholz 1990). Information in bureaucracies can symbolize competency and legitimacy; IS development and user-involvement rituals symbolize *rationality*.

These attributed values are called **IT values**, often expressed as polar dimensions (e.g., equality vs. subordination, community vs. isolation, progressivism vs. conservatism). They form over time through use and remain malleable.

## Two routes into IT culture

- **Values-based** (the review's primary lens) — IT values as polar value pairs; still nascent empirically.
- **Assumptions-based** — **Kaarst-Brown (1995); Kaarst-Brown & Robey (1999)** derive IT *assumptions* and identify five **IT cultural patterns**:
  1. Fearful
  2. Controlled
  3. Revered
  4. Demystified
  5. Integrated

  Distinguished by assumptions about who controls IT, how central it is to strategy, how IT skills are valued, how spend is justified, and who benefits — emerging through a recursive enculturation process.

## Role in the theory

IT values are one of the three value sets whose clashes produce [[IT-Culture Conflict]] (specifically contribution and vision conflict). Managerial shaping of shared IT values is the lever Proposition 4 proposes for reducing all conflict.
