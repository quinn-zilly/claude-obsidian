# Bond Strength and Coupling

Nav: [[index]] | [[Multilevel Theory & Methods MOC]]

The **"where"** of multilevel theory: not everything is linked to everything. One overgeneralization of the systems metaphor is that all levels are related; in reality some units and levels are **much more strongly linked** than others, and the theorist must choose the right ones or risk a misspecified model.

## The family of related concepts

- **Bond strength** (Simon 1973) — the extent to which the characteristics, behaviors, dynamics, and processes of one unit **influence** those of another. Simon views organizations as **nearly decomposable systems** whose subsystems can be studied without compromising the whole.
- **Coupling** (Weick 1976) — loose vs tight linkage among decomposable subsystems.
- **Inclusion** (House et al. 1995) — the proportion of a lower-level unit's activity devoted to a higher level; highly included units are more tightly linked upward.
- **Embeddedness** (Kozlowski & Salas 1997) — alignment of lower-level phenomena with higher-level contextual factors; shaped by technostructural factors (goals, technology, structure) and enabling processes (leadership, socialization, culture).

> [!key-insight]
> Meaningful linkages **increase with proximity and inclusion** and **decrease with distance and independence.** Proximal, included, embedded, directly coupled levels show stronger relations than distal, loosely coupled ones — *and* the content underlying the constructs must be meaningfully related. Organization-level **strategy** likely has only weak, indirect effects on individual jobs because bond strength is weak and content is remote.

See [[Entrainment]] for how coupling **varies over time**.
