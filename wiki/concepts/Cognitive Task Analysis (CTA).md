# Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA)

**CTA** (a.k.a. cognitive job analysis) is a family of methods that identify and model the **unobservable cognitive processes** underlying task performance, with particular focus on what separates **expert from novice** performance. The term emerged in the late 1970s; methods are largely adapted from research on cognition and expertise.

## Contrast with behavioral task analysis

Where behavioral [[Task Analysis (Training)|task analysis]] captures *observable* task activities (what people do), CTA seeks the *knowledge and thought processes* that guide behavior (how people do their jobs). Proponents argue rising workplace technology makes jobs more mentally demanding, so behavioral methods alone may miss what matters. Per [[Sackett-Walmsley-Laczo-2013-job-and-work-analysis|Sackett et al. (2013)]], CTA is best treated as a **supplement**, not a replacement — both observable and cognitive information are usually needed for a complete picture.

## Common techniques

PARI (Prediction, Action, Results, Interpretation), DNA (Decompose, Network, Assess), GOMS (Goals, Operators, Methods, Selection), COGNET (Cognition as a Network of Tasks), plus borrowed expertise methods (interviews, protocol analysis).

## Limitations

Time-consuming, labor-intensive, and expensive; poorly standardized (little documentation on *how* to perform techniques, hampering replication); scarce guidance on going from data to a design solution; and limited evidence on reliability/validity of the different methods. Best applied to highly complex jobs (air-traffic controllers, technicians) and increasingly to **team tasks and shared cognition**.
