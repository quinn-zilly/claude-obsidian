# Reliability vs Legitimate Variance (Job Analysis)

A core reframing from [[Sanchez-Levine-2012-rise-fall-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine (2012)]]: **not all interrater disagreement among incumbents of the same job title is measurement error.** Some of the variance conventionally treated as "random" (and discarded via carelessness indices or low interrater-reliability estimates) reflects **legitimate, systematic differences in how incumbents interpret and actually perform the job.**

## The classical assumption being challenged

Traditional job analysis assumed a job is a **stable object with fixed properties** — a reasonable premise under **Taylorism** (task standardization, division of labor). In today's discretion-rich, technology-mediated work, that assumption holds less well: incumbents are **active agents** who shape their roles ([[Job Crafting|job crafting]], role theory), not passive recipients of a work assignment.

## Evidence that disagreement is meaningful

- High vs low performers report different **time-spent** and **task-importance** profiles, with tangible correlates (e.g., high-performing stockbrokers/branch managers emphasize sales-oriented tasks and sell more; Borman et al. 1992; Sanchez et al. 1998).
- Prior experience shapes ratings (assistant public defenders with trial experience rate themselves more likely to litigate; social workers' tenure alters how they perform).
- Occupational context (interdependence, routinization, autonomy) predictably raises or lowers within-title agreement (Dierdorff & Morgeson 2007).

## Design implication

**Interrater** vs **intrarater** reliability designs are not interchangeable — interrater estimates cannot separate random variance from legitimate between-incumbent differences. The recommendation: separately examine each reliability design, and **embrace disagreement** as a window into how jobs are crafted, rather than suppressing it.
