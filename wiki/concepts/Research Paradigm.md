# Research Paradigm

A **paradigm** is a "set of interrelated assumptions about the social world which provides a philosophical and conceptual framework for the organized study of that world" (Filstead 1979). The paradigm a researcher operates from guides their philosophical assumptions and their choice of tools, instruments, participants, and methods (Denzin & Lincoln 2000).

## The four paradigms (Guba & Lincoln schema, per Ponterotto 2005)

- [[Positivism]] — naïve realism; the "received view"; anchors quantitative research.
- [[Postpositivism]] — critical realism; theory falsification; also anchors quantitative research.
- [[Constructivism–Interpretivism]] — relativism; multiple constructed realities; anchors qualitative research.
- [[Critical–Ideological Paradigm]] — reality mediated by power; research as emancipation; anchors qualitative multicultural research.

Each paradigm takes a distinct position on five [[Philosophy of Science Parameters|parameters]]: ontology, epistemology, axiology, rhetorical structure, and methodology.

## Why locating matters

Strong qualitative research can emanate from multiple paradigms, each valid, each with its own quality criteria. So researchers must **"locate"** their study in a paradigm — Elliott, Fischer & Rennie (1999) call this "owning one's perspective." Evaluating a study's rigor depends on knowing its anchoring paradigm (see Morrow 2005). The failure mode is [[Postpositivism|postpositivizing]] a constructivist study by smuggling in incompatible assumptions.
