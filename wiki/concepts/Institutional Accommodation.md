# Institutional Accommodation

One of four change pathways in the [[Institutional Change Pathways]] typology. Characterized by **revolutionary pace** and **developmental scope**: rapid, disruptive conflict between field actors whose interaction results in only *incremental, developmental* change — not field-level transformation.

## The Core Dynamic

Revolutionary forces are initiated (disruptive agents challenge incumbents), but the result is *dampened* through "accommodation" — settlement, cooptation, negative feedback loops, increasing negative returns. The fire burns but does not consume.

This reveals that revolutionary pace and transformational scope are not causally linked. Disruptive mobilization can be absorbed and deflected, producing only minor revisions to dominant arrangements.

## Characteristics

- Fields as fragmented social spaces where competing interests and power are contested (Fligstein & McAdam 2012)
- Challengers seek to profoundly reconfigure redistribution of material/symbolic resources (power, authority, legitimacy, status)
- Incumbents resist; outcome is negotiated settlement preserving existing arrangements with limited revision
- Relatively understudied: only 15 of 119 studies in Micelotta et al. review

## Why It Is Understudied

Studies typically foreground either challengers *or* incumbents, not both. The *relational* dynamic between them — how conflict produces settlement without transformation — is neglected. Most institutional entrepreneurship studies treat change as the successful outcome by design.

## Key Gaps (per Micelotta et al.)

- How is the competition/coexistence between logics *constructed and negotiated* on the ground — not just instantiated but actively built through interaction (e1)?
- How do opposing parties engage in accommodation processes in pluralistic environments (e2)?
- How does institutional complexity experienced by organizations shape negotiation dynamics (e3)?

## Dampening Mechanisms

- Negative feedback loops
- Increasing negative returns on mobilization
- Settlement and negotiation
- Cooptation of challengers
- Strategic ambiguity preserving multiple interpretations

## Connections

- [[Institutional Displacement]]: accommodation is the "failed" or "dampened" version of displacement — same pace, less scope
- [[Institutional Isomorphism]]: the three isomorphic mechanisms (coercive, mimetic, normative) are resources incumbents deploy to resist displacement and steer outcomes toward accommodation
- [[Organizational Field]]: fields as arenas of power struggle, not just legitimacy-conferring environments — connects to Fligstein & McAdam's "theory of fields"
