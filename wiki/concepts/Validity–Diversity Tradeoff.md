# Validity–Diversity Tradeoff

Nav: [[index]] | [[concepts/_index]]

The **validity–diversity tradeoff** (a.k.a. diversity–validity dilemma): the selection procedures with the **highest criterion-related validity** often also produce the **largest mean subgroup differences** (adverse impact), forcing a tradeoff between predicted performance and workforce diversity.

## The canonical tension

- **Cognitive ability**: high validity (revised ρ = .31) but the largest Black–White difference (*d* ≈ .79).
- **Structured interviews**: now the top validity (ρ = .42) with a much smaller difference (*d* = .23).
- **Integrity tests**: moderate validity (.31), small difference (*d* = .10).
- **Personality (conscientiousness)**: low validity (.19), near-zero or slightly favorable difference.

## Sackett et al. (2022) contribution

The paper deliberately **pairs each revised validity estimate with a mean Black–White subgroup *d*** (mostly from Dahlke & Sackett 2017), so practitioners can reason about both dimensions at once. Because the recalibration *narrowed* the validity gap between cognitive ability and lower-adverse-impact alternatives (interviews, integrity), it strengthens the case for those alternatives on tradeoff grounds. See [[Sackett-et-al-2022-validity-overcorrection]].

> [!note] Not a solved problem
> Composite/mechanical combinations, banding, and criterion redefinition are among the mitigation strategies debated elsewhere in the selection literature; this concept page is the anchor for that thread as the vault grows.
