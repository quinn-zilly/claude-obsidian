# Employee Benefits

**Employee benefits** are discretionary, largely non-cash components of the employment exchange — nearly anything given to employees that is not cash compensation (Barringer & Milkovich 1998). Though sometimes called "fringe" benefits, they account for ~31% of total US employer compensation costs (up to ~38% in state/local government).

In the US, most benefits are voluntary (not statute-required), with costs mostly borne by employers (increasingly cost-shared with employees).

## Three core benefit types (Fulmer & Li)

- **Retirement** — pensions, defined-contribution plans (e.g. 401(k)). Research remains thin; some strategic/sorting work (e.g. supplemental plans as a sorting device).
- **Health & well-being** — health/dental insurance, wellness and mindfulness programs. Research on employee reactions and on well-being interventions (mixed on burnout).
- **Work-life** — flexibility, telecommuting, family-friendly policies; conceptually overlaps **work-family supports (WFS)**: discretionary policies aimed at reducing work-family conflict. Dominated by employee-reaction studies. See [[Work-Family Balance]].

## Strategic gap

Despite the assumption that benefits have significant sorting/retention effects on the workforce, very little rigorous research has examined strategic considerations in benefits *design* (Murray & Dulebohn 2021). Emphasis has been on employee reactions, not organization-level antecedents/outcomes.

Benefits + pay together constitute [[Total Rewards|total rewards]].
