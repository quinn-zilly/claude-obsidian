# Learning Goals vs Performance Goals

Nav: [[index]] | [[Goal-Setting Theory]]

Critical GST distinction, especially for **complex or novel tasks**.

## Definitions

| Type | Focus | Example |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Performance goal** | Outcome score/level on a task | "Increase sales by 15%" |
| **Learning goal** | Number of strategies/ideas to acquire | "Find 5 different ways to handle this negotiation" |

## When Each Is Superior

**Performance goals better when:**
- Task is well-learned / automatized (loggers, truck drivers)
- Strategy is known and just needs execution
- Task is simple to moderate complexity

**Learning goals better when:**
- Task is complex or new to the person
- Strategy is not yet known
- Performance goal creates anxiety → unsystematic strategy search → worse outcomes

Key finding: Winters & Latham (1996) — specific difficult *learning* goal on complex task → significantly higher performance than "do best"; refutes Kanfer & Ackerman (1989) claim that goals impair complex task performance.

Seijts & Latham (2001): proximal + distal outcome goals on moderately complex tasks → higher SE + profits vs. distal-only or do-best.

## Goal Orientation (Trait)

Dweck's learning goal orientation (LGO) vs. performance goal orientation (PGO):
- LGO → seek challenges, learn strategies → high performance
- PGO (I/O version) → seek easy success + praise → low performance (GST predicts)

BUT: assigned goals are "strong situations" that override trait effects:
- Assigning a specific learning goal → high PGO people perform = high LGO people (Seijts & B.W. Latham 2001)
- Assigned goals neutralize goal orientation effects

## Harackiewicz et al. 1997 (College Students)

- Performance goals → better grades, no effect on interest
- Learning goals → more interest, no effect on grades
- Effects separable → potentially additive (combining both understudied as of 2002)
