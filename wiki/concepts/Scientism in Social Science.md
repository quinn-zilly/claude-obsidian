# Scientism in Social Science

**Scientism** (Hayek's "scientistic attitude") = the unthinking imitation of physical-science procedures in domains where they don't fit, producing what Hayek called the "pretence of knowledge" — treating as important only what is accessible to measurement, and disregarding the unmeasured.

[[Joel Lefkowitz|Lefkowitz]] (2008) uses this to attack organizational psychology's claim to be **"value-free":**

- The claim conflates *instrumental* (epistemic, method) values with *terminal* (goal) values. Even objective methods serve economically driven goals.
- Many "facts" the field studies are value-laden: test bias and fairness, criterion relevance, [[Organizational Justice|organizational justice]].
- The fact/value distinction is "arguably spurious" in applied social science, where "betterment of the human condition" is an avowed goal — you cannot define "better" without value choices.

He cites Ghoshal (2005): social scientists carry greater moral responsibility than physical scientists, because "if they hide ideology in the pretense of science, they can cause much more harm."

Relates to the broader [[Positivism]] / [[Postpositivism]] debate on value-neutrality in science.

> [!example] Illustrative blind spots Lefkowitz raises
> Validation studies show validity coefficients vary with the investigator's motivational agenda (Russell et al. 1994); evidence of rater bias in supervisory appraisals (Stauffer & Buckley 2005) and measurement-invariance problems in selection testing (Millsap) have been largely ignored — arguably *because* they threaten lucrative practice.
