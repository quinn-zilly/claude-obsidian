# Prospective Evaluation

Forecasting the likely results of a program or policy change **before** it is implemented. Formalized by the GAO's Program Evaluation and Methodology Division (PEMD) (GAO 1989c), and highlighted as "a landmark" by [[Sechrest-Figueredo-1993-Program-Evaluation|Sechrest & Figueredo (1993)]].

## Rationale

Congress often asks GAO/PEMD to forecast the effect of a proposed program. "**Prevention is better than cure**": if ways can be found to *avert* investment in ineffective or harmful programs, everyone is better off.

Cautionary example the authors cite: New York's policy of paying relatives to house otherwise-homeless children collapsed under runaway costs — people already caring for children unpaid demanded pay, and new cases were arguably created. In hindsight the outcome "seems an inescapable outcome," i.e. it was foreseeable. Prospective evaluation aims to make such foresight systematic.

Complements [[Theory-Driven Evaluation]] (a program's causal logic model is what makes forecasting possible) and the [[Ecological Paradigm in Evaluation]] emphasis on anticipating multi-determined, multi-stakeholder side effects.
