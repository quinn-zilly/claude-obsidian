# Work Design Questionnaire (WDQ)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]]

Comprehensive self-report measure of work characteristics developed by Morgeson & Humphrey (2006). Addresses limitations of JDS (5 motivational characteristics only) and MJDQ (10 factors, measurement gaps).

## Development

- Reviewed all work design literature + ONET database
- Identified 107 distinct work characteristics
- Reduced via sorting/classification to **18 categories** in 4 domains
- Validated: N=540 incumbents, 243 different jobs
- Excellent reliability (avg α ≈ .87; only ergonomics < .70), convergent + discriminant validity vs. archival DOT/O*NET

> [!key-insight] 18 categories → 21 scales
> The a priori model has 18 categories, but the best-fitting CFA (CFI .91, RMSEA .04) splits **autonomy into 3 facets** (scheduling, decision-making, methods) and **interdependence into 2 directions** (initiated, received) — a **21-factor** solution. The reductionist 4-factor model (task/knowledge/social/contextual) fit poorly (CFI .40).

## 18 Characteristics

**Task (5):** autonomy (3 facets: scheduling, decision-making, methods), task variety, task significance, task identity, feedback from job

**Knowledge (5):** job complexity, information processing, problem solving, skill variety, specialization

**Social (4):** social support, interdependence (initiated + received), interaction outside org, feedback from others

**Contextual (4):** ergonomics, physical demands, work conditions, equipment use

## Why WDQ Over JDS?

JDS (Hackman & Oldham 1980) problems:
1. Only 5 motivational characteristics → misses social, contextual, knowledge domains
2. Factor structure inconsistent across studies (Dunham 1976; Idaszak & Drasgow 1987)
3. Funnels research toward narrow motivational lens

WDQ gains: captures knowledge characteristics (related to cognitive ability + training costs), social characteristics (social support → org commitment ρ=0.82), contextual characteristics (work conditions → 16% incremental variance in stress).
