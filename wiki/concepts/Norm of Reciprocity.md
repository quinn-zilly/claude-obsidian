# Norm of Reciprocity

The best-known **rule of exchange** in [[Social Exchange Theory]]: if a person supplies a benefit, the receiving party should respond in kind. [[Alvin Gouldner|Gouldner]] (1960) speculated it is a **universal moral principle** — those who don't comply are punished.

## Gouldner's Three Senses of Reciprocity

1. **Interdependent exchange** — contingent moves; outcomes depend on a combination of both parties' efforts (distinct from independence and dependence). Interdependence reduces risk and encourages cooperation. A "reciprocal exchange" involves **no explicit bargaining** — one party's action is contingent on the other's behavior (Molm).
2. **Folk belief** — the cultural expectation that "everything works out in the end" / people get what they deserve (cf. Lerner's just-world belief). Organizationally under-explored.
3. **Moral norm** — a standard one *ought* to follow (the "ought" quality distinguishes a norm from a folk belief). Possibly a human universal.

## Positive vs. Negative Reciprocity

- **Positive reciprocity orientation** — tendency to return good for good.
- **Negative reciprocity orientation** — tendency to return bad for bad; high-negative individuals view others more malevolently and are angrier (cathartic approach; Eisenberger et al. 2004). Underlies revenge-seeking and Meeker's "competition" rule.

## Exchange Ideology / Orientation (Individual Differences)

People differ in how strongly they endorse reciprocity. **High exchange orientation = "score keeping"** — carefully tracking obligations. **Low exchange orientation** — less concerned whether exchanges are reciprocated.

Empirically, exchange ideology **moderates** social-exchange relationships:

- Strengthens [[Perceived Organizational Support|POS]]→absenteeism (Eisenberger et al. 1986), POS→felt obligation, POS→citizenship, POS→effort/performance.
- Strengthens participative-decision-making→norm acceptance, equal-opportunity→attitudes, income-sufficiency→satisfaction.
- Exception: Sinclair & Tetrick (1995) found no moderation in a union context.

## Place in the Exchange-Rules Landscape

Reciprocity is only one of **Meeker's (1971) six rules** (reciprocity, rationality, altruism, group gain, status consistency, competition). [[cropanzano-mitchell-2005-social-exchange-theory|Cropanzano & Mitchell (2005)]] argue management research's near-exclusive focus on reciprocity, ignoring the other five, is a major gap. In the justice literature, reciprocity is *the* operative rule connecting fair treatment to reciprocated behavior.
