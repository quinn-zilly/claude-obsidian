# Job Performance (Outcomes of Job Attitudes)

**Definition of job performance (Judge & Kammeyer-Mueller 2012):** Employee behaviors consistent with role expectations that contribute to organizational effectiveness. Multidimensional:
- **Task performance** — formally required behaviors
- **Citizenship behavior (OCB)** — extra-role, contextual, interpersonal behaviors
- **Withdrawal/counterproductivity** — responses to dissatisfaction, against org. interests
- **Creative performance** — generation of novel, useful ideas

> [!key-insight] Bandwidth-Fidelity Principle
> Broad attitudes (global satisfaction) best predict broad behavioral composites; specific attitudes (satisfaction with supervisor) best predict specific behaviors (supervisor-directed OCB). Match attitude and behavior bandwidth.

## Task Performance

Meta-analysis (Judge et al. 2001): substantial job satisfaction–performance relationship. Riketta (2008) longitudinal meta-analysis confirms **satisfaction → performance** direction is stronger than performance → satisfaction.

Satisfaction–performance link moderated by affective–cognitive consistency (Schleicher et al. 2004): when affective and cognitive satisfaction components align, the relationship is stronger.

Org. commitment–performance: positive but weak (Wright & Bonett 2002). Relationship **declines with tenure** — less-tenured employees show stronger attitude–behavior link.

## Citizenship Behavior (OCB)

Strong conceptual and empirical link. Meta-analysis (Ilies et al. 2009): overall satisfaction → citizenship; this relationship mediates personality (agreeableness, conscientiousness) → citizenship.

Facet-specific: supervisor satisfaction especially strongly related to OCB (rc = .45 from JDI; Kinicki et al. 2002). Pay, coworkers, and work facets show similar magnitude (rc = .16–.23).

## Withdrawal and Turnover

Satisfaction with work itself has strongest JDI-facet relationship with withdrawal cognitions and turnover intentions (Kammeyer-Mueller et al. 2005).

Satisfaction → turnover is typically **mediated**: satisfaction → quit thoughts → job search/comparison → turnover (Hom & Kinicki 2001). Satisfaction more likely to produce turnover when opportunities exist (Trevor 2001; Lee et al. 2008) — "opportunity to act."

Org. commitment–turnover: affective commitment most predictive. Steeper declines in commitment → higher quit intentions and actual quitting (Bentein et al. 2005). Commitment × satisfaction interaction: high commitment especially reduces absence among satisfied employees (Hausknecht et al. 2008).

## Creative Performance

Debate: positive moods → flexible, open thought (Lyubomirsky et al. 2005) vs. negative moods → active attention and critical thinking needed for creativity (George & Zhou 2002). Integrative view: all **activated** moods (regardless of valence) → higher creativity (De Dreu et al. 2008).

Dissatisfied employees more creative when high continuance commitment + organizational/coworker support (Zhou & George 2001). Aggregate job satisfaction predicts organizational innovation two years later (Shipton et al. 2006). Form of satisfaction–creativity relationship remains uncertain; may differ at individual vs. group level.

## Organizational-Level Performance

Meta-analysis (Harter et al. 2002): unit-level employee satisfaction and engagement substantially related to customer satisfaction, productivity, profit, turnover, and safety.

Cross-lagged evidence (Schneider et al. 2003): employee job attitudes → subsequent organizational financial/market performance (35 companies). Manager satisfaction → customer satisfaction and store performance (Netemeyer et al. 2010).
