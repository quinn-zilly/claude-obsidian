# Knowledge Retention

## Definition

Knowledge retention is the process by which organizations embed acquired knowledge in repositories so it persists across time, personnel changes, and disruptions. Without retention, learning from experience does not accumulate — organizations must relearn what they have already learned.

## The Distributed Repository Model

Knowledge is not stored in a single location. In Argote & Miron-Spektor's (2011) framework, knowledge is embedded simultaneously in:

- **Individual members** — expertise, tacit know-how, relationships. Vulnerable to turnover.
- **Tools and technology** — databases, process documentation, software. Persists beyond individuals but requires ongoing maintenance and interpretation.
- **Routines and practices** — standardized task sequences that encode collective know-how. Robust to individual turnover but can atrophy without practice.
- **Transactive memory systems** — distributed awareness of who knows what in the team. Social and fragile; degrades with team composition changes.

## Knowledge Depreciation

> [!key-insight] Knowledge is not permanently retained
> The Lockheed L-1011 case (Argote & Epple, 1990): when production slowed, costs rose back *above* previously achieved levels. Tacit knowledge embedded in practice — in routines and transactive memory — degrades when not actively used. This is "organizational forgetting."

Implications:
- Learning is not a one-time investment; it must be actively maintained
- Retention strategies must account for which repositories are most at risk
- Personnel turnover is especially damaging for tacit knowledge

## Retention Strategies

| Risk | Strategy |
|---|---|
| Key people leave | Codify explicit knowledge; build overlapping expertise |
| Routines atrophy | Practice and refresh; build in redundancy |
| TMS breaks down | Maintain team stability; explicit "expertise directories" as partial substitute |
| Technology obsolescence | Document-independent knowledge; train on principles not tools |
