# Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)

The most widely researched relationship-based approach to leadership. Central construct: the quality of the unique dyadic relationship between a leader and each individual follower.

## Core Premise
Leaders treat each follower differently. This differentiation produces variability in follower outcomes that average-style leadership theories cannot explain. LMX is unique in treating the *jointly determined leader-follower relationship* as the unit of study.

## Historical Roots
- **Dansereau, Graen & Haga (1975)**: Vertical Dyad Linkage (VDL) — original formulation
- **Graen & Uhl-Bien (1995)**: Traced LMX evolution from VDL; proposed aggregating dyads into collectives; described LMX life cycle: Stranger → Acquaintance → Maturity

## LMX Life Cycle
1. **Stranger phase**: Basic, transactional exchanges
2. **Acquaintance**: Trust begins developing
3. **Maturity**: Mutual trust, respect, and obligation

*Liden, Wayne & Stilwell (1993)*: Expectations, perceived similarity, and liking assessed in first 5 days predict LMX quality 6 months later — relationship quality crystallizes quickly.

## Key Meta-Analytic Findings
**Gerstner & Day (1997)** [Table 1, JAP]: First comprehensive LMX meta-analysis. Key findings:
- LMX → job performance, satisfaction, organizational commitment, OCB, reduced turnover
- Leader–member *agreement* on LMX quality: r=.29 (corrected: .37) — surprisingly low given both are rating the same relationship
- "Supervisor relationship as a lens through which the entire work experience is viewed"

**Ilies, Nahrgang & Morgeson (2007)**: LMX → OCB; stronger for individually-targeted than organizationally-targeted citizenship.

**Settoon, Bennett & Liden (1996)** [Table 1]: POS → organizational commitment; LMX → citizenship and in-role behavior (nested SEM).

## Connections to Broader Framework
- LMX quality predicts ILT match (Epitropaki & Martin 2005) — see [[Implicit Leadership Theories]]
- LMX and POS operate through different social exchange paths (Settoon et al. 1996) — see [[Job Attitudes]]
- Multilevel: LMX (individual level) vs. leadership climate (team level) → different empowerment outcomes (Chen et al. 2007) — see [[Team Leadership]]

## Criticisms
- Inconsistent construct definitions and measurement across studies
- Most research does not study the dyadic level of analysis as specified by theory (Schriesheim et al. 1999)
- Static, cross-sectional measurement dominates despite theory specifying dynamic developmental process
- Low leader–member agreement unexplained for decades
