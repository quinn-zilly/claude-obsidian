# Relational Model of Appraisal Reactions

The best-supported of three competing models of the performance-appraisal process tested in [[pichler-2012-social-context-appraisal|Pichler (2012)]]. It positions **[[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)|rater–ratee relationship quality]] as antecedent to appraisal session characteristics (participation, performance ratings) AND as a direct predictor of [[Appraisal Reactions|appraisal reactions]]**.

## The three competing models

1. **Independent antecedents** — relationship quality, participation, rating favorability are parallel, separate predictors of reactions. (Poor fit.)
2. **Instrumental** — relationship quality predicts reactions *only* via its effect on participation + favorable ratings; no direct path. Consistent with the instrumental theory of [[Procedural Justice|procedural justice]]. (Poor fit.)
3. **Relational** — relationship quality drives participation + ratings *and* directly predicts reactions. Consistent with the non-instrumental / group-value theory of procedural justice. (**Good fit**: CFI=.99, RMSEA=.09, SRMR=.04.)

## Why it matters

The direct relationship-quality → reactions path survives controlling for the instrumental pathways. This means the appraisal process is **largely social/relational**, not merely a resource-allocation exchange. In its essence Model 3 **integrates** both instrumental and relational propositions: a fair (participatory) process yields more favorable reactions, *and* relationship quality with the authority carries meaning above and beyond any outcome influence.

## Theoretical contribution

- Extends [[Social Exchange Theory]] to appraisal: exchange *quality* predicts favorable treatment (participation, ratings), reversing the ordering typical of org SET models where treatment/rewards predict exchange quality.
- Challenges the narrow instrumental-mediation view (e.g., Elicker et al. 2006, which found session characteristics mediate the relationship-quality → reactions link).
- Robustness: the relationship-quality → reactions link is **not moderated** by rating favorability or participation.
