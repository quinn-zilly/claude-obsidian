# Descriptive Norms

Subjective estimates of the *frequency* of a behaviour in a particular population (what *most people do*), as distinct from [[Injunctive Norms]] (what people *approve of*). First systematically distinguished by Cialdini and colleagues in focus theory of normative conduct (1991).

## Definition

> "Frequency of a behaviour in a particular population." — Albarracín et al. 2024 (Table 3)

Operationally: "Most residents would vaccinate their child against COVID-19" (self-report of perceived prevalence).

## Correlational Evidence

Medium-sized associations with behaviour across domains:
- Blood donation: r=0.03 (negligible)
- Recycling: r=0.33
- Adolescent sexual behaviour: r=0.40
- Consumer behaviour: r=0.31
- Smoking initiation: OR=1.88–2.53

Overall mean: medium (OR≈3.01 correlational; see [[albarracin-2024-determinants-behaviour-change]] Fig. 1a).

## Intervention Efficacy

**Small** overall (mean OR≈2.20 in intervention studies).

Mechanisms:
- **Misperception correction**: communicating accurate prevalence when people overestimate undesirable behaviour (e.g., college drinking). Reduces drinking (d=0.29 for alcohol, d=0.36 for condom use).
- **Comparative feedback**: showing individuals how their behaviour compares to peers/neighbours. Exercise apps with comparative feedback: d=0.96 (highly effective).
- **Role models**: presenting individuals who embody the target behaviour. d=0.51 — strongest within the descriptive norm category.
- Demographically similar role models more effective for condom use than dissimilar ones.

## Relationship to Injunctive Norms

Descriptive (what others *do*) and injunctive (what others *approve of*) norms correlate only r=0.1–0.4 — they are meaningfully distinct. Interventions should not conflate them. See [[Injunctive Norms]].
