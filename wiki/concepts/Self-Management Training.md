# Self-Management Training

Nav: [[index]] | [[Goal-Setting Theory]] | [[Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)]]

A structured training approach derived from **Kanfer's (1970–1982) self-regulation framework** and **Bandura's (1977) social learning theory**. Teaches people to manage their own behavior by controlling antecedents, setting goals, monitoring behavior, and self-administering consequences.

## Theoretical Roots

**Kanfer's self-regulation loop:**
1. Self-observation — monitor one's own behavior
2. Self-evaluation — compare behavior to a standard/goal
3. Self-reinforcement — administer reinforcers/punishers contingent on goal progress

**Bandura's social learning theory adds:**
- Role of **perceived self-efficacy** — belief in capability to execute required behaviors
- **Outcome expectancies** — beliefs that behavior will produce favorable outcomes
- Self-reactive influences: goal–performance discrepancy → self-dissatisfaction → increased effort

## Standard Training Package (Frayne & Latham 1987)

| Component | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Self-assessment | Identify problem behaviors, eliciting conditions, coping strategies |
| Goal setting | Distal goal (target outcome) + proximal goals (enabling behaviors) |
| Self-monitoring | Charts, diaries, daily attendance tracking |
| Self-reinforcement | Identify powerful self-administered reinforcers and punishers |
| Behavioral contract | Written: goals, timeframe, consequences, required behaviors |
| Relapse prevention | Anticipate high-risk situations, plan coping responses |

> [!key-insight] Why the package works
> Campbell (1982): little gained from teasing apart goal setting, feedback, and reinforcement. The combination is necessary — goal setting without feedback = no effect (Erez 1977); feedback without goals = no effect (Latham et al. 1978); self-monitoring without goals = no effect (Simon 1979).

## Mechanism

Training raises **perceived self-efficacy** to cope with personal and social obstacles. High SE → people focus on situational demands, mobilize effort, persevere. Low SE → obstacles feel more formidable than they are → avoidance.

Outcome expectancies matter only *when* SE is adequate. High expectancies + low SE = still no behavior change.

## Clinical Precursors (pre-organizational)

- Smoking cessation (Kanfer & Phillips 1970)
- Drug addiction (Kanfer 1974)
- Weight reduction (Mahoney et al. 1973)
- Study habits (Richards 1976)
- Academic achievement (Glynn 1970)

## Organizational Applications

- **Employee attendance** — [[frayne-latham-1987-self-management-attendance|Frayne & Latham (1987)]]: 12-hr program, unionized government workers, attendance *M* = 458.4 vs. control 403.2 hrs over 12 weeks; ω² = .10

## Key Metrics (Frayne & Latham 1987)

- Self-efficacy × attendance: *r* = .49 post-training
- Skill application × attendance (3-mo follow-up): *r* = .45–.48
- Learning test: trained *M* = 29.95 vs. control 16.4, *F*(1,38) = 6.30, *p* < .02
- Reactions positive immediately and at 3-month follow-up (*M* = 4.32, 4.46 on 5-point scale)
