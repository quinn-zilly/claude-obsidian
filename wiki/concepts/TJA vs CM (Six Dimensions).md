# TJA vs CM (Six Dimensions)

Sanchez & Levine's (2009) framework distinguishing **Traditional Job Analysis (TJA)** from **Competency Modeling (CM)** along six dimensions. Core claim: they **supplement, not replace** each other — TJA describes/measures work; CM is a conduit to influence behavior along strategic lines.

| Dimension | TJA | CM |
|-----------|-----|-----|
| **Purpose** | Describe behavior | Influence behavior |
| **View of job** | External object to describe | Role to be enacted |
| **Focus** | Job | Organization |
| **Time orientation** | Past | Future |
| **Performance level** | Typical | Maximum |
| **Measurement** | Latent trait | Clinical judgment |

## Notes per Dimension
- **Purpose**: CM raises *situation strength* — signals "behavioral themes" (critical success factors) to build a strong org climate. TJA lives in applied measurement.
- **View**: job is a social construction / role (Katz & Kahn 1978), interpreted + enacted per incumbent.
- **Focus**: competencies cut across all jobs (employer brand, employee value proposition; FedEx, Disney, GE).
- **Time**: TJA past/incumbent/bottom-up; CM future/strategist/top-down.
- **Performance**: TJA typical (transactional contract, task performance); CM maximal (relational contract, contextual/prosocial, on-brand).
- **Measurement**: competencies are **fuzzy composites**, best read as **global/clinical judgments**, NOT construct-valid latent traits — so internal-consistency reliability is the wrong metric.

## Implication
To satisfy job-relatedness under the [[EEOC-1978-uniform-guidelines-employee-selection|Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection (1978)]], orgs may need to **supplement CM with TJA**. Strategic job analysis ([[Strategic Job Analysis (SJA)]]) concerns strategy *formulation*; CM concerns *execution* — SJA cannot replace CM.
