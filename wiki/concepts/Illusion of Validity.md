# Illusion of Validity

## Definition

**Unwarranted confidence** in a prediction produced by a good match (fit) between the predicted outcome and the input information — even when the judge knows the factors that limit predictive accuracy.

Produced by the [[Representativeness Heuristic]]: when the outcome "looks like" the input (matches its stereotype), confidence is high, regardless of actual predictive validity.

## Key Evidence (Tversky & Kahneman 1974)

**Student teacher study:** Predictions for a teacher's success 5 years later were just as extreme as quality evaluations of a single practice lesson — despite subjects knowing that long-range prediction from a single lesson has very limited validity. Predictive confidence tracked input favorableness, not predictive reliability.

**Clinical interviewers:** Psychologists conducting selection interviews often experience considerable confidence in their predictions even when they know the literature showing interviews are highly fallible. Continued reliance on clinical interviews despite repeated demonstrations of inadequacy.

## The Consistency Paradox

Internal consistency of inputs *increases* confidence while *decreasing* accuracy:

- A student with all B's → high confidence in grade-point average prediction.
- A student with mixed A's and C's → lower confidence.
- But statistically: when inputs are redundant (correlated), they add less information. Independent inputs with same individual validities yield more accurate predictions. *Redundancy decreases accuracy even as it increases confidence.*

> "People tend to have great confidence in predictions based on redundant input variables. However, an elementary result in the statistics of correlation asserts that, given input variables of stated validity, a prediction based on several such inputs can achieve higher accuracy when they are independent of each other than when they are redundant or correlated." — Tversky & Kahneman (1974)

## Persistence of the Illusion

The illusion persists even when:
- The judge is explicitly aware of factors limiting predictive accuracy
- Feedback has been provided on past prediction failures
- The judge has statistical training

This robustness distinguishes it from simple ignorance.

## Connection to Other Concepts

- **[[Procedural Overfitting]] / [[P-hacking]]**: The illusion of validity operates at the field level — results that "fit" a compelling theoretical story are judged more valid, regardless of actual reliability. This parallels Giner-Sorolla's [[Aesthetic Standards in Science]].
- **[[Motivated Reasoning]]**: The illusion of validity can interact with motivated reasoning — seeing what one wants to see in the data increases apparent fit and thus confidence.
