# Fair Process Effect

The finding that a **fair process can mitigate the ill effects of an unfavorable outcome** — people accept bad outcomes more readily when the procedure (and treatment) were just. "If you can't give people the outcome they want, at least give them a fair process."

## Key patterns

- **Process-by-outcome interaction**: procedural (and interactional) justice buffers the negative reactions to unfavorable distributive outcomes (Brockner & Wiesenfeld 1996 integrative framework).
- **[[Procedural Justice]] shapes system-level attitudes** — outcome favorability affects satisfaction with a *particular decision*, but procedural justice affects what workers believe about the **organization as a whole** (Tyler & Blader 2000): loyalty, willingness to act in the org's interest, institutional legitimacy.
- Illustrated by strategy execution (Kim & Mauborgne's *Blue Ocean* fair-process argument), conflict arbitration, layoffs, and pay administration in [[cropanzano-bowen-gilliland-2007-management-organizational-justice|Cropanzano et al. (2007)]].

## Relation to the three-component interaction

A special case of the broader claim that the [[Four Justice Dimensions|justice components]] **interact**: ill effects of injustice are attenuated if *at least one* component is upheld ("three bites at the apple"). The fair process effect is the version where **procedural** justice does the buffering.
