# Social Identification

**Definition ([[ashforth-mael-1989-social-identity-theory-organization|Ashforth & Mael 1989]]):** the *perception of oneness with, or belongingness to, some human aggregate*. The individual perceives themselves as an actual or symbolic member of a group and perceives the group's fate as their own. It is a partial answer to the question **"Who am I?"** *Organizational identification* is simply a specific form of social identification (Ashforth & Mael use "social" and "group" identification interchangeably).

## Four Principles (from the group-identification literature)

1. **Perceptual-cognitive, not behavioral or affective.** To identify, one need not exert effort toward group goals or feel loyalty — only perceive oneself as psychologically intertwined with the group's fate. Behavior and affect are potential antecedents/consequences, not part of the construct. *(This is where Ashforth & Mael depart from Tajfel, who folded affective/evaluative dimensions into identity.)*
2. **Experiencing the group's successes and failures** as one's own — often maintained even under loss, suffering, missed benefits, task failure, or expected failure.
3. **Distinct from [[Internalization (SDT)|internalization]].** Identification = self in terms of social categories (**"I am"**); internalization = incorporating values as guiding principles (**"I believe"**). One can accept a category as self-definition without accepting its values.
4. **Analogous to identification with a person or role** (father, doctor–patient) — one defines oneself via a social referent.

## Matter of Degree

Identification is not all-or-none. Even where categories are categorical (Canadian, female, member of XYZ Co.), the *extent* of identification varies. Identities tend to be valued positively (people vest more self in valued personas); menial-job holders distance themselves from implied identities (Jackall's bank workers: "this is only a stopgap job").

## Antecedents (in organizations)

Categorization, group **distinctiveness**, group **prestige**, **out-group salience**, and traditional group-formation factors. See [[ashforth-mael-1989-social-identity-theory-organization|the source]] for detail.

## Consequences

Identity-congruent activity, institutional support, **self-stereotyping** ([[Self-Categorization and Self-Stereotyping]]), norm adherence, and reinforcement of its own antecedents.

## Distinct From Commitment

A central claim: identification ≠ [[Organizational Commitment|organizational commitment]]. Mael's (1988) CFA against the OCQ supports a two-factor solution. See the contradiction callout on the [[Organizational Commitment]] page.
