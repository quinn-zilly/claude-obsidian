# Experience-Sampling Methodology (ESM)

**Definition:** A data-collection method where psychological states (job attitudes, moods, emotions, behaviors) are measured repeatedly over time — typically once a day or multiple times per day across one to several weeks — providing ecological momentary assessments.

Also called **ecological momentary assessment (EMA)** or **daily diary** designs.

## Why ESM in Job Attitudes Research

Job attitudes have both stable (between-person) and dynamic (within-person) components. If affect is central to job attitudes, then attitudes need to be measured in ways consistent with the ephemeral nature of affect — one-shot designs cannot capture this.

**Key finding:** ESM studies consistently show **~1/3 to 1/2 of variance in job satisfaction is within-person**. Treating this as transient error misses a large portion of the construct.

## Multilevel Modeling Advantage

ESM designs permit multilevel (HLM) modeling that simultaneously estimates:
- **Within-person (Level 1):** How attitude varies day-to-day with events, moods, context
- **Between-person (Level 2):** How stable individual differences (personality, dispositions) moderate within-person relationships

Important distinction: ESM ≠ multilevel. ESM is common in multilevel job attitudes research, but multilevel models can use other repeated-measurement approaches (e.g., annual surveys over 10 years).

## Key ESM Findings in Job Attitudes

- Within-person variability in job attitudes nearly as large as in moods/emotions (supports AET)
- Daily justice perceptions → daily job satisfaction (Loi et al. 2009)
- Dispositional affect influences typical satisfaction level AND moderates sensitivity to workplace events (Bowling et al. 2005)
- Within-person attitude variation → within-person OCB variation (Ilies et al. 2006)
- Within-person attitude variation → workplace deviance (Judge et al. 2006)
- Affect as both antecedent and consequence of job attitudes (lagged designs: Judge & Ilies 2004; Ilies & Judge 2002)

## Challenges

- Discrete emotions are theoretically separable but empirically less so (especially positive emotions; Watson 2000)
- Broad mood factors (PA/NA vs. hedonic tone/arousal) have their own structural debates
- Causal direction between attitudes and moods/emotions remains difficult to establish definitively
- ESM findings should not be uncritically generalized to between-person level (and vice versa)
