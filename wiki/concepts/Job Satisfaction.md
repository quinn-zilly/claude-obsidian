# Job Satisfaction

**Definition (Judge & Kammeyer-Mueller 2012):** An evaluative state that expresses contentment with, and positive feelings about, one's job.

Includes both cognition (contentment) and affect (positive feelings). Most researched attitude in organizational behavior — 33,348+ PsycINFO records as of 2012.

## Global vs. Facet Satisfaction

**Global:** Single overarching evaluation — best predicted by broad antecedents (affective disposition, aggregate job characteristics) and best predicts broad outcomes (overall performance, work withdrawal).

**Facets (JDI — Smith et al. 1969):** work, supervision, coworkers, pay, promotion. Inter-facet meta-analytic correlations ≈ r = .20–.30. **Work satisfaction** is most closely related to global satisfaction (Ironson et al. 1989; Rentsch & Steel 1992) and most strongly related to motivation and withdrawal cognitions.

Other measures: **MSQ** (Minnesota Satisfaction Questionnaire), **IOR** (Index of Organizational Reactions) — add dimensions like job security, social status, advancement.

## Pay Satisfaction

Four dimensions (Judge & Welbourne 1994): pay level, benefits, pay raise, structure/administration.

Based on discrepancy between received and deserved pay:
- Social comparisons dominate: internal comparisons rc = .94, external rc = 1.00 (Williams et al. 2006)
- Distributive justice rc = .79 with pay satisfaction
- Merit pay raises have surprisingly small relationship with pay satisfaction (rc = .08) — only matters when employee believes pay is performance-contingent

## Stability and Within-Person Variation

Job satisfaction shows both **traitlike stability** (Staw & Ross 1985 — stable across job/employer changes) and **statelike variation** (ESM studies show 1/3–1/2 of variance is within-person).

"Honeymoon-hangover" pattern (Boswell et al. 2005, 2009): high initial satisfaction after hire, followed by deterioration.

## Relationship with Performance

Meta-analysis (Judge et al. 2001): substantial satisfaction–performance relationship. Riketta (2008) longitudinal meta-analysis: satisfaction → performance direction stronger than reverse.

Affective consistency moderates: when affective and cognitive components of satisfaction align, the satisfaction–performance relationship is stronger (Schleicher et al. 2004).

See [[Job Performance (Outcomes of Job Attitudes)]] for full outcomes review.

## Dispositional Influences

Staw et al. (1986): childhood affective disposition (ages 12–14) correlates r = .34 with satisfaction at ages 54–62. Arvey et al. (1989): monozygotic twins reared apart show consistent satisfaction levels.

Big Five: neuroticism (rc = −.29), extraversion (rc = .25), conscientiousness (rc = .26) — Judge et al. (2002).

**[[Core Self-Evaluations]]** most consistent dispositional predictor: rc = .37 (Judge & Bono 2001 meta-analysis).

## Situational Influences

- **Job characteristics** (Hackman & Oldham 1976): skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, feedback
- **Social environment**: coworker support, social network ties, demographic similarity, leadership
- **Leadership**: leader consideration rc = .78 with leader satisfaction; transformational leadership buffers dissatisfaction
- **Person–org. value congruence**: associated with more positive job attitudes (Edwards & Cable 2009)
