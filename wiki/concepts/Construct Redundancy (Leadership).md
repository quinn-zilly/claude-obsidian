# Construct Redundancy (Leadership)

The problem that arises when a "new" leadership construct is promoted without demonstrating that it is conceptually and empirically **distinct from, and adds predictive value beyond,** existing constructs. Also called construct proliferation. Leadership is especially prone to it — [[derue-et-al-2011-trait-behavioral-leadership-integration|DeRue et al. (2011)]] noted scholars keep inventing theories "without attempting to compare and contrast the validity of existing theories."

## Two Kinds of Non-Redundancy

- **Conceptual non-redundancy** — sound theory shows the construct is distinct (Singh 1991).
- **Empirical non-redundancy** — the construct is not too highly correlated with existing ones *and* has a distinct pattern of relationships with outcomes.

## How It's Tested

1. **Correlation** — a very high corrected correlation with an incumbent construct (e.g., ρ > .70) signals redundancy.
2. **Incremental validity** — hierarchical regression: does the new construct add ΔR² after the incumbent is entered? The stricter test.
3. **[[Relative Weights Analysis]]** — partitions shared variance to compare relative contribution on equal footing (a more lenient complement to incremental tests).

## Worked Example

[[hoch-et-al-2018-ethical-authentic-servant-vs-transformational|Hoch et al. (2018)]] applied all three to ethical, authentic, and servant leadership vs. transformational leadership:

- **Authentic** (ρ=.75 with TL) and **ethical** (ρ=.70) — redundant: high correlation, same outcome pattern, tiny ΔR².
- **Servant** (ρ=.52) — the least redundant; adds the most incremental variance.

## Takeaway

High correlation + low incremental validity = redundancy, regardless of whether relative-weights analysis makes the new form look competitive. The discipline's proliferation of leadership labels often outruns evidence of distinctiveness.
