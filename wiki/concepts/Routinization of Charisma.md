# Routinization of Charisma

A Weberian concept (Gerth & Mills 1946) used by [[ashforth-mael-1989-social-identity-theory-organization|Ashforth & Mael (1989)]] to explain how **classical identification with a charismatic leader can be generalized to the organization itself**.

## The Mechanism

Charismatic/transformational leaders ([[Transformational and Charismatic Leadership]]) are adept at manipulating symbols and so engender **classical identification** (identification with the *leader*, wanting to be like them) and/or **social identification** (identification with the *organization*). Where identification is classical, it can be transferred to the organization by **routinizing** the founder's charisma into durable structures — an administrative apparatus, rites and ceremonies, oral and written tradition, myths, and so forth.

## Illustration: AA vs NCA (Trice & Beyer 1986)

Two social-movement organizations founded by charismatic individuals:

- **Alcoholics Anonymous (AA)** — the founder's charisma was *successfully routinized* through administrative structure, rites/ceremonies, and tradition.
- **National Council on Alcoholism (NCA)** — charisma was *poorly routinized*.

Result: NCA had greater difficulty maintaining the support of members and donors. Routinization converts a fragile, person-dependent bond into a stable, organization-level identification.

## Why It Matters

It is one route by which **symbolic management** builds organizational identification — making membership salient and providing "compelling images of what the group or organization represents" (Pondy et al. 1983). It also explains why organizations invest so heavily in advertising, names, logos, jargon, leaders, and mascots: the search for a distinctive, prestigious identity that attracts loyalty from members and other constituents.
