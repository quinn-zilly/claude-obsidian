# Enactive and Vicarious Learning

Nav: [[index]] | [[Social Cognitive Theory]]

In SCT, learning occurs two ways (Bandura 1977):

- **Enactive learning** — learning from the *consequences* of one's own actions. Actions convey information about accuracy/appropriateness; rewards and punishments inform and motivate.
- **Vicarious learning** — learning by *observing* modeled performances (live, filmed, symbolic). An efficient alternative to direct experience — "humans would be hopelessly inefficient if their involvement were required for all learning" — and it spares people undesirable consequences.

Complex skills are typically learned **both** ways: observe a model, then refine through practice and feedback.

## Learning vs. performance

SCT separates *new learning* from *performance* of already-learned actions. Learned behavior may go undemonstrated depending on motivation, incentives, social pressures, and competing activities — e.g., able students may mask their competence to fit peers. This is why authentic assessment matters.

## Types of vicarious process (Bandura 1986)

- **Response facilitation** — modeled acts prompt already-known behavior (not learning).
- **Inhibition/disinhibition** — models strengthen or weaken tendencies (often moral/legal or emotional).
- **Observational learning** — genuinely new behavior; four sub-processes: **attention → retention → production → motivation**.

**Model characteristics**: perceived similarity (age/gender signal appropriateness; **competence** dominates for actual learning), and **coping models** (who struggle then improve) benefit prior-difficulty learners more than flawless **mastery models**.

## Extension to organizations (Myers 2018)

The classic Bandura account casts vicarious learning as **intrapersonal and one-way** — [[Independent Vicarious Learning (IVL)]]. [[myers-2018-coactive-vicarious-learning|Myers (2018)]] argues this breaks down in tacit, ambiguous knowledge work and proposes [[Coactive Vicarious Learning (CVL)]]: a **relational, coconstructed** process where model and learner jointly process an experience through discursive interaction (experience + analysis + support).
