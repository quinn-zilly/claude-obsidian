# Autonomy (SDT)

One of three [[Basic Psychological Needs (SDT)]] in [[Self-Determination Theory]].

**Definition**: Feeling of volition; acting from internal perceived locus of causality. NOT synonymous with independence, individualism, or detachment.

> [!key-insight] Autonomy ≠ Independence
> Within SDT, autonomy = volition that can accompany *any* act — dependent or independent, collectivist or individualist. Research in Korean and U.S. samples found *more positive* relation between autonomy and collectivistic attitudes than autonomy and individualistic attitudes (Kim, Butzel & Ryan 1998). Positive links between relatedness to parents and autonomy in teenagers (Ryan & Lynch 1989).

## Role in Intrinsic Motivation

Competence alone insufficient — must be accompanied by autonomy for intrinsic motivation to be in evidence. Controlling events shift perceived locus of causality externally → undermine intrinsic motivation.

Events that enhance autonomy: choice, acknowledgment of feelings, opportunities for self-direction.

Events that undermine autonomy: tangible contingent rewards, threats, deadlines, directives, pressured evaluations, imposed goals.

## Role in Internalization

Critical for **integration** specifically (deepest form of internalization). Integration requires holistic processing — synthesizing new regulation with existing values — which requires freedom from excessive external pressure. Identified and integrated regulation both involve autonomy; introjected regulation does not.

## Autonomy Support

Autonomy-supportive teachers, parents, coaches → greater intrinsic motivation, deeper learning, more internalization. Controlling socializing agents → loss of initiative, surface-level learning, introjection (not integration).
