# Ecological Paradigm in Evaluation

The framing at the heart of [[Sechrest-Figueredo-1993-Program-Evaluation|Sechrest & Figueredo (1993)]]: social programs live in a **behavioral/social ecology**, and the discipline of program evaluation should be understood as *adapting* to that ecology.

## Newtonian vs. Darwinian

- **Newtonian (physicalistic) paradigm** — inherited from Fechner/Weber/Wundt: mechanistic, elementistic, universalistic; the randomized controlled single-variable experiment, "maxmincon," Fisherian null-hypothesis testing. Yields *chronically inadequate [[External Validity|external validity]]*; "catastrophically inappropriate to a biological science."
- **Darwinian (ecological) reality** — the actual social world programs enter: alive, competitive, strategic, deceptive, because *information is power*. Nothing "waits passively to be acted upon by an external force."

## The path-model illustration

An intervention *I₁* → determinant *D₁* → outcome *O₁* → utility *U₁* is "straight-line Newtonian thinking." Reality: *O₁* has other determinants (*D₂, D₃*) *I₁* doesn't touch — or touches counterproductively; *D₁* has unanticipated other outcomes; and *O₁* carries different utilities for different stakeholder populations. Regulating such a system needs additional, strategically designed interventions — i.e. **ecological (Darwinian) thinking**.

## Why it matters

The ecological view reframes program-evaluation history as a *selective ecological adaptation* to a shifting (industrial → postindustrial, information-based) society, not merely a maturation of ideas. It motivates the authors' preference for **theory + multivariate models** (which can represent multi-determined, multi-outcome, multi-stakeholder systems) over both the sterile single-variable experiment and the qualitative "flight."
