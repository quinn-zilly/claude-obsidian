# Resource Allocation Theory (Motivation)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Motivation Meta-Framework (Kanfer)]]

Views motivation as a process by which **personal resources are allocated across an array of possible actions (goals)** in response to task demands, affect, incentives, and feedback. (Kanfer & Ackerman 1989; Naylor, Pritchard & Ilgen 1980.)

## Core Claims

- Resources = attentional effort (cognitive resources of limited availability)
- Self-regulation during goal striving requires attentional resources
- Beneficial effects of self-regulation occur only when sufficient resources are available
- On complex tasks in early learning stages, assigning performance goals siphons resources away from skill acquisition → impaired learning (Kanfer & Ackerman 1989 air traffic simulation)
- Fix: learning goals or do-best goals during resource-scarce skill acquisition; performance goals once skills are automatized

## Integration with GST

Kanfer & Ackerman (1989) integrated cognitive ability and motivational processes during complex skill acquisition. Key finding: goal-setting benefits depend on whether there are attentional resources left over *after* task performance demands are met. When tasks are novel and demanding, self-regulation competes for the same resources needed for learning.

## Multiple Goal Allocation

Real-world: people allocate resources across multiple simultaneous goals. Early work (Atkinson & Birch 1970) examined two-task switching. More recent: Schmidt & DeShon (2007) — incentive × goal progress drives dynamic resource allocation across tasks. Vancouver et al. (2010) — computational model of multiple-goal pursuit integrating goal choice and striving.

## Resource Depletion Debate

[[Ego Depletion]] extends this framework by arguing regulatory resources deplete with use. But: some self-regulatory activities may not require resources (culturally entrained behaviors; Johnson et al. 2015); ego depletion effect challenged by meta-analysis (Carter et al. 2015).
