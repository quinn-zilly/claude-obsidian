# Big Five of Teamwork

Nav: [[index]] | [[Teams & Org Behavior MOC]]

Proposed by **Salas, Sims & Burke (2005, "Is there a Big Five in teamwork?", *Small Group Research*)**, this is a parsimonious model claiming that across domains, goals, and tasks there are **five core components of teamwork** plus **three coordinating mechanisms** that enable them. Each component's *importance* varies by context, but each is essential in some form for any teamwork.

## The Five Core Components

1. **Team leadership** — a *functional* view: "social problem solving that promotes coordinated, adaptive team performance." Leaders act through searching/structuring information, using it in problem solving, and managing personnel and material resources. Can be centralized or **shared/distributed** (leadership function transfers among members per member strengths and environmental demands).
2. **Adaptability** — changing team performance processes in response to environmental cues to produce functional outcomes. Underlies most other team functions; central to the [[Team Adaptation (Stimulus-Based Model)|team-adaptation]] literature (Burke et al., 2006: situation assessment → plan formulation → plan execution → team learning).
3. **Mutual performance monitoring** — tracking teammates' work to catch deviations. *Double-edged*: only helps when framed as a performance aid, not as "keeping tabs" to avoid blame. Requires shared models of what *should* be happening.
4. **Backup (supportive) behavior** — discretionary provision of resources/effort when a workload imbalance is recognized. Depends on mutual performance monitoring as a prerequisite; lets teams shift workload and self-correct.
5. **Team orientation** — propensity to coordinate with, evaluate, and use teammates' inputs (more than mere preference for group work). Under stress, teams lose this via **attentional narrowing** onto individual taskwork, harming performance.

## The Three Coordinating Mechanisms

These distribute information in a timely, appropriate way so the five components can be enacted:

- **[[Shared Mental Models]]** — overlapping knowledge structures enabling anticipation and implicit coordination. **Accuracy > sharedness** (Edwards et al., 2006): sharing inaccurate models yields no gain.
- **Closed-loop communication** — (1) sender initiates message, (2) receiver acknowledges/interprets, (3) sender confirms correct receipt. Effective teams shift between implicit and explicit communication as demands change.
- **Mutual trust** — shared belief that members will perform their roles and protect each other's interests. Without it, resources are squandered "checking up" on teammates.

## Relation to Other Frameworks

- **Parsimony vs inclusiveness**: the Big Five prioritizes explanatory breadth with fewest constructs; contrast [[Teamwork Processes (Marks et al. Taxonomy)|Marks et al. / Rousseau et al.]] which prioritize comprehensive coverage of the behavior literature.
- The Big Five components map onto the **B**ehaviors and **A**ttitudes of the [[ABCs of Teamwork Competencies]].
