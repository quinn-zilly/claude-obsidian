# Learning vs. Performance Distinction

Nav: [[concepts/_index]] | [[Desirable Difficulties]] | [[Spacing Effect]] | [[Testing Effect]]

## Core Distinction

| Dimension | Learning | Performance |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Definition | Relatively permanent changes in behavior/knowledge | Temporary fluctuations observable during acquisition |
| Measurable when | After a delay; via retention and transfer tests | During or immediately after training |
| Index in theory | Storage strength (New Theory of Disuse) | Retrieval strength |

The distinction is critical because **the same manipulation can have opposite effects on learning and performance**. Conditions that boost acquisition performance often impair long-term retention; conditions that impair performance often boost learning.

## Historical Roots

Early learning theorists made corresponding distinctions:
- Hull (1943): *habit strength* (learning) vs. *momentary reaction potential* (performance)
- Estes (1955): *habit strength* vs. *response strength*
- Tulving & Pearlstone (1966): *availability* (stored) vs. *accessibility* (retrievable)
- Bjork & Bjork (1992): *storage strength* vs. *retrieval strength* → [[New Theory of Disuse]]

## Empirical Evidence

Three main manipulation classes produce learning–performance dissociations:

### 1. Distribution of Practice
Massed practice → fast acquisition, fast forgetting.
Spaced practice → slower acquisition, durable retention.
→ [[Spacing Effect]]

### 2. Variability of Practice
Fixed practice → high performance on trained variant, poor transfer.
Variable practice → more errors during training, better transfer.
→ Mechanism: variable practice broadens the range of memory cues for a skill.

### 3. Retrieval Practice
Restudying → high immediate performance, poor long-term retention.
Testing → lower immediate recall, much better long-term retention.
→ [[Testing Effect]]

## Why the Distinction Matters

1. **Evaluation problem**: using acquisition performance to evaluate training programs leads to choosing inferior methods.
2. **Metacognitive illusions**: learners conflate fluency during acquisition with long-term learning ([[Judgment of Learning]], [[Illusions of Competence]]).
3. **Institutional problem**: training programs that make learners feel good (smooth acquisition) may produce less actual learning than programs that feel difficult.

## Cross-Domain Connections

- [[On-the-Job Development]]: stretch assignments produce more errors (performance cost) but greater leadership development (learning gain) — the OJD field doesn't always distinguish these
- [[Learning Agility]]: high learning agility = tolerance for performance dips during desirable difficulty conditions; low agility = abandons difficult practices
- [[Organizational Learning]]: org-level learning curves (Argote) are performance metrics; underlying knowledge accumulation is harder to measure — same distinction at aggregate level
- [[Replication Crisis]]: researchers and reviewers may treat *performance signals* (p < .05 on an immediate test) as evidence of durable learning in science
