# Goal Contents Theory (GCT)

Fifth mini-theory of [[Self-Determination Theory]] (Kasser & Ryan 1993, 1996). Distinguishes intrinsic from extrinsic goal contents and predicts their differential effects on well-being via [[Basic Psychological Needs (SDT)]].

## Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Aspirations

**Intrinsic goals**: community contribution, personal growth, affiliation, health. Inherently satisfying; tend to satisfy basic needs.

**Extrinsic goals**: wealth, fame, image/appearance. Contingent on external validation; tend not to satisfy basic needs and may actively frustrate them.

## Key Principle: Relative Centrality

What matters is not only what goals people pursue but how central extrinsic goals are relative to intrinsic ones. High relative centrality of extrinsic goals predicts lower well-being.

## Meta-Analytic Evidence (Ryan et al. 2022)

- **Intrinsic aspirations → well-being**: r = .24 (Bradshaw et al. 2022)
- **Extrinsic relative centrality → well-being**: r = −.22 (Bradshaw et al. 2022)
- **Mediation by need satisfaction**: confirmed (Dittmar et al. 2014)
- Cultural universality: autonomy need ≠ independence; moderation analyses show effects replicate cross-culturally

## Why Extrinsic Goals Undermine Well-Being

1. Pursuing extrinsic goals diverts time/attention from need-satisfying activities
2. Attainment of extrinsic goals does not satisfy basic needs (competence, autonomy, relatedness)
3. External contingencies can actively frustrate autonomy by making self-worth contingent

## Distinction from OIT

GCT concerns the *content* of goals (what people pursue). [[Organismic Integration Theory]] concerns the *regulation* of behavior (why people pursue it). Both matter independently for well-being.
