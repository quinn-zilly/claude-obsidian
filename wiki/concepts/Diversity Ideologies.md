# Diversity Ideologies

**Diversity ideologies** are individuals' beliefs regarding the importance of demographic differences and how to navigate them. Studied across organizational, social, cultural, counseling, and developmental psychology under many labels (intergroup ideologies, diversity perspectives, diversity beliefs, diversity climate) — all the same construct.

## Two Broad Categories

- **Identity-blind** ("melting pot") — differences are *not* important and should be minimized.
- **Identity-conscious** ("salad bowl") — differences *are* important and should be acknowledged.

## Four Types (Leslie et al., 2020)

The key contribution of [[leslie-et-al-2020-diversity-ideologies-meta-analysis|Leslie et al. (2020)]] is disaggregating the identity-blind category into three distinct ideologies:

| Ideology | Category | How differences are handled |
|---|---|---|
| **Colorblindness** | identity-blind | Ignore differences (cognitive) |
| **Meritocracy** | identity-blind | Treat groups equitably; prevent discrimination |
| **Assimilation** | identity-blind | Nondominant groups adopt dominant practices |
| **Multiculturalism** | identity-conscious | Acknowledge / learn / maintain / value differences |

> [!key-insight] Conflation causes mixed findings
> Because measures (e.g., the CoBRAS colorblindness scale) blend colorblindness, meritocracy, and assimilation items into one index, prior literature reported contradictory effects. Once separated, each ideology shows **divergent, sometimes opposite** relations to different intergroup outcomes. The same disaggregation applies to the outcomes: prejudice (affect), discrimination (behavior), and stereotyping (cognition) are distinct, only moderately correlated bias forms.

## Theoretical Grounding

Rooted in **[[Social Identity Theory|social categorization / social identity]]**: group membership is salient and drives ingroup favoritism. Minimizing salience (identity-blind) *or* valuing difference (identity-conscious) are competing routes to better relations — hence the long-running debate. Leslie et al. add a **consistency** premise: an ideology most affects the outcome most consistent with its content.

## Practical Takeaway

**Multiculturalism** most consistently improves intergroup relations; **assimilation** most consistently harms them; **colorblindness** and **meritocracy** help on some outcomes and hurt on others. All three identity-blind ideologies reduce **diversity policy support** — the one consistent identity-blind effect.
