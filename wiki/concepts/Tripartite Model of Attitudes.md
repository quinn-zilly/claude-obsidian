# Tripartite Model of Attitudes

The **tripartite (ABC) model** holds that an attitude has three components:

- **A**ffective — feelings/emotions toward the object (e.g., **prejudice** = negative affect toward outgroups).
- **B**ehavioral — action tendencies and behavior toward the object (e.g., **discrimination** = negative treatment of outgroups).
- **C**ognitive — beliefs about the object (e.g., **stereotyping** = beliefs that groups possess different traits).

These components are related but distinct, and only moderately correlated — a recurring theme in the bias literature, where prejudice, discrimination, and stereotyping map onto affect, behavior, and cognition respectively.

## Why It Matters Here

The tripartite structure underpins the **[[Attitude Dimension Consistency|principle of compatibility]]**: an intervention or predictor best influences the outcome that shares its attitude dimension. [[costa-2024-discrimination-interventions|Costa (2024)]] uses the ABC model to build a taxonomy of bias interventions; [[Diversity Ideologies|Leslie et al. (2020)]] independently rely on the same affect/behavior/cognition distinction among intergroup bias outcomes.
