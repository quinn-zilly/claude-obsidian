# Strategic Framing

## Definition

Strategic framing refers to the **purposeful use of rhetorical and communicative devices by leaders or managers to shape others' interpretive frames**, gaining acceptance and support for organizational change, strategy, or new practices.

Fiss & Zajac (2006, p. 1174): "by framing strategic change and thereby articulating a specific version of reality, organizations may secure both the understanding and support of key stakeholders for their new strategic orientation, because it shapes how people notice and interpret what is going on, influencing the strategic choices that they subsequently make."

## Tools of Strategic Framing

Cornelissen et al. (2012): metaphors, catchphrases, slogans, contrast, spin, stories. Fairhurst (2010); Sonenshein (2010). These work by:
- Invoking existing cognitive frames (priming effect)
- Associating a proposed change with legitimate, salient cultural categories
- Making others' interests appear aligned with the framer's goals

## Kaplan's Framing Contests Model (2008)

Key study of a communication technology company. Actors use frames strategically in social interactions, reflecting their political interests. Finding: "which interests became salient depended on how actors framed the situation. Skilled social actors found frames that made others think that what was proposed was in their best interests."

Framing is both **symbolic** (language/gesture) and **cognitive** (activates shared schemas). Skilled actors deftly mediate between their own political interests and others' beliefs and expectations. Frames prevail when judged *resonant* and *legitimate* by a majority of organizational members.

This model links strategic framing back to Goffman (1974): frames as plastic, socially constructed interpretive principles.

## Strategic Frames vs. Strategic Framing

**Strategic frame** (Nadkarni & Narayanan 2007): a *noun* — the jointly constructed cognitive representation shared by managers of firms in an industry; defines competitive assumptions, industry boundaries, and strategy-environment relationships. Product/output.

**Strategic framing**: a *verb* — the process by which actors use language and symbols to construct, contest, or reinforce frames. Process/action.

Most research has focused on the static frame and its consequences (capabilities binding, competitive blind spots) rather than how frames are actively constructed (Kaplan 2008 as a corrective).

## Change Framing Literature

Key studies on leaders shaping interpretive frames during organizational change:
- Bartunek (1993); Gioia & Chittipeddi (1991) — "world class" metaphor to initiate university change
- Kellogg (2009) — "relational frames" that accommodated opponents' concerns while justifying new surgical practices → successful implementation
- Fiss & Zajac (2006) — framing of strategic change affects stakeholder compliance
- Mantere et al. (2012) — framing tactics in strategy discourse

**Limitation of this literature:** Asymmetric, linear model of communication — speaker (manager) → listener (employees). Employees are cast as "speakers-in-waiting" rather than active co-constructors of meaning (Bavelas et al. 2000). Chreim (2006) advocates for more balanced views of appropriation, contestation, and co-construction.

## Discursive Opportunity Structures

Kellogg (2011) — Koopmans & Statham (1999): the same framing arguments may fail in one historical moment and succeed in another, depending on whether broader societal discourses have shifted to make the frame resonate. Illustrates that strategic framing is not just about individual skill — the macro cultural context constrains what can be said and heard.

## Cross-Level Links

Strategic framing at the meso level feeds into macro-level framing contests and field-frame settlements:
- Skilled framers who successfully build coalitions → contribute to field-level meaning settlements
- Cultural entrepreneurs (Fligstein 2001) operate via strategic framing but at field scale
