# Construct Definition

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]]

## Why It Matters

"Failing to [define the construct properly] undermines every scientific activity that follows" (Mackenzie 2003; Podsakoff et al. 2016). Locke (2012): ~90% of journal submissions suffer from conceptual clarity problems. Jebb et al. (2021) synthesize guidance into three aspects.

## Three Aspects (Podsakoff et al. 2016 + Jebb et al.)

### Aspect 1: Consider the Construct
1. Think about essence — "What is this phenomenon? What is its inherent nature?"
2. Bring to reality — where does it concretely manifest? (behaviors, feelings, cognitions)
3. Think about what it is *not* — identify opposing constructs
4. Compare to similar constructs — isolate the point of difference

### Aspect 2: Create a Formal Definition
1. Use simple language — complexity ≠ clarity
2. Define subconcepts — if your definition relies on other concepts, define those too
3. Consider genus and differentia — genus = larger class; differentia = what makes it unique
4. Keep it short — state essential nature only; distinguish from secondary properties

### Aspect 3: Consult Alternative Opinions
1. Dictionaries — lay definitions expose core meaning
2. Cross-disciplinary literature — overlap = essential; divergence = gaps
3. Subject matter experts, key informants, practitioners, laypersons
4. Academic peers unfamiliar with the construct

*Source: Mackenzie (2003), Locke (2012), Podsakoff et al. (2016), as synthesized in Jebb et al. (2021) Table 2.*

## Specifying the Latent Continuum (Tay & Jebb 2018)

Many psychological constructs are degree-based (low/moderate/high), representable on a continuum. Developers often leave the continuum implicit — this causes problems.

**Questions to answer:**
- **Unipolar vs. bipolar?** Unipolar = absence at one end (e.g., happiness vs. no happiness). Bipolar = opposing construct at both ends (e.g., happy vs. sad).
- **What do the poles represent?** Absence of the construct, or presence of its opposite?
- **What do degrees represent?** Intensity of experience? Frequency?

**Operationalization implications:**
- Reverse-worded items measure an *opposite* construct — fine if bipolar, contaminates if unipolar
- Numerical format (0–4) implies unipolar; (−3 to +3) implies bipolar
- Labels "Not at all" → "Extremely" imply unipolar; "Strongly disagree" → "Strongly agree" imply bipolar
- Response format must match the declared polarity
