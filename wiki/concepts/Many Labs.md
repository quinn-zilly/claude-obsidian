# Many Labs

## Definition

A series of multilab replication initiatives in psychology in which 36–125 independent laboratories each replicate the same set of published findings, allowing for both replication assessment and cross-cultural/cross-population variation analysis.

## Key Iterations

| Project | Labs | Findings | Replicated | Key result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Many Labs 1 (Klein et al., 2014) | 36 | 13 | 10 (77%) | High replication rate; lab membership did not predict success |
| Many Labs 3 (Ebersole et al., 2016) | 20 | 10 | 3 (30%) | Most effects unaffected by time of semester |
| Many Labs 2 (Klein et al., 2018) | 125 sites | 28 | 14 (50%) | Heterogeneity highest for large effects; non-replicable effects fail consistently across cultures |

## Key Finding Across All Many Labs Projects

**Non-replicable effects fail consistently.** There is low heterogeneity in effect-size estimates for effects that don't replicate — they don't replicate anywhere. This undermines the argument that "hidden moderators" explain away replication failures. High heterogeneity is observed primarily for effects that do replicate but vary in magnitude.

**Lab expertise doesn't matter.** Highly published researchers and less prolific researchers are similarly likely to replicate a given effect. This supports inclusive (rather than select-expert-only) crowd designs.

## Relationship to OSC 2015

Many Labs complements [[open-science-collaboration-2015|OSC (2015)]]: where OSC used one lab per study, Many Labs uses many labs per study. Many Labs enables cross-population analysis; OSC enables breadth across many different findings.

## Connections

- **[[Crowdsourcing Science]]:** Many Labs is the paradigmatic example of crowdsourcing replication — crowd of labs, distributed across the globe, using same materials
- **[[Horizontal Distribution (Science)]]:** Many Labs is a selective-expert, low-communication, high-scale implementation (top-left quadrant of the 2×2)
- **[[Replication Crisis]]:** Many Labs provides the benchmark replication rates and the strongest evidence that failures are structural, not laboratory-specific
