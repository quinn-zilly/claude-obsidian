# Contingency Theories of Leadership

A family of second-wave leadership theories holding that leader effectiveness depends on the fit between a leader's style/traits and situational characteristics. Rejected the "one best way" approach.

## Fiedler's Contingency Model (1964)
Leader orientation (task vs. interpersonal) measured by the **Least Preferred Coworker (LPC)** scale. Situational favorability = combination of:
1. Task structure
2. Leader–member relations
3. Leader position power

Task-oriented leaders more effective in high or low favorability; relationship-oriented more effective in moderate favorability.

**Assessment:** Widely cited but inconsistent replication. Meta-analyses (Strube & Garcia 1981) showed general support; only 4 of 8 predicted correlations received consistent meta-analytic support (Peters et al. 1985). Later elaborations incorporating stress as situational moderator (Judge, Colbert & Ilies 2004) improved the model.

## Leadership Substitutes and Neutralizers (Kerr & Jermier 1978)
Some organizational or follower characteristics can *substitute* for leadership (make it unnecessary) or *neutralize* it (make it impossible). Examples: high follower ability and motivation, organizational formalization.

**Assessment:** Podsakoff et al. (1996) meta-analysis of 435 relationships from 36 samples — substitutes and leader behaviors both predict employee attitudes, but the key moderation hypothesis (substitutes reduce leader–outcome links) has generally *not* been supported after 20 years of research.

## General Assessment
Contingency theories captured the intuition that context matters, but produced inconsistent empirical results. Sampling error was a major culprit — meta-analysis helped clarify this. The broader insight (situations moderate trait/style–outcome relationships) was incorporated into later multi-level research.
