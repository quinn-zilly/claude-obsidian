# Field Frame

## Definition

A **field frame** (Lounsbury, Ventresca & Hirsch 2003) is an endogenous, durable interpretive convention within an institutional field — a shared understanding that emerges from framing struggles among field actors and, once established, structures expectations and guides behavior at the field level.

Lounsbury et al. (2003, p. 72): the field frame is "an intermediate concept that has the durability and stickiness of an institutional logic, but akin to strategic framing, … is endogenous to field actors and is subject to change and modification."

## Why the Concept Was Needed

Early institutional logics research positioned logics as **exogenous** to actors — deeply ingrained societal beliefs that actors could not meaningfully contest (Friedland & Alford 1991; Thornton & Ocasio 1999). This created the **embedded agency paradox**: if actors are fully constituted by institutions, how can they change them?

The field frame concept solves this by providing a construct that:
- Has institutional durability (not just one actor's framing)
- Remains endogenous to actors (produced and contested through their interactions)
- Bridges micro framing struggles and macro institutional outcomes

## How Field Frames Emerge: The Mechanism

1. **Novel framing in local context** — actors in an institutional field construct framings in interaction (analogical or metaphorical)
2. **Repeated interaction and common ground** — sustained engagement leads to shared vocabulary and interpretive schema
3. **Common ground propagation** (Clark 1996; Fay et al. 2008) — personal common ground (built in dyadic/small-group interaction) spreads to become communal common ground across a field
4. **Conventionalization** — the metaphorical/analogical framing abstracts into a conventional category; actors use the frame automatically rather than consciously
5. **Naturalization** → [[Institutional Frame]]: if the field frame is widely diffused and repeatedly instantiated, actors "forget" its metaphorical origin → taken-for-granted institutional frame

**Powell & Colyvas (2008)** — "career of metaphor" trajectory: making metaphor dead = institutionalization.

**Example (Rao et al. 2003):** French chefs framed as "creators" (initially active metaphor) → conventionalized category → naturalized institutional frame in which chefs are simply *are* creative agents.

## Framing Contests and Settlements

Field frames rarely emerge smoothly. They arise through:

**Framing contests** (Ryan 1991): competing groups assert incompatible framings. Contests intensify during periods of field upheaval when previous frames lose legitimacy.

**Cultural entrepreneurs** (Fligstein 2001, 2011): "skilled social actors" who frame stories that induce cooperation from allies while opposing opponents — mediating between group identities and interests to forge compromise frames.

**Discursive opportunity structures** (Koopmans & Statham 1999): the broader cultural context determines which framings resonate. A frame that fails in 1975 may succeed in 2005 if macro discourse has shifted (Kellogg 2011).

**Settlement types** (Ansari et al. 2013): settlements need not require full consensus — actors may settle on minimal agreement while maintaining divergent logics (e.g., climate change "commons logic": political + corporate actors agreed on need for action without sharing same frames).

Key insight (Rao & Kenney 2008): power distribution between groups affects whether frames get "patchworked" vs. "integrated" into compromise frames.

## Field Frame vs. Institutional Frame

| | Field Frame | Institutional Frame |
|---|---|---|
| **Status** | Temporary settlement; endogenous convention | Naturalized; taken-for-granted |
| **Agency** | Subject to contest and modification | Largely invisible; no longer recognized as a frame |
| **Origin** | Visible as a constructed framing | "Dead" — original source domain forgotten |
| **Example** | Chefs = "creators" (active comparison) | Chefs ARE creative agents (automatic) |

## Implications for Research

- Research should trace **how** field frames emerge (framing processes) not just **that** they exist (outcomes)
- Mechanism: personal → communal common ground → requires fine-grained interaction data
- Connections between frames and **categories** in institutional theory remain underexplored (Navis & Glynn 2010)
- Practice grounding: frames stabilize when anchored in habitual enactment (Reay et al. 2013)
