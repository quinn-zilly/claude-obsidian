# Behaviour Change Determinants

The variables — internal to the person or external in the social/structural environment — that predict behaviour and that can be targeted by behavioural change interventions. The key empirical question is not just whether a variable correlates with behaviour, but whether intervening on it actually changes behaviour.

> [!key-insight] Central Lesson (Albarracín et al. 2024)
> The most salient, theoretically popular determinants (knowledge, beliefs, general attitudes) are among the *least effective* intervention targets. The most effective targets (access, social support, habits) are structurally enabling rather than persuasive.

## Taxonomy (Albarracín et al. 2024)

### Individual Determinants

Variables internal to the person, ordered from least to most effective as intervention targets:

1. **Knowledge** — facts about an object or behaviour, held with certainty. Correlates weakly with behaviour (r=0.09–0.29). Intervention efficacy: **negligible** (OR≈1.11). Educational campaigns change knowledge but not behaviour.

2. **General skills** — broad cognitive capacities (self-control, attention inhibition, mindfulness). Small correlations with behaviour. Intervention efficacy: **negligible** (OR≈1.23).

3. **General attitudes** — evaluations of objects (e.g., "attitude towards recycling"). Moderate correlations (r=0.14–0.39). Intervention efficacy: **negligible** (OR≈1.35). Mass media campaigns change stated attitudes but not behaviour.

4. **Beliefs** — subjective probability judgements about outcomes. Small to medium correlations. Intervention efficacy: **negligible** (OR≈1.43). Includes conspiracy beliefs, cultural beliefs, growth mindset.

5. **Emotions** — visceral feelings about objects/events; fear, disgust, guilt, gratitude. Small to medium correlations. Intervention efficacy: **small** (OR≈1.60). Fear appeals negligible to small (d=0.14–0.20).

6. **Behavioural skills** — routines enabling execution of a specific target behaviour; reflected in perceived behavioural control/self-efficacy. Medium correlations (r=0.35–0.39). Intervention efficacy: **small** (OR≈1.99). Skills training more effective than general skills.

7. **Behavioural attitudes** — evaluations of a specific *behaviour* (not an object). Large correlations (r=0.38–0.58). Intervention efficacy: **small** (OR≈2.09). Lab studies overstate; field effects are modest.

8. **Habits** — automated, repeated behaviours; occur without awareness and persist without rewards. Large correlations (r=0.33–0.50). Intervention efficacy: **medium** (OR≈2.65). Best individual target.

### Social-Structural Determinants

Variables in the social/institutional environment, ordered from least to most effective as intervention targets:

1. **Legal and administrative sanctions** — bans, mandates, taxes. Intervention efficacy: **negligible** (OR≈1.24). Psychological reactance undermines compliance; deterrence theory overestimated.

2. **Institutional trustworthiness** — perceived justice/fairness of authorities or organizations. Medium correlations with behaviour. Intervention efficacy: **negligible** (OR≈0.80). Trustworthiness-building programmes have near-zero behavioural effects.

3. **Injunctive norms** — perceptions that others *approve* of a behaviour. Medium correlations (r≈0.17–0.22). Intervention efficacy: **small** (d=0.34). Social approval messaging.

4. **Monitors and reminders** — tracking and alerting devices. No correlational baseline (structural, not dispositional). Intervention efficacy: **small** (OR≈1.90). Useful adjunct, especially combined.

5. **Descriptive norms** — perceptions of how *common* a behaviour is. Medium correlations (r≈0.31–0.40). Intervention efficacy: **small** (OR≈2.20). Role models most effective within this category.

6. **Material incentives** — financial/non-financial rewards for behaviour. Variable correlations. Intervention efficacy: **small** (OR≈2.47). Substance use shows stronger effects.

7. **Social support** — informational, instrumental, or emotional help from others. Small to large correlations. Intervention efficacy: **medium** (OR≈2.53). Support groups, family involvement.

8. **Access** — material and logistic resources enabling performance; includes defaults, proximity, affordability. Small correlations (r≈0.16–0.26). Intervention efficacy: **large** (OR≈4.89). Most effective overall target.

## The Correlation-Intervention Gap

See [[Correlation-Intervention Gap (Behaviour)]]. For every individual determinant, correlational effect sizes exceed intervention effect sizes. This is a systematic warning: do not use correlation data to select intervention targets.

## Theoretical Roots

This taxonomy synthesizes across:
- [[Reasoned Action Approach]] — beliefs, attitudes, intentions, norms
- [[Information-Motivation-Behavioural Skills Model]] — information, motivation, behavioural skills
- [[Social Cognitive Theory]] — self-efficacy, modelling, environmental constraints

It extends these frameworks by treating social-structural factors as first-class determinants rather than background conditions.

## Cross-Domain Applicability

The hierarchy holds for health behaviours, environmental behaviours, and all behaviours combined. Minor differences: beliefs have slightly larger effects in the environmental domain; trustworthiness and legal sanctions are particularly ineffective in health and environmental domains, respectively.
