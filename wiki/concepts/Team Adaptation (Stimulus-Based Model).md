# Team Adaptation (Stimulus-Based Model)

## Definition

Team adaptation encompasses (a) **adaptability** — the capacity to respond to disruption, (b) **adaptation** — the process of detecting, assessing, and responding to stimuli, and (c) **adaptive outcomes** — performance and learning resulting from that process. The common thread is a team "dealing with" some type of change.

Distinct from **continuous improvement** (ongoing incremental kaizen without a specific impetus) and **proactivity** (self-initiated, anticipatory change without a clear external demand).

## The Stimulus-Based Phase Model (Pearsall et al., 2025)

Proposed as a corrective to response-driven research that (1) collapses across stimulus types, (2) neglects early phases and uncertainty, and (3) treats team attributes as antecedents rather than phase-specific moderators.

```
Phase 1: Stimulus Detection
    ↓
Phase 2: Urgency Assessment (Proximity × Magnitude)
    ↓
Phase 3: Duration Assessment (Temporary ↔ Sustained)
    ↓
Phase 4: Adaptive Response & Performance  ←→  Phase 5: Reflexive Learning
                                                     ↑↓ (recursive cycle)
Phase 6: Knowledge & Skill Acquisition
    ↓ (feeds back to Phase 1 for future stimuli)
```

## Key Phases

### Phase 1: Stimulus Detection
Team must notice and collectively identify that an adaptive demand exists. Factors enabling detection: external focus, boundary spanning, coordination of information, experience with interruptions. Uncertainty and attentional limits cause delays. Early detection preserves response options.

### Phase 2: Urgency Assessment
Prioritizing stimuli by **proximity** (how soon consequences will be felt) and **magnitude** (how large those consequences will be). Proximal events seem more concrete and actionable (construal level theory); distal events feel abstract. Higher magnitude "jolts" teams from habitual routines. Emotions serve as "alarm bells" modulating urgency perception.

### Phase 3: Duration Assessment
Whether the team expects the situation to revert ("more temporary") or persist ("more sustained / new normal"). Duration is **continuous**, not binary. Accurate duration assessment is prerequisite to an effective adaptive response — misclassification leads to misaligned strategies and wasted effort.

### Phase 4: Adaptive Response & Performance
Duration-matched behavioral responses:
- **Temporary**: closed-loop communication, reallocation of assets, implicit coordination (if TMS is strong), bricolage
- **Sustained**: plan formulation, role discussion, information sharing, action identification, reflection

### Phase 5: Reflexive Learning
Within-episode cycle: feedback → updating urgency/duration assessments → microadjusting behaviors. Effective updating = "revising provisional sensemaking to incorporate new cues" (Christianson, 2019).

### Phase 6: Knowledge and Skill Acquisition
Post-episode learning producing more adaptive strategies, higher collective efficacy, and improved mental models. Loops back to Phase 1 — prior experience enhances future detection, urgency assessment, and response quality. Learning from both success and failure.

## Contextual Factors

**Team Attributes**: Regulatory focus (Phases 1–2), goal orientation (Phases 5–6), cognitive ability, personality (conscientiousness, emotional stability), psychological safety, collective efficacy, team maturity.

**Leadership**: Directive leadership fits high-urgency stimuli (Phase 2–4); empowering leadership fits pre-detection and learning phases (Phases 1, 5–6).

**Training**: Adaptive training, perturbation training, concept mapping cross-training, team charters.
