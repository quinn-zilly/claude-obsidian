# Injunctive Norms

Perceptions of the degree to which *others approve of* (or would support) a person's behaviour. One of two main types of social norms, distinct from [[Descriptive Norms]] (what others *do*).

## Definition

> "Perceptions of the degree to which others support a person's behaviour." — Albarracín et al. 2024 (Table 3)

Example measure: "People who are important to me think I should use condoms."

## Theoretical Background

Central to the [[Reasoned Action Approach]] (Fishbein & Ajzen): subjective norm → intention → behaviour. Also featured in the theory of normative focus (Cialdini et al.). Injunctive norms mobilize a sense of social obligation — *ought*.

## Correlational Evidence

Small to medium associations with behaviour:
- Blood donation: r=0.17
- Recycling: r=0.21
- Adolescent sexual behaviour: r=0.22

Overall: medium correlation across domains (OR≈2.48 correlational).

## Intervention Efficacy

**Small** overall (d=0.34 across domains; negligible d=0.10 for environmental behaviour specifically).

Social normative interventions communicate that others *approve* of the recommended behaviour. They can work when people hold misperceptions about true injunctive norms (e.g., students believing peers approve of heavy drinking when they do not).

## Relationship to Descriptive Norms

Injunctive and descriptive norms correlate weakly (r=0.1–0.4) and are theoretically distinct:
- Injunctive: what others approve (*moral obligation signal*)
- Descriptive: what others do (*prevalence/popularity signal*)

Both show small intervention effects, but mechanisms differ. Do not conflate in intervention design.
