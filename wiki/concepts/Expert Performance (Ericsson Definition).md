# Expert Performance (Ericsson Definition)

Following Ericsson & Smith (1991), **expert performance = consistently superior performance on a specified set of representative tasks for a domain**, reproducible on demand under controlled laboratory conditions.

## Why the restrictive definition

The virtue of defining expertise this narrowly is methodological: it makes performance **scientifically analyzable**. A mark of true experts is that they can display superior performance *reliably upon demand*, so representative tasks can be captured in the lab and their mediating mechanisms studied. The definition deliberately **excludes** domains where no valid superiority measure exists.

## Experience vs. expertise

Crucially, this **dissociates expertise from experience**. Individuals with much domain experience often do *not* outperform less-experienced ones — clearest in judgment/decision domains (auditing, medical diagnosis). This is why the vault's [[Mechanical vs Clinical Prediction|mechanical-vs-clinical]] finding holds: experience without feedback-rich [[Deliberate Practice|deliberate practice]] does not build valid judgment.

## Captured mechanisms

Expert performance is mediated by acquired, domain-specific mechanisms — anticipation from advance cues, [[Long-Term Working Memory]], [[Chunking|knowledge-based chunks]] — that constitute [[Maximal Adaptation to Task Constraints|maximal adaptation]] and, contrary to the automatization belief, remain guided by active planning and reasoning.
