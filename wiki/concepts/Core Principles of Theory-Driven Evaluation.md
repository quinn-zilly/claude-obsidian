# Core Principles of Theory-Driven Evaluation

Five core principles derived by [[coryn-et-al-2011-theory-driven-evaluation-review|Coryn et al. (2011)]] from major TDE theorists (Chen, Donaldson, Rogers, Weiss, White). Principles 1–4 are evaluation **processes**; Principle 5 is an evaluation **outcome** and is what most distinguishes TDE from other approaches. They are **situational, not absolute** criteria — no claim that a good TDE applies all of them.

1. **Formulate a plausible program theory** — from existing theory/research, implicit/stakeholder theory, observation/exploratory research, or a combination.
2. **Formulate and prioritize evaluation questions around the program theory.**
3. **Use the program theory to guide planning, design, and execution** — considering contingencies (time, budget, use); decide whether tailored or comprehensive.
4. **Measure the constructs postulated in the program theory** — process, outcome, and contextual constructs.
5. **Identify breakdowns and side effects, determine effectiveness, and explain cause-and-effect** — including:
   - identify breakdowns (implementation failure, unsuitable context, theory failure);
   - identify unanticipated/unintended outcomes (positive and negative);
   - [[Causal Description vs Causal Explanation|causal description]] (that constructs are associated);
   - **causal explanation** (moderation: direction/strength differences; mediation: one construct accounting for the relationship between others).

> [!key-insight] Principle 5 is the demarcation line
> Nearly all evaluation forms formulate questions and measure constructs. What sets TDE apart is using a program theory to explain *how and why* — subprinciples 5.d.i (moderation) and 5.d.ii (mediation). Coryn et al. find this is also the **least consistently enacted** principle in practice.
