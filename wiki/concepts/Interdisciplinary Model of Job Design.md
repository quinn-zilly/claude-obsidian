# Interdisciplinary Model of Job Design

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]]

Campion (1988, 1989; Campion & Thayer 1985). Recognizes four distinct disciplinary approaches to job design with different outcome profiles. Developed MJDQ to measure all four.

## Four Approaches

| Approach | Discipline | Recommendations | Outcomes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mechanistic** | Industrial engineering (Taylor, Gilbreth) | Simplification, specialization, repetition | Efficiency, lower staffing/training costs |
| **Motivational** | Org psychology (Hackman & Oldham) | Task variety, autonomy, enrichment | Satisfaction, intrinsic motivation, retention |
| **Perceptual** | Human factors / experimental psych | Reduce info processing demands | Fewer errors, accidents, less overload |
| **Biological** | Ergonomics / medical sciences | Reduce physical stress, posture | Less physical discomfort, fatigue |

## Key Tension

Motivational ↔ Mechanistic: enriching work typically reduces efficiency; simplifying reduces satisfaction. Campion & McClelland (1993) originally thought tradeoffs unavoidable. Later Edwards et al. (2000) + Morgeson & Campion (2002) showed partial resolution possible via **specialization** (efficient + skill-using simultaneously).

## MJDQ Measurement

10-factor solution (Edwards et al. 1999): mechanistic = specialization + task simplicity; motivational = feedback + skill + rewards; perceptual-motor = ergonomic design + cognitive simplicity; biological = physical ease + work conditions + work scheduling. Still limited — some dimensions single-item only. WDQ (Morgeson & Humphrey 2006) supersedes.

## Employee Preferences

Campion & McClelland (1991): workers prefer motivational > perceptual design; ambivalent about mechanistic/biological. Consistent with all-positive-direction finding: even low-GNS workers show small satisfaction gains from enriched work.
