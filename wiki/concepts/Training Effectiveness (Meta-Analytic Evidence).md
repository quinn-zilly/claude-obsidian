# Training Effectiveness (Meta-Analytic Evidence)

## Bottom Line

Training works. Across 397 effect sizes and 162 studies (1960–2000), Arthur et al. (2003) found mean d = 0.60–0.63 across all four [[Kirkpatrick Four-Level Model]] levels. Cohen's benchmarks: small=0.20, medium=0.50, large=0.80 — so training lands solidly in the medium-to-large range.

## What Moderates Effectiveness

Three factors demonstrated to matter:

### 1. Evaluation Criterion Type
Overall means are deceptively similar across levels. Within-study comparisons reveal a **clear drop from learning to behavioral criteria** (mean decrease in d = 0.77). This is consistent with:
- Transfer difficulty (skills learned ≠ skills applied)
- Skill decay over time
- Lack of opportunity to perform trained behaviors
- Unsupportive post-training work environment

### 2. Needs Assessment Comprehensiveness
Systematic needs assessment (organizational + task + person analysis) predicts larger effect sizes. Acts as alignment mechanism — specifies what to train, for whom, and how to evaluate.

### 3. Skill Type × Training Method Match
Content-method fit matters. Key pairings:
- **Cognitive** content → lecture/discussion, programmed instruction
- **Interpersonal** content → behavioral role modeling, simulation
- **Psychomotor** content → hands-on practice, simulator

Mismatch reduces effectiveness. Multi-method programs targeting multiple skill types (cognitive + interpersonal) may outperform single-method.

## Practical Implication

Skepticism about training ROI is empirically unwarranted *on average*. But variance is large — design choices drive outcomes. The question is not "does training work?" but "what design features make training work better?"
