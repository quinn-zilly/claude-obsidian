# Habits (Behaviour)

Repeated behaviours that have acquired automaticity — occurring without intention, without awareness, and persisting even after they no longer provide benefits. The strongest individual-level target for behavioural change interventions.

## Definition

> "Behavioural routines that have acquired features of automaticity, meaning that they occur efficiently, without awareness, or continue even without intention and after they are no longer adaptive." — Albarracín et al. 2024 (Table 1)

Note: habits ≠ merely "past behaviour." Contemporary usage requires demonstrated automaticity, measured by habit scales (e.g., "Washing my hands would require effort not to do").

## Correlational Evidence

Large associations with behaviour:
- Health-provider habits (handwashing): r=0.33
- Car habits → car use: r=0.50
- Past condom use → current condom use: r=0.36
- Past recycling → future recycling: r=0.41

Overall: large correlation (OR≈6.17 across meta-analyses).

## Intervention Efficacy

**Medium** overall (mean OR≈2.65) — best among all individual targets, despite fighting against entrenched automaticity.

### Intervention types

1. **Stop-signal / inhibitory training**: training to stop behaviour when facing triggering cues. Stop signal training: d=−0.39; attentional bias modification: d=−0.51 on eating behaviour.
2. **Habit reversal training**: identify tic/habit trigger → implement competing incompatible response. Effective for tic disorders (d=0.94).
3. **Environmental regularity**: structuring environments to promote habit formation for *new* desired behaviours.
4. **Distraction from cues**: cognitive reappraisal (g=0.45), attentional bias modification (g=0.44), distraction (g=−0.31) for food intake.

### Challenge

Habit interventions face the bootstrapping problem: behaviour must be elicited before it can be automated. Access and social support interventions may help prime habit formation.

## Gap vs. Correlational Evidence

As with all individual determinants, the intervention effect (medium) is smaller than the correlational association (large). Past behaviour/habit strength predicts future behaviour strongly, but targeting habits is harder than this correlation implies. Still, habits remain the most powerful individual-level lever.
