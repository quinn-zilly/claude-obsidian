# Person–Situation Debate

Nav: [[index]] | [[concepts/_index]]

The long-running dispute over whether **traits** or **situations** better explain behavior. Behavior = f(person, situation) is a truism, but beyond the generality it "continues to generate an exceptional level of controversy."

## The two critiques

1. **Trait side**: personality measures have "relatively meager" effects on complex behavior — Mischel's (1968) low cross-situational-consistency critique, echoed in I-O by Morgeson et al. (2007) ("very low validity for predicting overall job performance") and Murphy & Dzieweczynski (2005).
2. **Situation side**: situational explanations lack **taxonomic progress** — "the development of a comprehensive taxonomy of situations" has yielded "stunningly modest success" (Swann & Seyle 2005); situational variables in research are "almost completely ad hoc" (Funder 2008).

## Interactionist resolution

The productive path is **interactionism** — modeling person × situation. Two theories supply the situational structure:

- **General interactionism** → [[Situation Strength]]: strong situations suppress *all* trait effects; weak situations let personality predict.
- **Specific interactionism** → [[Trait Activation Theory]]: a trait predicts only in jobs whose cues **activate** it.

[[Judge-Zapata-2015-person-situation-revisited|Judge & Zapata (2015)]] meta-analytically integrated both and showed Big Five validity rises predictably in weak situations and in trait-activating contexts — demonstrating that the "low validity" averages hide strong, theory-consistent moderation. This connects to the [[Units Problem (Personality)|functionalist]] point that much stable disposition lives at the level of *trait-in-situation*.
