# Psychological Group

**Definition (Turner 1984):** "a collection of people who share the same social identification or define themselves in terms of the same social category membership."

## Key Property

A member of a psychological group **need not interact with, like, or be liked/accepted by other members.** It is the *perception* of being (e.g.) a loyal patriot or sports fan that incorporates that status into social identity. The individual **reifies** or credits the group with a psychological reality apart from their relationships with its members.

## Why It Matters

This is, per [[ashforth-mael-1989-social-identity-theory-organization|Ashforth & Mael (1989)]], "perhaps the greatest contribution that SIT makes to organizational behavior": a psychological group is **far more than an extension of interpersonal relationships** (Turner 1985). Identification with a collectivity can arise *without* interpersonal cohesion, similarity, or interaction, yet still powerfully shape affect and behavior.

This **reverses the conventional logic** that interpersonal regard is cognitively aggregated up into an individual–organization bond. Instead, identification with a group can arise quite separately from interpersonal interaction ("I am a Marine"). Reification is what lets an individual feel loyal to an organization *per se* — even despite wrongdoing by management or complete turnover of personnel.

- One may "like people as group members at the same time as one dislikes them as individual persons" (Turner 1984; Dion 1973).
- See [[Minimal Group Paradigm]] for the experimental basis (mere categorization suffices).
