# Pay-for-Performance (PFP)

A compensation approach that links individual or group pay to performance. Virtually all private-sector organizations use some form of PFP (Fulmer et al., 2023).

---

## Taxonomy

PFP varies along three dimensions:

**Dimension 1 — Performance measure type:**
- *Results-based (objective):* physical output, sales, profit, TSR — easily separable but "excessively narrow"
- *Behavior-based (subjective):* supervisor/customer ratings — susceptible to contamination but captures unmeasured dimensions
- Corrected mean r between the two ≈ .27–.39 (Bommer et al., 1995; Heneman, 1986); shared variance only 15%. Organizations typically need both.

**Dimension 2 — Level of performance measurement:**
- Individual incentive plans (commissions, individual bonuses)
- Aggregate plans (gainsharing, profit sharing, stock)
- Merit pay = behavior-based, individual

**Dimension 3 — [[PFP Intensity]]:**
How large the pay-performance gradient is. Higher intensity → stronger incentive effect but greater risk of unintended consequences ([[Equal Compensation Principle]]).

---

## Two Core Mechanisms

### [[Incentive Effect (Compensation)]]
PFP changes what current employees *do* — via motivation, effort, and goal-directed behavior. Most theory and research focuses here.

### [[Sorting Effect (Compensation)]]
PFP changes *who* the employees are. High performers prefer stronger PFP; low performers leave under strong PFP. Often ignored but can be *larger* than the incentive effect.

> Cadsby, Song & Tapon (2007) lab experiment: PFP advantage = 10% under random assignment vs. 38% under free worker choice → sorting ≈ 74% of total PFP effect.

> Lazear (1986) Safelite Glass: +44% total productivity; ~22% incentive effect, ~22% sorting effect.

---

## Evidence on Effectiveness

Kim, Gupta & Shaw (2022) meta-analysis (82 effect sizes, N = 7,978): performance +0.54 SD (~24%) under incentive vs. fixed pay. Net advantage ≈ 20% even after accounting for higher payout costs.

Most research **underestimates** total PFP effects because:
1. Studies use random assignment → no sorting observed
2. Studies focus on annual merit increases (3–4%) and ignore the **promotion channel** (15% grade-to-grade; 48% more promotions per 1-pt rating advantage over 6 years — Gerhart & Milkovich, 1989)
3. Short time horizons miss long-term incentive/retention effects

---

## Unintended Consequences

When PFP intensity is high and performance measurement is narrow, the [[Equal Compensation Principle]] predicts employees over-focus on incentivized metrics:
- Excessive risk-taking
- Within-firm competition undermining cooperation
- Quality/service neglect
- Gaming of metrics

*Novartis example:* $678M DOJ settlement over incentivized overprescription → response: added behavioral values ratings + capped bonus at 35% of comp.

**Mitigation:** Add behavior-based measures; reduce results-only intensity; avoid exceptions for below-standard performance (weakens pay-performance signal for peers).

---

## Performance Distribution Complications

Some jobs have non-normal (power-law/Paretian) performance distributions (O'Boyle & Aguinis, 2012). Standard merit budgets and rating compression may decouple pay from performance for "star" employees — creating poaching risk. PFP systems must be designed to accommodate the actual performance distribution.
