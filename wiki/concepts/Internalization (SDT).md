# Internalization (SDT)

Process by which people "take in" external values and regulations and progressively transform them into their own.

**Internalization**: taking in a value or regulation.
**Integration**: further transformation of that regulation into one's sense of self, so it emanates from self.

## Why It Matters

Most adult behavior is not intrinsically motivated. People must acquire motivation for activities that are not inherently interesting. The quality of that motivation (degree of internalization) determines behavioral persistence, quality, and well-being outcomes.

## Conditions That Facilitate

All three basic needs involved, with different roles:

- **[[Relatedness (SDT)]]** — prerequisite for *initial* internalization: people first adopt behaviors valued by significant others they want to feel connected to
- **[[Competence (SDT)]]** — supports adoption; needed to feel efficacious with valued behaviors
- **[[Autonomy (SDT)]]** — critical specifically for *integration* (deepest level); requires holistic processing = synthesizing new regulation with existing values; facilitated by choice, volition, freedom from excessive external pressure

## Applied Evidence

**Education**: more autonomous extrinsic motivation → engagement (Connell & Wellborn 1991), better performance (Miserandino 1996), lower dropout (Vallerand & Bissonnette 1992), higher quality learning (Grolnick & Ryan 1987).

**Health care**: greater internalization → medication adherence (Williams et al. 1998), weight loss maintenance (Williams et al. 1996), glucose control in diabetics (Williams, Freedman & Deci 1998), addiction treatment attendance (Ryan, Plant & O'Malley 1995).

**Other**: religion (Ryan, Rigby & King 1993), physical exercise, political activity, environmental activism, intimate relationships.

## Not a Fixed Stage Sequence

People can internalize at any point along the continuum depending on prior experiences and current situational factors. The range of behaviors that can be fully integrated does grow with cognitive development and ego maturity.
