# Dictionary of Occupational Titles (DOT)

The **DOT** was, for decades, the most comprehensive source of U.S. occupational information, containing data on **over 12,000 jobs** (U.S. Department of Labor). It was the predecessor to the [[Occupational Information Network (O-NET)|O*NET]].

## Why it was replaced

Per [[Sackett-Walmsley-Laczo-2013-job-and-work-analysis|Sackett et al. (2013)]] (citing Dunnette), three features limited its usefulness: (a) a focus on occupation-specific **narrative** information, limiting cross-job comparison; (b) a focus on **tasks** rather than worker attributes; and (c) difficulty keeping information **current** given update time and expense. A 1993 advisory panel (**APDOT**) blueprinted its replacement, leading to O*NET.

> [!note] Still used where O*NET falls short
> The U.S. Social Security Administration judged O*NET unsuitable to replace the DOT for **disability determination** (per [[Sanchez-Levine-2012-rise-fall-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine 2012]]), prompting a separate occupational-information project to evaluate physical and mental work demands.
