# Rating-less Reviews

Part of the recent PM-reform wave: **removing formal numeric/summary performance ratings**, often replacing them with regular check-ins, coaching, and feedback. Adopted or reduced by Accenture, Deloitte, Microsoft, GAP, Adobe, and others.

## The debate
- **Proponents of keeping ratings**: legal/regulatory documentation; transparency; evaluation happens whether or not it is recorded.
- **Opponents**: ratings are almost always inflated and low-value; keeping them maintains a *non-transparent* second layer of "real" judgments (e.g., hidden hi-po lists).
- **The strongest pro-rating rationale** ([[adler-et-al-2016-getting-rid-of-ratings|Adler et al. 2016]]): ratings are ineffective at *driving* performance but at least ensure *some* performance information reaches employees; performance is *always* evaluated in some form, and [[Differentiated Evaluations|differentiated evaluation]] attracts and retains top performers. Many firms that "eliminated" ratings still enter numbers for merit/bonus — i.e., still rate.

## The evidence caveat
CEB (2016): employees in rating-less orgs reported **lower** engagement and perceived fairness. But that study conflated "dropped ratings **and** added check-ins" with "just dropped ratings." [[pulakos-2019-evolution-of-pm|Pulakos et al.]] conclude: **do not remove ratings without adding mechanisms** for clear expectations and regular feedback.

> [!key-insight] Reframe
> Pulakos et al.: the keep-or-drop question is the *wrong first question* — start from the performance outcomes you want and how to enable them. [[schleicher-2019-pm-effectiveness-review|Schleicher et al.]]: ask what a rating is *for* — its latent value may be the **manager learning and decision quality** it produces (see [[PM Effectiveness Value Chains]]).
