# Explained vs Unexplained Pay Dispersion

The central organizing distinction in modern **[[Pay Dispersion]]** research (Shaw et al. 2002; Kepes, Delery & Gupta 2009; Trevor, Reilly & Gerhart 2012; consolidated by [[shaw-2014-pay-dispersion|Shaw 2014]]).

- **Explained / legitimate dispersion** — pay variation attributable to normatively accepted inputs: individual incentives, seniority, education, tenure, historical performance. Perceived as fair under [[Distributive Justice|equity theory]]. **Generally positively related** to organizational productivity/safety, team performance, and individual performance.
- **Unexplained / residual dispersion** — variation remaining once legitimate inputs are statistically partialled out (e.g., the residual from a wage regression on individual factors). Perceived as illegitimate. **Generally negatively related** to performance, especially at the team level.

> [!key-insight] Why the field looked contradictory
> The raw dispersion–performance relationship is direction-unpredictable because studies mix these two components. Decomposing them resolves most of the apparent contradictions in the literature. Studies that "control for" performance may inadvertently remove the *legitimate* component, leaving only residual variation — which then looks harmful (a likely reading of Pfeffer & Langton 1993).

Methodologically: residual is often operationalized as the residual of a within-unit regression of pay on individual inputs (talent, tenure, bonus, historical performance).

This is the mechanism underlying the "how dispersion arises matters as much as its magnitude" claim on [[Pay Dispersion]].
