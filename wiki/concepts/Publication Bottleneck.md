# Publication Bottleneck

The publication bottleneck is the structural condition in academic science where the number of peer-reviewed publication slots grows substantially slower than the number of research-active scientists and submitted manuscripts, creating intense competitive pressure on what gets published.

## The Information-Economic Account

[[Roger Giner-Sorolla|Giner-Sorolla (2012)]] identifies the bottleneck as the **root cause** of the [[Replication Crisis]], distinct from — and more fundamental than — fraud, p-value abuse, or individual misconduct. Under tight information economics:

1. Journal rejection rates stay above 70–80% even as articles per issue grow
2. Editors evaluating papers after all methodologically fatal papers are culled must choose among many acceptable papers on non-scientific grounds
3. **Conjunction ("Anna Karenina") rules** dominate: "nothing wrong with it" beats "highest overall excellence" — aesthetic flaws become disqualifying

## Quantification (Social/Personality Psychology)

SPSP annual meeting attendance as a proxy for research-active scientists:

| Year | SPSP Attendance | Articles/Head |
|---|---|---|
| 2003 | ~1,500 | 1.32 |
| 2010 | ~3,500 | 0.90 |
| 2012 | ~4,000 | declining |

Studies per article in JPSP: 2.20 (2000) → 3.33 (2008), reflecting the post-1970s multi-study norm *raising* the aesthetic bar without solving the bottleneck.

## Why Prior Solutions Failed

Attempts to solve the bottleneck through **new low-standard journals** consistently fail:
- *Replications in Social Psychology* (1979): 3 volumes, then folded
- *Representative Research in Social Psychology* (1970): last articles ~2006
- *Journal of Articles in Support of the Null Hypothesis* (2002): 1–7 articles/year

The reason: career incentives push everyone toward top journals. Publishing in low-prestige outlets can be *negatively* valued — like "throwing pennies out of your house because having them makes you look cheap" (Giner-Sorolla, 2012).

## The Moral Hazard

Under severe bottleneck conditions, two researchers with equally good ideas may have unequal empirical luck. The researcher with significant results survives; the one with honest null results does not. This **structural unfairness** creates moral cover for aesthetic cleaning of results — it feels like self-defense, not fraud. Systemic change requires changing the structure, not exhorting individuals to resist it.

## Solutions

See [[giner-sorolla-2012-science-or-art]] for the full argument. The key insight: the bottleneck must be *opened*, not just filtered differently. This requires:

- **Carrot**: professionally recognized arenas for all results (following [[nosek-bar-anan-2012-scientific-utopia-i]])
- **Stick**: if self-regulation fails, mandatory full dissemination as condition of funding/IRB approval
