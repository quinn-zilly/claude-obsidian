# Diversity Training Myths

Common misconceptions about diversity training that act as barriers to effective program design and implementation. Identified and systematically challenged by [[cheng-et-al-2019-diversity-training-myths|Cheng et al. (2019)]].

> [!key-insight] Why Myths Matter
> Pre-training beliefs — including myths — directly influence training effectiveness. Addressing myths is not just academic; it removes practical barriers to impact.

## Myth Taxonomy (19 Myths Across 3 Categories)

### Category 1: Effectiveness & Evaluation

**Myth 1: "Diversity training does not work"**
Reality: Works under appropriate conditions. The binary question is wrong — focus on moderating conditions.

**Myth 2: "There is always backlash"**
Reality: Reactions are typically positive (Bezrukova *g* = .61). Backlash is possible but not inevitable; design choices mediate it.

**Myth 3: "Diversity training always does more good than harm"**
Reality: Potential harms include reinforcing stereotypes, creating false sense of fairness (which can suppress future discrimination claims), and increased conflict if poorly designed.

**Myth 4: "If people didn't like it, it didn't work"**
Reality: Trainee satisfaction (affective reactions) is a poor proxy for effectiveness. Utility reactions (perceived relevance) predict learning better. Training can be uncomfortable and still effective.

**Myth 5: "The ROI is unfavorable"**
Reality: When distal outcomes are counted (turnover, litigation, EEOC costs, discrimination-related disengagement), ROI is likely favorable.

### Category 2: Content

**Myth 6: "Diversity is just common sense"**
Reality: People have implicit biases they are unaware of. Self-enhancement motives and cognitive limitations prevent accurate self-assessment.

**Myth 7: "It's only about championing minority groups"**
Reality: Also produces business outcomes (competitive advantage, productivity), learning outcomes (self-knowledge, cultural skills), and social justice outcomes.

**Myth 8: "It's only about doing the right thing"**
Reality: Also serves organizational self-interest — reputation, talent attraction, legal protection (EEOC: $484M in 2017).

**Myth 9: "One size fits all"**
Reality: Needs analysis is essential. EEOC explicitly recommends tailoring. Off-the-shelf programs create disingenuous atmospheres.

**Myth 10: "Diversity is just race and gender"**
Reality: Diversity = surface-level (visible: race, gender, age) + deep-level (invisible: values, religion, SES, sexual orientation, political beliefs, physical ability).

**Myth 11: "Training should make people colorblind"**
Reality: Colorblindness is counterproductive. Multicultural approach (acknowledge + appreciate differences) is more effective, predicts psychological engagement, lowers perceived racial bias.

**Myth 12: "Training blames white men"**
Reality: Broad framing benefits everyone including white men (freedom from stereotypes, ally role). Narrow framing (race-only) alienates both minority (tokenism) and majority (exclusion) participants.

### Category 3: Design & Delivery

**Myth 13: "Diversity training is just like any other training"**
Reality: Content-outcome relationships and design-effectiveness links are unique (e.g., mandatory/voluntary effects differ from other training types).

**Myth 14: "One session is all that's needed"**
Reality: Single sessions can be meaningful; spaced, longer programs (~24 hrs total) are consistently more effective per meta-analytic evidence.

**Myth 15: "It should always be mandatory"**
Reality: Mixed evidence. Mandatory signals buy-in; voluntary increases motivation. Context-dependent — organizations should assess their specific culture.

**Myth 16: "Anyone can lead diversity training"**
Reality: Trainers need content expertise (evaluation, group dynamics, historical diversity issues) AND interpersonal skills (communication, perspective-taking, conflict resolution). Trainer demographics affect perceived effectiveness.

**Myth 17: "Leaders don't need to be involved"**
Reality: Leadership support is critical at strategic (vision/culture), tactical, and operational (training) levels. Visible leader engagement is one of the strongest predictors of training effectiveness.

**Myth 18: "Marginalized groups don't need to go"**
Reality: Biases and discrimination exist within all groups (e.g., racism in LGBTQ community, homophobia in Black community). Including marginalized group voices is necessary for some outcomes.

**Myth 19: "Diversity training is the only initiative needed"**
Reality: Effects are amplified when paired with organizational support, policies, follow-up sessions, consequences, rewards, and resources.
