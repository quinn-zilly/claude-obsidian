# Psychological Safety

Nav: [[index]] | [[concepts/_index]]

"A mutual belief among team members that the team can take interpersonal risks and that a 'sense of confidence that the team will not embarrass, reject, or punish someone for speaking up' exists" — Edmondson (1999, p. 354).

## Why It Matters for TDIs

Psychological safety is a **cross-cutting enabler** for multiple TDI types:

- **Team Training**: enables members to openly discuss errors and learn from them; without it, error-based learning is suppressed (Bell & Kozlowski 2008)
- **Team Debriefing**: essential prerequisite — debriefs surface constructive criticism; a blame-hostile climate renders them performative rather than developmental
- **Team Building**: interpersonal-relationship management component works via trust development, which overlaps with psychological safety

Google's Project Aristotle (Rozovsky 2015) identified psychological safety as the #1 predictor of effective team functioning, above even individual talent.

## Key Properties

- **Mutual/shared** — a team-level belief, not just individual comfort
- **Task-focused** — safety to speak up about work-related concerns, not unconditional comfort
- **Distinct from trust** — trust = beliefs about others' intentions; psychological safety = beliefs about the team's response to interpersonal risk-taking
- **Emergent state** — develops over time through team interactions; can be deliberately cultivated

## Relationship to Learning

Learning from errors requires psychological safety: if members fear punishment for revealing mistakes, errors go undisclosed, error patterns persist, and individual and team performance stagnates. Debrief discussions and training exercises that expose skill gaps both require a safe climate.

## Role in Team Adaptation

In the [[Team Adaptation (Stimulus-Based Model)]], psychological safety is particularly important in **[[Reflexive Learning (Team Adaptation)|Phase 5: Reflexive Learning]]** — enabling teams to course-correct after misclassifying a stimulus's duration, acknowledge ineffective responses, and openly update their adaptive strategies. Edmondson et al. (2001) found that psychological safety helps teams effectively adapt to change. See also [[pearsall-christian-croitoru-2025-team-adaptation]].
