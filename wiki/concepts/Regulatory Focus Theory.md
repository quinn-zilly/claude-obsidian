# Regulatory Focus Theory

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Motivation Meta-Framework (Kanfer)]]

Higgins (1997). Posits two rival self-regulatory orientations for goal pursuit:

| Focus | Strategy | Emotions | Work outcomes |
|-------|----------|----------|---------------|
| **Promotion** | Maximize gains to ensure success (approach) | Joy (when succeed), sadness (when fail) | Productivity, creativity, well-being |
| **Prevention** | Minimize losses to avoid failure (avoidance) | Relief (when succeed), anxiety (when fail) | Safety, compliance |

Both foci can accomplish the same ends but through different means and with different consequences.

## Key Findings

- Promotion focus → stronger positive emotions from success (joy > relief) → generally higher well-being
- Prevention focus → better predictor of safety behavior (Wallace & Chen 2006)
- Promotion focus → better predictor of productivity
- Lanaj, Chang & Johnson (2012) meta-analysis: regulatory focus and work outcomes comprehensively reviewed

## Relation to Goal Orientation

Both are motivation orientations — individual differences in *how* goals are pursued rather than *what* goals are set. LGO (Dweck) = appetitive/promotion-flavored; PGO-avoid = prevention-flavored. But conceptually distinct constructs.

## Relation to SDT

Prevention focus parallels controlled motivation (avoiding negative consequences); promotion focus parallels autonomous motivation (pursuing positive outcomes). Both capture the phenomenology of motivation quality without the same emphasis on need satisfaction as mechanism.

## Malleability

Regulatory focus can be situationally induced (e.g., framing a task in gain vs. loss terms) → downstream effects on planning, strategy use, and self-regulation even without trait-level differences.
