# Team Debriefing

Nav: [[index]] | [[concepts/_index]]

> "One of the most promising methods for accelerating learning from experience" — Eddy, Tannenbaum & Mathieu (2013, p. 976)

A **process intervention** for **intact teams**. Team members reflect on a past performance episode: discuss what happened, uncover problems, confirm successes, plan for future performance periods. Targets **team processes** directly — not competencies or interpersonal relationships.

## Meta-Analytic Evidence

- **20–25% increase in team performance** on average (Tannenbaum & Cerasoli 2013; 46 samples, avg debrief = ~18 min)
- Improved patient outcomes in critical care (return of spontaneous circulation; Couper et al. 2013)
- Reduced employee burnout over time (Chen et al. 2017)
- Effective across health care, aviation, military, first responders, virtual teams

Mechanism: engages Kolb's (1984) experiential learning cycle → builds **shared mental models** (collective knowledge structures about task and team) → increases team effectiveness (DeChurch & Mesmer-Magnus 2010).

## Evidence-Based Design Principles

1. **Psychologically safe climate** — essential; enables members to surface constructive criticism without fear of blame or retaliation (Edmondson 1999)
2. **Trained facilitator** — preferred when feasible; guides discussion, creates positive learning environment, encourages equal participation, demonstrates proper feedback practices
3. **Structure by category, not chronology** — organize discussion by performance/teamwork themes (communication, decision making, backup behavior) rather than "what happened first"
4. **Discuss positive AND negative examples** — builds accurate shared mental models; provides concrete action targets
5. **Document and follow up** — conclusions and agreements must be recorded and revisited; accountability essential for transfer

## Debrief Types

| Type | Description | Origin |
|---|---|---|
| After-Action Review (AAR) | Post-episode discussion of teamwork issues | U.S. Army |
| Guided Team Self-Correction | Trained facilitator focuses discussion; coaches members | Smith-Jentsch et al. 2008 |
| Self-Led Debrief | Team conducts debrief without external facilitator (if structured properly) | Eddy et al. 2013 |

## Relationship to Teamwork Process Framework

Debriefs primarily develop **transition processes** — teams reflect on past episodes, re-analyze their mission, revise goal specifications and strategies before the next episode. They also address action processes (coordination failures) and interpersonal processes (conflict, affect issues). See [[Teamwork Processes (Marks et al. Taxonomy)]].

## Relationship to Organizational Learning

Team debriefs are the team-level instantiation of [[Organizational Learning]] processes — specifically the *search* and *knowledge creation* phases (identifying what went wrong, generating new understanding). Guided team self-correction builds team-level metacognition. The documentation and follow-up requirements prevent knowledge loss.

## Psychological Safety as Prerequisite

[[Psychological Safety]] is a cross-cutting enabler. Without it, members suppress problem identification; errors go undiscussed; debrief becomes performative. Psychological safety is also required for effective [[Team Training]] — the two TDI types share this precondition.
