# Adaptive Leadership Theory

Nav: [[index]] | [[Leadership MOC]]

DeRue's ([[derue-2011-adaptive-leadership-complex-adaptive-process|2011]]) theory conceptualizing **leadership as a socially complex, adaptive process** of recurring leading–following interactions that co-construct leader/follower identities, relationships, and group-level structures.

> [!note] Not Heifetz's "adaptive leadership"
> This is a complex-adaptive-*systems* theory of the leading–following process. It is distinct from Heifetz & Linsky's practitioner framework of the same popular name.

## What It Rejects

Four limiting assumptions in existing theory: leadership = hierarchy; one-directional influence; leadership as a personal attribute; and the environment as exogenous. (See [[derue-2011-adaptive-leadership-complex-adaptive-process|the source]] for detail.)

## Core Mechanism

The atomic unit is the [[Leading-Following Double Interact]]. Repeated double interacts feed [[Leadership Identity Construction|claiming and granting]] of leader/follower identities, which aggregate into group-level leadership structures. Per **requisite variety** (Ashby 1956), a group's adaptive capacity rises with the *variability* of its leading–following pattern.

## Four Structural Patterns

Characterized by **magnitude** (density of reciprocated double interacts) and **dispersion** (centralization + centrality variance over time):

| Pattern | Magnitude | Centralization | Centrality variance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Centralized | Low | High | Low |
| Distributed (sequential rotation) | Low | Low | High |
| [[Shared Leadership\|Shared]] (concurrent) | High | Low | Low |
| Leadership void | Low | Low | Low |

A key contribution is separating **distributed** (sequential) from **shared** (concurrent) leadership, and insisting shared-leadership research model dispersion, not just magnitude.

## Significance

[[zhu-et-al-2018-shared-leadership-review|Zhu et al. (2018)]] cite adaptive leadership theory as a leading candidate for the unifying, contextually-grounded, group-level theory of leadership emergence the field lacks.
