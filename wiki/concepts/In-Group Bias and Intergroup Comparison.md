# In-Group Bias and Intergroup Comparison

Because social identities are maintained by **intergroup comparison** and people seek positive distinctiveness to enhance self-esteem, groups are biased toward perceiving — even provoking — favorable differences between themselves and reference groups.

## Evidence

- Groups sacrifice **larger absolute gains** for smaller gains that establish a *positive difference* over an out-group (Brewer & Silver 1978; Turner et al. 1979).
- Members adopt **more extreme positions** after out-group comparison; selectively recall information suggesting intergroup *differences* over similarities (Wilder; Wilder & Allen 1978).
- Bias is exacerbated when the in-group is made salient — e.g., by threat to the group's domain/resources, or where the group's identity is **insecure** (Tajfel 1978).

## In Organizations (ideographic case)

Biased comparison among subunits yields: negative out-group stereotypes + depersonalization; self-serving/group-serving attribution biases; justification of social distance and out-group subordination (Perrow 1970 — functional subunits less self-critical, claim more power; Bates & White 1961 hospital authority study). Conflict can erupt **without objective causes** (e.g., scarce resources): "hostility erupts more readily between [groups] than between individuals" (Horwitz & Rabbie 1982). The more *comparable* the out-group, the greater the bias (Turner 1978).

## Status Asymmetry

A **low-status** group is threatened by a high-status one and goes to great lengths to differentiate (defensive bias). The **high-status** group feels less threatened, may be indifferent — and that indifference is itself the *greatest threat* to the low-status group's identity, which then remains socially unvalidated.

## Qualifications (conflict-mitigating)

1. Groups restrict comparisons to **similar/proximal/salient** out-groups (purchasing ignores shipping).
2. Comparison on **different/complementary dimensions** enables mutual appreciation (Mummendey & Schreiber 1984: in-group favoritism on important dimensions, out-group favoritism on unimportant ones).
3. Ethnocentrism drops when the resource distribution / comparison outcome is seen as **legitimate or institutionalized** (Caddick 1982) — systems of authority and expertise often serve this legitimating function, lending stability.

> [!note]
> Contrasts with **realistic group conflict theory** (Campbell 1965), which sees conflict as competition over rewards *external* to the intergroup situation. SIT locates much conflict in the mere existence of groups.
