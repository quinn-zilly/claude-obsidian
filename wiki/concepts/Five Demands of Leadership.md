# Five Demands of Leadership

Nav: [[index]] | [[Learning & Development MOC]]

McCall (2010) reduced thousands of experience-derived leadership lessons (sorted into 32 categories) to **five demands that every leader faces**, regardless of style. Leadership is defined as *"creating a context in which other people can reach their full potential in serving the organization's mission,"* and that context is created by how a person addresses these five demands:

1. **Setting and communicating direction** — establishing purpose, vision, mission, and an architecture (structure, processes, rewards, HR practices) consistent with it.
2. **Aligning critical constituencies** — via authority, persuasion, or negotiation, ensuring the people/groups needed for the mission understand and are aligned with it (and obstacles are dealt with).
3. **Developing an executive temperament** — the ability and confidence to cope with the pressures, ambiguity, complexity, and frustrations of a leadership role.
4. **Setting and living values** — conveying and reinforcing, through actions and words, what the organization (and the leader) stands for.
5. **Growth of self and others** — taking the actions needed to keep oneself and one's people learning, growing, and changing.

## Why it matters

The framework is deliberately **non-prescriptive about style**. The demands "can be met in remarkably different ways, but all leaders face them" — this avoids the "monotheistic search for a single style, personality, or set of abilities" (the "one best way" that repeatedly dead-ends). It reframes leadership development as **acquiring the expertise to meet these demands**, connecting to [[Deliberate Practice|deliberate-practice]] research (Ericsson): mastery of a large K/S domain, over a lifetime, requiring intentional practice, teachers at inflection points, and "playing on edge."

Contrast with **competency models** (finite lists of general attributes): the five demands describe *what leadership must accomplish* rather than a checklist of traits, and admit many different attribute-profiles achieving the same outcome.
