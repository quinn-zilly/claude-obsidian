# Organizational Commitment

**Definition (Solinger et al. 2008, adopted by Judge & Kammeyer-Mueller 2012):** An individual's psychological bond with the organization, as represented by an affective attachment to the organization, internalization of its values and goals, and a behavioral desire to put forth effort to support it.

## Three-Component Model (Meyer et al. 2002)

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Affective** | Emotional attachment, identification, and involvement with the organization |
| **Continuance** | Perceived cost of leaving; calculative ("I have to stay") |
| **Normative** | Felt obligation to remain ("I ought to stay") |

**Critique (Solinger et al. 2008):** Affective commitment is an attitude toward the *organization*; normative and continuance commitment are attitudes toward the *behavior* of staying or leaving. Mixing them confuses attitude-object types and suppresses convergent validity. Recommends replacing three-component model with a social-attitudes tripartite model: affect toward the org, cognition (identification/internalization), and action readiness (generalized support behaviors).

**Affective commitment has the highest validity** for predicting organizational behaviors like performance (Dunham et al. 1994).

## Dynamics Over Time

- Organizational commitment tends to **decline** among newcomers (Bentein et al. 2005)
- Psychological contract breach associated with negative commitment trajectory (Ng et al. 2010)
- Steeper declines in commitment → increased intention to quit and actual turnover

## Interaction with Job Satisfaction

Commitment and satisfaction have independent and complementary effects on turnover (Kammeyer-Mueller et al. 2005):
- Low org. commitment → high group absenteeism regardless of satisfaction
- High org. commitment → especially low absence among most satisfied employees (Hausknecht et al. 2008)

## Commitment and Performance

Positive commitment–performance relationship, but not strong (Wright & Bonett 2002). Moderators:
- Relationship **weakens with tenure** — less-tenured employees have stronger attitude–behavior link
- High continuance + low affective commitment → poorer performance (Sinclair et al. 2005)
- High affective commitment buffers against stress → performance decrements (Hunter & Thatcher 2007)
