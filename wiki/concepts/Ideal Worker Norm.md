# Ideal Worker Norm

The cultural expectation (Williams 2000, *Unbending Gender*) that the committed, promotable employee makes **work the central focus of life** — available, unencumbered by care responsibilities, and willing to **overwork**. Central to the work–family perspective in **[[kossek-su-wu-2017-opting-out-pushed-out|Kossek, Su & Wu (2017)]]**.

## How it produces inequality
- Employers preferentially promote **work-centric** employees; women — who are more often "dual-centric" (equally high work and family identity; Kossek & Lautsch 2012) and heavier users of flexibility — are disadvantaged.
- **Overwork** as a status signal (Cha & Weeden 2014): long hours (often unpaid) equated with commitment/performance; the "always-on" digital workplace (Kossek 2016) spreads the norm from executives to nearly all professionals.
- Conflicting role prescriptions: the "ideal mother" vs the "ideal worker/physician" (Pas et al. 2014).

Feeds directly into the **[[Flexibility Stigma]]** and the **[[Motherhood Wage Penalty]]**. Intervention: leaders modeling balanced behavior; not using "face time" as a performance proxy; shared/partial leaves to foster gender egalitarianism.
