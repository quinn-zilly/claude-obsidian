# Positivism

A form of philosophical realism adhering closely to the **hypothetico–deductive method**: systematic observation, theory, a priori hypotheses, tightly controlled experiments, inferential statistics, interpretation against theory. Goal: explanation leading to **prediction and control**. Often called the **"received view"** for its dominance in social science.

## Lineage

Rooted in the Enlightenment (Descartes, Locke) — centrality of the individual, an objectively knowable world, language (including numbers) as the conveyor of truth. As an organized philosophy, traced to Mill's *A System of Logic* (1843). Mill held that social and natural sciences share goals (laws → explanation/prediction), share the hypothetico–deductive method, that nature is uniform, and that large samples suppress idiosyncrasy to reveal general laws — an [[Etic vs Emic|etic]], [[Idiographic vs Nomothetic|nomothetic]] preference. Dominant in psychology for 150+ years.

## Stance on the five [[Philosophy of Science Parameters|parameters]]

- **Ontology**: naïve realism — one true, apprehendable, measurable reality.
- **Epistemology**: dualism + objectivism — researcher and participant independent; researcher studies without influencing or being influenced.
- **Axiology**: values have no place; researcher emotionally detached "like a chemist in a sterile lab."
- **Rhetoric**: objective, precise, "scientific."
- **Methodology**: true experiments and analog methods; manipulate one variable, hold others constant.

Together with [[Postpositivism]], positivism is the primary anchor for **quantitative research**. Contrast with [[Constructivism–Interpretivism]] and the [[Critical–Ideological Paradigm]].
