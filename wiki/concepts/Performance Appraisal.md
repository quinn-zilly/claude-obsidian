# Performance Appraisal

**Performance appraisal (PA)** is a formal, usually infrequent process in which an employee is evaluated by a judge (typically a supervisor) along a set of dimensions, assigned a score, and informed of the rating. It is the evaluation core inside the broader [[Performance Management]] process.

## The eight heyday research areas (DeNisi & Murphy 2017)
Research from ~1970–2000 clustered into eight areas:

| Area | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Scale formats | Format has only modest effect on quality; search effectively ended by [[Landy & Farr]] (1980) |
| Criteria for evaluating ratings | [[Rater Error Measures]] uncorrelated with accuracy (Murphy & Balzer 1986) |
| Rater training | Error-avoidance training can *hurt* accuracy; [[Frame-of-Reference Training]] works |
| Reactions to appraisal | Justice/fairness perceptions matter; participation predicts reactions |
| Purpose of rating | Multiple, conflicting purposes; rater goals shape ratings |
| Rating sources | Foundation for [[360-Degree Feedback]]; sources differ systematically |
| Demographic effects | Lab overstates bias; field effects small ("a success story") |
| Cognitive processes | Spiked in 1980s, faded — appraisal isn't a pure judgment task |

## The historical arc of scale formats
Graphic rating scale (Paterson 1922) → ranking / paired comparisons → weighted checklists (Knauft 1948) → **critical incidents** (Flanagan 1954) → **BARS** (Smith & Kendall 1963) → mixed-standard, forced-choice, forced distribution. The lesson: no format solved subjectivity; the search still forced useful behavioral anchoring.

## The big reframe
Murphy & Cleveland (1995): appraisal is a **motivated act embedded in a social/organizational context**, not a measurement problem. This shifted the criterion from *accuracy* to *reactions, fairness, and context* — and set up the modern [[PM Effectiveness Value Chains|effectiveness]] question.

## The social context turn
Building on Murphy & Cleveland's reframe, [[pichler-2012-social-context-appraisal|Pichler (2012)]] meta-analyzed the social context and found **[[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)|rater–ratee relationship quality]] is the strongest predictor of [[Appraisal Reactions|reactions]]** (Mr ≈ .66) — beating participation and rating favorability, and supporting a [[Relational Model of Appraisal Reactions|relational model]] of the appraisal process.
