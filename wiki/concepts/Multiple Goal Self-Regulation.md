# Multiple Goal Self-Regulation

Nav: [[index]] | [[Control Theory (Self-Regulation)]] | [[Temporal Motivation Theory]]

How individuals allocate finite resources (time, attention, effort) across competing goals. The practical context for most real-world motivation — workplaces involve dozens of simultaneous demands.

## Core Resource Allocation Dynamics

**Schmidt & DeShon (2007) paradigm** — foundational two-goal lab studies:
- More time to goal with **largest discrepancy** early on
- Near deadline: shift toward goal **closest to completion**
- Incentive → more sensitivity to rewarded goal's discrepancy
- **Loss framing** → stronger allocation pull than equivalent gain frame

**Schmidt & Dolis (2009):** dual-goal expectancy (belief both goals achievable) determines strategy:
- High dual-goal expectancy → allocate to most discrepant
- Low dual-goal expectancy → allocate to most achievable by deadline

**Schmidt et al. (2009):** results replicate under high environmental volatility; when progress is actor-controlled, serial task completion emerges.

## Feedback Effects

- DeShon et al. (2004): individual feedback → individual performance; team feedback → team performance; both → intermediate on each (fundamental trade-off)
- Northcraft et al. (2011): more frequent/specific feedback → more time allocated to that task; feedback frequency is a lever for prioritization

## Expectancy and Valence

- Kernan & Lord (1990): higher valence + higher expectancy → more time
- Losses loom larger: loss-framed goals receive more time + stronger discrepancy-allocation coupling

## Affect and Multiple Goals

- Automatic: affect influences goal prioritization below conscious awareness
- Far from goal + high positive affect → persistence; near goal + high positive affect → premature disengagement (Louro et al. 2007), mediated by expectancy
- Competing goal present: positive affect → **less** activation of focal goal (Orehek et al. 2011)

## Computational Model (Vancouver, Weinhardt & Schmidt 2010)

Dynamic expectancy = (current discrepancy) / (perceived pace)
Dynamic value = discrepancy magnitude × consequence of success/failure
Selection: task with max(E × V) pursued at each moment

Integrates TMT's hyperbolic discounting with control theory dynamics. Reproduces Schmidt & DeShon patterns.

## Applied Contexts

**Speed vs. accuracy:** promotion focus → speed; prevention → accuracy (Förster et al. 2003). Deep evolutionary roots (Chittka et al. 2009).

**Safety vs. efficiency:** between-person research dominant; within-person moment-to-moment trade-offs understudied (critical gap).

**Development vs. performance:** mastery goal orientation → initial performance dip, long-term gain; performance-prove → no initial dip, no long-term gain (Ahearne et al. 2010). Development more likely when performing well, believe skills malleable, low fear of failure, low time pressure.

**Ethical decisions:** difficult goals → narrow attention + short-termism → unethical shortcuts (Ordóñez et al. 2009). Sleep deprivation → regulatory depletion → unethical behavior (Barnes et al. in press).

**Work–life conflict:** personal goals often superordinate to work goals; personal = "oughts" → shield work goals from competing activation. Multiple-goal lens underutilized in work-life research.
