# Rationalized Myth

A **rationalized myth** is an institutional rule that prescribes a particular organizational practice or structure as the rational, proper, modern way to pursue some social purpose — and is **taken for granted as legitimate independent of evidence that it actually works**. Coined by [[John W. Meyer]] and [[Brian Rowan]] in [[meyer-rowan-1977-institutionalized-organizations]].

## Two Defining Properties

1. **Rationalized and impersonal.** The myth casts a social purpose as a *technical* one and specifies, in a rulelike way, the appropriate means (e.g. "to manage people, you need a personnel department running validated selection procedures").
2. **Beyond individual discretion.** It is so highly institutionalized that no single participant or organization is free to ignore it. It must be taken for granted as legitimate *apart from* any evaluation of its impact on outcomes.

## Why "Myth"

Not "myth" in the sense of false, but in the sense of a **shared, binding belief** that organizations enact ceremonially. Professions, formal programs, and technologies (accounting methods, R&D units, credentialing systems) all function as myths: organizations adopt them to *display* rationality and responsibility, not because their efficiency has been demonstrated.

## Consequences

- Adopting the right myths confers **legitimacy, resources, and survival** (Meyer & Rowan, Proposition 3).
- Organizations in the same domain become structurally similar — the seed of [[Institutional Isomorphism]].
- Because the myths often conflict with the demands of actual work, organizations resort to [[Decoupling]] and the [[Logic of Confidence]] to keep the ceremonial structure intact while real work proceeds informally.

## Relation to Later Theory

[[Paul DiMaggio]] and [[Walter Powell]] (1983) build directly on this: their three mechanisms ([[Institutional Isomorphism|coercive, mimetic, normative]]) describe *how* rationalized myths diffuse across an [[Organizational Field]].
