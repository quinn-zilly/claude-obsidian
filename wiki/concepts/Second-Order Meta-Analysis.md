# Second-Order Meta-Analysis

Quantitative aggregation **across existing meta-analyses** rather than across primary studies (Cooper & Koenka 2012; Zell & Krizan 2014). Used when a research area is mature enough to have accumulated many first-order meta-analyses on related questions.

## Procedure (per [[zell-lesick-2022-big-five-performance-synthesis|Zell & Lesick 2022]])

1. **Exhaustive search** for relevant meta-analyses.
2. **Formal inclusion criteria** (e.g., must report effects for all constructs of interest; per-effect k/N thresholds).
3. **Extract** effect sizes + methodological/sample/measurement codes.
4. **Aggregate** — often via **unweighted averages** of meta-analytic effects (shown to predict population effects as well as or better than sample-size weighting; Bonett 2009); with sensitivity analyses weighting by N, by k, and inverse-weighting by **sample overlap**.

## Handling overlap

Because meta-analyses share primary studies, **sample overlap** (SO) is estimated for all pairs (Zell & Lesick found mean SO = 36%). Overlapping meta-analyses are still retained: each contributes a unique estimate (different samples/measures/questions), and their inclusion lets the analyst **test whether effects replicate across independently conducted meta-analyses**.

## Value

- Yields **comprehensive, high-power** estimates and enables comparison across sub-domains under equivalent conditions.
- Uniquely positioned to assess the **replicability of meta-analyses** themselves — a rarely studied question. Zell & Lesick found personality–performance estimates highly consistent across independent teams (e.g., university-GPA conscientiousness ≈ .23–.27).
- Limitation: inherits the aggregation of its inputs — it necessarily works at the level of **broad constructs**, which critics of broad-factor analysis ([[hough-oswald-ock-2015-beyond-the-big-five|Hough et al. 2015]]) argue **obscures** [[Facet-Level Personality Measurement|facet-level]] signal.
