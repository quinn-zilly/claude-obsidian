# Sources of Self-Efficacy

Nav: [[index]] | [[Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)]]

The four informational sources from which **[[Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)|self-efficacy]]** beliefs are constructed (Bandura 1997; Schunk & Usher 2012):

| Source | Description | Relative strength |
|---|---|---|
| **Mastery experience** | Interpreted results of one's own past performances — successes raise efficacy, failures lower it. | **Most influential**; one direct mastery experience can outweigh a year of verbal persuasion. |
| **Vicarious experience** | Observing similar others succeed (or fail) — see [[Social Comparison]]. Passing peers builds efficacy; being surrounded by failing peers erodes it. | Potent when models are *similar*; a vicarious gain can be negated by subsequent poor performance. |
| **Verbal/social persuasion** | Messages from credible others ("I know you can do this"). Effectiveness depends on source credibility, message valence, and frequency. | Weaker and less durable; lofty/empty praise is quickly dismissed. Best coupled with tasks that build mastery. |
| **Physiological & emotional states** | Anxiety, stress, arousal, mood interpreted as signs of (in)capability. Reducing arousal or reframing it can raise efficacy. | Context-dependent; strong negative affect can undermine efficacy. |

> [!key-insight] Sources are interpreted, not automatic
> Information does not affect efficacy automatically — individuals **interpret and weight** the sources, and people differ in which source they rely on. In one middle-school study, girls and African American students weighted **social persuasion** more heavily than boys and White students. Sources may also combine **multiplicatively**.

Source: [[schunk-usher-2012-social-cognitive-theory-motivation|Schunk & Usher (2012)]]; originally Bandura (1997).
