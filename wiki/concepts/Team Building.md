# Team Building

Nav: [[index]] | [[concepts/_index]]

A set of strategies designed to improve interpersonal relations and social interactions in *existing* teams, also targeting goal achievement and task accomplishment. Used to address problems already occurring in teams (Klein et al. 2009).

## Four Approaches (Buller & Bell 1986)

1. **Goal setting** — individual + team-level goals; strongest effect on outcomes
2. **Interpersonal relationships** — trust development, conflict resolution
3. **Role clarification** — communication about role specification; second strongest
4. **Problem solving** — identifying solutions to task-related problems

All four show moderate effects; goal setting and role clarification strongest (Klein et al. 2009 meta-analysis, 20 studies).

## Meta-Analytic Effects

| Outcome | ρ | % Variance |
|---|---|---|
| Affective | **0.44** | 19.36% |
| Process | 0.44 | 19.36% |
| Performance | 0.26 | 6.76% |
| Cognitive | 0.13 | 1.69% |
| Combined | 0.31 | 9.61% |

**Strongest for affective and process outcomes.** Weakest for cognitive outcomes — team building does not build shared mental models or transactive memory.

## When to Use

- Team members lack cohesion or trust
- Role ambiguity is causing conflict or inefficiency
- Team has performance problems rooted in interpersonal dynamics
- Team needs to align on goals
- Task interdependence is **low** (high interdependence → prefer [[Team Training]])

## Caveats

- Requires team member willingness to diagnose own problems; outside facilitation needed but insufficient alone
- Most applicable to intact teams with prior shared experience
- Frequently misused as a trivial "fun activity" → no empirical effect without proper design
- Does not prepare teams for future challenges — reactive, not proactive

## Mechanism

Goal-setting component works via [[Goal-Setting Theory]]: specific + challenging goals → ↑ motivation + ↓ conflict. Role clarification affects individual motivation by setting clear purpose.

## Trust Meta-Analytic Evidence

Trust enables team members to overcome uncertainty and accept vulnerability → better coordination and performance. Meta-analytically demonstrated across field and lab samples (De Jong, Dirks & Gillespie 2016). Virtually all teams experience conflict; team building provides constructive conflict management techniques (targeting content not persons; discussing work assignment rationale; assigning by expertise — Behfar et al. 2008).

## Role Clarity ≠ Role Rigidity

Role clarity = shared understanding of how roles should be performed, including adaptive switching. Teams can have role clarity while requiring flexibility. Role clarity decreases confusion, facilitates backup behavior, and yields more effective outcomes (Eys & Carron 2001). Lacerenza et al. (2018) emphasize: during team building, teams can identify which situations require role switching — building clarity for adaptive contexts.
