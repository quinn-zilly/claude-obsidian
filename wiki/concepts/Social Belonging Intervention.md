# Social Belonging Intervention

A brief [[Wise Interventions|wise intervention]] that gives students an **alternative narrative** for negative experiences: worries about belonging are *normal* in the transition to a new school and *dissipate with time* — rather than evidence that "people like me don't belong here." Flagship study: Walton & Cohen (2011), reviewed in [[Walton-2014-Wise-Psychological-Interventions|Walton (2014)]].

## Mechanism

The 1-hour reading-and-writing exercise (often using a [[Saying-Is-Believing]] component) changes **social construal**: intervention students no longer read daily slights as portending a *global* lack of belonging. Daily-diary data showed this change in construal **mediated** the downstream academic gain. Once everyday encounters feel less threatening, students interact more positively, build better relationships, and get embedded in supportive networks — a [[Recursive Processes (Wise Interventions)|recursive]] loop that carries the effect forward.

## Results

- African American college students earned **higher grades over the next 3 years**, halving the racial-achievement gap, and reported being happier and healthier.
- A belonging intervention helped women in male-dominated engineering majors form more friendships with male engineers (Walton, Logel, Peach, Spencer & Zanna 2013), placing them in a genuinely different social situation.

Because effects run through relationship formation, effectiveness **depends on the context affording** those ties. Closely related to [[Stereotype Threat]].
