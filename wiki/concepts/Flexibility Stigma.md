# Flexibility Stigma

The career penalty attached to **using work–family flexibility policies** — reduced hours, flextime, leave, telework (Williams, Blair-Loy & Berdahl 2013). A key work–family mechanism in **[[kossek-su-wu-2017-opting-out-pushed-out|Kossek, Su & Wu (2017)]]**.

## Dynamics
- Because women use family-friendly policies more, they are **more likely to be stigmatized and penalized** — lower pay, skipped promotions, higher turnover.
- Attributions matter: flexibility used "for work" is rated more positively than the same flexibility "for family" (Leslie et al. 2012). Supervisors perceive women as having more family–work conflict and as **less promotable** (Hoobler et al. 2009).
- Formal policy availability ≠ safe use: telework did not reduce conflict or improve well-being in a quasi-experiment (Kossek, Lautsch & Eaton 2006); most reduced-load professionals (~90%) are women.

> [!tip] Men are stigmatized too
> **[[shockley-et-al-2017-gender-work-family-conflict|Shockley et al. (2017)]]** note men feel *more* stigmatized than women for using flexibility (Vandello et al. 2013) — flexibility stigma is a two-sided enforcement of the **[[Ideal Worker Norm]]**, not solely a women's penalty.

Connected to the **[[Motherhood Wage Penalty]]**; intervention requires removing the stigma, not just adding policies.
