# After-Event Reviews (AERs)

Organizational learning procedure giving individuals (or teams) a structured opportunity to analyze their performance and evaluate causal contributions to outcomes. AERs shift information processing from automatic to conscious mode — forcing deliberate hypothesis generation and validation about past behavior.

## Three Functions (Ellis & Davidi 2005)

1. **Self-explanation** — generate causal explanations for actions; builds if–then rules; integrates new knowledge with existing schemas
2. **Data verification** — cross-validate perceptions; counter confirmation bias and hindsight bias
3. **Feedback** — process-focused (not just outcome); cognitive/task/functional validity feedback types (Balzer et al. 1989)

## Failure-Only vs. Failure+Success AERs

Traditional AERs focus on failures because failures naturally trigger attributional search (Weiner 1985). Ellis & Davidi (2005) showed:

- Before AER intervention: failure mental models are richer (more constructs, more causal links) than success mental models
- After **FSAER** (failure + success review): success mental models catch up and eventually exceed failure models in richness
- FSAER group showed significantly greater performance improvement than FAER group despite starting lower

**Mechanism:** Reviewing successes forces examination of internal root causes (planning, knowledge, studying) rather than leaving successes explained by situational factors. This produces more generalizable mental models.

## AER vs. Feedback

AER is a structured *organizational procedure* for guided self-explanation. Feedback (from an external authority) conveys already-elaborated information. Webb (1989): giving elaborate explanations → stronger achievement correlations than receiving them. AER produces the giving, not just the receiving.

## Design Principles

- Balance failure and success review
- Focus on decision-making points (not just outcomes)
- Ask "why" questions about *both* what worked and what didn't
- Individual (one-on-one) or group formats viable
- ~15 min per individual review sufficient (Ellis & Davidi 2005)
- Commander/trainer preparation workshop required to shift default failure-focus habits

## Connections

**Transfer:** AERs function as a posttraining mechanism for transfer support. Burke & Hutchins (2007) identify feedback and practice as strong transfer predictors. AER provides structured, process-focused feedback.

**Mental models:** Richer mental models → better performance (Carley 1997; Neisser 1976). AER directly increases model richness — especially for successes which are under-analyzed by default.

**High-reliability orgs:** AERs allow organizations to detect when routines *stop* working without requiring engineered failures — relevant to nuclear, pharma, aviation, military.
