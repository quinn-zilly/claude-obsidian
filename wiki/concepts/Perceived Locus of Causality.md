# Perceived Locus of Causality (PLOC)

The subjective sense of whether the origin of one's behavior is internal (self) or external (environment/other). Introduced by de Charms (1968); central to SDT's account of autonomous vs. controlled motivation.

## Internal vs. External PLOC

**Internal PLOC**: person experiences self as the origin of behavior; acts from choice, interest, or values. Associated with intrinsic motivation and identified/integrated regulation.

**External PLOC**: person experiences self as a pawn of external forces; acts because of rewards, threats, or social pressure. Associated with external and introjected regulation.

## Role in SDT

PLOC is the phenomenological correlate of the regulatory styles in [[Organismic Integration Theory]]:
- Intrinsic motivation → internal PLOC
- Integrated/identified regulation → internal PLOC
- Introjected regulation → somewhat external PLOC
- External regulation → external PLOC
- Amotivation → impersonal (no perceived causality)

## Distinction from Locus of Control

Locus of control (Rotter 1966) = expectancy that outcomes are controllable. PLOC = whether self is the origin of *behavior*, regardless of outcome control. A person can have internal PLOC (self as origin) but external locus of control (outcomes uncontrollable) — e.g., an artist who paints from intrinsic motivation but cannot control market reception.

[[Causality Orientations Theory]] describes stable individual differences in PLOC tendencies.
