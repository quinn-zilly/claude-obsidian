# Wise Interventions

A class of psychological interventions that are **ordinary, brief, and precise**: they aim to alter *one specific way people think or feel* in the normal course of their lives, rather than delivering dramatic or multifaceted programs. Named and synthesized by [[Gregory Walton]] in [[Walton-2014-Wise-Psychological-Interventions|Walton (2014)]].

## Etymology

*Wise* comes from 1950s gay culture (Goffman, 1963): straight people who understood the full humanity of gay people. [[Claude Steele]] (1997) adopted it for "wise schooling" that defuses [[Stereotype Threat]]. An intervention is *wise* when it is attuned to the precise underlying psychological process that harms outcomes.

## Three defining properties

1. **Psychologically precise** — grounded in a specific, well-founded theory, instantiated in a brief exercise. Exports lab precision to the field ("nothing so practical as a good theory," Lewin). Inaccurate theory misfires.
2. **Targets [[Recursive Processes (Wise Interventions)|recursive processes]]** — changes a "movie," not a "snapshot"; effects can grow over time, largest at the most distal measurement.
3. **Context-dependent, not silver bullets** — effective only if the targeted process matters in that setting, the exercise actually changes it, and the context sustains the recursive gain.

## Direct vs. indirect

Interventions that change psychology **directly** (reading/writing exercises) tend to be more durable than those that rely on a **new external experience** (a program, controlled visits) — the latter are fragile if the experience is withdrawn (Schulz & Hanusa 1978 nursing-home reversal).

## Relation to nudges

Related to but distinct from Thaler & Sunstein "nudges": wise interventions specifically target a *psychological process / construal*, aiming for recursive, self-sustaining change rather than a one-time choice-architecture push.
