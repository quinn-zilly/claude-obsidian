# Representativeness Heuristic

One of the three core heuristics identified by [[Tversky and Kahneman (1974)|tversky-kahneman-1974-heuristics-biases]].

## Definition

Probability is assessed by the degree to which A *resembles* B — the degree to which A is representative of (or similar to) B's stereotype or prototype. High similarity → high judged probability; low similarity → low judged probability.

**Canonical question type:** "What is the probability that object A belongs to class B?" or "What is the probability that event A originated from process B?"

## Why It Works (Ecological Validity)

Resemblance *is* correlated with class membership in many domains. A bird that looks like a robin probably is a robin. The error is treating resemblance as sufficient for probability assessment while ignoring other relevant factors.

## Biases Produced

**[[Base Rate Neglect]]** — Prior probabilities (base rates) are ignored when descriptive information is present. The engineer/lawyer experiment: groups told 70% engineers vs. 30% engineers gave nearly identical probability judgments for the same personality sketch. Even an *uninformative* description (Dick, 30, well-liked, high ability) wiped out base-rate sensitivity — subjects judged P(engineer) = .5 regardless of whether base rate was .7 or .3.

**Insensitivity to sample size** — The probability of a sample statistic is judged independent of N, because the statistic's resemblance to the population parameter doesn't change with sample size. Hospital study: most subjects said large and small hospital record equally many days with >60% boy births (correct: small hospital has far more, due to higher variance with small N). Research implication: over-trust in small-sample results; inadequate sample sizes; overinterpretation of findings.

**Law of Small Numbers** — Even experienced research psychologists believe small samples are highly representative of their populations; they overestimate replicability of small-sample results (Tversky & Kahneman, 1971).

**Misconceptions of chance** — People expect random sequences to look locally random (alternating, balanced). H-T-H-T-T-H is judged more likely than H-H-H-T-T-T (not random-looking) and H-H-H-H-T-H (not fair-looking). Leads to [[Gambler's Fallacy]].

**Insensitivity to predictability** — Predictions are made with the same extremeness as input favorability evaluations, regardless of the reliability of the predictor. Student teacher evaluation study: predictions for teaching success 5 years later were as extreme as quality ratings of a single practice lesson.

**[[Illusion of Validity]]** — High confidence produced by good match between predicted outcome and input, even when the judge knows the predictor is unreliable. Clinical interviewers confident in selection predictions despite knowing interview validity is low. Internal consistency of inputs increases confidence but *decreases* accuracy (redundant inputs add noise, not information).

**Misconceptions of [[Regression to the Mean]]** — Because the predicted outcome "should" be as extreme as the input (by representativeness), regression is not expected and is misattributed causally when it occurs. Flight instructor fallacy: praise → worse next performance (regression); punishment → better next performance (regression) → false conclusion that punishment is more effective.

## Connection to [[Correlation-Intervention Gap (Behaviour)]]

The [[Correlation-Intervention Gap (Behaviour)|Albarracín et al. 2024]] finding — that correlational effect sizes systematically exceed intervention effect sizes — is structurally related to representativeness: researchers selecting intervention targets based on correlational salience are implicitly applying a representativeness heuristic (high correlation → target "looks like" a good intervention lever).
