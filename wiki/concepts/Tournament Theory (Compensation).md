# Tournament Theory (Compensation)

Economic theory (Lazear & Rosen origins) frequently used to predict that **higher [[Pay Dispersion|pay dispersion]] → higher performance**. Prizes (promotions, bonuses) are allocated by *relative* rank, so wide, convex pay spreads serve two functions:

1. **Motivation / effort** — larger prize gaps raise the incentive to compete, especially in sequential-elimination tournaments and in later rounds (Ehrenberg & Bognanno's professional-golf studies).
2. **[[Sorting Effect (Compensation)|Sorting]]** — because there are more "losers" than "winners," dispersion sorts the workforce: winners are retained, losers exit. This links tournament theory directly to **turnover** predictions.

## Evidence & limits (per [[shaw-2014-pay-dispersion|Shaw 2014]])
- Heavily tested with **sports samples** (golf, auto racing, baseball, hockey, basketball, football, soccer, tennis) — convenient because individual performance is measurable.
- An "atypically unmeasured assumption" is that the dispersion is genuinely **performance-based**; when residual (unexplained) dispersion is isolated, tournament predictions weaken.
- A ceiling effect: positive effects of explained variation on performance can flatten at high levels (Trevor et al. 2012).

Complements — rather than competes with — [[Distributive Justice|equity theory]] once dispersion is decomposed into [[Explained vs Unexplained Pay Dispersion|explained vs unexplained]] components.
