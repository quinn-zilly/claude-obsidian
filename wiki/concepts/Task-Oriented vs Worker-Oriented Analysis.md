# Task-Oriented vs Worker-Oriented Analysis

The classic dilemma in work-analysis orientation (Gael 1988; Brannick & Levine 2002).

## Task-Oriented
Duties, functions, tasks are the basis. Jobs treated as **independent of the people** who perform them; emphasis on the relatively stable org structure. Worker attributes not of interest.
- **Methods**: Time & Motion Study, **Functional Job Analysis** (Fine 1988 → feeds O*NET), Task Inventory / CODAP, Work Performance Survey System, **Critical Incident Technique** (Flanagan 1954 — for high money/consequence decisions).

## Worker-Oriented
Individual characteristics required for adequate performance. Two subtypes:
- **Traits** — stable (aptitudes, personality)
- **Competencies** — trainable behavioral core characteristics leading to success (Boyatzis 1982; McClelland 1973)

Mostly KSAOs. Growing consensus around the competency model shrank worker-trait research. **Competence Modelling** ties to org goals/mission, applied across the org, not necessarily linked to specific tasks — contrast with [[Traditional Job Analysis]] and see [[TJA vs CM (Six Dimensions)]].

## Verdict
Both orientations are necessary; choice depends on purpose. For **personnel selection**, worker orientation is unavoidable, but the two are usually complementary (García-Izquierdo et al. 2015).
