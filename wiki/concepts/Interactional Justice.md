# Interactional Justice

The perceived fairness of the **interpersonal treatment** people receive from authority figures during the enactment of decision-making procedures. Introduced by **Bies & Moag (1986)**; later split by **Greenberg (1993)** into two facets, now standard in the [[Four Justice Dimensions|four-factor model]] (Colquitt 2001).

## Two facets

- **Interpersonal justice** — treating people with **dignity, courtesy, and respect**; propriety (no rude/cruel remarks). The "social side of [[Distributive Justice|distributive justice]]."
- **Informational justice** — providing **truthful, adequate explanations and justifications** for decisions. The "social side of [[Procedural Justice|procedural justice]]."

Bies & Moag's four norms: respect, propriety, truthfulness, justification.

## Why it matters

- Emphasizes **one-on-one transactions**, so employees seek it mainly from **supervisors** — a lever managers directly control (Skarlicki & Latham 1996: training leaders to behave more justly raised subordinate OCB three months later).
- **Buffers procedural injustice**: interactional justice can attenuate the negative effects of an unfair process (part of the "three bites at the apple" logic in [[cropanzano-bowen-gilliland-2007-management-organizational-justice|Cropanzano et al. 2007]]).
- Practical stakes: apologies + supportive treatment during pay cuts / layoffs sharply reduce theft, turnover, and litigation (Greenberg 1993; Lind et al. 2000).

## Debates

- **Three-factor vs. four-factor** — some retain a combined "interactional justice" (interpersonal + informational) as one dimension (e.g., Blader & Tyler 2009); the four-factor split is more common.
- Whether interactional justice is truly distinct from [[Procedural Justice|procedural justice]] or a social aspect of it (Tyler & Bies agent–system model; resolved largely in favor of distinctness).
