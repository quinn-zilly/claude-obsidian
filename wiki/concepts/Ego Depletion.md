# Ego Depletion

Nav: [[index]] | [[Resource Allocation Theory (Motivation)]]

Theory (Baumeister, Bratslavsky, Muraven & Tice 1998) proposing that individuals have a **finite self-regulatory resource** that depletes through sustained acts of self-control. Once depleted, self-regulation fails.

## Core Claims

- Self-control is a limited resource (analogized to muscle)
- Sustained exertion of self-control depletes the resource
- Post-depletion: task + citizenship + voice behaviors decline; deviant + unethical behaviors rise
- Recovery: positive social events, autonomy, workday respites, off-job mastery, sleep, vacations

## Depleting Activities at Work

- Vigilant monitoring for problems (Lin & Johnson 2015)
- Suppressing/faking emotional displays (Trougakos et al. 2015)
- Acting consistent with procedural justice rules (Johnson, Lanaj & Barnes 2014)

## Criticisms and Replication Status

> [!contradiction] Replication challenges
> Carter et al. (2015) meta-analysis: self-control does not reliably rely on a limited resource; lab-based ego depletion effects challenged. Inzlicht & Schmeichel (2012): alternative explanations include conscious shifts to reward-seeking, implicit willpower beliefs (Job, Dweck & Walton 2010), and moral licensing. Basic tenets of the resource model under pressure.

**Not all self-regulation is depleting:** Johnson et al. (2015) — procedural justice rules = depleting; interpersonal justice rules = not depleting. Culturally entrained behaviors may have near-zero resource demands.

## Relation to Motivation Theory

Part of the Process segment of [[Work Motivation Meta-Framework (Kanfer)]]. Extends [[Resource Allocation Theory (Motivation)]] by specifying depletion as a dynamic constraint on resource availability over the work day/session. Future directions: what resources actually are; physiological basis; when depletion effects occur.
