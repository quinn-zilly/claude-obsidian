# Appraisal-Tendency Framework (ATF)

Lerner & Keltner (2000, 2001). Connects cognitive-appraisal theories of emotion to judgment and decision making. Explains how specific emotions — not just positive/negative valence — have distinct, predictable effects on decisions.

## Core Premise

Emotions = adaptive responses to eliciting events. Each emotion involves:
1. **Appraisal dimensions** (certainty, control, pleasantness, etc.)
2. **Core meaning/theme** (what harm or benefit is at stake)
3. **Action tendencies** (motivations toward specific behaviors)

These appraisal tendencies persist beyond the eliciting event and shape subsequent decisions, even for unrelated objects.

## Integral vs. Incidental Emotions

**Integral**: directly connected to decision task; may have normative basis (e.g., anticipated regret about vaccination decision).

**Incidental**: result of events outside the decision; no normative relevance. But carry over anyway.

Example: Academic achievements weighted more heavily in admissions on cloudy days (negative mood) than sunny days (Simonsohn 2007). No normative justification for this.

## Key Findings

| Emotion | Appraisal | Decision Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Fear | High uncertainty, low control | Risk averse; prefer low-risk/low-reward (Raghunathan & Pham 1999) |
| Anger | High certainty, high individual control | Risk seeking; perceive less risk (Lerner & Keltner 2001) |
| Sadness | Situational cause | Prefer high-risk/high-reward (Raghunathan & Pham 1999) |
| Disgust | — | Eliminates endowment effect (Lerner, Small & Loewenstein 2004) |
| Guilt | — | More cooperative behavior (DeSteno et al. 2010) |
| Envy | — | More deceptive in negotiation (Moran & Schweitzer 2008) |

Fear vs. anger reversal: for *chance-based* risks, fearful > angry in risk aversion; but for risks from *uncertain choices of others*, effect reverses (Kugler, Connolly & Ordóñez 2012).

## Roles of Affect in Judgment (Peters et al. 2006)

1. **Information** (Schwarz & Clore 2003): feelings consulted as valid input ("how do I feel about this?")
2. **Common currency**: affective responses enable comparison across incommensurable options
3. **Spotlight**: focuses attention on particular attributes
4. **Motivation**: action propensities (Lazarus 1991; Lerner & Keltner 2000)

## Earlier Work (General Valence)

Pre-ATF: negative affect → pessimistic expectations, more analytic processing; positive → optimistic, more heuristic use (Forgas 2003; Johnson & Tversky 1983). ATF shows this valence-only view is too coarse — two negative emotions (fear vs. anger) have opposite risk effects.
