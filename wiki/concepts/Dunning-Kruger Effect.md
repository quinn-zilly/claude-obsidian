# Dunning-Kruger Effect

Nav: [[index]] | [[Calibration (Judgment)]]

The unskilled suffer a **dual burden**: they make errors *and* their incompetence deprives them of the **metacognitive skill to recognize those errors**. Because the abilities that produce a correct answer largely overlap with the abilities needed to *evaluate* an answer, "to lack the former is to be deficient in the latter" (Kruger & Dunning 1999).

## The core empirical pattern

- Bottom-quartile performers place themselves near the **62nd percentile** while actually scoring around the **12th** — a large overestimate.
- **Top-quartile performers slightly underestimate** their relative standing (a *false-consensus* error — assuming their competence is common), not a metacognitive deficit.
- The bottom quartile **fails to recalibrate** even when shown superior peers' work; the top quartile recalibrates.
- **The fix is competence**: training the skill improves calibration (their Study 4).

> [!key-insight] Two asymmetric errors, one curve
> The low end's overconfidence is an **error about the self** (metacognitive deficit); the high end's underconfidence is an **error about others** (false consensus). Both feed the [[Above-Average Effect]].

## Common misreadings

> [!warning] What it is *not*
> The effect is *not* "dumb people think they're geniuses." Everyone's *absolute* self-estimate rises modestly with skill; it is the **gap between perceived and actual** that shrinks with competence. The bottom quartile still rates itself above average, but the striking claim is the *lack of insight*, not a belief in supremacy. (Later methodological debates — e.g., regression-to-the-mean and better-than-average artifacts — are noted in the literature; Study 4's causal training manipulation was designed partly to rule out the pure regression account.)
