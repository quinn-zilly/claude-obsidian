# Relative Weights Analysis

A variance-partitioning technique for estimating each predictor's proportional contribution to a model's R² when predictors are correlated. Because correlated predictors make raw regression coefficients hard to interpret, relative weights (Johnson 2000; Tonidandel & LeBreton 2011) assign each predictor a share of explained variance that accounts for both its direct effect and its effect when combined with the others. The weights sum to the model R², so each predictor's percentage of R² is its weight ÷ total R².

## Why It's Used

- **Ranking correlated predictors** — e.g., which leader trait or behavior matters most for a given criterion.
- **A fairer footing than incremental validity** — instead of forcing a new construct to clear a ΔR² bar after an incumbent is entered, relative weights split the shared variance between them. This is why it can make a construct look competitive even when incremental validity is negligible.

## In the Leadership Literature

- [[derue-et-al-2011-trait-behavioral-leadership-integration|DeRue et al. (2011)]] use Johnson's epsilon to compute each trait's and behavior's % of explained R² across four effectiveness criteria.
- [[hoch-et-al-2018-ethical-authentic-servant-vs-transformational|Hoch et al. (2018)]] use relative weights as the lenient complement to incremental-validity tests — servant/ethical/authentic averaged 54–58% relative weight vs. transformational, even though authentic and ethical add almost no incremental variance. Illustrates why both lenses matter for [[Construct Redundancy (Leadership)]].
