# Glass Cliff

## Definition

The glass cliff is the phenomenon whereby minority individuals (women, racial/ethnic minorities) are disproportionately appointed to leadership positions that are precarious, high-risk, or set up for likely failure — particularly during organizational crises or periods of poor performance.

Coined by Ryan & Haslam (2005, 2007) following analysis of FTSE 100 company board appointments; they found that companies whose stock had declined in the previous months were more likely to appoint a woman to their board than companies with stable or rising stock.

## Mechanism

Glass cliff appointments arise from several dynamics:
1. **Crisis context reduces majority-group competition:** when a role is visibly risky, majority candidates are less willing to pursue or accept it; minority candidates (already fighting for access) may have fewer alternatives
2. **Tokenism logic inverted:** crisis framing creates a logic of "we need someone different" — minority candidates become attractive precisely because of outsider status during crisis
3. **Savior framing:** "the only one who can save us" attribution sets up minority leaders for disproportionate blame when turnarounds fail (as most do)
4. **Hyperscrutiny amplification:** minority leaders on glass cliffs face compounded [[Hypervisibility]] — normal leadership visibility + crisis spotlight + minority status

## Savior Effect

A variant of the glass cliff in which a minority executive is explicitly framed as the organization's "savior" — implicitly or explicitly. This framing is particularly common for racial minority CEOs appointed to distressed firms. The savior attribution creates a performance attribution trap: if the organization recovers, the recovery is attributed to structural factors; if it fails, it is attributed to the person.

## Connection to Developmental Experiences

[[Developmental Experiences]] like turnarounds are among the most powerful OJD catalysts (Yost & Plunkett, 2010; McCall et al., 1988). The glass cliff is the dark-side version: minority leaders are appointed to turnarounds not to develop them, but because majority candidates avoided the risk. The experience has OJD potential but without organizational support, the failure risk is real and the career penalty is disproportionate.

## Empirical Pattern

- Women appointed to boards after stock declines (Ryan & Haslam, 2005)
- Women appointed as Fortune 500 CEOs more often in distressed firms (Cook & Glass, 2014)
- Racial minority executives appointed to struggling firms with shorter tenures
- Glass cliff dynamics documented cross-culturally and in political contexts (women candidates for unwinnable seats)

## Related Phenomena

- **Glass ceiling** — blocked vertical advancement for minority employees; glass cliff is what happens when ceiling is nominally broken
- **[[Hypervisibility]]** — compounded scrutiny on glass cliff leaders
- **Managed visibility** — impossible under glass cliff conditions; all eyes on the crisis
