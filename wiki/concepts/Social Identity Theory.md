# Social Identity Theory

A social-psychological theory developed principally by **Henri Tajfel** (1978, 1981) and **John Turner** (1975, 1982, 1984, 1985). SIT holds that people classify themselves and others into social categories (organizational membership, religion, gender, age cohort), and that part of the self-concept — the **social identity** — derives from these category memberships.

## Two Functions of Social Classification

1. **Cognitive segmentation** — it orders the social environment and gives a systematic means of defining others (a person is assigned the prototypical characteristics of their category; cf. stereotypes, not always reliable).
2. **Self-location** — it lets the individual locate/define themselves in the social environment. This second function is **[[Social Identification|social identification]]**, the focus of [[ashforth-mael-1989-social-identity-theory-organization|Ashforth & Mael's]] organizational application.

## Self-Concept Structure

The self-concept comprises:

- **Personal identity** — idiosyncratic attributes (body, abilities, traits, interests).
- **Social identity** — salient group classifications.

Definitions of self and others are "relational and comparative" (Tajfel & Turner 1985): *young* is meaningful only against *old*. Social identities are maintained primarily by **intergroup comparison**, and people seek positive differences to enhance self-esteem.

## Core Premises

- Categories are defined by **prototypical characteristics** abstracted from members.
- Identification can occur from **mere categorization** alone (see [[Minimal Group Paradigm]]) — interaction, similarity, and cohesion are facilitating, not necessary.
- Self-esteem motive: people identify partly to enhance self-esteem; positive/negative intergroup comparisons move self-esteem accordingly.
- "Social identity is the cognitive mechanism which makes group behaviour possible" (Turner 1982).

## Significance

SIT reframes the **psychological group** as more than aggregated interpersonal ties (see [[Psychological Group]]). [[ashforth-mael-1989-social-identity-theory-organization|Ashforth & Mael (1989)]] applied SIT to organizational socialization, role conflict, and intergroup relations, and used it to disentangle organizational identification from [[Organizational Commitment|commitment]] and internalization.
