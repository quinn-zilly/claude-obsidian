# Readability Tests for Survey Items

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]]

## The Problem

- Average US adult reads at ~8th grade level; 20% below 5th grade (Doak et al. 1998)
- Psychologists hold doctorates — poor judges of difficulty for non-expert respondents
- Social science samples are often university students — higher reading level than general population, masking items that would confuse broader samples

## Recommended Test: Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level

Jebb et al. (2021) recommend the **Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level Studies** test because it is:
- Among the most widely used
- Expressed in US grade levels (intuitive)
- Built into Microsoft Word (trivial to implement)

**Target:** ≤8.0 per item.

**Critical rule:** Test items *individually*, not as a whole scale. Averaging across items hides single difficult items.

## Other Available Tests

| Test | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Flesch Reading Ease | Inverse score (higher = easier) |
| Gunning Fog Index | Grade level estimate |
| SMOG Index | Grade level; focuses on polysyllabic words |
| Automated Readability Index | Grade level |
| Coleman-Liau Index | Grade level; uses characters not syllables |
| Coh-Metrix | Syntactic simplicity score; clauses, conditionals, negations |
| QUAID (Question Understanding Aid) | Survey-specific; flags vague wording, jargon, working memory overload |

Coh-Metrix and QUAID (Peter et al. 2018) are freely available online and supplement traditional tests.

## Respondent Miscomprehension (Hardy & Ford 2014)

Even readable items can be misunderstood. Two sources: (a) specific words with multiple/vague meanings, (b) sentence-level ambiguity. Remedies: reduce vague phrases, collect qualitative data on how respondents interpret items.

## Key Papers
- Calderón et al. (2006) — key readability reference cited by Jebb et al.
- Peter et al. (2018) — Coh-Metrix and QUAID
