# Control Theory (Self-Regulation)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Multiple Goal Self-Regulation]] | [[Goal-Setting Theory]]

Carver & Scheier (1998); Powers (1973); Lord & Levy (1994). Cybernetic model of goal pursuit. The dominant framework for understanding *goal striving* (as opposed to GST's focus on *goal setting*).

## Core Loop

**Goal level** (desired state) → compare to **input** (perceived current state) → if discrepancy: **action** → reassess → repeat

Feedback = critical; aligns perceived state with actual state. Problems: vague, delayed, inaccurate, infrequent, untrusted feedback.

## Velocity / Rate of Progress

Carver & Scheier: *rate* of discrepancy reduction matters, not just magnitude.

- Positive velocity (improving) → positive affect
- Negative velocity (worsening) → negative affect
- Chang et al. (2010): velocity predicts job satisfaction above and beyond discrepancy magnitude
- High velocity compensates for large discrepancies; low velocity + large discrepancy → low commitment + low expectations

## Goal Hierarchies

Goals nested in means–ends chains. Superordinate goals:
1. Set standards for subordinate goals
2. Amplify reactions to subordinate discrepancies
3. Activate means (spreading activation) while inhibiting competitors

Higher-order goal discrepancy → stronger reactions to lower-level discrepancies (Hyland 1988).

## External Disturbances

Progress partly determined by factors outside actor's control ("disturbances"). Stewart & Nandkeolyar (2006): referral volume → salesperson performance above own behaviors. Critical for realistic modeling of organizational performance.

## Nonconscious Operation

Lord & Levy (1994): massively parallel monitoring required; conscious processing alone would overburden attention. Discrepancies can capture conscious attention when needed but operate automatically otherwise. Evidence: physiological monitoring, priming studies.

## Goal Revision

Responses to discrepancy: (a) increase performance, (b) lower goal (downward revision), (c) disengage. Response shaped by:
- Attributions (internal → stronger revision)
- Self-efficacy (higher SE → upward revision after success)
- Deadline proximity (early: reduce discrepancy; late: align goal to performance)
- Goal orientation (learning → resilient to negative feedback)

## Discrepancy Production

Scherbaum & Vancouver (2010) computational model: negative feedback loops (discrepancy reduction) at one level can produce *upward* goal revision (discrepancy production) at higher level. Superordinate goal requires increasing subgoal to reduce its own discrepancy.

## SE Debate (Control Theory vs. Social Cognitive)

Vancouver et al.: within-person SE → negative performance effect (high SE → underallocate resources). Schmidt & DeShon: ambiguity and challenge moderate direction. Both consistent with efficient resource allocation interpretation. See [[Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)]].
