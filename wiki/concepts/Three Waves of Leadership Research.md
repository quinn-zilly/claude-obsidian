# Three Waves of Leadership Research

Organizing framework from [[lord-et-al-2017-leadership-in-applied-psychology]] tracing 100 years of leadership research in the *Journal of Applied Psychology*. Each wave was driven by a combination of theoretical shifts, methodological innovations, and contextual pressures (wars, economic change, workforce diversification).

## The Waves

| Wave | Approximate Dates | Dominant Focus | Key Method | Catalyzed By |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------|--------------|
| Pre-wave | ~1917–1947 | Traits and individual differences | Correlational, personality tests | WWI, Army Alpha |
| First | 1948–1961 | Leader behavior, follower attitudes | Questionnaires, factor analysis | Ohio State program |
| Second | 1969–1989 | Cognition, contingency, gender | Cognitive paradigm, early meta-analysis | Psychology's cognitive turn |
| Third | 1999–2007 | Transformational, LMX, teams, meta-analysis | Multilevel modeling, meta-analysis | New School leadership theories |

## Recurring Themes Across Waves
Despite apparent discontinuities, several themes persist:
- Leader–follower interdependence (recognized as early as Bogardus 1934)
- Role of situational context in moderating leader effects
- Interplay between leader cognition/identity and follower perceptions
- Co-production of leadership: "always co-produced by leaders and others" (Shamir 2005)

## Key Insight: Meta-Analysis as Wave Shaper
Meta-analysis (introduced to applied psychology by Schmidt & Hunter 1977) played a pivotal role in the third wave: it both closed out some research lines (showing conventional wisdom wrong) and opened new ones by revealing sampling error as explanation for previously "inconsistent" findings.

## Emerging Post-Wave Directions (as of 2017)
Virtual/distance leadership, shared/collective leadership, neuroscience applications, leadership development, social network approaches, complexity theory.
