# Causality Orientations Theory (COT)

Third mini-theory of [[Self-Determination Theory]] (Deci & Ryan 1985). Describes stable individual differences in how people orient toward regulating their own behavior — their characteristic [[Perceived Locus of Causality]].

## Three Orientations

**Autonomy orientation**: tendency to seek, interpret, and rely on interest, relevance, and personal values as guides to behavior. Predicts autonomous motivation, well-being, and integration.

**Controlled orientation**: tendency to rely on external controls, deadlines, contingencies, or ego-involvement. Predicts introjected and external regulation; associated with lower well-being.

**Impersonal orientation**: tendency to perceive behavior as beyond intentional control; associated with amotivation and poor outcomes. Predicts anxiety and depression.

## Key Properties

- Orientations are continuous (not categorical) and relatively stable personality-level variables
- All three orientations coexist within each person; their relative strengths vary
- Measured by General Causality Orientations Scale (GCOS; Deci & Ryan 1985)

## Meta-Analytic Evidence

- **Autonomy orientation → autonomous motivation**: confirmed across meta-analyses (Ryan et al. 2022)
- Priming effects on autonomy vs. controlled orientations are evidentially sound (Murphy & Steel 2021)
- COT predicts regulatory style above and beyond Big Five personality traits
- Distinct from locus of control (which conflates control and autonomy)

## Applied Relevance

Environments that afford autonomy support activate autonomy-oriented processing even in people with moderate autonomy orientation. COT predicts who benefits most from autonomy-supportive contexts.
