# Skilled Memory Theory

Chase & Ericsson (1981, 1982): a theory of how experts and trained subjects achieve vastly superior memory by **encoding material rapidly into long-term memory (LTM)** rather than relying on limited short-term memory (STM).

## The digit-span demonstration

College students, after **50–100 h of practice** on the digit-span task, surpassed most professional mnemonists: subject **SF reached 82 digits**, subject **DD over 100**. Their recall pattern was indistinguishable from exceptional subjects — strong evidence that exceptional memory is *acquired*, not innate.

## Three principles

1. **Meaningful encoding** — associate new material with pre-existing knowledge/patterns.
2. **Retrieval structure** — a reusable, pre-trained scheme for indexing stored items.
3. **Speed-up with practice** — encoding/retrieval accelerate with training.

## Significance

Skilled memory theory shows the "limited STM capacity" is not a hard ceiling within a trained domain — it can be circumvented. It was later generalized into [[Long-Term Working Memory]] (Ericsson & Kintsch 1995), which subsumes the earlier [[Chunking|chunking]] account and explains superior expert memory in chess, medicine, and mental calculation.
