# Likert Scale Development

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]]

## What It Is

The process of creating, validating, and refining self-report scales that ask respondents to endorse items on a graded response format (e.g., 1–5 strongly disagree → strongly agree). One of the most common measurement methods in modern psychology.

## Classic Tutorials

- **Clark & Watson (1995)** — foundational tutorial; ~6,500 citations.
- **Hinkin (1998)** — another widely-cited tutorial; ~3,000 citations.
- **Clark & Watson (2019)** — updated version; acknowledges space limitations.

These remain essential starting points but are ~25 years old; [[jebb-ng-tay-2021-likert-scale-advances]] serves as a companion update.

## Stages of Scale Development

1. **Conceptualize** the construct — see [[Construct Definition]]
2. **Generate items** — readability, wording, format
3. **Content validation** — subject matter experts, focus groups, statistics (Anderson & Gerbing 1991; Colquitt et al. 2019)
4. **Pilot study** — minimum n ≈ 24–36 for stable confidence intervals (Johanson & Brooks 2010)
5. **Assess factor structure** — EFA/CFA; check unidimensionality
6. **Estimate reliability** — prefer [[Coefficient Omega]] over alpha; consider IRT for precision mapping
7. **Validate nomologically** — convergent/discriminant, incremental validity; watch for [[Construct Proliferation]]
8. **Create short form** (if needed) — [[Ant Colony Optimization (Scale Shortening)]]

## Key Best Practice Updates (post-1995)

- **Validity is unitary** — do not speak of "content validity" or "criterion validity" as separate types. All evidence bears on the core construct validity inference. See [[Construct Validity]].
- **Specify the latent continuum** — is the construct unipolar or bipolar? What do the poles represent? Neglecting this leads to response format mismatches and reverse-item contamination.
- **Response options:** ≥6 options improves reliability; adding a midpoint has no psychometric effect (Simms et al. 2019).
- **Block presentation matters:** splitting a scale's items across survey blocks lowers intercorrelations and can produce artefactual factors (Weijters et al. 2014).
- **Alpha ≠ best reliability estimate** — use omega when factor loadings differ across items (common).
- **Construct proliferation warning:** new constructs need both importance and distinctiveness established *theoretically* before development begins (Shaffer et al. 2016; Jebb et al. 2021).
