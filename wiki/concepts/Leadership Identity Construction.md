# Leadership Identity Construction

Nav: [[index]] | [[Leadership MOC]]

DeRue & Ashford's (2010) constructionist account of how leader and follower identities are **co-constructed through a reciprocal claiming and granting process**.

- **Claiming**: an individual asserts a leader (or follower) identity — e.g. referring to oneself as a leader, or displaying identity cues ("looking the part").
- **Granting**: others bestow the claimed identity and claim a complementary identity for themselves — "I grant you a leader identity and claim for myself a follower identity."

Leadership (and followership) is **constructed only when claims are met with reciprocal grants**, and vice versa. A titled "manager" is not a leader if subordinates neither grant a leader identity nor claim a follower identity. Because grants require someone to take on a follower identity, **followership is intrinsic to constructing leadership** — a foundational point for [[Followership Theory]].

## Why It Matters

- Identities are **dynamic and permeable**, not intrapersonal, one-way, or static — they shift across time and situation.
- Constructions range from stable hierarchical role relationships to a "shifting leadership structure" continually renegotiated — the theoretical bridge to [[Shared Leadership]].
- Provides the identity engine inside [[Adaptive Leadership Theory]] (each [[Leading-Following Double Interact]] drives claiming/granting) and the constructionist strand of the [[uhl-bien-et-al-2014-followership-theory-review|Uhl-Bien et al. followership review]].
