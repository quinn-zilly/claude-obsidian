# On-the-Job Development (OJD)

**On-the-job development (OJD)** — learning and growth that occurs through work experiences themselves, as opposed to formal training programs. The dominant form of leadership development.

> [!key-insight] The 70% Principle
> Most leadership development (≈70%) occurs through on-the-job experience. Yet organizations systematically over-invest in formal training (10%) relative to its developmental return. See [[70-20-10 Rule]].

## Core Claim

McCall et al. (1988) established that effective executives develop primarily through challenging work experiences: turnarounds, start-ups, cross-functional moves, failures. Formal training plays a smaller role.

The problem: organizations *know* this but don't act on it. Yost & Plunkett (2010) frame this as a "knowing-doing gap" — the central challenge of strategic OJD.

## Mechanisms

Development via experience requires three conditions:
1. **The right experience** — sufficient challenge, novelty, accountability (see [[Developmental Experiences]])
2. **Capturing the lessons** — active reflection, feedback-seeking, learning goals
3. **Learning agility** — dispositional capacity to extract lessons (see [[Learning Agility]])

## Organizational Levers (from [[yost-plunkett-2010-ten-catalysts]])

Ten catalysts to embed OJD in HR systems:
1. [[Leadership Map]] — four-dimension taxonomy (experiences, competencies, relationships, learning agility)
2. Publish OJD experience list — make developmental moves visible
3. Help leaders capture lessons — specify competencies + actions per experience
4. Separate perf/dev discussions — prevent development crowding
5. [[70-20-10 Rule]] — hardwire ratio into dev plan forms
6. Require dev plans — track completion + quality
7. Turn training into OJD — 90% outside-classroom design
8. [[Personal Advisory Board]] — developmental networks over single mentors
9. [[Transition Checklist]] — leverage high-openness-to-learning moments
10. Reward OJD — compensation/promotion signals
