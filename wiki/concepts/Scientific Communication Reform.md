# Scientific Communication Reform

The program of changes to how scientific knowledge is produced, communicated, evaluated, and archived — motivated by the [[Replication Crisis]] and the recognition that current communication infrastructure creates systematic distortions.

## Core Diagnosis

Current scientific publishing bundles four distinct functions that are better served separately:

| Function | Current mechanism | Reformed alternative |
|----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Registration** | Journal submission date | Timestamped preprint deposit |
| **Dissemination** | Journal publication | Open repository + OA |
| **Peer review** | Editor-controlled gating | Overlay review services |
| **Archiving** | Journal hosting | Persistent repositories (DOI, OSF) |

Nosek & Bar-Anan (2012) argue that bundling these functions creates *all five* major inefficiencies: no communication (file drawer), slow communication (serial review), incomplete communication (page limits), inaccurate communication (publication bias), and unmodifiable communication (static PDFs).

## Six Proposed Reforms (Nosek & Bar-Anan 2012)

1. Full digital communication — no print, no page limits
2. [[Open Access]] — eliminate subscription paywalls
3. Disentangle publication from evaluation — arXiv/preprint model
4. Grading system + diversified dissemination — multi-journal articles, separate review services
5. Publishing peer review — reviews as citable scholarly products
6. Open continuous peer review — community commenting post-publication

## Relationship to Open Science Movement

Scientific communication reform is one pillar of [[Open Science]]. The others include:
- **Preregistration** (separating prediction from postdiction) — see [[Preregistration]]
- **Open data and materials** — enabling [[Analytic Reproducibility]]
- **Registered Reports** — see [[Registered Reports]]
- **Badges and incentives** — see [[munafo-2017-manifesto]]

## Key Insight: Social, Not Technical Barriers

Nosek & Bar-Anan's central claim: the technology to implement all six reforms already existed in 2012. The barrier is social — inertia in prestige hierarchies, vested interests of incumbent journals, and the use of journal prestige as a proxy for researcher quality in hiring and promotion. **Reform requires changing incentive structures** (addressed in Scientific Utopia II), not inventing new technology.

## Cross-Domain Connections

- **[[Knowledge Transfer]] (KM)**: Scientific publishing is fundamentally a knowledge transfer infrastructure. Current system introduces noise (publication bias), delay (677-day avg), and selection bias into the transfer channel. Reform is, at root, a knowledge management intervention.
- **[[Institutional Isomorphism]]**: The persistence of the current system despite its known inefficiencies is a textbook case of institutional lock-in — normative isomorphism (shared prestige norms), mimetic isomorphism (following high-impact journals), and coercive isomorphism (funder/tenure requirements) all reinforce the status quo.
- **[[metaBUS]]**: metaBUS represents a partial implementation of the "diversified dissemination + grading" vision — a machine-readable layer over published findings that partially compensates for incomplete publication.
