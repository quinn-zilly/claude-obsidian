# Predictor vs Performance Domain

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]]

A distinction from [[binning-barrett-1989-validity-personnel-decisions|Binning & Barrett (1989)]]: both predictor constructs and job performance are behavioral **domains**, but they are constituted differently — and conflating them distorts validation.

## Discovered vs imposed

- **Predictor construct domains** are **discovered** — research psychologists identify naturally occurring clusters of covarying behavior (abilities, personality) to explain general behavioral regularities. Covariance arises from individual dispositions × environment.
- **Performance domains** are **created/imposed** by organizations — clusters of **behavior-outcome units**, differentially valued according to organizational goals. Campbell (1983): "the meaning of performance is not something to be 'discovered'; it should be imposed." Weick: a "consensually validated grammar" of the organization.

## Consequences

Because a performance domain *is itself a construct* (albeit an imposed one), it deserves the same validation rigor as predictors — yet rarely gets it, because organizational values shift, criteria are believed "dynamic," and rigorous criterion development is costly. This asymmetry is the seed of the **[[Criterion Problem|criterion problem]]**.

## Links to other distinctions

- **[[Composite vs Multiple Criteria]]** — composite model = unitary *economic* worth (job-oriented, "what is accomplished"); multiple model = behavioral dimensions (worker-oriented, "what is done"). Which job-analysis data justify Inference 8 depends on which model is used.
- **Terminology** — predictor side is dispositional ("she is extraverted"); performance side is goal-related ("she is customer-service oriented").
