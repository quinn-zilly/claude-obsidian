# Occupational Prestige and Job Complexity

Two structural moderators of the [[Gender Gap in Rewards vs Performance|gender reward–performance gap]] in [[joshi-son-roh-2015-women-close-the-gap|Joshi et al. (2015)]].

## Occupational prestige
The socioeconomic value/status of an occupation (measured via **Nakao–Treas** prestige scores; sample mean ≈ 60). Operates partly independently of sex composition.

> [!key-insight] The equal-performance / unequal-reward result
> In **highly prestigious occupations**, women performed **equally** to men (no performance penalty — H2a *not* supported) but were **rewarded significantly lower** (H2b supported). Prestige is a **hierarchy-enhancing** context (see [[Social Dominance Theory (Rewards)]]). In prestige professions (academia, law), performance criteria are objective (billable hours, research productivity) but reward allocation is subjective, opaque, adversarial, and high-stakes ("up-or-out") — the locus of inequity.

## Job complexity
Measured via **O*NET** skill scores (complex ≈ scores > 70). Complexity **enhances** the gap in both performance and rewards (H4a/b supported) through several mechanisms:
- Ambiguous/subjective performance criteria and gender-based skill attributions.
- Idiosyncratic roles + face-time/after-hours expectations that don't favor women.
- Greater autonomy and idiosyncratic bargaining → **less opportunity to compare conditions** and detect unequal treatment (Baron & Pfeffer 1994; Lansberg 1989).

QCA corroboration: both configurations where **women received equal/higher evaluations** involved **routine (low-complexity)** jobs (e.g., bank tellers).

> [!note] Link to compensation theory
> The "low scrutiny of individual inputs in complex jobs" mechanism is the gender-inequality analogue of [[Identifiability of Individual Inputs|Shaw's (2014) identifiability]] contingency: where individual contributions can't be cleanly assessed, subtle bias enters reward decisions.
