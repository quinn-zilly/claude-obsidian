# Duration Assessment (Team Adaptation)

**Phase 3** of the [[Team Adaptation (Stimulus-Based Model)|Pearsall et al. (2025) stimulus-based model]].

## Core Idea

How long the team expects the stimulus to persist determines what type of adaptive response is optimal. Teams must choose between:
- **Temporary / workaround response**: rapid, makeshift, designed to bridge until things revert
- **Sustained / transformational response**: investing in new routines, role structures, and cognitive frameworks as the "new normal"

Duration is **continuous**, not binary. "Somewhat temporary" vs. "more sustained, but not permanent" can generate very different attitudes and strategies than a simple temporary/permanent split.

## Critical Point: Expectation, Not Actual Duration

Duration assessment is based on members' *expectation* that the situation will or will not revert. The true duration may be unknown (e.g., team member sick with flu). Teams must act on current expectations. This means:
- Misclassification → misaligned response → wasted effort or ineffective adaptation
- Updating is essential when duration expectations prove wrong (→ [[Reflexive Learning (Team Adaptation)]])

## Empirical Illustrations

- Stachowski et al. (2009): nuclear power plant crews facing *temporary* novel events → best served by makeshift workarounds + simple, concise interaction patterns
- LePine (2003, 2005): teams adapted to temporary communications breakdown via short-term behavioral adjustments
- Johnson et al. (2006); Marks et al. (2000): *sustained* reward or environmental changes required thoughtful plan formulation and cognitive restructuring

## Factors Supporting Accurate Assessment

- High internal communication + communication with managers and stakeholders
- Feedback-seeking (Sherf & Morrison, 2020)
- Boundary-spanning (ambassador/scout roles; Ancona & Caldwell, 1990)
- Structural interdependence — forces interaction → more accurate inferences
- Reflective action sessions built into teamwork (Vashdi et al., 2013)

## Future Directions

- Remarkably little research directly examines how teams assess stimulus duration
- COVID-19 pandemic is an example of extreme uncertainty defeating even experienced teams' duration expectations
- Need for error-identification and correction studies: how do teams revisit Phase 3 after misdiagnosis?
- Role of feedback-seeking as a formal model component
