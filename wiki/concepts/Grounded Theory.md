# Grounded Theory (GT)

One of the most established qualitative methods — the "market leader" (McLeod 2001) at the "front of the qualitative revolution" (Charmaz 2000). First explicated by Glaser & Strauss (1967), originating in studies of the "awareness of dying." Its developers came from different traditions — Glaser from quantitative sociology (Columbia), Strauss from qualitative sociology (Chicago) — so GT can be located across **multiple paradigms**, with both positivist (objective coding, data reduction) and postpositivist (verification) elements in the original formulation.

GT later diverged: Glaser (1992) vs Strauss & Corbin (1990), plus ~five variations (McLeod 2001). The dominant version in counseling psychology today is the **constructivist-leaning** approach of Charmaz (2000) and Rennie (1998).

## Paradigmatic location (per Ponterotto 2005)

Predominantly **[[Constructivism–Interpretivism|constructivist]]**:

- **Ontology**: constructivist — no single objective reality; Glaser & Strauss noted the constant comparative method is *not* designed to guarantee two independent analysts reach the same results, so multiple interpretations/realities are allowed.
- **Epistemology**: constructivist — face-to-face interviews up to 3 hr permit intense interaction and hermeneutic understanding of lived experience (*Erlebnis*).
- **Axiology**: constructivist — biases acknowledged and explicated.
- **Rhetoric**: constructivist — first-person, extensive participant "voice" via representative quotations.
- **Methodology**: constructivist — theoretical sampling, lengthy interviews with an evolving semistructured protocol; more discovery-oriented than [[Consensual Qualitative Research|CQR]].

Ponterotto places the preponderance of counseling-psychology GT studies within the constructivist paradigm, while noting ongoing debate about its anchoring.
