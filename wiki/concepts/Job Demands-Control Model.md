# Job Demands-Control Model

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]] | [[Job Demands-Resources Model]]

Karasek's (1979) model of how work design affects mental and physical health. One of five historical work design clusters in [[parker-morgeson-johns-2017-100-years-work-design|Parker et al. (2017)]] (the blue cluster, ~101 terms — heavily health/strain focused).

> [!info] Scope
> This page covers both the original two-factor demand-control model **and** its demand-control-**support** (JDCS) extension. The broader generalization — where control is one of many resources and a motivational/engagement path is added — lives at [[Job Demands-Resources Model]].

## Core Idea

Two key job features:

- **Demands** — workload, pace, conflicting requirements.
- **Control** (decision latitude) — autonomy over how and when work is done.

**Key predictions:**

- Demands and control **interact**: control can buffer the negative effects of demands.
- **Strain is greatest** when demands are high *and* control is low ("high-strain" jobs).

## Origins

Bridged two literatures that had ignored each other: motivational job-design research disregarded demands, while large epidemiological stress research (e.g. Caplan et al., 1975) ignored discretion. Karasek (1979) joined them. Earlier roots: Whyte (1948) on heavy workload + limited latitude.

## Extension: Social Support

Karasek & Theorell (1990) and Johnson & Hall (1988) added **social support** as a third buffer → demand-control-**support** (JDCS) model. Evidence for the social-support buffering term specifically is mixed. Individual differences — self-efficacy, proactive personality, locus of control — moderate the demands–control relationship.

## Evidence

- Strong, copious support for **main effects**: demands → stress/burnout; control → well-being.
- The specific **demands × control interaction** is mixed and mostly unsupported, even in quality-vetted longitudinal studies (van der Doef & Maes, 1999; de Lange et al., 2003).
- Effects vary by person: e.g. those high in psychological flexibility benefit most from control (Bond & Bunce, 2003).
- Landmark applied evidence: Whitehall II civil-servant studies (Marmot et al., 1991) — lower-status (lower-control) jobs → higher morbidity.

## Relationship to JD-R

The [[Job Demands-Resources Model]] (Demerouti et al., 2001; Bakker & Demerouti 2007) generalized this model: control is just one of many resources, and a motivational (engagement) path was added alongside the strain path. The [[Job Crafting|JD-R typology of job crafting]] (Tims, Bakker & Derks 2012) derives its activity types from that demands/resources logic.

## Relation to WDQ / Work Design

Social characteristics (social support, feedback from others) and contextual characteristics (physical demands, work conditions) in the [[Work Design Questionnaire|WDQ]] map directly to JD-C(S)/JD-R demands and resources. Work conditions explain ~16% incremental variance in stress beyond motivational + social characteristics ([[humphrey-nahrgang-morgeson-2007-work-design-meta-analysis|Humphrey et al. 2007]]).
