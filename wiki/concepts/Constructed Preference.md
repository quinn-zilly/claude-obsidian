# Constructed Preference

The hypothesis that people **infer their preferences at decision time** from context cues, rather than retrieving stable pre-formed preferences (as assumed by neoclassical economics).

## Origin

Revealed preference theory (economics) assumes stable preferences expressed in all choices. Lichtenstein & Slovic (2006) assembled evidence that people instead construct preferences on the fly, especially for unfamiliar choices.

Evidence: preference reversals, high exclusion rates on abstract elicitation tasks (standard gambles, contingent valuation), sensitivity to irrelevant context.

## Research Forms

1. **Experimental manipulation** — compare formally equivalent tasks evoking psychologically different processes (Pedroni et al. 2018 on risk preference construction)
2. **Natural variation** — stable risk/time preferences but not social preferences across time (Chang & Schechter 2015)
3. **Choice architecture field experiments** — but these reveal too little about construction processes to test the theory properly (Szaszi et al. 2017)

## Process Tracing Methods

Revived interest due to constructed preference theory:
- Think-aloud protocols (concurrent, not retrospective)
- Eye-tracking and mouse-tracing
- Coding observed behavior in analytical terms (options, uncertainty sources)
- Greater acceptance of **reactive measurement** risk (asking people to describe thinking)

## Measurement Failures as Data

When abstract tasks produce high exclusion rates (standard gambles: 61% exclusion in earliest Annual Review article), this is not just failure — it reveals how context affects preferences and where utility theory axioms break down.
