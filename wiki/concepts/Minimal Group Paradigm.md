# Minimal Group Paradigm

The experimental method (Tajfel and colleagues) showing that **merely assigning an individual to a group is sufficient to generate in-group favoritism** — even with no interaction, no prior similarity or liking, anonymous membership, and no link between self-interest and group responses (Brewer 1979; Tajfel 1982; Turner 1984).

## Key Findings

- Even **explicitly random** assignment to groups produces discrimination against out-groups and increased intragroup cooperation/cohesion (Billig & Tajfel 1973; Locksley, Ortiz & Hepburn 1980).
- Favoritism does **not** depend on perceived interpersonal similarity, liking, interaction, or self-interest.

## Significance

This is the empirical backbone of [[Social Identity Theory]]'s claim that **categorization is sufficient for identification**. It is contradictory to conventional views of group relations, which assume in-group favoritism requires strong leadership, interdependence, interaction, or cohesion.

For organizations ([[ashforth-mael-1989-social-identity-theory-organization|Ashforth & Mael 1989]]): although mere categorization suffices, the *pervasiveness* of formal and informal groups means categorization is seldom the *only* factor — so the consequences of identification are likely **intensified** in organizational settings. The paradigm underwrites the concept of the [[Psychological Group]].
