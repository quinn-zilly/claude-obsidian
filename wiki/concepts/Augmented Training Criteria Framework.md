# Augmented Training Criteria Framework

Proposed by Alliger, Tannenbaum, Bennett, Traver & Shotland (1997) as a more fine-grained coding scheme built on [[Kirkpatrick Four-Level Model]].

## Structure

| Kirkpatrick Level | Augmented Sub-Category | Code |
|---|---|---|
| Reactions | Affective reactions (liking, enjoyment) | 1a |
| Reactions | Utility judgments (perceived job relevance) | 1b |
| Reactions | Combined reactions | 1c |
| Learning | Immediate post-training knowledge | 2a |
| Learning | Knowledge retention (delayed test) | 2b |
| Learning | Behavior/skill demonstration (in training context) | 2c |
| Behavior | **Transfer** (on-the-job performance) | 3 |
| Results | Organizational outcomes | 4 |

## Key Distinctions

**Reactions 1a vs. 1b**: Affective reactions tap emotional response ("I enjoyed this"). Utility reactions tap perceived transferability ("This will help me do my job"). Attitude specificity theory (Ajzen & Fishbein) predicts utility reactions should better predict job behavior — confirmed empirically.

**Learning 2a vs. 2c**: Immediate declarative knowledge (2a) vs. behavioral/skill performance *within training context* (2c). Both are pre-transfer; neither requires job application.

**Level 2c vs. Level 3**: The critical distinction is context — training environment vs. work environment. This maps onto "can do" (2c) vs. "does do" (3).

## Empirical Key Finding

Utility reactions (1b) correlate with transfer (3) *more strongly* than immediate learning (2a) or retained learning (2b) do. This is counterintuitive but may reflect that utility judgments implicitly incorporate trainees' knowledge of their work environment.

> [!key-insight] Reactions as "Will" vs. Learning as "Skill"
> One reviewer's formulation: utility reactions may index *will* (motivation to apply), while learning measures index *skill* (capability). Transfer requires both.
