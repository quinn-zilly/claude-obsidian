# File-Drawer Problem

The file-drawer problem (Rosenthal, 1979) refers to the systematic non-publication of null or negative results. Studies that fail to find significant effects are disproportionately left unpublished — sitting in researchers' "file drawers" — creating a distorted published literature that overstates the prevalence and magnitude of positive effects.

## Why It Occurs

- Journals historically preferred statistically significant, novel findings
- Researchers anticipate rejection and do not submit null results
- Career incentives reward publications over null contributions

## Consequences

The file-drawer problem is a primary driver of [[Publication Bias]] and inflates effect size estimates in meta-analyses. It interacts with [[P-hacking]] to produce a literature that appears more reliable than it is.

## Solutions

- [[Preregistration]]: committing to hypotheses before data collection makes null results citable
- [[Registered Reports]]: in-principle acceptance before results eliminates null-result suppression
- [[Open Data]]: posting datasets enables others to verify and reanalyse
- Dedicated null-result journals and registered replication reports
