# Transactive Memory Systems

## Definition

A **transactive memory system (TMS)** is a group-level cognitive structure in which knowledge is distributed across individual members, with shared awareness of *who knows what*. The group collectively "knows" more than any individual, and members can access each other's knowledge by knowing where to look.

Key components:
- **Specialization** — members hold different, complementary knowledge domains
- **Credibility** — members trust each other's expertise in their domains
- **Coordination** — members can locate and access each other's knowledge efficiently

## Why It Matters for Knowledge Integration

TMS is a primary micro-level mechanism for [[Knowledge Integration]]. Instead of requiring all knowledge to be in one place (or one person), TMS enables groups to integrate distributed specialist knowledge through coordinated access. This is why Grant (1996) argues the *firm* exists: to integrate specialist knowledge that individuals hold separately.

## TMS as a Knowledge Repository (Argote Framework)

In Argote & Miron-Spektor's (2011) framework, TMS is part of the **active context** — specifically embedded in the member-member network (social network) and the member-task network (who knows how to do what). TMS can be lost when:
- Key members leave the organization
- Team composition changes and tacit knowledge of "who knows what" dissipates
- Coordination patterns break down

## Implications for Organizational Learning

- Teams with well-developed TMS learn faster because they can efficiently access distributed expertise
- TMS is a form of organizational memory that is inherently social — it cannot be fully captured in documents or databases
- Building TMS requires time and shared work experience; it cannot be designed top-down

## Connections

- [[Knowledge Integration]] — TMS is the micro-level mechanism for group knowledge integration
- [[Organizational Learning]] — TMS is one of the active context repositories through which organizational learning is retained
- [[Tacit vs Explicit Knowledge]] — the "who knows what" component of TMS is largely tacit; explicit directories are only a partial substitute

## Three Dimensions of TMS

**Memory Differentiation (Knowledge Specialization)**
- Members develop expertise in different domains
- Reduces cognitive load on any one person
- Creates differentiated knowledge structures
- Forms through [[Group Training]] and [[Shared Experience]]

**Task Credibility (Trust)**
- Members trust each other's expertise and judgment
- Builds through repeated interaction and demonstrated competence
- Crucial for willingness to rely on others' knowledge
- Can be undermined by high relationship conflict

**Task Coordination**
- Ability to work together smoothly
- Know how to combine knowledge effectively
- Coordinate activities with minimal communication
- Based on [[Metaknowledge]] of who knows what

## How TMS Develops

### Most Important Antecedents
1. **[[Group Training]]**: Members trained together develop stronger TMS than individually trained
2. **[[Shared Experience]]**: Working together on similar tasks
3. **[[Communication]]**: Face-to-face superior to CMC in early stages
4. **Task Interdependence**: Reward structures that create cognitive interdependence
5. **[[Team Familiarity]]**: Prior relationships increase expertise awareness

### Secondary Antecedents
- Member demographics & technical competence
- [[Member Personality]] (assertiveness)
- Imposed knowledge structures (explicit role assignment)
- Stress type (challenge stressors help; hindrance stressors hurt)

## TMS Consequences

### Team Learning
- Teams with developed TMS learn faster
- Can access distributed expertise efficiently
- [[Team Reflexivity]] improved

### Team Performance
- **Better decisions**: Fewer errors, higher quality problem-solving
- **Better accuracy**: More effective knowledge retrieval
- **Better coordination**: Fewer misunderstandings
- **Efficiency gains**: Faster task completion (field evidence)
- Positive effect on [[Team Creativity]]

### Member Outcomes
- Higher [[Team Member Satisfaction]]
- Stronger team identification
- Increased organizational commitment

## Moderators of TMS Effects

**TMS Benefits Larger For:**
- Smaller group size (3-4 member teams optimal)
- Complex tasks requiring knowledge integration
- Stable team membership
- High task interdependence

**TMS Benefits Reduced By:**
- Large groups (coordination becomes problematic)
- Simple, repetitive tasks
- High membership turnover
- High relationship conflict
- Geographic distribution (virtual teams)
- High environmental turbulence
- Purely electronically-mediated communication

## Measurement

**Lewis (2003) Scale** (most adopted):
- 15 items covering three dimensions
- Works in lab and field
- Single composite or three subscales
- Focus on [[Memory Differentiation|specialization]], credibility, coordination

**Austin (2003) Approach** (most precise):
- Identifies all relevant skills via interviews
- Creates person-by-skill matrix
- Calculates: knowledge stock, specialization, consensus, accuracy
- Labor-intensive but fine-grained

**Faraj & Sproull (2000) Approach** (field studies):
- [[Expertise Location]] (awareness of who knows what)
- Expertise utilization (accessing it)
- Simpler than Austin, more detailed than Lewis

## Key Empirical Findings

### Virtual Teams
- Totally dispersed teams (one member per site) develop stronger TMS than sub-grouped teams
- Unequal sub-grouping creates problems
- Face-to-face communication crucial for utilization, less critical once TMS established
- CMC viable if properly structured

### Membership Change
- Total turnover: complete loss of TMS benefits
- Partial turnover: structure stable but processes less efficient
- Minimized by: similar expertise in replacement, information about newcomer

### Task Transfer
- TMS doesn't directly transfer to different tasks
- Creates better understanding of underlying principles (helps learning)
- Benefits most when expertise stability high

### Communication Timing
- Early stage: F2F > phone > email
- Later stage: mature teams can use CMC effectively
- Role-identification communication critical

## Distinction from Related Concepts

**vs. [[Team Mental Models]]:**
- TMM: shared representations of task, goals, strategies
- TMS: "who knows what" with specialization
- TMS is narrower in scope, requires division of labor

**vs. [[Shared Mental Models]]:**
- SMM: assumes shared understanding
- TMS: requires complementary (different) knowledge
- Different cognitive mechanism

**vs. Cross-Understanding:**
- Cross-understanding: accurate mental models of others' mental models
- TMS: knowing where expertise lies
- TMS is a component of cross-understanding

## Research Gaps (Ren & Argote 2011)

- TMS in virtual teams (under-researched)
- Entrepreneurial ventures (largely unexplored)
- Organizational-level TMS (very limited)
- Dynamic evolution and decay (poorly understood)
- Role of social networks (under-studied)
- Motivational factors (neglected)
- Knowledge forgetting and obsolescence (unclear)
- Relationship between trust and TMS (complicated)

## Practical Implications

**For Managers:**
- Use [[Group Training]] to build TMS
- Maintain team stability when possible
- Facilitate early F2F communication
- Assign complementary (not redundant) expertise
- Provide explicit role definitions
- Monitor [[Member Turnover]] costs

**For Organizations:**
- Expertise directories and directories help
- [[Web 2.0 Technologies]] can auto-populate expertise maps
- Member rotation spreads knowledge but disrupts TMS
- May trade off: strong team-level vs. org-level TMS
