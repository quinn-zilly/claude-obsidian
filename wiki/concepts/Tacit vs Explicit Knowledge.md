# Tacit vs. Explicit Knowledge

## The Distinction

Originating with Polanyi (1962) and central to knowledge management theory:

**Explicit knowledge** — codifiable, articulable, transferable through documents, instructions, or databases. Can be written down without significant loss of meaning. Example: a recipe, a process manual, a formula.

**Tacit knowledge** — experiential, context-specific, difficult or impossible to fully articulate. "We know more than we can tell" (Polanyi). Example: riding a bike, clinical judgment, aesthetic taste, knowing who to call in an organization.

## Why the Distinction Matters

The tacit/explicit distinction is the single most consequential design variable in knowledge management:

- **Transfer**: Explicit knowledge transfers through documentation; tacit knowledge requires socialization, observation, and shared practice
- **Retention**: Explicit knowledge survives personnel changes if captured; tacit knowledge leaves when people do
- **Integration**: Tacit knowledge is harder to integrate across organizational boundaries because it resists codification
- **Systems design**: Most KMS are better at capturing explicit knowledge — tacit knowledge requires different mechanisms (communities of practice, apprenticeship, job rotation)

## Related Taxonomies

**Declarative vs. procedural** (Singley & Anderson, 1989):
- Declarative = know-what (facts, concepts)
- Procedural = know-how (skills, routines)
Procedural knowledge tends to be more tacit; declarative knowledge tends toward the explicit.

**Component vs. architectural knowledge** (Henderson & Clark, 1990):
- Component = knowledge of individual parts
- Architectural = knowledge of how parts connect
Architectural knowledge is often more tacit because it reflects embedded system understanding.

**Causal ambiguity** (Szulanski, 1996): when even the holder of tacit knowledge cannot articulate why a practice works, transfer is especially difficult.

## The SECI Model (Nonaka & Takeuchi, 1995)

Knowledge conversion cycles through four modes:
- **Socialization** — tacit to tacit (shared experience)
- **Externalization** — tacit to explicit (articulation)
- **Combination** — explicit to explicit (systematizing)
- **Internalization** — explicit to tacit (learning by doing)

Referenced extensively in Alavi & Leidner (2001) as the framework for understanding how KMS can support knowledge conversion.
