# Libertarian Paternalism

Policy approach (Thaler & Sunstein 2008 — *Nudge*): **manipulate choice architecture** to induce choices that fully informed, rational individuals would make, while preserving freedom to choose otherwise.

## Examples

- Default organ donation opt-out (instead of opt-in)
- Default enrollment in retirement savings plans
- Arranging cafeteria food to promote healthy choices
- Social norm messaging ("most people in your building recycle")

## Decision Science Critique (Fischhoff & Broomell)

Libertarian paternalism is only *appropriate* when individual decision analysis has been done:

- **Organ donation default** → appropriate only when survivors would accept that choice without family consultation
- **Social norm nudges** → appropriate only when people have resources to adopt the behavior and a safety net if things go wrong
- **Retirement savings** → appropriate only when expected financial returns outweigh psychological cost of market corrections + economic risk of being in market when funds are needed

**Analyzing each individual's decision is too demanding** for most interventions — so libertarian paternalism typically imposes one-size-fits-all defaults whose rationality for any individual is unverified.

## Connection to Constructed Preference

Nudges assume preferences are constructable and can be directed. [[Constructed Preference]] theory provides the micro-mechanism: if preferences are built from context cues, changing context cues (defaults, framing, ordering) changes preferences.
