# Institutional Entrepreneurship

The agentic turn in institutional theory: the study of actors who leverage resources and strategies to create, transform, or maintain institutions. Emerged in the 1990s as a corrective to the over-socialized, structurally deterministic picture of early neo-institutionalism (DiMaggio 1988; Hirsch & Lounsbury 1997).

## The Core Paradox

The **paradox of embedded agency** (Holm 1995): how can actors deeply embedded in taken-for-granted institutional arrangements ever reflect on and act upon those arrangements? If institutions shape cognition and interest, how do anyone escape their grip?

Seo & Creed (2002) argued that structural positions at the *interstice* of different institutional logics generate the reflexivity needed. Others focused on structural peripherality (Leblebici et al. 1991) or centrality (Greenwood & Suddaby 2006).

## Who Are Institutional Entrepreneurs?

- Actors at the **periphery** of a field: less vested in the status quo; more likely to challenge it (Leblebici et al. 1991; Maguire & Hardy 2009)
- Actors at the **core** of mature fields: sufficient resources and legitimacy to drive change from within (Greenwood & Suddaby 2006)
- Actors in **institutional voids**: spaces that are institutionally less developed provide entrepreneurial opportunity (Mair & Marti 2009)
- Actors experiencing **logic contradictions**: the interstice of multiple logics enables reflexivity

## How Change Is Introduced

### Nondisruptive (Cultural Entrepreneurship)
Innovations framed as non-threatening to the institutional order (Lounsbury & Glynn 2001):
- **Bricolage**: novel combinations of existing materials (Rao, Monin & Durand 2005)
- **Robust design**: wrapping innovations in familiar forms (Hargadon & Douglas 2001)
- **Framing/theorization**: articulating legitimating accounts (Greenwood, Hinings & Suddaby 2002; Werner & Cornelissen 2014)
- **Rhetoric and stories**: building narrative legitimacy

### Disruptive
Direct challenge to incumbents; informed by social movement research:
- Adversarial framing contests (Guérard et al. 2013)
- Collective resource mobilization
- Protests, boycotts, campaigns (Zietsma & Lawrence 2010)

## Key Blind Spots (per Micelotta et al. 2017)

1. Change is treated as synonymous with *successful, intended* change — "institutional entrepreneurship," "institutional work," and "institutional change" used as synonyms, hiding failure
2. The institutionalization step (how innovations become durable) is understudied
3. "Heroic actor" imagery: oversimplified narratives; little appreciation for collective nature
4. Innovations that never become institutionalized are nearly invisible (proto-institutions)
5. Unintended consequences of institutional entrepreneurship rarely examined

## Institutional Work

Broader category that encompassed "institutional entrepreneurship" as the field moved on: categorizes tactics and strategies as practice work, boundary work, identity work. Shifts focus from dramatic moments to the ongoing maintenance of institutions (Lawrence, Suddaby & Leca 2009, 2011).
