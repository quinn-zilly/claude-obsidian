# Work Motivation Meta-Framework (Kanfer)

Nav: [[index]] | [[wiki/sources/kanfer-frese-johnson-2017-work-motivation|Kanfer et al. 2017]]

Tripartite organizing framework for 100 years of work motivation theory (Kanfer, Chen & Pritchard 2008; extended in Kanfer, Frese & Johnson 2017). Clusters theories by their primary focus.

## Three Segments

### Content
*What motivates?* — psychological traits, motives, and orientations that instigate motivational processes

- **Universal motives:** need fulfillment (Maslow), intrinsic motivation/SDT (Deci & Ryan), justice motives (Adams)
- **Trait-based:** achievement/power/affiliation motives (McClelland), Big Five traits, core self-evaluations (Judge et al.), approach/avoidance motivation, goal orientation (Dweck), regulatory focus (Higgins)

### Context
*Where does motivation operate?* — features of the job, team, and broader environment

- **Job level:** Job Characteristics Model (Hackman & Oldham 1975); relational work design (Grant); task meaningfulness = strongest mediator
- **Team level:** social loafing, cohesion, climate, shared identity → individual motivation states; leadership effects
- **Socio-cultural:** occupation, culture, economic conditions as omnibus contextual variables (Johns 2006)

### Process
*How does motivation operate?* — cognitive and affective mechanisms of goal choice and goal striving

- **Goal choice:** Expectancy/VIE (Vroom 1964), Theory of planned behavior (Ajzen 1991)
- **Goal striving:** Self-regulation (Bandura 1977; Carver & Scheier 1981; Kanfer 1977), Action regulation theory (Frese & Zapf 1994)
- **Integrative:** Goal setting theory (Locke & Latham 1990), Resource allocation theory (Kanfer & Ackerman 1989), Ego depletion (Baumeister et al. 1998), Rubicon model (Heckhausen 1991)

## Key Organizing Principle

Content and context variables exert their effects **indirectly** via motivational processes (goal choice + goal striving). Direct effects exist but are smaller. Goals = nexus connecting "why" (motives, needs) to "how" (purposive action).

## Contemporary Synthesis

Work motivation is now understood as a **dynamic, goal-directed, resource allocation process** that changes over time as a function of reciprocal person–context interactions. Three hallmarks distinguishing contemporary from older views: (1) primacy of goals, (2) emphasis on goal pursuit and affective processes, (3) motivation as active self-directed process (personal initiative, voice, taking charge).
