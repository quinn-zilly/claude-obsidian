# Deliberate Practice

**Individualized training activities specifically designed (typically by a coach or teacher) to improve particular aspects of performance through repetition and successive refinement, guided by informative feedback** (Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Römer 1993).

## Distinguishing features

- **Not mere experience.** Work, competition, play, and leisure afford few chances for effective learning; the number of years of domain experience is only weakly related to performance. Deliberate practice — focused, effortful, feedback-driven — is what predicts attained level.
- **Requires full concentration** → effortful → cannot be sustained indefinitely. Elite performers cap out around **~4 h/day** of fully concentrated practice sustainable long-term without burnout.
- **Feedback is essential.** To benefit maximally, the learner monitors training with full attention. Domains lacking rapid feedback (many judgment tasks) show little experience→accuracy gain (see [[Mechanical vs Clinical Prediction]]).
- **Extends to unstructured domains.** Even in chess (no formal training system) Ericsson et al. found deliberate-practice-like activity: studying published games and predicting masters' moves one-by-one.

## Predictive evidence

Amount of deliberate practice (from diaries/retrospective estimates) predicts attained level for musicians and athletes; elite performers begin deliberate practice systematically *younger* than less accomplished ones. Chess skill correlates with total time devoted to studying chess (Charness et al. 1995).

## Relation to other concepts

Deliberate practice is the mechanism behind the [[10-Year Rule]] and the engine of the acquired-skill side of the [[Talent vs Deliberate Practice]] debate. It produces the domain-specific [[Maximal Adaptation to Task Constraints|adaptations]] — cognitive, perceptual-motor, physiological — documented in [[ericsson-lehmann-1996-expert-exceptional-performance|Ericsson & Lehmann (1996)]].
