# Availability Heuristic

One of the three core heuristics identified by [[tversky-kahneman-1974-heuristics-biases]].

## Definition

Frequency of a class or probability of an event is assessed by **the ease with which instances or occurrences can be brought to mind** — retrieved from memory, constructed mentally, or associated with other concepts.

**Canonical question type:** "How frequent is class X?" or "How likely is event Y?"

## Why It Works (Ecological Validity)

Instances of large, frequent classes *are* generally more retrievable than instances of rare classes. The heuristic exploits a real regularity in memory. The error is that retrievability is also affected by factors other than frequency — salience, recency, familiarity, vividness, and associative strength.

## Biases Produced

**Retrievability bias** — Classes with more famous or salient instances are judged larger. In celebrity list studies, the sex with more famous names was judged more numerous even when both sexes were equally represented. Heart attack risk is assessed by recalling acquaintances who had heart attacks, which is influenced by one's social network composition, not population rates.

**Search set bias** — Frequency is judged by ease of searching a particular category. Words beginning with R are judged more common than words with R in the third position — opposite of reality — because first-letter search is much easier than third-letter search. Applies to consonants (r, k) that are actually more frequent in the third position.

**Imaginability bias** — When instances must be generated (not retrieved), frequency is judged by ease of construction. Committee example: committees of 2 from a group of 10 seem more numerous than committees of 8 (median estimates: 70 vs. 20; correct: 45 for both). Constructing small committees is easier because disjoint sets work; constructing large committees is hard. Real-world: expedition risk is inflated by vividly imaginable dangers and deflated by hard-to-conceive hazards.

**[[Illusory Correlation]]** — Co-occurrence frequency is judged by strength of associative bond. Chapman & Chapman (1967/1969): subjects examining clinical data "rediscovered" unfounded associations (suspiciousness → peculiar eyes in draw-a-person tests) because these are strongly associated concepts. Effect was resistant to contradictory data and prevented detection of real correlations.

## Connection to Other Concepts

- **[[Publication Bias]]**: Media coverage of dramatic events (plane crashes, shark attacks) increases availability, inflating perceived frequency — a societal-level availability effect.
- **[[Aesthetic Standards in Science]]** ([[Giner-Sorolla 2012]]): Narrative conformity pressure means results that fit a compelling story are more "available" for publication — a field-level availability effect on the scientific literature.
- **[[Correlation-Intervention Gap (Behaviour)]]**: Salient correlational findings are more available to researchers when selecting intervention targets, contributing to the gap.
