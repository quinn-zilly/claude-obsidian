# Adaptive Stimulus

## Definition

An **adaptive stimulus** is "a demand, problem, disruption, threat, or opportunity that requires the team to purposefully and collectively change its behaviors and/or processes to either mitigate, overcome, or benefit from the situation" (Pearsall et al., 2025, p. 462).

A response is "required" in the sense that ignoring the stimulus will cause negative outcomes — in performance, affect, attitude, viability, expense, or missed opportunities.

## Scope

Adaptive stimuli include: environmental/task changes, reward structure changes, emerging threats, team membership/leadership changes, communications breakdowns, unexpected failures, competitive threats, and missed opportunities.

**Excluded**: small routine demands not requiring collective adaptation; proactive changes initiated by the team without an external impetus.

## Relationship to Change Type

The definition draws on **discontinuous change** (Weick & Quinn, 1999; Ning et al., 2023) — a concrete change with a clear starting point or impetus. This distinguishes adaptive stimuli from gradual drift or continuous improvement processes.

## Stimulus Typology Dimensions

Stimuli can be characterized along at least two dimensions that drive the adaptive process:

| Dimension | Low end | High end |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| **Proximity** | Temporally distant | Imminent/immediate |
| **Magnitude** | Minor, low-consequence | Severe, high-consequence |

And a third dimension driving response strategy:

| **Duration expectation** | Temporary (will revert) | Sustained (new normal) |

## Why Stimulus Type Matters

Different stimuli require categorically different collective responses. Collapsing across stimulus types — as most prior research has done — obscures which factors are effective for which demands. The stimulus-based perspective enables stimulus-specific action identification and cross-stimulus comparison.
