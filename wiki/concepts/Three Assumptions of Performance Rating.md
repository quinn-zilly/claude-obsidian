# Three Assumptions of Performance Rating

[[pulakos-2019-evolution-of-pm|Pulakos et al. (2019)]] argue that a century of rating research rests on three assumptions that **largely fail** in practice:

1. **Everyone has a stable "true" performance level.**
   Rater agreement is used as the proxy for capturing this true score — but interrater reliability sits around **.50** (Viswesvaran et al. 1996). Much disagreement is not error: employees genuinely **behave differently with different raters** (responsive to managers, dismissive of peers). This raises the deep question of whether a cross-rater "true performance" even *exists*.

2. **Raters can rate accurately.**
   Managers reliably sort employees into broad categories (capable / failing / exceptional) but **cannot** make the nuanced, multi-level, multi-factor judgments that overengineered scales demand. They see only a slice of behavior and often lack the expertise to judge what they do see.

3. **Raters are motivated to rate accurately.**
   Often false. [[Murphy & Cleveland]]'s **four competing goals** — task, interpersonal, strategic, internalized — mean accuracy is frequently not the driver. Managers rationally inflate ratings to keep good-but-imperfect employees engaged, and **retrofit** ratings to pay decisions rather than the reverse.

> [!key-insight] Implication
> If true performance may not exist, raters can't rate nuances, and aren't motivated to try — then demanding precise, differentiated ratings is a **false sense of confidence**. Simpler judgments matching the overall impression raters naturally form are usually sufficient. This underpins the [[Rating-less Reviews]] debate.
