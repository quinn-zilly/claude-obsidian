# Units Problem (Personality)

Nav: [[index]] | [[concepts/_index]]

The **units problem** (Allport): *what are the constructs used to describe personality, and how are they organized?* Allport called it the foundational question of personality psychology — no substantive research can proceed without a provisional answer. Definitions are consequential because they drive variable selection and measurement.

## Three views of what personality attributes *are*

| View | Attributes are… | Champion |
|---|---|---|
| **Realist** | Latent, biologically-driven dispositions that **exist objectively** in the person; self-report reveals biology | Allport; Five-Factor Theory (McCrae & Costa) |
| **Constructivist** | Perceiver-driven **reifications** shaped by bias, stereotype, lay theory; strong version denies trait validity | Mischel; D'Andrade |
| **Functionalist** | **Units of perception that serve a purpose** for the perceiver — "important to whom, for what?" | Saucier & Srivastava; Srivastava (2010) |

Strong constructivism was **not supported empirically** and its collapse revived personality research in the 1980s. The **functionalist** middle ground is Saucier & Srivastava's preferred stance: descriptions can correspond to real characteristics yet still reflect the perceiver's goals and context. Personality includes what we infer about a person's **worth or usefulness** for recurrently important social decisions (whom to hire, marry, trust).

## Why it matters here

The functionalist framing explains *why* lexical factors look the way they do (they aggregate perceivers' decision-relevant concerns) and connects to the [[Person–Situation Debate]] — much stable disposition may live at the level of **trait-in-situation** rather than trait-across-situations. Anchored by [[Saucier-Srivastava-2015-structural-models-personality|Saucier & Srivastava (2015)]].
