# Training as Catalyst (OJD)

**Training as catalyst** — the design principle that formal training programs should be built primarily to *trigger and accelerate* on-the-job development, rather than to maximize in-class learning outcomes.

## The Argument

If 90% of leader development occurs outside the classroom ([[Experience-Driven Development]]), then a well-designed training program should produce more learning *outside* the classroom than inside it.

This is a design target, not a description of typical programs. Most programs optimize for in-class learning (knowledge acquisition, skill practice, satisfaction ratings). Catalyst-design optimizes for what happens before and after.

## Catalyst Design Patterns (from Yost, 2013)

**Pre-training catalysts:**
- Assign leaders to meet with their boss: "What are my next career steps?"
- Assign leaders to meet with clients/employees before class; bring that data into workshop
- Leaders bring development plans for 2 direct reports (will practice building OJD plans in class)

**In-class catalysts:**
- Senior leaders share their own experience lessons (models OJD value)
- Workshop format: work on real business problems, coach each other (bring work *into* class)
- Identify the short list of key developmental experiences to pursue
- Practice building OJD dev plans for direct reports

**Post-training catalysts:**
- Peer coaching groups formed in class → continue meeting independently
- 6-month follow-up session: share what happened, lessons learned
- Boss conversations about applying specific skills/experiences

## Distinction from Transfer of Training

[[Training Transfer]] research asks: how do we get trainees to apply what they learned in class?

Catalyst design asks: how do we design the class so it triggers *more development* than the class itself contains?

The distinction matters because transfer research still treats the classroom as primary. Catalyst design treats experience as primary.
