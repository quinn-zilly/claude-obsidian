# Statistical Conclusion Validity

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]]

The validity of inferences about the **correlation (covariation)** between treatment and outcome — the first component of the [[Validity Typology]]. Two inferences are at stake: (1) *do* cause and effect covary, and (2) *how strongly*.

## Two kinds of error

- On existence: concluding covariation where none exists (**Type I**) or missing real covariation (**Type II**).
- On magnitude: over- or under-estimating effect size and the confidence that estimate warrants.

## Reporting stance

SCC prefer **effect sizes bounded by 95% confidence intervals**, followed by the exact p-value — over dichotomous NHST. NHST is widely misunderstood (p is not the probability the null is true) and says little about effect magnitude. It should play a smaller role but need not be banned.

## Nine threats

Low Statistical Power · Violated Assumptions of Statistical Tests · Fishing and the Error Rate Problem · Unreliability of Measures · Restriction of Range · Unreliability of Treatment Implementation · Extraneous Variance in the Setting · Heterogeneity of Units · Inaccurate Effect Size Estimation. See [[Threats to Validity]].

## Accepting the null

You **cannot prove the null** — any nonzero effect emerges with enough power, reliability, and sample size. When a no-effect hypothesis is the target (e.g. equivalence, absent side effect), maximize power, specify a minimally interesting effect size, and consider **equivalence testing**.
