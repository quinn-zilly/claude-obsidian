# Competency Models vs Experience (Leadership Development)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Learning & Development MOC]]

The central polemic of [[mccall-2010-recasting-leadership-development|McCall (2010)]] (and of Hollenbeck & McCall's "competence, not competencies" line): **experience makes a better foundation for leadership development than competency models do.**

## The case against competency models

- **What organizations actually want is *competence*, not a list of attributes.** Successful leaders have different styles (Kelleher, Welch, Mulcahy) — equifinality rules; there are equally effective but different ways to achieve the same outcome.
- A single set of competencies applied to all leaders creates a **common language** and an integrated HR system — but to the extent there is *no one best way to lead* and experience drives development, this focuses development effort **in the wrong place**.
- Competency models are **limited in breadth, usually general**, and imply a single attribute-set for something achievable by people with many different attributes. The "more-is-better" view ignores the complex relationships among strengths and weaknesses and the dynamics of [[Derailment|derailment]] (strengths becoming weaknesses).

## Why HR retreats to them anyway

The competency approach's **theoretical elegance** and **utility for integrating HR systems** (360, performance management, training) "trumps the inherent messiness of experience-based development." Lacking control over assignments — and given the ambiguity of using experience — HR finds competency models a **"comforting illusion":** attributes that can be developed with an integrated set of known tools.

## The alternative

Reframe development as acquiring the **expertise to meet the [[Five Demands of Leadership]]**, and put **[[Developmental Experiences|challenging experiences]]** (challenge + feedback + reflection) at the center. Even experience-flavored HR practices (job rotation, action-learning) often fail to exploit what is known about *how* experience teaches — job rotation without carefully chosen assignments is inefficient; action-learning teams can let learning take a backseat to solving the problem.

> [!note] Testing vs developing
> McCall's sharpest point: the "you have it or you don't" belief turns "throwing people into fires" into **testing** rather than **developing** — there's "little investment in helping people learn from the experience," and no mechanism to discover whether those who didn't "land on their feet" could have developed with help.

## Debate
- [[yost-plunkett-2010-ten-catalysts|Yost & Plunkett (2010)]]: don't abandon HR systems — **reform** them to hardwire on-the-job development. See [[Morgan McCall]] §Position in Debate.
