# Web 2.0 Technologies

## Definition

**Web 2.0 technologies** are participatory, user-generated web platforms — wikis, blogs, social-networking sites, tagging systems, and activity feeds — in which content is contributed and continuously revised by the userbase rather than published top-down. In the transactive-memory and knowledge-management literature they appear as a *technological substrate* for building and maintaining group cognition at scales larger than the face-to-face work group.

## Why It Matters for Transactive Memory

[[Transactive Memory Systems]] (TMS) develop naturally in small, co-located teams through shared experience and direct observation of *who knows what*. They do **not** scale cleanly to the organizational level: organizations are larger than work groups, fragmented into subgroups with limited cross-boundary communication, geographically distributed, and rich in tacit knowledge that is hard to access across distance.

Ren & Argote (2011) position Web 2.0 technologies as one of the **technological approaches** to extending TMS-like coordination across these boundaries — alongside expertise directories/repositories, email and messaging mining for expertise inference, and small-world networks for connecting expertise. The core problem these tools address is [[Expertise Location]]: sustaining accurate, organization-wide metaknowledge of who knows what when no one can directly observe everyone.

## How Each Technology Contributes

| Technology | TMS function | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Wikis** | Coordination + explicit memory | Capture and continuously revise shared knowledge; reveal contributors as de facto domain owners |
| **Blogs** | Credibility + expertise signalling | Members surface their thinking publicly, making expertise visible and assessable over time |
| **Social networks** | Specialization + location | Profiles, connections, and activity expose skill distributions and who is connected to whom |
| **Tagging / folksonomies** | Coordination | User-generated metadata makes distributed knowledge findable without central taxonomy |

The recurring theme: Web 2.0 platforms **auto-populate expertise maps** as a byproduct of normal participation. Rather than asking members to maintain a directory (which decays), the system infers a living map of specialization from contributions, edits, links, and tags.

## Strengths and Limits

> [!tip] Where they help
> Web 2.0 tools partially substitute for the direct observation that small co-located teams get for free. They externalize the *coordination* and *specialization-awareness* components of TMS into a persistent, searchable layer — valuable precisely where face-to-face TMS breaks down (large, dispersed, high-turnover organizations).

> [!warning] Where they fall short
> They mainly externalize **explicit** signals. The *credibility* dimension of TMS — trust in another's judgment — is harder to manufacture from profiles and edit histories. As [[Transactive Memory Systems]] notes, purely electronically-mediated communication weakens TMS in early team formation; technology supports utilization of an established TMS better than it builds one from scratch. A populated expertise map is necessary but not sufficient for the trust that makes members actually *rely* on located expertise.

## Connections

- [[Transactive Memory Systems]] — Web 2.0 tools are a technological route to organizational-level TMS, addressing the scaling problem direct-observation TMS cannot.
- [[Expertise Location]] — the primary capability these platforms enable: maintaining accurate, organization-wide "who knows what."
- [[Organizational Memory]] — wikis and tagged repositories act as a persistent explicit-knowledge store complementing the social TMS layer.
- [[Tacit vs Explicit Knowledge]] — Web 2.0 captures explicit signals well; tacit expertise and credibility resist full externalization.

## Open Questions (from Ren & Argote 2011)

Organizational-level TMS is flagged as under-researched, and the role of these technologies within it more so. Open issues include whether inferred expertise maps are accurate enough to act on, how Web 2.0-mediated TMS evolves and decays over time, and how the trust/credibility dimension can be supported (not just the structural specialization dimension) through technological means.
