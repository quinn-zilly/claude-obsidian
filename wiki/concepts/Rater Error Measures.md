# Rater Error Measures

For decades (~1920–1990), the quality of performance ratings was judged by **rater-error measures** — the "holy trinity":
- **[[Halo Error]]** — rating dimensions too similarly (one global impression bleeding across dimensions). Named by Thorndike (1920), the first PA article in *JAP*.
- **Leniency / severity** — ratings systematically too high or too low.
- **Central tendency / range restriction** — everyone rated near the middle.

The logic: fewer errors → more accurate ratings → better system. Training aimed to *reduce* these errors.

> [!key-insight] Why the trinity fell
> Murphy & Balzer (1986): traditional rater-error measures are **uncorrelated with the four accuracy indices** of Cronbach (1955). Reducing "errors" did not mean more accurate ratings — and error-avoidance training could actually **decrease** accuracy (Bernardin & Pence 1980). This ended the field's reliance on error measures and pushed it toward accuracy measures, then toward ratee **perceptions of fairness/accuracy**, and eventually to the social-context view of [[Performance Appraisal]].

The successor to error-avoidance training was [[Frame-of-Reference Training]].
