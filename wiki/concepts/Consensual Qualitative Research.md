# Consensual Qualitative Research (CQR)

A qualitative approach developed by counseling psychologists (Hill, Thompson & Williams 1997; Hill et al. 2005) studying psychotherapy processes. It integrates the descriptive richness of constructivist methods with a postpositivist reliance on **interpretive consensus** — research-team agreement plus an external auditor arriving at domains and core ideas. Its creators acted as [[Bricoleur|bricoleurs]], drawing on [[Grounded Theory|grounded theory]], phenomenology, and comprehensive process analysis.

## Paradigmatic location (per Ponterotto 2005)

CQR sits **between [[Postpositivism]] and [[Constructivism–Interpretivism]], leaning postpositivist** ("postpositivist–constructivist"):

- **Ontology**: postpositivist — consensus/auditor model implies one approximal reality.
- **Epistemology**: between postpositivism and constructivism — medium-length (30–60 min) semistructured interviews kept consistent across participants, so limited intense interaction.
- **Axiology**: leans constructivist — the team brackets expectations and biases.
- **Rhetoric**: between the two — domains/categories in tabular form with frequency labels (general / typical / variant); limited participant "voice."
- **Methodology**: between constructivism and postpositivism — full semistructured protocol covered each interview; verification of literature-based themes plus some discovery.

Ponterotto notes Hill et al.'s (2005) update drifted somewhat toward constructivism vs the original (1997) explication. Contrast [[Grounded Theory]], which lands more squarely in the constructivist camp.
