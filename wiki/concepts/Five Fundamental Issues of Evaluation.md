# Five Fundamental Issues of Evaluation

The organizing scheme from Shadish, Cook & Leviton (1991), adopted by [[Sechrest-Figueredo-1993-Program-Evaluation|Sechrest & Figueredo (1993)]] as encompassing "most of the activity in program evaluation today":

1. **Social programming** — how social programs and policies develop, improve, and change. Leads directly to [[Theory-Driven Evaluation]] (Chen; Chen & Rossi): make the program's causal theory explicit. Strong theory can *compensate for weak method* (Lipsey) — better treatments, measures, predictions; saves degrees of freedom.
2. **Knowledge construction** — the epistemology of learning about social action; the quantitative/qualitative dispute. Early evaluation assumed the two were *complementary*; what is new is the insistence that qualitative can *substitute* for quantitative.
3. **Valuing** — how value is attached to program descriptions; whether evaluation can be value-free. Sechrest & Figueredo caution that openly partisan (justice-serving) evaluation risks losing credibility: "begin with values, but demonstrate objectivity."
4. **Knowledge use** — how evaluation information modifies programs and policies. Utilization is often *conceptual* ("enlightenment," Weiss) rather than direct; little known about state/local or private-sector use.
5. **Evaluation practice** — the tactics and strategies evaluators actually follow under their constraints; centrally, [[Formative vs Summative Evaluation]].

These five points recur throughout the vault's evaluation cluster and structure the second half of Sechrest & Figueredo's review.
