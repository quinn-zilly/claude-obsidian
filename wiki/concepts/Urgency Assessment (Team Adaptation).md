# Urgency Assessment (Team Adaptation)

**Phase 2** of the [[Team Adaptation (Stimulus-Based Model)|Pearsall et al. (2025) stimulus-based model]].

## Definition

**Urgency** = the team's perceived need to prioritize a response, based on a combined assessment of:
- **Proximity** — how temporally close the consequences will be felt
- **Magnitude** — how large or severe those consequences will be

Teams must jointly assess and balance both dimensions to determine overall urgency and prioritize among multiple simultaneous demands.

## Proximity Effects

Proximal events appear more concrete and actionable (construal level theory; Trope & Liberman, 2003). Distal events feel abstract, less "real," making them harder to envision and act upon. This creates a dangerous pattern: teams under-attend to distant but growing threats until they become proximal, by which point optimal planning time is lost.

Examples: Koseoglu et al. (2017) — deadline changes late in work cycles harmed creativity; Geiger et al. (2021) — firefighters drove more slowly to less proximal alarms (cat on roof) but abandoned meals for urgent ones (car accident with casualties).

## Magnitude Effects

High-magnitude stimuli are more likely to "jolt" teams from existing habitual routines (Gersick & Hackman, 1990). Higher magnitude → greater collective attention, faster behavioral change. Zellmer-Bruhn (2003) found greater interruptions led to more knowledge acquisition; Porter et al. (2003) — high-magnitude demands led to more backup helping behavior.

DeRue et al. (2008) and Siegel Christian et al. (2014): loss of more "critical" team members prompted faster behavioral change.

## Emotional Component

Urgency is not purely cognitive. Stimuli perceived as urgent generate visceral emotional reactions (Stephens, 2021), acting as "alarm bells" that motivate faster response. Too much positive team affect may cause teams to underestimate crisis severity (Kaplan et al., 2013).

## Leadership Influence

Prevention-focused leaders overemphasize threat detection relative to opportunities; promotion-focused leaders may underweight threats. Leadership regulatory focus is a key moderator of what gets prioritized.

## Future Directions

- How do teams determine urgency across multiple *simultaneous* demands?
- Only one study (Stokes et al., 2010) has examined prioritization across multiple stimuli
- Teams' responses to co-occurring changes (downsizing + lost funding simultaneously)
- Opportunities are almost entirely unstudied relative to threats
- Extreme urgency contexts (Majchrzak et al., 2007 — Hurricane Katrina) may require "swift trust" over experience-based credibility
