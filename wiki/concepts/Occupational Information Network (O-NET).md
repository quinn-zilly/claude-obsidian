# Occupational Information Network (O*NET)

The **O*NET** is the U.S. Department of Labor's comprehensive, flexible occupational-information system developed to replace the [[Dictionary of Occupational Titles (DOT)]]. Built on the 1993 **APDOT Content Model**, it covers ~1,102 occupations in a public relational database (online.onetcenter.org).

## Structure

Nine questionnaires tap distinct descriptor domains: abilities, skills, knowledge, training/education, **generalized work activities (GWAs)**, work context, organizational context, occupational values, and work styles. Across them, ~300 pieces of job information were collected; separate factor analyses yielded **38 first-order factors**, which a second-order analysis reduced to **4 factors** (management/achievement, manual/physical, office work, technical vs interpersonal). An occupation can thus be characterized at 300, 38, or 4 levels of detail.

Design premise (per [[Sackett-Walmsley-Laczo-2013-job-and-work-analysis|Sackett et al.]]): **purpose drives the information needed**, so a wide range of descriptor types and rating scales is provided (e.g., skills rated on level, importance, and point-of-entry). It focuses on cross-occupation-comparable information rather than occupation-specific task detail.

## Critiques

> [!contradiction] Redundancy of importance vs level scales
> [[Sanchez-Levine-2012-rise-fall-job-analysis|Sanchez & Levine (2012)]] report O*NET importance and level scales are **largely redundant** (r ≈ 0.92–0.95 for GWAs), and that ability determinations rely on single-item scales of limited discriminant validity. The Social Security Administration judged O*NET unsuitable to replace the DOT for disability determination, prompting a separate occupational-information project.

A 2010 National Research Council panel judged O*NET a useful, widely accessed database and prioritized continued data quality and user service.
