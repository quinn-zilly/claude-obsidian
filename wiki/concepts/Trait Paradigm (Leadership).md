# Trait Paradigm (Leadership)

The earliest dominant perspective in leadership research, holding that leaders can be distinguished from non-leaders by stable personal characteristics — intelligence, personality traits, physical attributes.

## Origins
Emerged from WWI-era interest in intelligence testing (Terman 1916, Army Alpha). Early JAP work (Kohs & Irle 1920; Bingham & Davis 1924) found moderate links between intelligence and leadership but noted diminishing returns at higher tenure and intelligence levels.

## Peak and Backlash
By 1940, Bird (1940) catalogued 79 leadership traits from ~20 studies. But the proliferation of inconsistent traits fueled skepticism:
- Murphy (1941): "Leadership does not reside in a person"
- Jenkins (1947): No single trait consistently sets leaders apart across situations
- **Stogdill (1948)**: Influential review arguing situational factors primarily determine leader emergence — catalyzed the shift to behavioral approaches

## Revival via Meta-Analysis
The trait paradigm was revitalized in the second and third waves:
- **Lord, De Vader & Alliger (1986)**: Meta-analysis showed prior inconsistencies attributable to sampling error; traits reliably predict leadership perceptions
- **Judge et al. (2002)**: Big Five → leadership emergence (R=.53) and effectiveness (R=.39)
- **Judge et al. (2004)**: Intelligence → leadership; corrected ρ = .21–.27; moderated by stress and leader directiveness

## Key Insight
Traits matter most for *leadership perceptions* (emergence) and somewhat less for *leadership effectiveness* (between-group performance outcomes). These criteria are often conflated in research.
