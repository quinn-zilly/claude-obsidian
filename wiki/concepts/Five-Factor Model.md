# Five-Factor Model

Nav: [[index]] | [[concepts/_index]]

The FFM (Big Five) provides the dominant personality taxonomy in organizational research: **Neuroticism (N), Extraversion (E), Openness (O), Agreeableness (A), Conscientiousness (C)**. Originated with Tupes & Christal (1958); replicated widely.

## Work Performance Validity

Barrick & Mount (1991) meta-analysis: **Conscientiousness** = best overall predictor across job families; **Extraversion** = predictive for management and sales.

Notable variability within trait–job family pairings suggested untapped situational specificity → foundation for [[Trait Activation Theory]].

## TAT Implications

All five dimensions show TAT-based moderator effects; significance rates (2011–2019 review):

| Dimension | Overall % sig | Demands only % sig |
|---|---|---|
| Emotional stability | 82% | 90% |
| Neuroticism/NA | 79% | 100% |
| Openness | 59% | 72% |
| Agreeableness | 59% | 64% |
| Extraversion | 53% | 52% |
| Conscientiousness | 45% | 49% |

Neuroticism/emotional stability especially sensitive to work stress. Openness especially sensitive to demands for creativity/innovation.

**Bidirectionality of agreeableness in management**: positively related to performance in some managerial jobs, negatively in others — classic TAT bidirectionality case. Meta-analyses ignoring this underestimate personality validity.
