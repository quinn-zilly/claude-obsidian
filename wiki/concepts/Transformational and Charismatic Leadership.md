# Transformational and Charismatic Leadership

"New School" leadership approaches that revitalized the field beginning in the 1980s. Dominant perspective from ~1985 to present. Focuses on leaders who inspire followers, activate identities and values, and produce performance "beyond expectations."

## Origins
- **Burns (1978)**: Political scientist; introduced transformational vs. transactional distinction; transformational leaders develop followers and model higher ethical standards
- **Bass (1985)**: Incorporated charisma as a component; developed multidimensional theory of transactional/transformational leadership

## Core Dimensions (Bass 1985)
- **Idealized Influence (Charisma)**: Leader as role model; follower identification
- **Inspirational Motivation**: Articulating compelling vision
- **Intellectual Stimulation**: Challenging followers to think differently
- **Individualized Consideration**: Attending to each follower's development needs

Contrasted with **Transactional Leadership** (contingent reward and management-by-exception).

## Key Empirical Findings (JAP)

### Effects on Outcomes
- **Hater & Bass (1988)** [Table 1]: Charisma and individualized consideration distinguish top from ordinary performers
- **Howell & Avolio (1993)** [Table 1]: Transformational leadership → business unit performance; internal locus of control as antecedent
- **Bass et al. (2003)** [Table 1]: Transformational → team potency and cohesion → team performance
- **Judge & Piccolo (2004)** [Table 1]: Transformational and transactional contingent reward produce similar effects on performance; augmentation partially supported
- **Dirks & Ferrin (2002)**: Trust in leadership mediates/moderates transformational leadership → outcomes

### Follower Mechanisms
- **Kark, Shamir & Chen (2003)**: Dual effects — personal identification with leader → dependence; social identification with team → empowerment
- **Brown & Treviño (2009)**: Socialized charisma → leader–follower value congruence
- **Yaffe & Kark (2011)**: Leader OCB role modeling → follower OCB
- **Den Hartog & Belschak (2012)**: Low self-efficacy followers benefit most from transformational leadership, especially with high job autonomy
- **Bono et al. (2007)**: Transformational leaders produce more positive follower emotions; reduce follower stress

### Antecedents
- **Personality**: Big Five explains <5% of variance in transformational leadership (Bono & Judge 2004; Judge & Bono 2000)
- **Leader identity**: Collective identity → transformational behaviors; relational identity → consideration; individual identity → abusive behaviors (Johnson et al. 2012)

### Developability
- **Barling et al. (1996)**: Short training interventions increase transformational ratings and unit performance
- **Dvir et al. (2002)**: Field experiment confirming transformational leadership can be developed
- **Avolio et al. (2009)**: Meta-analysis across 75 years — transformational, transactional, and traditional styles all developable

## Criticisms
- **van Knippenberg & Sitkin (2013)**: Fundamental problems with conceptualization and measurement; unclear theory linking component dimensions to mediators and performance
- **Yukl (2012)**: Conceptual and measurement problems persist
- Personality explains little variance — suggests either social construction or other antecedents needed
- Charisma may be in the eye of the beholder more than the leader's actual behavior
