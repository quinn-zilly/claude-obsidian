# Cascading Goals & SMART

A standard feature of formal [[Performance Management]] systems: **cascading goals** link the organization's strategic goals down to each employee, so individual objectives align with firm strategy (Rodgers & Hunter 1991). Rooted in MBO (Drucker) and [[Goal-Setting Theory]] (Locke & Latham). Employees are typically trained to set **SMART** goals (Specific, Measurable, Aligned, Realistic, Time-bound).

## Practical challenges (Pulakos & O'Leary 2010)
- Cascading is time-consuming and unfamiliar to many managers.
- Goals get distorted as they cascade — "like a game of telephone."
- Objective **quality varies wildly** and is rarely comparable across similarly-situated employees.
- Year-long goals can't anticipate mid-year change; updates are rarely done in practice.
- Achieving an easy goal often scores higher than partially delivering a hard one.

> [!contradiction] Goal-setting works in the lab, but PM goals may not
> Goal-setting research is strong — but mostly **laboratory** work. In real PM: (a) little evidence that goal-achievement measures are reliable/valid/fair; (b) year-long horizons are too long for motivational effect (shorter is better — Latham & Locke 2007); (c) when goals are *rewarded*, employees angle for **easy** goals, making PM goals less aspirational than learning-context goals. Hence the reform push toward **agile, near-term goals**.
