# Behavioural Access Interventions

Interventions that increase the material, logistic, or structural availability of a desired behaviour. The *most effective* category of behavioural change intervention across all domains (large effect, mean OR≈4.89).

## Definition

> "Material or logistic resources to facilitate the performance of a behaviour." — Albarracín et al. 2024 (Table 3)

Access determinants reflect a person's position in the social hierarchy and physical environment — whether the desired behaviour is literally available to them.

## Correlational Evidence

Small associations with behaviour overall (r≈0.16–0.26):
- Recycling bin ownership → recycling: r=0.16
- Home ownership → recycling: r=0.24
- Income → healthy pregnancy behaviour: r=0.26
- Price of pesticides → environmentally friendly application: d=−0.36

The small correlational size is partly a measurement artefact (access is usually dichotomous in the environment).

## Intervention Efficacy

**Large** (OR≈4.89) — the largest mean effect of any category.

### Specific interventions

- **Vaccination site access** (transportation, proximity): doubles coverage (S. Liu et al.)
- **Co-payment reduction for medication**: large increases in adherence
- **Health insurance provision**: strong effects on care-seeking behaviours
- **Basic income**: structural poverty-reduction to enable health behaviours
- **Default settings** (choice architecture): organ donation opt-out defaults (d=0.68)
- **Alcohol taxation**: large effect on use reduction (OR=5.92)
- **Microfinancing**: negligible effect on women's household financial control (d=−0.01) — nuance: not all structural interventions succeed

> [!key-insight] Policy Implication
> Access interventions outperform persuasion/attitude-change approaches by large margins. Policymakers who focus only on changing minds (knowledge, beliefs, attitudes) leave the most powerful lever untouched.

## Relationship to Other Determinants

Access is theoretically grounded in [[Social Cognitive Theory]] (environmental constraints determine behaviour). It operates differently from social norm or individual-level interventions: rather than *motivating* behaviour, it *removes obstacles* to an already-motivated or at least receptive person.

Access + [[Social Support (Behaviour)]] interventions are the two structural levers with medium-to-large effects; together they represent the core of an evidence-based public health strategy.
