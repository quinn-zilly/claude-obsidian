# Microfoundations

Microfoundations are the individual-level (or sub-collective-level) mechanisms that explain how collective-level outcomes arise. They answer the **aggregation question**: how do individual actions, cognitions, and choices combine to produce organizational properties?

## Core Definition (Barney & Felin 2013)

> Microfoundations specify the causal mechanisms linking lower-level inputs to higher-level outcomes. They are not reductionism (which denies emergence) but rather a demand for causal specification in theories that currently treat aggregation as unproblematic.

## Two Modes of Aggregation

### 1. Additive Aggregation
The collective property is the sum, average, or simple function of individual properties.
- Example: mean employee conscientiousness → team performance
- Empirical strategy: individual-level measurement, aggregated statistically

### 2. Emergent Aggregation
The collective property is *qualitatively different* from any individual property; it arises from interaction patterns, structural configurations, or dynamic processes.
- Example: team psychological safety, organizational routines, culture
- Empirical strategy: relational data, process studies, mixed methods

**The key methodological implication**: emergent aggregation cannot be assessed through simple aggregation of individual-level surveys. It requires understanding the interaction structure.

## Four Half-Truths Corrected (Barney & Felin 2013)

| Common Claim | Why It's Only Partially True |
|--------------|------------------------------|
| "Microfoundations are about individuals only" | Sub-collective entities (dyads, teams) can be microfoundations for higher-level collectives |
| "Microfoundations require methodological individualism" | Network structures and role systems are legitimate microfoundational elements |
| "Collectivist theories lack microfoundations" | They implicitly assume them; making them explicit is the contribution |
| "Microfoundations are reductionist" | Emergent aggregation is anti-reductionist |

## Relevance to Knowledge Management

The major KM theories have under-specified microfoundations:

- [[Knowledge Integration]] (Zahra et al.) — what individual-level processes enable multi-unit knowledge integration? [[zahra-2020-knowledge-integration]] explicitly identifies this gap
- [[Organizational Learning]] (Argote) — the three subprocesses (creation, retention, transfer) are collective; their microfoundations (individual learning, memory, communication acts) are underspecified
- [[Knowledge-Based View of the Firm]] — how does individual specialist knowledge add up to firm-level competitive advantage?

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[Transactive Memory Systems]] — one of the few explicit microfoundational theories in organizational knowledge research: "who knows what" is an emergent property of group interaction
- [[Learning Curves]] — learning curve effects are collective; their microfoundations are individual learning and knowledge retention patterns

## Status in Organizational Research

As of 2013 (Barney & Felin), microfoundations was an ascendant research agenda. Subsequent work has begun specifying microfoundations for dynamic capabilities, absorptive capacity, and knowledge integration — but the field is still developing measurement approaches for emergent aggregation.
