# Coherent Pattern Matching

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]]

One of the three principles [[William R. Shadish|SCC]] use to make alternative causes implausible in a [[Quasi-Experimental Design]]. A **complex, differentiated prediction** is made about the outcome such that few alternative explanations could produce the same pattern. The more elaborate the predicted pattern that is successfully confirmed, the less likely a rival cause generated it — and the stronger the causal inference. (Also called *coherence*, Rosenbaum; *pattern matching*, Campbell & Trochim.)

## The detective analogy

Scriven's murder-investigation metaphor: detectives succeed because of a **salient effect** (a body), a **pattern of clues** fixing time and manner (multiple posttests), and the ability to link clues to a criminal's **modus operandi** (known alternative causes). Pathologists and epidemiologists reason the same way — matching a data pattern to signatures in the literature.

## The retrospective/prospective asymmetry

The analogy is strongest when the **effect is known and the cause is searched for retrospectively** (as in forensics/epidemiology). Most quasi-experiments are the reverse — the **cause is known, the effects searched for prospectively** — where an innovation's pattern of effects is often unknown. Adding multiple posttests prospectively can *increase* Type I error, because humans over-find patterns even in random data. So the predicted pattern must be **specified in advance** and be **unique** to the hypothesized cause.

## Instantiations in these chapters

- [[Nonequivalent Dependent Variable]] — predict A moves, B doesn't.
- **Predicted interaction** — Seaver's teacher-expectancy study predicted a specific crossover among four sibling/teacher cells.
- **Removed / repeated treatments** — outcome must track the introduce-remove-reintroduce schedule.
- **Crossover outcomes** in the [[Nonequivalent Control Group Design]] — the hardest pattern for scaling/regression artifacts to fake.
