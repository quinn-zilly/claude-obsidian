# PFP Intensity

**Definition (Fulmer et al., 2023):** "How large the payoff is to performance under PFP" — the *magnitude* of the pay-performance relationship, expressed as how sensitive actual payouts are to differences in performance and how large the potential payouts are relative to overall compensation.

PFP is not simply present or absent. Its intensity exists on a continuum and is one of the most understudied aspects of compensation research.

---

## Why It Matters

PFP intensity mediates the core trade-off in incentive design:

- **Higher intensity** → stronger [[Incentive Effect (Compensation)]] and [[Sorting Effect (Compensation)]] → better performance on incentivized metrics
- **Higher intensity** → greater risk of unintended consequences → [[Equal Compensation Principle]] violations, gaming, excessive risk-taking

The relationship is not linear: moderate intensity may capture most of the motivational benefit while limiting unintended consequences.

---

## Annual Merit Pay vs. Real PFP Intensity

In practice, annual merit pay systems have *extremely low* effective PFP intensity:
- Average merit increase: 3–4% of salary
- 85% of employees rated 3 or 4 on a 5-point scale → difference between adjacent ratings ≈ $800/year on an $80,000 salary

This near-flat pay-performance gradient provides little incentive effect. Real PFP intensity operates through:
1. **Bonuses** tied to specific strategic goals
2. **Promotions** — modal mid-point progression between adjacent pay grades = 15%; 3 promotions = 1.52× pay increase; 1-pt rating advantage → 48% more promotions over 6 years (Gerhart & Milkovich, 1989)
3. **Long-term incentives** (stock, deferred compensation)

---

## Monitoring Requirements

PFP programs with higher intensity or graduated payouts that increase with performance require *closer monitoring* for unintended consequences than low-intensity programs.

**Novartis case:** High-intensity individual incentives tied solely to prescription volume → $678M DOJ settlement. Response: (1) added behavioral values ratings to the formula; (2) capped bonus at 35% of compensation (i.e., reduced intensity).

---

## Research Gap

Most PFP research operationalizes PFP as a binary (present/absent) rather than capturing its intensity. The field needs research that theoretically and empirically accounts for the magnitude and shape of the pay-performance gradient and its interactions with individual and situational moderators.
