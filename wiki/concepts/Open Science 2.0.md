# Open Science 2.0

A target state for the research ecosystem proposed by [[thibault-2023-open-science-2-0|Thibault et al. (2023)]], by analogy to **Web 2.0**. Where Web 2.0 turned passive web consumers into active participants, Open Science 2.0 should turn researchers from passive *sharers* of outputs into active *collaborators* on each other's open scholarship.

## The two criteria

A research ecosystem is "Open Science 2.0" when:

1. The vast majority of research products **and processes** are openly available; **and**
2. Scientific actors **directly and regularly interact** with that open scholarship to increase research impact and rigor.

## 1.0 vs 2.0

**Open Science 1.0** is a retronym for criterion 1 alone — widespread openness without the collaborative interaction layer. The authors note we are not yet close to even 1.0 (data sharing remains uncommon), so 2.0 is aspirational, used as a lens to judge whether current initiatives aim high enough.

> [!note] Means, not an end
> The framework's punchline: openness is **necessary but not sufficient**. A study can be fully open and still poorly designed. Without quality control and genuine reuse, openness becomes box-ticking. See recommendation 7 in [[thibault-2023-open-science-2-0]].

## Four requirements

1. **Modular, dynamic research record** — share components as they're ready; link via persistent IDs; version control and forking; a "record of versions," not a "version of record."
2. **Standardization and interoperability** — shared structures/vocabularies/metadata so humans and machines integrate data (GenBank/UniProt/AlphaFold as the gold standard).
3. **Ongoing quality control** — distributed, partly automated, throughout the cycle, not just at the end.
4. **Reorganization of scientific labor** — task specialization across larger teams (see [[Big Team Science]], [[CRediT]]).
