# Self-Categorization and Self-Stereotyping

The mechanism (Turner 1984, 1985) by which [[Social Identification|identification]] reshapes the self. Just as classifying *others* into a category produces stereotypical perceptions of them, classifying **oneself** into a category and identifying with it produces the **attribution of the category's prototypical characteristics to oneself**.

## Depersonalization

Self-stereotyping amounts to **depersonalization of the self** — the individual comes to be seen (by self and others) as an *exemplar of the group* rather than a unique person. Consequences:

- Increased perceived **similarity** to other group members.
- Increased **conformity** to group norms.
- Internalization of and adherence to group values/norms; homogeneity in attitudes and behavior.

## Link to Conformity Pressure

Per Turner (1985; Hogg & Turner 1987), the self-stereotyping occasioned by **psychological grouping** ([[Psychological Group]]) leads one to *expect* attitudinal and perceptual agreement with group members. Disagreement triggers doubt → attitudinal/perceptual change. Thus a newcomer's perceptions gravitate toward the group's (relevant to socialization and subunit identification).

## Why It Matters for Organizations

Self-stereotyping is the bridge from **identification → internalization**: in adopting prototypical group characteristics, the identifier takes on the group's values without needing direct value-instruction. This underlies [[ashforth-mael-1989-social-identity-theory-organization|Ashforth & Mael's]] indirect path *socialization → identification → internalization*.
