# Ultimate Criterion

Thorndike's (1949) term for the **full conceptual domain of performance** — everything that ultimately defines success on the job. It is *ultimate* because one cannot look beyond it for any further standard by which to judge outcomes.

Key properties:

- **Strictly conceptual** — embodies "true," "total," "long-term," "ultimate worth" to the organization. It **cannot be measured or observed** directly.
- Still essential: it anchors the **[[Criterion Relevance, Deficiency & Contamination|relevance]]** of every operational measure. Documenting the conceptual stage clearly makes operational criterion choices intelligible (Astin 1964).
- Impractical for day-to-day use, so **substitute criteria** — immediate, intermediate, summary — are used; all are *partial* approximations (see [[Dynamic Criteria]]).

Example (Cascio & Aguinis 2011): a salesperson's ultimate criterion includes lifetime sales volume, all new accounts over a career, customer loyalty built, influence on colleagues' morale/records, and effectiveness in planning, expense control, and reporting.
