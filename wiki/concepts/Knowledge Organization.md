# Knowledge Organization

## Definition

The structure of connections between pieces of knowledge in memory. Determines how efficiently knowledge can be retrieved and how appropriately it is applied.

## Core Claim (CMU Eberly)

Students naturally form connections between knowledge pieces. When those connections are **accurately and meaningfully organized**, retrieval and application are effective. When connections are **inaccurate or random**, retrieval fails and transfer breaks down.

## What Good Organization Looks Like

- Hierarchical and causal structures (not just lists)
- Deep features drive categorization (not surface features)
- Schemas and mental models that support analogical transfer (Holyoak 1984)
- Chunking: experts store patterns as single units, freeing working memory

## What Poor Organization Looks Like

- Knowledge in isolated silos (can't transfer across contexts)
- Superficial associations (memorized without structural understanding)
- Random links — recall is cue-dependent and brittle

## Instructional Implications

- Explicitly teach organizational frameworks, not just content
- Use concept maps, outlines, and graphic organizers to make structure visible
- Show worked examples that reveal the *structure* of a problem, not just the solution
- Compare and contrast cases to build discriminating schemas
