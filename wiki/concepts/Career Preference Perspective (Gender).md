# Career Preference Perspective (Gender)

One of the three perspectives in **[[kossek-su-wu-2017-opting-out-pushed-out|Kossek, Su & Wu (2017)]]** — the leading **"opting-out" / pull** account.

## Core claim
Gendered career paths follow from the interaction between women's **interests, values, and goals** and characteristics of the **work environment**. Drawing on **[[Person–Environment Fit|P–E fit]]** theory, people choose environments congruent with their preferences; women "opt out" of jobs/fields perceived as a poor fit.

## Evidence
- **Interests**: Su, Rounds & Armstrong (2009) meta-analysis (503,188 respondents) — large gender difference on the **things–people dimension** (men → realistic/things, d = .84; women → social/people, d = −.68). Explains underrepresentation in "things-oriented" STEM.
- **Communal goals**: women endorse communal (other-caring) goals and forgo low-communion opportunities even outside STEM ("leaning out"; McCarty et al. 2014). Goal-congruity account (Diekman et al. 2010).
- **Values**: preference theory (Hakim 2000) — women more likely to prioritize work–family balance; differences intensify at parenthood (Ferriman et al. 2009).

> [!note] Ambition is not the gap
> Gender differences in *leadership interest* are small-to-negligible and shrinking (Su et al. 2009); women aspire to leadership as much as men (Eagly 2013). Ambition is **constrained by low communal affordance** in leadership roles, not absent.

## Interventions
Redesign jobs, work environment, and culture to include more **relational/communal** elements; highlight a role's communal affordance; increase mentoring/sponsorship. Caution (Bailyn 2011): firms position women's support as "accommodation" within existing norms rather than fundamental work redesign — placing the burden on women to "fit in."
