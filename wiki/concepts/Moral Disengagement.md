# Moral Disengagement

Nav: [[index]] | [[Human Agency]]

Moral agency has **dual aspects** — *inhibitive* (power to refrain from behaving inhumanely) and *proactive* (power to behave humanely). Self-sanctions keep conduct aligned with personal standards, but these self-regulatory mechanisms **only operate when enlisted**. **Moral disengagement** = the psychosocial maneuvers by which moral self-reactions are selectively decoupled from inhumane conduct.

## Mechanisms (by locus in the control process)

- **Reconstruing the conduct** — moral justification (portraying harm as serving worthy purposes), sanitizing/euphemistic language, advantageous comparison with worse acts.
- **Obscuring agency** — diffusion and displacement of responsibility.
- **Disregarding consequences** — ignoring, minimizing, or disputing injurious effects.
- **Devaluing the victim** — dehumanization, attribution of blame.

High moral disengagers experience low guilt over harmful conduct, are less prosocial, and are more prone to vengeful rumination (Bandura et al. 1996). Selective disengagement lets otherwise considerate people perpetrate transgressions in specific spheres.
