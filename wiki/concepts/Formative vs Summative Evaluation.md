# Formative vs Summative Evaluation

Distinction originating with **[[Michael Scriven]]** (1967), discussed in [[Sechrest-Figueredo-1993-Program-Evaluation|Sechrest & Figueredo (1993)]] as two parts of one process:

- **Formative evaluation** — guides program *development* to produce the best possible version of an intervention. Answers "How can we be better?"
- **Summative evaluation** — determines whether that best version has effects commensurate with its goals, costs, and effort. Answers the harder "How do you know you are any good at all?"

## The drift toward formative

Sechrest & Figueredo detect a **shift toward formative** (and qualitative) evaluation. Reasons:

- Summative evaluation is *hard*: it requires supporting a causal inference — strong design, adequate sample size and statistical power, long time commitment — and effects don't occur instantly.
- Formative findings are *more comforting*. Rossi's near-"metallic law": the expected value of an impact assessment's outcome is "zero or close to it," so summative work is discouraging. Formative work at least concludes "the program can be improved."
- **Organizations resist self-summative evaluation** — it questions their reason for existence (few summative studies of university education exist). As formative work grows, more evaluators are employed *within* the organizations they evaluate.

Evidence cited: whole issues of *New Directions for Program Evaluation* on training, AIDS prevention, and homelessness contained *no* outcome reports; only 12 of 51 sampled AEA-1991 paper titles suggested summative data.

Ties to [[Strength and Integrity of Treatment]] (quantifying the intervention itself) and the [[Ecological Paradigm in Evaluation]] account of the evaluator's changing role.
