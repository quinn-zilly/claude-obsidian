# Job Evaluation

**Job evaluation** is the systematic, objective process of comparing one job to another within an organisation to establish job levels / a hierarchy and, ultimately, a defensible pay structure. Its outcome is **job sizing** — measuring the relative "size" or "weight" of each job.

> [!key-insight] Grade the job, not the person
> Job evaluation ignores individual characteristics, personality, performance, individual effort, and labour-market conditions. Those enter only later, at the salary-structure stage. This impersonality is what makes it defensible and legally useful.

## Purpose & benefits

A logical graded hierarchy; reduced inequality; control over wage/salary administration; a common reference point for negotiation and collective bargaining; and compliance with **equal-pay-for-work-of-equal-value** legislation.

## Two stages (Bussin & Swart-Opperman)

1. **Grading** — grade the job in its current state, impersonally, ignoring incumbents. Rule-bound and inflexible.
2. **Salary structure** — place individuals on the pay scale; personal qualities, performance, skill scarcity now count. Flexible. → [[Salary Structure]].

## Categorisation of systems

- **Basis of comparison**: job-vs-job, or job-vs-scale.
- **Method of analysis**: whole job, or job elements/factors.
- Examples: factor comparison (HAY, IPE); points methods (Peromnes, Paterson Points); ranking, benchmarking, market pricing; classification (Paterson, TASK).

## Key facts

- All popular systems produce nearly identical hierarchies — **~0.94 correlation** across systems (~6% of jobs differ).
- "No such thing as a scientific method" — all are *systematic* approaches; good implementation and ongoing management matter more than the system chosen.
- **Major cause of collapse**: upgrading a job without a corresponding change in job content.

## Distinction from job analysis

[[Traditional Job Analysis|Job analysis]] examines and describes job *content*; job evaluation grades job *worth/complexity*. Job descriptions (analysis output) are the input to grading.
