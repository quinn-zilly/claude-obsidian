# Cue Feedback Types

Three feedback types studied in multiple-cue probability learning within the [[Lens Model]] paradigm — they differ sharply in what they teach and how well people learn from them.

| Type | What it tells the judge | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Outcome feedback (OF)** | The criterion value for each case ("you were right/wrong") | Weak; helps only simple/few-cue tasks; can **degrade** consistency once familiarity exists |
| **Cognitive feedback (CF)** | The judge's *own* decision policy (their cue weights) | Modest |
| **Task-information feedback (TIF)** | The *true* cue–criterion relations in the environment ("feed-forward") | **Most effective** |

## The headline finding (Karelaia & Hogarth 2008)

**Task-information feedback is the most effective form** — it magnifies the positive effect of learning on achievement, matching ($G$), and consistency ($R_s$), alone or combined with other feedback. Other feedback types are largely unhelpful and can even be detrimental for acquiring nonlinear knowledge.

**Outcome feedback is double-edged**: beneficial for consistency only when the judge is *unfamiliar* with the task (delivered on early trials), detrimental once familiarity exists, and only relevant in few-cue tasks. It doesn't move achievement, matching, or linear cognitive ability.

## Why

Outcome feedback is uninformative about *which cues to weight* — under probabilistic cue–criterion relations it adds noise and can impede development of correct task knowledge. Task-information feedback supplies the ecology's structure directly, which is exactly what raises matching ($G$).

## Implication

To improve human judgment, **teach the task structure**, don't just report whether each decision was right. This generalizes the "feedback ≠ learning" caution in many applied training settings.
