# Incentive Effect (Compensation)

One of two core mechanisms by which [[Pay-for-Performance]] affects organizational performance.

**Definition:** The incentive effect describes how PFP changes the *behavior and performance of the current workforce* — primarily through its impact on motivation. Higher PFP intensity increases expected payoff to performance, thereby increasing effort and goal-directed behavior.

Contrast with: [[Sorting Effect (Compensation)]] — which changes *who* the workers are, not how current workers behave.

---

## Theoretical Grounding

Multiple theories describe the incentive effect:

- **Expectancy theory** — PFP creates instrumentality between performance and valued outcomes; higher PFP intensity strengthens the performance→pay linkage
- **Equity theory** — performance-based pay is the most legitimate allocation criterion; failure to link pay to performance creates perceived inequity
- **Goal-setting theory** — PFP can set and reinforce performance goals; digital documentation of goals may enhance this mechanism
- **Agency theory** — PFP aligns agent interests with principal interests; higher intensity increases motivation but imposes risk-bearing costs on agents

---

## Empirical Evidence

Kim et al. (2022) meta-analysis: even under random assignment (no sorting), incentive plans produce +0.54 SD (~24%) higher performance vs. fixed pay. Net advantage ≈ 20% after accounting for payout costs.

**Caveat:** Random-assignment designs isolate the incentive effect but cannot capture [[Sorting Effect (Compensation)]]. Cadsby et al. (2007) found that sorting accounted for ~74% of total PFP advantage — implying the incentive effect alone is a substantial underestimate of total PFP impact.

---

## Limits and Failure Modes

High-intensity incentive effects can trigger unintended consequences when the [[Equal Compensation Principle]] is violated (Milgrom & Roberts, 1992): employees over-focus on incentivized metrics and neglect the rest. Risk-taking, gaming, and quality neglect are common failure modes.

**Intrinsic motivation concern:** Despite widespread belief that PFP crowds out intrinsic motivation for creative/interesting work, Gerhart & Fang (2015) and Kim et al. (2022) find little evidence for this effect in organizational settings.
