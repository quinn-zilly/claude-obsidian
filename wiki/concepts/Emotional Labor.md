# Emotional Labor

Nav: [[index]] | [[Control Theory (Self-Regulation)]]

Hochschild (1983); Grandey (2000). Employees managing felt or displayed emotions in accordance with organizationally defined display rules. Integrated by Grandey (2000) with Gross (1998) emotion regulation framework and Affective Events Theory (Weiss & Cropanzano 1996).

## Two Strategies

| | Surface Acting | Deep Acting |
|---|---|---|
| Type | Response-focused | Antecedent-focused |
| Mechanism | Change external display only | Change internal felt emotion ("faking in good faith") |
| Speed | Faster to deploy | Slower; requires time to modify internal state |
| Costs | Exhaustion, depersonalization, low job attitudes, reduced attention, turnover | Lower costs; sense of accomplishment |

## Key Findings

- **Surface acting costs:** emotional exhaustion (Grandey 2003), depersonalization (Brotheridge & Grandey 2002), reduced attentional resources (Goldberg & Grandey 2007), turnover (Chau et al. 2009)
- **Autonomy buffer:** high autonomy → no exhaustion following surface acting (Grandey et al. 2005; Johnson & Spector 2007)
- **Rest breaks:** mitigate costs + amplify benefits of emotional labor (Trougakos et al. 2008)
- **Deep acting benefits:** sense of personal accomplishment (Brotheridge & Grandey 2002)

## Display Rules as Goals (Diefendorff & Gosserand 2003)

Display rules function as standards in a control theory loop. Display rule commitment moderates display rule perceptions → surface/deep acting → positive affective delivery (Gosserand & Diefendorff 2005). Motivational processes for emotional goals parallel those for task goals.

## Connections

- [[Control Theory (Self-Regulation)]]: emotional goals = standards for emotion control loops
- [[Self-Determination Theory]]: autonomy satisfies need → buffering exhaustion consistent with need-support
- [[Resource Allocation Theory (Motivation)]]: surface acting depletes attentional resources
