# Policy Capturing

Quantitative modeling of an individual judge's decision-making strategy. Also called *judgment analysis* (Stewart 1988). Widely used in I/O psychology for hiring, salary, performance appraisal, medical diagnosis, and expert judgment studies.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify cues the judge uses
2. Code cue values across a set of cases
3. Collect judge's evaluations/ratings for same cases
4. Estimate regression: J = b₀ + b₁x₁ + b₂x₂ + … + bᵢxᵢ
5. Interpret weights as judge's "policy"

## Established Generalizations

From Brehmer & Brehmer (1988), Slovic & Lichtenstein (1971):

- Judges actually use **few cues**, and their use is **adequately modeled by simple first-order linear models**
- Judges **describe** themselves as using complex, nonlinear, interactive strategies (self-insight is poor)
- **Test-retest reliability** is modest
- **Inter-judge agreement** often low, even among experts

> These generalizations are working hypotheses for new applications, not settled fact — methodology has important limitations.

## Methodological Problems

**Cue identification**: judges may be biased by factors they won't report (or don't know about). Simply asking is unreliable.

**Cue coding**: translating file inspection into scale scores requires significant work (Doyle & Thomas 1995 = exemplary procedure).

**Representative design violation**: orthogonal cue designs destroy natural cue intercorrelations → distorts judge's normal policy (see [[Lens Model]]). Random sample of actual cases is better but introduces truncation problems (e.g., only hired applicants have performance data).

**Weight interpretation**: regression weights are conceptually difficult to interpret (Stevenson, Busemeyer & Naylor 1991).

**Environmental representativeness**: pool parameters change over time (economy, self-selection, educational practices) → model may be temporally specific.

## Organizational Applications

- Salary determination (Sherer, Schwab & Heneman 1987)
- Alternative work arrangements (Powell & Mainiero 1999)
- Applicant ratings and starting salaries (Hitt & Barr 1989)
- Cross-cultural compensation (Zhou & Martocchio 2001)
