# Knowledge Integration

> "Knowledge integration is the raison d'etre for the existence of the firm." — Grant (1996); Kogut & Zander (1992)

## Definition

**Zahra, Neubaum & Hayton (2020):**
> "Knowledge integration is an organizational capability for creating novel combinations of different strands of knowledge, which have utility for solving organizational problems, from component knowledge sourced from within and beyond the organization, and across time, and which derive from individual and group contributions, facilitated by both formal and social processes."

The construct is widely studied but remains equivocally defined. Zahra et al. identify six dimensions any definition must address: what it is, who does it, what is done, what is integrated, the sources, and the outcomes.

## The Micro-Macro Problem

Knowledge integration research has split across two levels that rarely speak to each other:

**Micro-level** (individual and group):
- Individual cognition and memory encoding
- Transactive Memory Systems — distributed knowing across teams
- Group routines that encode collective know-how

**Macro-level** (organizational):
- Combinative capabilities (Kogut & Zander, 1992)
- Architectural competence (Henderson & Cockburn, 1994)
- Dynamic capabilities (Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997)
- Knowledge management systems (Alavi & Leidner, 2001)
- Organizational learning processes (Argote et al., 2021)

> [!gap] Key gap
> The interplay between micro and macro levels is frequently acknowledged but rarely modeled. Most studies operate at one level only, limiting cumulative theory-building.
