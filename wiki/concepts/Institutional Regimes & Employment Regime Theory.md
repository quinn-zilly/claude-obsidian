# Institutional Regimes & Employment Regime Theory

Nav: [[index]] | [[Institutional & Strategy MOC]]

Comparative-institutional theories explaining how national configurations of institutions shape organizational practices and [[Work Design (Concept)|work design]]. Two strands feature in [[parker-vandenbroeck-holman-2017-work-design-influences|Parker, Van den Broeck & Holman (2017)]]:

- **Varieties of capitalism** (Hall & Soskice, 2001) — emphasizes employers developing institutions to coordinate industrial relations, vocational training, and employee cooperation.
- **Employment regime theory** (Gallie, 2007) — emphasizes how institutions emerge from the relative power of employers vs. employees and the state's mediating role.

## The three regimes and the "Nordic advantage"

Employment regime theory distinguishes:

- **Social democratic** (Nordic — Denmark, Sweden): influential trade unions, high union membership, extensive welfare, active labor-market & training policies → **most enriched work** (highest job discretion and cognitive demand).
- **Continental** (France, Germany): influential unions and strong employment protection, but more consultative union role.
- **Liberal** (UK, Ireland, US, Australia): limited union participation, weak employment protection, limited welfare/active policy.

Cross-national evidence shows a consistent **"Nordic advantage"** in job discretion and cognitive demand, sustained over ~20 years while continental and liberal regimes declined (Eurofound, 2015; Holman et al., 2009; Lorenz & Valeyre, 2005). What remains unclear is whether the advantage flows from regime *strength* or from the distinctive way Nordic unions explicitly pursue better work design.

These regime effects are complemented by [[Institutional Isomorphism|neo-institutional isomorphism]] (coercive, mimetic, normative pressures).
