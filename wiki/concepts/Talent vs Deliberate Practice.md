# Talent vs Deliberate Practice

The nature/nurture debate over expert performance: are the highest achievements set by **innate talent** (Galton 1869 — fixed capacities, training necessary but not sufficient) or by **acquired skill** through experience and practice (de Groot 1946; Chase & Simon 1973)?

## Ericsson & Lehmann's verdict: practice dominates

[[ericsson-lehmann-1996-expert-exceptional-performance|Ericsson & Lehmann (1996)]] conclude the influence of innate domain-specific capacities is **"small, possibly even negligible."** Supporting evidence:

- **Physiology is trainable.** Heart size, capillary density, muscle-fiber conversion, ballet hip turnout, perfect pitch — long thought fixed talent markers — are **training-induced adaptations**, often reversible, sometimes requiring *early-age* practice.
- **Basic capacities don't distinguish experts.** No systematic elite advantage in basic perception or reaction time; IQ is weakly/unrelated to expert performance and its correlation *decays to non-significance* after ~5 years of professional experience (Hulin et al. 1990).
- **Practice predicts.** Amount and early start of [[Deliberate Practice|deliberate practice]] predict attained level; motivation and parental support predict improvement better than ability.

## The lone exception

**Height** (and body size) is the one clear domain-specific attribute not accounted for by practice — relevant in sports like basketball.

> [!note] Scope
> This is the mid-1990s Ericsson position. Later meta-analyses (e.g. Macnamara et al. 2014) argued deliberate practice explains a substantial but *smaller* share of variance than the strong talent-negligible claim implies. Flagged for a future ingest.
