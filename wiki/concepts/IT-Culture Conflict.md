# IT-Culture Conflict

## Definition

A theory (Leidner & Kayworth, 2006) holding that culture is mostly invisible to a group **until a counter-culture surfaces it as conflict**. In the IT context, conflict arises among three sets of values: a **group's own values** (national / organizational / subunit), the **values embedded in a specific information system** (those of its champions/developers), and the group's **[[IT Culture|IT values]]** (what the group attributes to IT in general).

## The three conflict types

| Conflict | Tension | Primarily implicates |
|---|---|---|
| **System conflict** | Group values ↔ values embedded in a *specific* IT | IT development, adoption, use, outcomes |
| **Contribution conflict** | Group values ↔ the group's general *IT values* | IT management & strategy (no specific system involved) |
| **Vision conflict** | General *IT values* ↔ values embedded in a *specific* IT | Adoption and reshaping of IT values |

Contribution conflict is theorized via **Bourdieu's** theory of capital/distinction: IT is valued where it is seen as a legitimate source of (or route to) capital, and legitimacy is set by the dominant actors in a "field."

## Propositions (abridged)

- **System** — conflict rises with cultural distance between champion and adopter (P1a) and with breadth of implementation (P1b); higher conflict → later adoption (P1c) and more workaround/modified use to fit group values (P1d).
- **Contribution** — low conflict in a group's powerful actors lowers it for the whole group (P2a) and for aspiring groups (P2b); higher conflict → less strategic (P2c) and less innovative (P2d) IT use.
- **Vision** — rises with the gap in IT values between champion and user groups (P3a) and falls with involvement of the *powerful* users in design (P3b); higher conflict → lower adoption (P3c) but, when adopted anyway, greater change to the group's IT values (P3d).
- **P4** — managers can reduce *all* forms of conflict by promoting **shared IT values**.
- **P5** — emergence and resolution of the three conflicts produces **cultural change** over time.

## Why it's significant

It reframes the field's question from "does culture predict *whether* IT is adopted?" to how value **conflicts** form and resolve — and it supplies a mechanism by which IT gradually **infuses and reshapes culture** (resolution reorients values, starting at subunit levels before national values move).
