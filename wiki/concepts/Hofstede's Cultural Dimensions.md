# Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions

## Definition

The dominant taxonomy of **national culture** (Hofstede 1980, 1983), describing cultures along value dimensions that persist everywhere but vary in magnitude by region:

- **Power distance** — acceptance of unequal power distribution.
- **Uncertainty avoidance** — discomfort with ambiguity and risk.
- **Individualism–collectivism** — ties to self vs. group.
- **Masculinity–femininity** — achievement/assertiveness vs. relationship/care orientation.
- (Later) **Confucian dynamism / long-term orientation** (Hofstede & Bond 1988).

Related national-culture frameworks: **Trompenaars** (universalism vs. particularism, etc.) and **Hall** (high vs. low context; polychronic vs. monochronic time).

## Use in IS-culture research

In Leidner & Kayworth's review, **over 60% of national-culture IT studies used one or more Hofstede dimensions**. Patterns found:

- **Uncertainty avoidance** — most-used dimension; generally associated with *slower / less* IT adoption (e.g., Png et al. 2001 on frame relay; Thatcher et al. 2003), though contradicted by Galliers et al. (1998) in Pakistan.
- **Power distance** — mixed: positively related to GSS acceptance in one study (DeVreede et al. 1998), negatively in another (Hasan & Ditsa 1999).
- **Individualism** — linked to greater willingness to report bad news on failing projects, and (paradoxically) both higher ethical awareness of *and* higher rates of software piracy (Shore et al. 2001; Husted 2000).

## Caveat

Myers & Tan (2002) critique heavy reliance on Hofstede in cross-cultural IS research. Leidner & Kayworth note that singling out individual dimensions lets researchers "argue either way" on many adoption questions — motivating their shift toward a [[Cultural Fit|fit]]- and [[IT-Culture Conflict|conflict]]-based framing.
