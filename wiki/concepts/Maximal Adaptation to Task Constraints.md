# Maximal Adaptation to Task Constraints

The organizing thesis of [[ericsson-lehmann-1996-expert-exceptional-performance|Ericsson & Lehmann (1996)]]: expert performance is best understood as a set of **domain-specific adaptations that maximally fit the constraints of a task**, acquired through extended [[Deliberate Practice|deliberate practice]]. Studying experts is studying *maximal adaptation* — the best window onto the invariants and limits of human capacity.

## Forms of adaptation

- **Cognitive** — [[Long-Term Working Memory]], integrated domain representations, [[Chunking|knowledge chunks]].
- **Perceptual** — extracting new, more informative cues; faster identification of critical features (e.g. radiologists at 0.5 s).
- **Perceptual-motor** — anticipation from advance cues that **circumvents reaction-time limits**; overlapping movement production (typing eye-hand span); millisecond timing adjustments as late as 50 ms pre-contact in hitting sports.
- **Physiological / anatomical** — heart, capillaries, muscle fiber, joints reshaped by training (see [[Talent vs Deliberate Practice]]).

## Key implication

Acquired skills can **supplant generic human limits** (STM capacity, reaction time) *within a restricted, specific activity*. This is why expert-vs-novice differences are "the largest reliable differences observed by behavioral researchers among healthy adults" — yet do not transfer outside the trained domain.
