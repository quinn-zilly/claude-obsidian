# Referent-Shift Consensus

Nav: [[index]] | [[Multilevel Theory & Methods MOC]]

One of [[Chan's Typology of Composition Models|Chan's five composition forms]]. Like **direct consensus**, it uses within-group agreement to compose a construct upward — but with a critical difference: the **referent of the item is shifted** to a new, conceptually distinct form **before** consensus is assessed. It is the **new referent** that is aggregated.

## Direct consensus vs referent-shift

| | Referent | Example item | Higher construct |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Direct consensus** | Self | "I am confident I can perform this task" (self-efficacy) | Aggregated self-efficacy |
| **Referent-shift consensus** | Shifted to the collective | "I am confident **my team** can perform this task" (collective efficacy) | Team efficacy |

> [!key-insight]
> The mean of members' **self**-efficacy is **not** the same construct as **team efficacy**. Recall the orchestra: every musician may rate their individual self-efficacy high yet rate the orchestra's collective efficacy low. Applying a *direct* consensus model to compose self-efficacy → team efficacy would be **misleading** (Guzzo et al. 1993). A person with high self-efficacy can hold high or low collective efficacy — the referent shift creates a genuinely distinct construct.

This distinction resolves real terminological confusion in the literature (e.g., what "collective efficacy" denotes) and directly informs the [[Aggregation Justification Indices|item-wording]] guidance in [[kozlowski-klein-2000-multilevel-approach|Kozlowski & Klein (2000)]] and [[klein-kozlowski-2000-micro-to-meso|Klein & Kozlowski (2000)]] — unit-referent items generally show higher within-group agreement than self-referent items.

Applies to the vault's [[Collective Efficacy]] page: Bandura's collective efficacy is a referent-shift (team-referent), not an average of self-efficacies.

Source: [[chan-1998-composition-models-typology|Chan (1998)]].
