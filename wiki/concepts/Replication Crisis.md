# Replication Crisis

The replication crisis refers to the widespread finding that a substantial proportion of published scientific results — particularly in psychology, medicine, and social science — cannot be reproduced or replicated in independent studies.

## Empirical Evidence

### Psychology (OSC 2015)

The [[open-science-collaboration-2015]] provided the landmark empirical estimate:
- 100 replications of 2008 psychology studies
- **Only 36% replicated** (significant in same direction)
- Replication effect sizes averaged **half** the original effect sizes
- Cognitive psychology replicated better than social psychology (50% vs 25%)

### Broader Science

- **Biomedical research**: Begley & Ellis (2012) replicated 11% of 53 landmark cancer biology studies (Amgen); Prinz et al. (2011) replicated 25% (Bayer)
- [[John Ioannidis]] (2005): mathematical argument that most published findings are false given low power, high flexibility, and publication bias
- [[munafo-2017-manifesto]]: 85% of biomedical research efforts estimated as wasted; 90% of Nature survey respondents agreed there is a "reproducibility crisis"

## Root Causes

| Cause | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| [[Publication Bias]] | Studies with significant results published; null results filed away |
| [[P-hacking]] | Flexible analysis decisions tune results toward significance |
| [[HARKing]] | Hypotheses reformulated after seeing data; presented as a priori |
| Low statistical power | Underpowered studies produce inflated effect sizes when published |
| Incentive misalignment | Novelty and positive results rewarded; replication and null results not |

These causes are **systemic and interactive** — a researcher acting rationally under current incentives will produce p-hacked, underpowered, unreplicated findings.

## The Conceptual Frame: Prediction vs. Description

Null-hypothesis significance testing (NHST) is designed for **prediction**: given a pre-specified hypothesis, what is the probability of the observed data under the null? When used post-hoc (after exploring data), it becomes a description test — and the p-value loses its inferential meaning. The replication crisis is, in part, a crisis of NHST being misapplied as a confirmatory tool in fundamentally exploratory contexts.

## Responses

The replication crisis has catalyzed:
1. **[[Preregistration]]** — separating confirmatory from exploratory research
2. **[[Registered Reports]]** — publication decisions before results
3. **[[Open Science]]** — open data, materials, and code; multiverse analysis
4. **Large-scale replication projects** — OSC 2015, Many Labs, Reproducibility Project: Cancer Biology
5. **[[Analytic Reproducibility]]** audits — checking whether published analyses reproduce from available data

## Is "Crisis" the Right Word?

[[munafo-2017-manifesto]] and others note that "crisis" may be too strong or too narrow — many fields are affected to varying degrees, and some (e.g., cognitive psychology) show better replication rates than others. The more neutral framing: there is **substantial room to improve research practices** to maximize efficiency of knowledge accumulation.

## Knowledge Management Perspective

The replication crisis is also a **knowledge quality problem**: the accumulated literature is a knowledge repository whose entries may be systematically incorrect. From a KM perspective ([[Knowledge Retention]], [[Knowledge Management]]), this means the organization (science) has stored unreliable knowledge, reducing the ROI on prior knowledge creation. The open science movement is a knowledge quality improvement program.
