# Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Goal-Setting Theory]]

Task-specific confidence — belief that one can attain a specific performance level. Distinct from global self-esteem. Measured across a *range* of possible performance outcomes (not a single outcome), which resolves the apparent contradiction between GST and expectancy theory.

## Role in Goal-Setting Theory

**When goals are self-set:** high self-efficacy → higher goals set.

**When goals are assigned:** self-efficacy moderates and mediates:
- High SE → more committed to assigned goals
- High SE → better task strategies found and used
- High SE → more positive response to negative feedback (goal maintained or raised; low SE → goal dropped)
- Assigning a *challenging* goal itself raises SE (implicit message of confidence from leader)

**Mediation:** Goals + self-efficacy jointly mediate effects of:
- Monetary incentives
- Personality traits (conscientiousness, achievement motivation, goal orientation)
- Participative decision-making
- Transformational/charismatic leadership

## How Leaders Raise Self-Efficacy (Bandura 1997)

1. Mastery experiences — adequate training → success → SE increase
2. Role modeling — find models the person can identify with
3. Persuasive communication — express confidence; convey effective strategies
4. Transformational leadership — inspiring messages + cognitive stimulation (Bass 1985)

## Within- vs. Between-Goal Expectancy

Resolves GST vs. Expectancy Theory contradiction (Locke, Motowidlo & Bobko 1986):
- *Within* goal level (held constant): higher expectancy → higher performance ✓ (consistent with VIE)
- *Across* goal levels: lower expectancy associated with higher (harder) goals → higher performance ✓ (consistent with GST)

SE is measured across the full range → no contradiction arises.

## Self-Efficacy and Feedback

After negative feedback, SE level determines whether the person raises or lowers subsequent goals. SE is thus a buffer against performance setbacks on difficult goals.
