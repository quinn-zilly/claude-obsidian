# Due Process Approach to Appraisal

A [[Procedural Justice|procedural-justice]] reframing of [[Performance Appraisal|performance appraisal]] (Folger, Konovsky & Cropanzano 1992): move away from the **"test metaphor"** (appraisal as assigning a valid rating to an objective quantity) toward a **due-process metaphor** that acknowledges subjectivity, conflicts of interest, and legitimate disagreement.

## Three core elements

- **Adequate notice** — tell people in advance when and on what criteria they'll be appraised; involve workers in devising standards; provide regular feedback.
- **Just hearing** — limit review to "admissible" evidence (performance, not personal attacks); give workers a chance to state their interpretation and disagree.
- **Judgment based on evidence** — accurate standards, gathered data, decisions based on a formal process; rater training to keep it free of political influence.

## Evidence & caveats

- Taylor et al. (1995): a due-process redesign in a state agency was **preferred by workers and managers** and seen as fairer/more effective — even though workers received **lower** ratings.
- **Caution**: due-process safeguards can **raise expectations** and hand employees tools to voice discontent. Mesch & Dalton (1992): adding a fact-finding stage to a grievance process caused grievances to *skyrocket* until the intervention was abandoned.
