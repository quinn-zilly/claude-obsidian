# Training Reactions: Affective vs. Utility

## Definitions

**Affective reactions (Level 1a)**: Emotional/hedonic response to training.
- Example item: "I found this training program to be enjoyable."
- Most common form of training evaluation ("smile sheets")

**Utility reactions (Level 1b)**: Perceived job relevance and transferability.
- Example items: "To what degree will this training influence your ability to perform your job?" / "Was this training of practical value?"
- Less common but more informative

**Combined reactions (Level 1c)**: Scales that mix both types — common in practice, but masks the distinction.

## Empirical Relations

From [[alliger-et-al-1997-training-criteria-meta-analysis]] (34 studies, 115 correlations):

| Reaction Type | ↔ Immediate Learning | ↔ Transfer |
|---|---|---|
| Affective | ~.00 | Weak |
| Utility | Small positive | **Moderate** |
| Combined | In between | Moderate |

> [!key-insight]
> Utility reactions predict transfer *better than learning measures do*. Affective reactions are essentially unrelated to any other criterion.

## Theoretical Explanation

Attitude specificity principle (Ajzen & Fishbein 1973, 1977): attitude–behavior correlations increase when the attitude and behavior are measured at the same level of specificity. Utility reactions are behaviorally specific (referencing future job performance), so they predict job behavior. Affective reactions are not.

Additionally: trainees providing utility judgments may implicitly consider situational constraints in their work environment (Peters et al., 1988), not just training content. This could explain why utility reactions outpredict learning measures for transfer.

## Independent Convergence

Warr & Bunce (1995) proposed a tripartite division independently: enjoyment, usefulness, difficulty. "Enjoyment" ≈ affective; "usefulness" ≈ utility.

## Practical Implication

- Collect affective and utility reactions as **separate scales**
- Use utility reactions to estimate transfer potential; use affective reactions for customer satisfaction / program viability
- Consider collecting utility reactions *after* return to job (hindsight improves judgment)
