# Job Demands–Control–Support Model (and JD-R)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]]

Two related models used widely in work design and occupational health research.

## Job Demands–Control–Support (Karasek 1979; Karasek & Theorell 1990)

**Job demands** (physical/psychological/emotional effort required) × **job control** (decision latitude) → strain outcomes. Control buffers demands. Later added **social support** as second buffer.

Evidence: mixed results, especially on social support buffering. Individual differences (self-efficacy, proactive personality, locus of control) moderate demands–control relationship.

## Job Demands–Resources (Bakker & Demerouti 2007)

Generalizes JDC-S. Two processes:
1. **Health impairment:** job demands → emotional exhaustion/burnout
2. **Motivational:** job resources (autonomy, feedback, support, leadership, safety climate) → engagement; buffers demands

Key meta-analytic findings (Nahrgang, Morgeson & Hofmann 2011):
- Complexity demands → burnout ρ_c=0.24; risks/hazards → burnout ρ_c=0.28
- Risks/hazards → engagement ρ_c=−0.67
- Job resources → engagement ρ_c range 0.47–0.80
- Burnout → unsafe behavior; engagement → safe behavior

Bakker et al. (2005): autonomy, feedback, or social support buffers burnout under high overload, emotional demands, physical demands.

## Relation to WDQ / Work Design

Social characteristics (social support, feedback from others) and contextual characteristics (physical demands, work conditions) in the WDQ directly map to JD-R resources and demands. Work conditions explain 16% incremental variance in stress beyond motivational + social characteristics.
