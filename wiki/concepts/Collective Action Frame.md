# Collective Action Frame

## Definition

A **collective action frame** (Snow & Benford 1988) is a jointly constructed group account that defines a problem or injustice, proposes solutions, and motivates collective action. The term captures how social movements strategically construct shared interpretations that mobilize activists and persuade neutral third parties.

Benford & Snow (2000): collective action frames "not only perform an interpretive function in the sense of providing answers to the question, 'What is going on here?', but they also are decidedly more agentic and contentious in the sense of calling for action that problematizes and challenges existing authoritative views and framings of reality."

## Three Core Framing Tasks

1. **Diagnostic framing** — defines the problem and assigns blame/responsibility to a person or institution (Benford 1993b; Snow & Benford 1988). Establishes that something is broken and who is responsible.

2. **Prognostic framing** — proposes solutions, strategies, and tactics for addressing the problem. Specifies what should be done and by whom.

3. **Motivational framing** — mobilizes individual activists into action. Creates urgency, moral obligation, or collective identity.

## Four Frame-Alignment Processes (Benford & Snow 2000)

Processes by which movements link their frames to potential constituents:

| Process | Definition |
|---------|------------|
| **Bridging** | Linking two ideologically congruent but previously disconnected frames |
| **Amplification** | Invigorating specific cultural values and beliefs already within a frame |
| **Extension** | Enlarging frame's reach to encompass interests of potential adherents beyond the core issue |
| **Transformation** | Counter-factual reframing; changing old understandings or generating new ones |

**Risk of extension:** Benford (1993a) — in the Austin disarmament movement, frame extension sparked intra-movement disputes over efficiency, territory, and ideological purity. Frames are identity-constituting, constraining how strategically they can be modified.

## Master Frames

Snow & Benford (1992): "master frames" are broad cultural themes that function as higher-order symbolic resources across multiple movements (e.g., civil rights, choice, (in)justice, environmental justice). They are "master algorithms that colour and constrain the orientations and activities of other movements" (Benford & Snow 2000, p. 618).

**Critique (Polletta 2006):** Empirically, activists don't reliably reproduce master frames. In 1960s sit-ins, injustices were described vaguely, yet thousands were mobilized without explicit master frame invocation. Movement researchers have neglected discursive processes in nascent movements, before formal recruitment pitches exist.

## Key Critiques (Benford 1997)

- **Descriptive bias:** cataloguing frames rather than analyzing framing processes
- **Static tendency:** conflating framing (dynamic) with frames (static outputs), leading to premise that meanings are lexically encoded and stable
- **Reification:** reducing socially situated meaning construction to disembodied, objective schemas (e.g., "master frames")
- **Strategic bias:** focus on external persuasion to audiences; neglects internal negotiation of meaning within movements

Oliver & Johnston (2000): collective action frames have drifted from Goffman's (1974) interactionist roots toward a one-way, top-down model of meaning transmission.

## Influence on Management Literature

The collective action frame tradition has directly influenced:
- **Strategic frames** (Kaplan 2008) — framing contests within organizations
- **Organizational change** (Kellogg 2009) — relational frames to accommodate opponents
- **Technological frames** (Barrett et al. 2013) — frame alignment in technology adoption
- **Institutional theory** (Lounsbury et al. 2003) — field frames emerging from framing contests
