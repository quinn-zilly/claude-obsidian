# Signaling Theory (Recruitment)

Under information asymmetry, applicants **infer unknown job/organizational attributes from observable cues**. Because candidates lack direct knowledge of what working somewhere is actually like, they read available cues as **signals** (Rynes, Bretz & Gerhart 1991).

## Examples

- A **personable, competent recruiter** → signal of a good working atmosphere.
- A **functional, well-designed website** → organizational professionalism.
- **Awards / reputation** → objective third-party endorsement of positive job attributes (Turban & Cable 2003).
- A **site-visit invitation** or **prompt response** → signal of offer likelihood; **delays** → signal of disinterest or not being first choice.
- **RJP video with diverse employees** → signal that diversity is valued.

## Relation to ELM

Signaling is the mechanism behind **low-elaboration** processing: early applicants convert cues into simple associations. Van Zee et al. (1992) / Rynes et al. (1991): information used for the early **screen-out** decision is often **not reused** in the final choice, and signals lose impact once richer information is known — consistent with [[Elaboration Likelihood Model|ELM's]] shift from peripheral to central processing across [[Recruitment Stages (Barber)|stages]].
