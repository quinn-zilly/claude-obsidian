# Sorting Effect (Compensation)

One of two core mechanisms by which [[Pay-for-Performance]] affects organizational performance.

**Definition:** The sorting effect describes how PFP changes *who the workers are* — the composition of the workforce. High performers, who expect to earn more under PFP, are attracted to and remain with organizations offering strong PFP programs. Low performers, who expect to earn less, are more likely to leave or not join. The net result is a higher-productivity workforce without any individual worker changing their behavior.

Contrast with: [[Incentive Effect (Compensation)]] — which changes how current workers behave.

---

## Why It Matters

The sorting effect is frequently *larger* than the incentive effect, yet most research designs (random assignment to pay condition; single cross-section) cannot observe it. This means most empirical estimates of PFP effects substantially understate the true total effect.

> **Cadsby, Song & Tapon (2007)** lab experiment: when workers could *choose* their pay condition (fixed vs. incentive), the PFP advantage increased from **10%** (random assignment, incentive effect only) to **38%** (free choice, incentive + sorting). Sorting accounted for ~74% of total PFP impact.

> **Lazear (1986)** — Safelite Glass: piece-rate conversion → +44% total productivity. ~22% came from incentive effect (workers present both before and after), ~22% from sorting (workforce composition change). Both equal in magnitude.

---

## Mechanisms

1. **Attraction:** Organizations with stronger PFP attract job seekers with higher confidence in their own productivity.
2. **Retention:** Under PFP, high performers earn more — so they have greater reason to stay. Low performers earn less — so they have greater reason to leave or are managed out.
3. **Self-selection:** Individuals who are more risk-tolerant or have higher ability prefer variable pay.

---

## Implications for PFP Design

- PFP programs designed only with *current employee motivation* in mind miss a major pathway of value creation.
- Budget constraints (pay caps, compressed distributions) that limit high-performer payouts may undermine the sorting function, creating poaching risk.
- I-deals / exceptions for below-standard performers weaken the PFP signal for peers and reduce sorting effectiveness (Abdulsalam et al., 2021).

---

## Research Gap

Most PFP studies use designs (random assignment, between-organization cross-sections) that preclude observing sorting. Utility analyses that account for sorting (e.g., Sturman et al., 2003) find substantially higher net value than standard analyses. The field needs more multi-stage designs that allow workers to choose pay conditions and track subsequent workforce composition.
