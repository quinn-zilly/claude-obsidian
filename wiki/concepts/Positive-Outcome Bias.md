# Positive-Outcome Bias

Positive-outcome bias is the tendency of the published literature to over-report results that *confirm* the tested hypothesis (statistically significant, "positive" support) relative to null or contradictory ("negative") results. It is the literature-level symptom whose upstream cause is [[Publication Bias]] / the [[File-Drawer Problem]] and whose author-level cause is [[Questionable Research Practices]].

## Distinction from Publication Bias

- **[[Publication Bias]]**: the *mechanism* — null studies don't get submitted or accepted.
- **Positive-outcome bias**: the *observable result* — the share of published papers reporting positive support is inflated and rising.

## Empirical Anchor: Fanelli (2012)

[[fanelli-2012-negative-results-disappearing|Fanelli (2012)]] gave the first direct longitudinal evidence: among 4,656 hypothesis-testing papers, positive-result frequency rose from 70.2% (1990–91) to 85.9% (2007), ~6%/year, robust to all confounds.

## The Hierarchy-of-Sciences Gradient

Positive-outcome bias is **not uniform**. It increases along a softness gradient:

physical sciences → biological sciences → social sciences, and pure → applied.

Fanelli's interpretation: where theories and methods are less codified and true replication is rare, both false positives and false negatives proliferate — and a system that suppresses negatives distorts those fields most. This is Fanelli's broader "hierarchy of the sciences" thesis (Fanelli 2010).

## Consequences

- Inflated meta-analytic effect sizes (only the large-effect tail is visible).
- Wasted replication of phantom effects; fields built on non-existent phenomena.
- A core empirical driver of the [[Replication Crisis]].

## Remedies

- [[Preregistration]] and [[Registered Reports]] — decouple publication from outcome.
- Negative-results journals, trial registration, reporting guidelines (Fanelli notes these had *not* worked as of 2012).
