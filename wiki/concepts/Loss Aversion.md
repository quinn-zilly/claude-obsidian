# Loss Aversion

The tendency to be **more sensitive to losses than to equal-sized gains** (e.g., losing $5 looms larger than winning $5). Central parameter of [[Cumulative Prospect Theory]].

## Status: Contested

Fischhoff & Broomell (2020) review reveals significant evidentiary problems:

- **Ert & Erev 2013** (qualitative): loss aversion magnitude depends heavily on task features (framing, stake size, experiment length)
- **Walasek et al. 2018** (meta-analysis): weak overall evidence of any loss aversion; authors lament poor quality of methods and reporting — unclear if loss aversion doesn't exist or was lost in noise
- **Davis-Stober et al. 2016, Regenwetter & Robinson 2017**: CPT parameters including loss aversion are highly variable across studies

> [!contradiction] Empirical status disputed
> Loss aversion is a cornerstone of behavioral economics and CPT, but quantitative meta-analysis found only weak evidence. Methodological noise (not absence) may explain this. See [[Cumulative Prospect Theory]] for fuller discussion.

## Decision by Sampling Explanation

**DbS model** (Stewart et al. 2006, 2015): loss aversion arises from sampling distributions in memory. When losses are more salient in experienced comparisons, loss aversion emerges. Task features that affect sampling → affect apparent loss aversion magnitude. This predicts why loss aversion varies by context.
