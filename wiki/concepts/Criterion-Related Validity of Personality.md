# Criterion-Related Validity of Personality

The magnitude of the correlation between personality predictors and work-related criteria. Once dismissed as too small to matter (Guion & Gottier 1965; Schmitt et al. 1984; the criticism revived by Morgeson et al. 2007), now well-established as useful — with counterarguments by Ones et al. (2007) and Tett & Christiansen (2007).

> [!key-insight] The matching principle
> **Validity is high when predictor and criterion are conceptually matched, and low when they are not** ([[hough-johnson-2013-personality-in-work-settings|Hough & Johnson 2013]]). Correlating everything with everything (early research) produced near-zero averages and a false impression of irrelevance. "We cannot expect all personality variables to predict all criteria."

## Benchmark magnitudes

**Conscientiousness** is the most robust broad-trait correlate of overall performance:

- Barrick & Mount (1991): ρ ≈ .23 (job performance).
- [[zell-lesick-2022-big-five-performance-synthesis|Zell & Lesick (2022)]] second-order synthesis (54 meta-analyses): overall ρ = **.19** for C, ≥46% larger than the others (E .10, A .10, N −.12, O .13). Domain-specific: C .28 academic vs .20 job; E and N near-zero for academic.
- Wilmot & Ones (2019): C ρ ≈ .17 across 48 meta-analyses; Wilmot et al. (2019): E ρ ≈ .10 across 45.

**Trait-criterion specificity** (Hough & Johnson tables): Sales Effectiveness ← Achievement + Dominance (not ES/A/O); CWB ← low Conscientiousness (≈ −.35), low Agreeableness (interpersonal deviance ≈ −.46); leadership ← Extraversion/Dominance. **[[Compound Traits (Personality)|Compound variables]] show the highest validity when the criterion is complex.** **[[Facet-Level Personality Measurement|Facets often out-predict their parent factor.]]**

## Correction effects

Meta-analyses correcting for **both unreliability and range restriction** yield larger effects than uncorrected ones (Zell & Lesick: .12–.22 vs .05–.13) — so reported validities are often **underestimates**.

## Relation to the criterion side

Validity is bounded by criterion quality; most personality meta-analyses are "limited to whatever criteria happen to be available," which do not represent the full performance domain — the [[Criterion Problem]]. Both authors call for better **criterion taxonomies** compatible with refined personality structures.
