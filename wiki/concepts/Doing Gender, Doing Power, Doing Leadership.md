# Doing Gender, Doing Power, Doing Leadership

The mechanism at the core of [[fletcher-2004-paradox-postheroic-leadership|Fletcher (2004)]]. Building on West & Zimmerman's **"doing gender,"** it holds that every social interaction — including at work — is an occasion on which we *enact* our social identity. Fletcher extends this: work interactions are simultaneously occasions to **do gender**, **do power**, and **do leadership**, and these three are linked.

## The linkage

- Heroic-leadership traits (control, assertiveness, domination) are socially coded **masculine**; relational/[[Postheroic Leadership|postheroic]] traits (empathy, collaboration, vulnerability, inquiry) are coded **feminine** — irrespective of the actor's sex.
- Because men long dominated work spheres, "doing masculinity" and "doing work/competence" became conflated — everyone feels subtle pressure to do masculinity to be seen as competent.
- The relational "self-in-relation" stance and the "power with" model are misread as **powerlessness** (in systems of unequal power, the less powerful are the ones required to anticipate and accommodate without reciprocity).

## The consequence

Enacting relational leadership therefore risks reading as **"doing femininity" and "doing powerlessness"** — displays not culturally associated with leaders. This is why:

- **The body matters**: the same behavior is interpreted differently depending on the actor's sex/race/status (a boss vs a peer saying "drop by my office"; a white man vs a woman slamming a fist).
- **Gender schema** make the *experience* of practicing relational leadership systematically different for women (whose relational acts get conflated with mothering/selfless giving) than for men (who can be seen as "doing something new").

This mechanism grounds all three paradoxes of [[Postheroic Leadership]] and complements [[Social Role Theory (Gender)]] and role-congruity accounts under [[Gender and Leadership]].
