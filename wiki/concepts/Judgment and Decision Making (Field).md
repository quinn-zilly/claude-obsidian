# Judgment and Decision Making (Field)

**JDM** is the research domain concerned with deliberate choices — how people form probabilistic judgments, predict outcomes, evaluate preferences, and select actions. Excludes purely reflexive responses (hand on burner), includes everything requiring some deliberation.

## Scope

Core tasks in JDM:
- **Diagnostic inference** — what state of the world is this?
- **Prediction** — what will happen if I do X or Y?
- **Preferential prediction** — will I like outcome X?
- **Decision/choice** — which action to take?

The term *judgment* is imprecise: a physician's "medical judgment" can mean any of the above.

## Dual Theme

JDM is structurally both **descriptive** and **normative/prescriptive**:

> "Serious descriptive work on decisions often reaches important normative questions, while intendedly prescriptive studies rise or fall on the realism with which they represent the psychology of the decision maker." — Connolly et al. 2012

This dual nature creates the central tension: normative models (Bayes, SEU, MAUT) define "error," but whether an error *matters* depends on how commonly it occurs and whether the normative model is appropriate.

## Major Research Programs

| Program | Core Claim | Status |
|---|---|---|
| [[Heuristics and Biases Program]] | 3 heuristics generate systematic errors | Waning — became error catalog; normative models questioned |
| [[Lens Model]] / Policy Capturing | Judgment = cue combination; expert judges use few linear cues | Established but methodologically complex |
| [[Prospect Theory]] | Descriptive model of risky choice; reference-point + loss aversion | Dominant descriptive model |
| [[Naturalistic Decision Making]] | Experts use recognition-primed matching, not deliberative DM | Active; challenges deliberative paradigm |
| [[Decision Justification Theory]] | Regret has outcome + process components | Active; explains reason-based choice effects |
| Signal Detection Theory | Threshold-setting under uncertainty | Underused in org research |
