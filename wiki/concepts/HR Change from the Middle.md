# HR Change from the Middle

The practice of I-O and HR professionals acting as catalysts for organizational change without relying on top-down mandate. The middle-out change agent designs initiatives that gain energy via adoption and spread, rather than waiting for senior sponsorship that may never arrive or may evaporate.

**Primary source:** Yost (2014), Ch. 6 in *Using Experience to Develop Leadership Talent.*

## Why Top-Down Fails in Dynamic Orgs

The dominant frameworks HR professionals are trained in — strategic HRM, project management, change management — all begin with "enlist top management support" and cascade down. Yost (2014) argues this is counter-productive in VUCA environments (Volatile, Uncertain, Complex, Ambiguous; Johansen 2009):

- CEO tenure: ~40% leave after 1.8 years (Gottfredson et al. 2008)
- 75% of COOs brought in to become CEOs do not make it to the top role (Ciampa & Watkins 1999)
- Market, strategy, technology, and HR leadership all change — any initiative dependent on a specific sponsor is fragile

## The Catalyst Metaphor

> "The person who leads from the middle needs to think of himself or herself as a catalyst for change."

A catalyst in chemistry doesn't drive the reaction by force — it lowers the activation energy so the reaction proceeds on its own. HR catalysts design conditions that lower the activation energy for OJD adoption, then let the system propagate the change.

## Historical Precedent: Minority Influence

Yost (2014) invokes social movement literature to legitimize middle-out change:

> "Historically, civil rights movements across the globe would not have happened if people waited for 'top management support.'"

Conditions for minority influence to produce change (Hogg 2010; Sharp 2010; Wood et al. 1994):
1. **Confident** — commits to the position without hedging
2. **Consistent** — same message across contexts and time
3. **Sustained** — doesn't abandon the position under pressure

## Key Tactics

| Tactic | Description |
|---|---|
| Plant the idea | HR professional mentions insight in senior conversation; it becomes "the leader's idea" |
| Pilot in a champion's unit | Choose a business unit led by someone recognized for developing others |
| Recruit sponsors proactively | Don't wait for top-down alignment; cultivate it from below |
| Create visible wins | Pilot success → pull from other units |
| Build tools that outlast you | Design for self-propagation, not dependency on the creator |

## Theoretical Grounding

- **Small wins** (Weick 1984): iterative, compounding change in uncertain environments; optimal wins = discrete, self-contained, high P(success)
- **Frohman (1997)**: igniting organizational change from below; personal initiative as driver
- **Reay, Golden-Biddle & Bermann (2006)**: legitimizing new roles through small wins and microprocesses of change

## Relation to Institutional Theory

Middle-out HR change is structurally identical to [[Institutional Accommodation]] (Micelotta et al. 2017): disruptive forces (the need for widespread OJD) are channeled into incremental settlement rather than displacement. The HR professional is an [[Institutional Entrepreneurship|institutional entrepreneur]] using the "social skill" (Fligstein 2001) of understanding what others want and framing the innovation accordingly.
