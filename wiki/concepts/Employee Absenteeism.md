# Employee Absenteeism

Nav: [[index]] | [[Self-Management Training]]

Chronic organizational problem. US annual cost ≈ $30 billion (Steers & Rhodes 1984). Disrupts scheduling, raises costs, reduces productivity.

## Theoretical Framework (Steers & Rhodes 1984 revision)

Key variable added: **perceived ability to come to work** — distinct from motivation to attend. Many employees are motivated but judge themselves inefficacious in overcoming obstacles.

**Obstacle categories (Frayne & Latham 1987 data):**
- Most frequent: family problems, supervisor/coworker conflict, transportation difficulties
- Also: legitimate illness, medical appointments, job stress, job boredom, alcohol/drug issues, perceived entitlement ("sick leave belongs to me")
- Sick leave = 49.8% of recorded absenteeism in their sample

**Cultural/normative factors (Chadwick-Jones et al. 1982):** group norms and culture independently affect attendance decisions.

**Work schedule rigidity:** blue-collar inflexibility (can't take 1 hr) → full-day absence for what white-collar workers resolve in 2–3 hrs.

## Measurement: Attendance vs. Absenteeism

> [!key-insight] Latham's measurement argument
> **Attendance** (hours present / hours scheduled) is superior to **absenteeism** (sick leave records). Absenteeism records = categorization behavior of recorders; reliability = .38–.42. Attendance reliability = .90–.92. Using sick leave alone risks Type II error in intervention evaluation.

Absenteeism records contaminated by: supervisor–subordinate negotiation (vacation coded as sick leave as performance reward), self-report inaccuracy, categorization subjectivity (Thorndike 1949).

**Remaining limitation:** neither measure distinguishes voluntary (choice) from involuntary (illness) absence.

## Intervention Evidence

- [[frayne-latham-1987-self-management-attendance|Frayne & Latham (1987)]]: [[Self-Management Training]] → significant attendance gain (ω² = .10) after 12 hrs training. Mechanism = raised [[Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)|perceived self-efficacy]], not changed outcome expectancies.
