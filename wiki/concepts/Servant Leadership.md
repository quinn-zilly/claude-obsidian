# Servant Leadership

A leadership philosophy from Robert Greenleaf (1970, 1977) putting the needs of followers and stakeholders first: "The servant-leader is servant first... conscious choice brings one to aspire to lead." The premise is that by first developing follower well-being, long-term organizational goals follow.

## Characteristics

Spears (2010) distilled ten from Greenleaf: listening, empathy, healing, awareness, persuasion, conceptualization, foresight, stewardship, commitment to others' growth, and building community.

## Vs. Transformational Leadership

Bass (2000) noted parallels (vision, influence, credibility, trust, service) but a key difference in *primary focus*: servant leaders center on **followers**; transformational leaders center on **organizational objectives** and inspiring commitment toward them. Most similar to TL's inspirational-motivation and individualized-consideration facets.

## Empirical Profile ([[hoch-et-al-2018-ethical-authentic-servant-vs-transformational|Hoch et al. 2018]])

Sparse but promising literature (41 studies, 49 samples). Links: OCB (ρ=.40), job satisfaction (ρ=.66), organizational commitment (ρ=.55), trust (ρ=.71), LMX (ρ=.65).

> [!tip] Least redundant — most promising
> Correlates only ρ=.52 with [[Transformational and Charismatic Leadership|transformational leadership]] (lowest of the three moral forms) and adds the **most incremental variance** across outcomes, with highest average relative weight (58%). The strongest candidate for a defensible **stand-alone** leadership form, though its evidence base is still thin. See [[Construct Redundancy (Leadership)]].

## Connections

- One of the three ethical/moral values–based leadership forms alongside [[Ethical Leadership]] and [[Authentic Leadership]]
- Distinctive strength on [[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)]] and extra-role behavior
