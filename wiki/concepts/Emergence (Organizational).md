# Emergence (Organizational)

Nav: [[index]] | [[Multilevel Theory & Methods MOC]]

**Bottom-up processes by which lower-level properties — the cognition, affect, behavior, and characteristics of individuals — combine through interaction, exchange, and amplification to yield collective phenomena at higher levels.** Emergence is the theoretical foundation for most collective constructs (climate, culture, collective efficacy, team performance, organizational learning).

Rooted in **general systems theory** but drawing on the later chaos/self-organization/complexity view that treats emergence as **both process and structure** — how lower-level dynamics unfold over time to produce higher-level structure. This is *not* reductionism: it seeks the full complexity of elements, their dynamics, and how they combine.

## Two ideal-type forms

Emergence ranges along a continuum anchored by two pure types (see [[Composition vs Compilation]]):

- **Composition** — isomorphic. Identical lower-level properties *coalesce/converge* into a higher-level property that is essentially **the same** (→ [[Global, Shared, and Configural Unit Properties|shared unit properties]]).
- **Compilation** — discontinuous. Related-but-different lower-level properties *configure* into a higher-level property that is **functionally equivalent but qualitatively different** (→ configural unit properties).

> [!key-insight]
> Emergence based on **isomorphism may be the exception, not the rule.** Historically the literature over-relied on composition; phenomena "marked by differentiation, conflict, competition, coalition formation, and disagreement are common" (Brown & Kozlowski). A given phenomenon (e.g., team performance) can emerge compositionally in some contexts and compilationally in others, depending on the interaction processes and workflow interdependence.

Emergence generally requires **longer time scales** than top-down effects, so bottom-up effects need longitudinal designs to detect (see [[Entrainment]] and differential time scales).
