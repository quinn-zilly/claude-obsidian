# Co-Production of Leadership

Nav: [[index]] | [[Leadership MOC]]

Shamir's (2007) constructionist thesis that **leadership is jointly produced by leaders and followers** when they form effective relationships that co-produce leadership outcomes. Building on LMX (Graen & Uhl-Bien 1995) and Hollander's active-follower view, co-production elevates followers from passive recipients of influence to **active co-contributors** in the leadership process, offering a more "balanced" view.

## Key Points

- The follower's role is to work *with* the leader to advance the goals, vision, and behaviors essential to unit and organizational success — "broader and more consequential" than in leader-centric theories.
- **Co-production beliefs** are measurable individual differences (Carsten et al. 2010; Carsten & Uhl-Bien 2013): followers with stronger co-production beliefs report more voice and constructive resistance, and are more willing to constructively resist a leader's *unethical* request — an effect moderated by romance of leadership.
- Sits within the constructionist strand of [[Followership Theory]], alongside [[Leadership Identity Construction|DeRue & Ashford's claiming–granting]].

> [!note] Tension with fully shared leadership
> Shamir (2012) argues that if leadership were *fully* shared it would lose its added value; co-production still requires some **disproportionate influence** — a useful counterweight to strong readings of [[Shared Leadership]].

## Recurring Theme

The [[Three Waves of Leadership Research|Lord et al. (2017)]] history already notes leadership is "always co-produced by leaders and others" (Shamir 2005) — co-production names and formalizes that recurring theme.

## Connections

- [[Followership Theory]] — co-production is its constructionist backbone.
