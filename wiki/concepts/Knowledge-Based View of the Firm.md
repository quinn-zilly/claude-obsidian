# Knowledge-Based View of the Firm

## Core Claim

The **knowledge-based view (KBV)** of the firm — associated primarily with Grant (1996) and building on Kogut & Zander (1992) and the resource-based view (Penrose, 1959; Barney, 1991) — holds that:

1. **Knowledge is the most strategically significant resource of the firm.** Because knowledge-based resources are difficult to imitate and socially complex, they form the basis of sustainable competitive advantage.
2. **The primary purpose of the firm is to integrate specialist knowledge.** Individuals hold specialist knowledge; the firm exists as a coordination mechanism to combine this knowledge productively (Grant, 1996). This makes [[Knowledge Integration]] the raison d'etre of the firm.
3. **Competitive advantage stems from the firm's ability to apply knowledge, not merely possess it.** It is less the knowledge at any given time per se than the ability to effectively apply existing knowledge and create new knowledge (Alavi & Leidner, 2001).

## Foundation in Resource-Based Theory

The KBV extends the **resource-based view (RBV)** by specifying knowledge as the critical resource:
- Resources that are rare, valuable, inimitable, and non-substitutable (VRIN) generate sustainable advantage (Barney, 1991)
- Knowledge-based resources — especially tacit knowledge — are inherently difficult to imitate
- The social complexity of organizational knowledge makes it especially hard to replicate

## Key Implications

**For organizational design:** Firms should be organized to facilitate knowledge integration across specialist functions. Hierarchy, rules, and routines are coordination mechanisms that serve this goal.

**For strategy:** Competitive advantage from knowledge erodes when knowledge becomes explicit and codifiable — competitors can then imitate it. Sustained advantage requires continual creation of new tacit knowledge.

**For IT/KMS:** IT can support knowledge-based advantage, but primarily by enabling the application and transfer of existing knowledge. Creating new knowledge requires human judgment and experience.

## Relation to Knowledge Management and Organizational Learning

- [[Knowledge Management]] — the KBV provides the strategic rationale for investing in KM; knowledge assets are the source of advantage
- [[Organizational Learning]] — learning is the process by which firms develop and renew their knowledge base
- [[Knowledge Integration]] — the KBV explains *why* firms integrate knowledge; integration is the mechanism through which distributed specialist knowledge becomes organizational capability
