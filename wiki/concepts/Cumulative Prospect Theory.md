# Cumulative Prospect Theory (CPT)

Extension of [[Prospect Theory]] (Tversky & Kahneman 1992). The dominant parametric model for description-based risky choice. Incorporates:

1. **Loss aversion** — sensitivity to losses > gains
2. **Risk tolerance** — curvature of value function
3. **Probability weighting** — S-shaped: overweight small probabilities, underweight large ones
4. **Choice stochasticity** — probabilistic choice component

## Parameter Estimation

Parameters estimated from choices among described gambles (e.g., "50% chance of winning $10 vs. certain $4").

**Critical problem**: parameters are highly variable across studies (Davis-Stober et al. 2016; Regenwetter & Robinson 2017). This undermines the theory's predictive power.

**One-qualitative-review finding** (Ert & Erev 2013): loss aversion magnitude depends on:
- How outcomes are framed
- Stake size
- Experiment duration

## Description vs. Experience

CPT studied almost entirely with **description-based choices** (summary probabilities given). When people *experience* outcomes trial-by-trial:
- They appear to underweight small probabilities — opposite of CPT prediction
- But: Broomell & Bhatia (2014) showed information-theoretically that standard experience stimuli cannot estimate CPT parameters in principle → comparison is moot; the gambles aren't equivalent

## Decision by Sampling Challenge

[[Decision by Sampling]] model (Stewart et al.) generates CPT-like behavior as a memory phenomenon without requiring stable CPT parameters. More parsimonious and task-sensitive.

## Status

CPT remains the canonical model and theoretical touchstone for behavioral economics and choice research. Its empirical instability is a live problem.
