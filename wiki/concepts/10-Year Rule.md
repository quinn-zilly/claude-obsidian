# 10-Year Rule

**No one — not even the most "talented" — attains internationally recognized performance in a domain without roughly a decade of intense preparation**; the vast majority take considerably longer.

## Evidence

- Simon & Chase (1973): ~10 years to reach international chess level. Even prodigy **Bobby Fischer** needed ~9 years.
- Generalizes across domains — music, sports, science — per Bloom (1985) and Hayes (1981).
- Experts reach peak a decade+ *after physical maturation*, underscoring the role of extended [[Deliberate Practice|deliberate practice]] over maturation or raw talent.

## Nuance

Raw years of experience predict performance only weakly; even refined counts (chess-competition hours, MLB games) are weak predictors among skilled performers. The "10 years" is necessary preparation time, but *quality* (deliberate practice) matters more than duration alone. Peak-performance age itself varies by domain — twenties for vigorous sports, thirties for chess, thirties–forties for arts and sciences.
