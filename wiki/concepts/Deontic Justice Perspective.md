# Deontic Justice Perspective

The **moral** explanation for why employees care about [[Organizational Justice|justice]]: justice is valued **as an end in itself**, not merely as a means to economic (instrumental) or relational (belonging) gains (Folger 1998, 2001; Folger & Cropanzano 2001).

## Core idea

- People hold **basic respect for human dignity and worth**; adhering to justice norms *symbolizes* that respect. Justice norms are treated as **universal standards** that should hold in every situation.
- Behaving justly is "**the right thing to do**" — a deontological stance (contrast with the consequentialist logic of instrumental/exchange models).
- Because **moral principles guide feeling, thinking, and action**, violations trigger **strong, negative emotional reactions** — anger, hostility, resentment — labeled **"moral outrage"** or **"deontic anger"** (Folger, Cropanzano & Goldman 2005; Folger & Skarlicki 2008).

## Evidence / signatures

- **Third-party reactions**: people react to injustice they merely **witness**, even when not personally harmed, sometimes taking risks to see a transgressor punished (Bies & Tripp; Spencer & Rupp 2006). Instrumental/relational models struggle to explain this; the deontic model predicts it.
- **Fairness theory** (Folger & Cropanzano 2001): justice perceptions arise from an **accountability** process — was harm avoidable, and did the accountable party violate moral norms? Only then is injustice perceived and targeted at that party (links to [[Multifoci Justice & Target Similarity]]).

## Why it matters

- Supplies the "ethical considerations" reason employees care about justice in [[cropanzano-bowen-gilliland-2007-management-organizational-justice|Cropanzano et al. (2007)]].
- One pillar of [[adamovic-2023-organizational-justice-review|Adamovic's (2023)]] integrative "multiple needs" account — the **meaningful existence** need.
