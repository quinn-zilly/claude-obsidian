# Sacred Values

Values that **people will not compromise or trade off** against other values, including money. Also called "protected values" (Baron & Spranca 1997).

## Relation to Utility Theory

Sacred values violate the utility theory axiom requiring willingness to make trade-offs among all outcomes. In economic terms, they are nonrational.

However: sacred values can be **central to thoughtful decisions**, not just irrational noise. Fischhoff & Broomell argue they deserve serious study, not dismissal.

## Empirical Examples

- **Genocide resistance**: sacred values overcome psychological numbing to immense-scale harms (Slovic & Slovic 2015)
- **Anti-extremism**: disrupting sacred value framing of violent causes reduces radicalization (Atran 2016)

## Methods

Mixed-methods research programs — surveys + qualitative + experimental manipulation. Increased methodological heterogeneity drove this research.

## Connections

- [[Constructed Preference]] — sacred values are the extreme end: preferences that are pre-formed, context-resistant, non-tradeable
- [[Motivated Reasoning]] — sacred values may activate motivated reasoning to protect them from challenge
- [[Framing (Organizational Theory)]] — collective action frames can invoke sacred values for mobilization
- [[Institutional Frame]] — naturalized field-level values may function like sacred values at organizational level
