# Composition vs Compilation

Nav: [[index]] | [[Multilevel Theory & Methods MOC]]

The two ideal-type forms of bottom-up [[Emergence (Organizational)|emergence]]. They are **end points of a continuum**, not a strict dichotomy; real phenomena fall somewhere between.

| | **Composition** | **Compilation** |
|---|---|---|
| Assumption | **Isomorphism** — same across levels | **Discontinuity** — different across levels |
| Process | Coalescence / convergence of *identical* lower-level properties | Configuration of *related-but-different* lower-level properties |
| Higher-level property | Essentially **the same** as its elements | **Functionally equivalent** but qualitatively different |
| Yields | [[Global, Shared, and Configural Unit Properties#Shared unit properties\|Shared]] unit properties | [[Global, Shared, and Configural Unit Properties#Configural unit properties\|Configural]] unit properties |
| Climate example | Psychological → organizational climate (shared perceptions) | — |
| Performance example | Team perf = sum/avg of members | Team perf = pattern of members' differential, interdependent contributions |

> [!example] Team performance both ways
> Does team performance emerge as the **average** of members' identical behaviors (composition), or as the **array** — one member's excellence on task A, another's on task B, a third's inconsequential effort on task C (compilation)? Neither is "right" universally. The determining factors are the performance dimension of interest, the team's workflow interdependence, and the organizational context.

## Why compilation matters

Composition (isomorphism) was historically the *primary* model for collective constructs, but it is only **one** possibility. Because compilation makes **less restrictive assumptions**, it permits many more emergent forms — capturing power, conflict, competition, networks, learning, and configural performance that composition cannot. Kozlowski & Klein's central agenda is to **elaborate compilation** and build a typology of emergence spanning the two anchors.

[[chan-1998-composition-models-typology|Chan (1998)]]'s five [[Chan's Typology of Composition Models|composition models]] sit largely within the composition family but add **dispersion** and **process** forms that bridge toward compilation.

The vault's [[Adaptive Team Performance]] page already invokes this distinction; team adaptation compiles over time from a recursive cycle.
