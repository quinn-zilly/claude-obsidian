# Return on Development Investment (RODI)

RODI is a utility-analysis-based methodology for estimating the financial return of leadership development interventions ([[Bruce J. Avolio|Avolio et al. (2010)]]). Adapts Cascio & Boudreau's (2008) HR utility framework to leadership contexts.

## Formula

```
RODI = (N × T × d × SDy) − C
```

- **N** — number of participants
- **T** — duration of behavioral change in years (e.g., 8 weeks = 0.167)
- **d** — effect size from validation data or published meta-analysis
- **SDy** — SD of dollar-valued performance ≈ **40% of annual salary** (conservative)
- **C** — total training cost (salary time + lost production + instructor + logistics)

## Empirical Foundation

Avolio et al. (2009) meta-analysis of 133 leadership intervention studies:
- Average d = 0.52 for leaders; d = 0.25 for followers (indirect cascade effects)
- Low d = −0.15; High d = 1.19 (constrained to 50% of CI to avoid implausible estimates)
- RODI capped at 200% for realism

## Typical Results

| Intervention | Location | Average RODI |
|-------------|---------|-------------|
| 1.5-day (mid-level leaders, n=100) | On-site | 72% |
| 1.5-day (upper-level leaders, n=30) | On-site | 61% |
| 3-day (mid-level leaders) | On-site | 87% |
| 3-day (top performers) | Any | 197–200% |

## Key Moderators of RODI

**Individual**: GMA, self-efficacy, developmental readiness, motivation to lead, span of control
**Contextual**: Transfer climate, generalization across levels, supervisor support, geographic colocation

## Strategic Uses

- Pre-investment planning tool (not binary go/no-go)
- Makes "development as investment" argument to executives
- When shared with participants, increases their engagement and transfer motivation
- Negative RODI doesn't necessarily mean don't invest (retention, culture, employer brand also matter)

## Limitation
- 8-week behavioral change duration is conservative; effects may last longer
- Assumes linear length-to-effect relationship (2× days → 2× effect)
- Derived from large Fortune 500 assumptions; small orgs need recalibration
- U.S.-centric meta-analysis base
