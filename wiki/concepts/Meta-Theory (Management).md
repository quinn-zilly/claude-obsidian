# Meta-Theory (Management)

A **meta-theory** is an umbrella theory that subsumes narrower theories and integrates disjointed, redundant, or contradictory ones by explaining phenomena at a higher level of abstraction (Zhao 1991). Where an individual theory describes relationships *between constructs*, a meta-theory captures connections *among constructs across theories*, orienting scholars toward the "settled science" (Cronin et al. 2021).

A meta-theory is built from **meta-theoretical constructs** — abstractions derived by grouping individual theories on established criteria and shared meaning (the process resembles naming factors in exploratory factor analysis). 

## Why it matters

Meta-theorizing is the proposed antidote to **theory proliferation** ("theorrhea," Antonakis 2017) — the accumulation of seemingly-novel theories that overlap and confuse. Parsimony favors simpler (not simplistic) theory.

## Two kinds (Pierce & Aguinis 2013)

- **Philosophical** — theories *about* theory in general: what theories are, what they should do, how to build them (Bacharach 1989; Popper 1963). Management has focused here.
- **Formalized** — overarching principles that transcend specific topics, describing phenomena more abstractly than any single theory. They trade depth for breadth; their value is accounting for a wide variety of phenomena. The [[Too-Much-of-a-Good-Thing Effect]] is a formalized meta-theory.

## Examples

- The [[CORE Model of Performance]] ([[marshall-aguinis-beltran-2024-theories-of-performance|Marshall, Aguinis & Beltran 2024]]) — 239 performance theories → six meta-theoretical constructs → three CORE components.
- The [[Too-Much-of-a-Good-Thing Effect]] ([[pierce-aguinis-2013-tmgt-effect|Pierce & Aguinis 2013]]) — one curvilinear principle accounting for countertheoretical findings across OB, HRM, entrepreneurship, and strategy.
