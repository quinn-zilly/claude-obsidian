# Sensemaking (Organizational)

## Definition

**Organizational sensemaking** is the process by which organizational members create and maintain a plausible, workable understanding of their environment in real time. Associated primarily with Karl Weick (1995).

Weick's basic unit: **cue + relation + frame = meaning**. Frames are past moments of socialization; cues are present moments of experience; meaning is created by constructing a relational connection between them.

Weick (1995, pp. 109–111): "Frames tend to be past moments of socialization and cues tend to be present moments of experience. If a person can construct a relation between the two moments, meaning is created."

## Core Mechanism

Sensemaking is **top-down / theory-driven** at the cognitive level: an activated frame creates expectations about the situation, guiding what is noticed, what is inferred, what actions are implied. Cognitive frames function as hypotheses about events — activating a frame generates expectations that pre-structure perception.

**Associativity of activated frames:** linked via contiguous relations (cause-effect, role-action, action-consequence). These links impose a configuration that "connects the dots" (Miller & Sardais 2013).

## Sensemaking Failure

**Sensebreaking** (Pratt 2000): when cues severely violate the expectations associated with an activated frame → meaning collapse. The activated frame fails to guide inferences, or worse, blinds individuals to correct interpretations.

Classic cases (Weick):
- **Mann Gulch (1993):** label "10 o'clock fire" → expectation of controlled outcome → failure to recognize catastrophic conditions
- **Bhopal (1988, 2010):** "a factory that's stopped" → routine framing blinds operators
- **Snook (2000) — Iraqi friendly fire:** "hits there" → surveillance frame → failure to recognize civilian aircraft

Common pattern: strong collective commitment to an activated frame (through prior socialization and in-group communication) makes it difficult to let go even as contradictory evidence accumulates.

Recovery from sensebreaking requires **frame-shifting** — switching to an alternative framing. This may be blocked by commitment escalation or occur through analogy and conceptual blending.

## Sensemaking and Language

Sensemaking is not purely an internal cognitive process — it is mediated by language and social interaction. Language invokes broader cognitive frames; frames shape the language available; interaction creates shared meaning (Weick 1995).

This connects sensemaking to the meso-level framing literature: framing in leadership communication (Gioia & Chittipeddi 1991) is partly a sensegiving process — the leader's framing shapes others' sensemaking.

## Key Distinction: Sensemaking vs. Sensegiving

**Sensemaking** = constructing meaning for oneself or one's group (often implicit, reactive).  
**Sensegiving** = strategic communication aimed at shaping others' sensemaking (proactive, intentional; Gioia & Chittipeddi 1991).

Strategic framing in organizations is primarily a sensegiving activity that works by activating shared cognitive frames in the audience.
