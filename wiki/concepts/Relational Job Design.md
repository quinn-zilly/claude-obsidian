# Relational Job Design

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]] | [[Job Characteristics Model]]

Contemporary, integrative work design perspective (Grant, 2007) focusing on the **relational architecture of jobs** — how job structure connects employees to the people their work affects. One of the integrative perspectives (not yet a discrete cluster) highlighted in [[parker-morgeson-johns-2017-100-years-work-design|Parker et al. (2017)]].

## Core Idea

Jobs can be designed to **increase the motivation to make a prosocial difference** by connecting employees to the **beneficiaries** of their work (Grant, 2007, p. 395). Seeing the impact of one's work on others boosts motivation and effort.

Builds on earlier social perspectives — Salancik & Pfeffer's (1978) social information processing and Morgeson & Campion's (2003) integrative model — and on the social characteristics later formalized in the [[Work Design Questionnaire|WDQ]].

## Evidence

Empirically supported and extended (Grant, 2008 — "the significance of task significance"; Parker, Johnson, Collins & Nguyen, 2013). Explicitly recognizes that work exists in a **social context** that profoundly affects employees, complementing the largely intrapersonal focus of the classic [[Job Characteristics Model|JCM]].

## Place in the Field

One of two contemporary perspectives Parker et al. single out (the other being the [[Learning and Development Perspective on Work Design]]). Adam Grant's popular book *Give and Take* (2013) is a practitioner-facing version of this approach. See [[Adam Grant]].
