# Replication Types Taxonomy

Shrout & Rodgers (2018) describe a continuum of replication types, each informative for different purposes. The distinctions are not categorical but represent increasing degrees of methodological difference from the original.

## The Continuum

| Type | Description | What it tests |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Direct (Exact)** | Same procedure, materials, measures, population; sample size may (should) be larger | Internal validity of original |
| **Systematic** | Direct replication with minor procedural variations (e.g., stimulus order) | Robustness to minor implementation differences |
| **Conceptual** | Different intervention, measures, or population; same theoretical construct | Generalizability; construct validity |

## Key Points

- **All types are informative** — the question is what inference they license
- A successful conceptual replication shows the phenomenon generalizes beyond the original context (Stroebe 2016)
- A failed conceptual replication is ambiguous: was the original wrong, or does the effect not generalize to the new context? (Fabrigar & Wegener 2016)
- Direct replication failure more cleanly implicates the original finding

## Sample Size for Direct Replication

A direct replication should use a *larger* sample than the original — not the same. Brandt et al. (2014): power analysis will usually indicate a substantially larger n. See [[Power Analysis (Replication Studies)]] for why even standard power calculations underestimate required n.

## Coordinated Replication Projects

Large-scale projects operationalize this taxonomy:
- **Many Labs** (Klein et al. 2014): same stimuli, different labs — direct systematic replication across contexts; reveals between-lab heterogeneity
- **Pipeline Project** (Schweinsberg et al. 2016): preregistered studies before results known
- **Open Science Collaboration (2015)**: 100 replications from top journals; predominantly direct; see [[open-science-collaboration-2015]]
- **Registered Replication Reports** (Simons et al. 2014, at *Perspectives on Psychological Science*): coordinated systematic replications published together

## Reform Implication

Shrout & Rodgers recommend that psychological science shift from single-study replication decisions to **multiple-study effect size distribution estimation**:
- Pool replications via meta-analysis
- Characterize τ² (between-study variance) in addition to average effect
- Use conceptual replications to probe moderators of [[Effect Size Heterogeneity]]
