# Leader Behavior and Ohio State Studies

The foundational first-wave research program at Ohio State University (late 1940s–1960s) that shifted leadership research from *who leaders are* (traits) to *what leaders do* (behaviors).

## Key Contributions

### Ohio State Research Program
**Stogdill & Shartle (1948)** initiated the program with the explicit aim to "discover what leaders do." Used questionnaire-based measures rated by followers.

**Fleishman (1953a)** factor-analyzed the Supervisory Behavior Description Questionnaire and identified two primary dimensions:
- **Initiating Structure** — clarifying roles, specifying rules and procedures, organizing work
- **Consideration** — being friendly and supportive to followers, showing concern for well-being

These two dimensions (task-oriented vs. relationship-oriented) dominated leadership research until the mid-1980s and correspond to Bales' (1950) task vs. socioemotional functional distinction.

### Leaderless Group Discussion (LGD)
**Bass (1949)**: Developed the leaderless group discussion technique — observing groups solving problems and evaluating members on leadership behaviors. Key predecessor of modern assessment centers. Still used to study emergent leadership.

### Assessment Centers
The behavioral techniques from this wave became the cornerstone of assessment centers. The AT&T Management Progress Study (Bray 1982) tracked 422 participants over 20 years; leadership motivation and status ambition were key predictors of career progression.

## Legacy
- Consideration and Initiating Structure were revisited by Judge et al. (2004) meta-analysis: Consideration ρ=.48 with follower satisfaction/motivation; Initiating Structure ρ=.29 — the "forgotten ones" are actually substantial
- Functional leadership perspective in team research (Hackman & Wageman 2005) is a direct descendant
- Warmth/competence dimensions in social cognition (Cuddy, Fiske & Glick 2008) likely underlie Consideration/Initiating Structure
