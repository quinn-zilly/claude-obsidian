# Gender Similarity in Work–Family Conflict

The headline finding of **[[shockley-et-al-2017-gender-work-family-conflict|Shockley et al. (2017)]]**: despite the pervasive lay belief that women have more trouble juggling work and family, **men and women report near-identical levels of [[Work–Family Conflict|work–family conflict]].**

## The evidence
- Meta-analysis of >350 samples, N ≈ 250,000. Overall gender differences in WIF and FIW are **statistically significant but negligible** (in the direction of women reporting slightly more) — practically null. Tightens Byron's (2005) small estimates.
- **Largest** (still modest) effect: **mothers report more FIW than fathers** (consistent with the **[[Rational View (Work–Family)]]** — children raise family demands borne mostly by women).
- Counter-stereotype: **men report more *time-based* WIF** than women.
- Society-level **gender egalitarianism** and study **publication date** did **not** moderate — the similarity is stable across cultures and time.

## Why it matters
Consistent with Hyde's (2005) gender-similarities hypothesis. The stereotype that women are inherently higher-WFC is **empirically unwarranted**, yet it drives real penalties: women face career costs from assumed family-orientation (Hoobler et al. 2009), and men are stigmatized for using flexibility (Vandello et al. 2013). See **[[Flexibility Stigma]]**.

> [!contradiction] Reconciling with Kossek et al. (2017)
> **[[kossek-su-wu-2017-opting-out-pushed-out|Kossek, Su & Wu (2017)]]** build a work–family perspective premised on gendered WFC. The tension resolves once you separate **experienced/self-reported conflict** (Shockley: ~equal) from **attributed conflict and its penalties** (Kossek: women *perceived* as higher-WFC and stigmatized). Both cite Hoobler et al. (2009). The near-null self-report *strengthens* Kossek's point that the penalty is driven by **bias**, not by an actual gender gap in conflict.
