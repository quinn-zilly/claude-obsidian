# Viral On-the-Job Development

The design strategy by which OJD initiatives launched in one part of an organization spread organically to others — "going viral" to infect talent management processes system-wide — without requiring top-down mandate.

## Core Insight

Most HR professionals launch OJD initiatives locally: one process, one business unit, one employee group. The conventional assumption is that top-down support is required for scale. Yost (2014) argues the opposite: **design for spread from the start**, and the initiative will propagate even through leadership turnover, reorganizations, and competing priorities.

The analogy is biological: a pathogen doesn't wait for central authorization. It spreads because it is well-designed for transmission.

## The Five Design Characteristics

Derived from Yost et al. (2011) interviews with 16 senior HR professionals about interventions lasting 10+ years. See [[Five Design Characteristics (OJD)]] for full detail.

1. **Strategic** — linked to business goals, framed in business language
2. **Simple** — one page; clear actions; user-interface simplicity hiding back-end complexity
3. **Sneeze-able** — sticky (people want to use it) AND transmissible (people want to pass it on)
4. **Systemic** — designed to plug into adjacent HR processes
5. **Sustainable** — self-maintaining; doesn't require ongoing energy to survive

## What "Sneeze-able" Means

Borrowed from Godin (2001): an idea virus spreads when the host *wants* to transmit it. Sneeze-able OJD tools are:
- Self-contained (no background context required)
- Designed to prompt interaction with others (prompts networking, coaching, feedback)
- So compelling the user "would be stupid not to use" them

Classic examples from Yost (2014):
- Leadership competency **card sorts** — senior leaders asked to take them home after assessment
- A one-page "**Development Plans That Aren't a Waste of Time**" quiz — went viral when managers forwarded it to their teams during the performance cycle

## Spread Mechanisms

| Pathway | How It Works |
|---|---|
| Succession management | High-visibility process signals valued experiences → others emulate |
| Performance management | Dev plan instructions + dev idea lists embed in annual cycle |
| Peer networks / CoPs | Mentees become mentors; communities recruit new members |
| Intranet / just-in-time links | Tools surface exactly when needed (during dev plan completion) |
| Advocate network | Leaders who benefited become champions; pilots generate pull |

## Relation to Small Wins (Weick 1984)

> "Optimal small wins are discrete, self-contained actions with a high probability of success that can serve as stable building blocks for future actions."

Viral OJD = an operationalization of small wins at the HR intervention level. Each well-designed tool is a small win; successes compound; the initiative gains energy over time rather than dying without top-down fuel.

## Contrast: Top-Down Integration

| Viral / Middle-Out | Top-Down |
|---|---|
| Survives leadership change | Dependent on sponsoring leader |
| Gains energy via use | Requires ongoing investment |
| Designed for plug-in compatibility | Requires prior alignment |
| Starts local, scales organically | Starts global, often never reaches local |
