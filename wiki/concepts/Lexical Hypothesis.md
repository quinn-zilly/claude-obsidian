# Lexical Hypothesis

Nav: [[index]] | [[concepts/_index]]

**Lexical hypothesis:** the most socially important individual differences become **encoded in natural language** as single terms; the more important a distinction, the more (and higher-frequency) words a language devotes to it. Therefore analyzing the personality lexicon reveals the basic dimensions of personality.

## Method

Allport & Odbert (1936) catalogued ~18,000 person-descriptive words from a dictionary. The lexical **paradigm** then:
1. Selects a representative set of **native-language** descriptors (not translated imports).
2. Collects self/peer ratings.
3. **Factor-analyzes** within each language separately.

Doing analyses *separately per language* imposes the demanding **cross-cultural generalizability** test — far more stringent than exporting one inventory (e.g., the NEO-PI-R) and checking whether it can be recovered (the "Procrustean" lenient test). See [[Saucier-Srivastava-2015-structural-models-personality|Saucier & Srivastava (2015)]].

## What it links

The lexical premise ties **semantic representation** directly to the **social-importance** criterion for a good structural model: an attribute represented by many terms tends to emerge as a factor, and high word-frequency underscores its importance.

## Caveats

Folk concepts are "basic but not exhaustive" (necessary but not sufficient) for a science of personality — the lexicon can omit scientifically important variables, and single-term meanings can be vague or context-dependent. Lexical studies have also focused almost entirely on **adjectives**, under-sampling type-nouns and attribute-nouns.

Feeds the [[Personality Structure Hierarchy]] and grounds the [[Five-Factor Model]] and [[HEXACO Model]] historically.
