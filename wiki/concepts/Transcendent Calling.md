# Transcendent Calling

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work as a Calling MOC]]

The integrative conceptualization of calling proposed by [[Thompson-Bunderson-2019-Work-as-a-Calling|Thompson & Bunderson (2019)]] to resolve the neoclassical-vs-modern definitional stalemate. It occupies the **high-inner / high-outer** cell of the [[Inner and Outer Requiredness]] 2×2: work felt as both an expression of consuming personal passion **and** a response to a genuine need beyond the self.

> "A transcendent calling is a conviction — often felt as a sense of destiny or fit — that a particular domain of work leverages one's particular gifts and consuming passions in service of a cause or purpose beyond self-interest."

## Derivation from Maslow

The authors ground this in **Maslow (1967)**, who studied what continues to motivate self-actualized people and identified two motivational priors: **inner requiredness** ("I want to," felt as self-indulgence) and **external requiredness** ("I must," felt as duty). Maslow argued the most powerful calling arises not from either alone but from the **matching** of the two — when "I want to" coincides with "I must," producing a perceived sense of harmony, inevitability, and preordained destiny. Maslow framed this as **self-transcendence** beyond self-actualization (Koltko-Rivera 2006); Thompson & Bunderson borrow the label.

## Function in the argument

The transcendent framing is the paper's lever for its other three questions: it distinguishes calling from related constructs by casting it as a strong form of [[Calling as Person-Job Fit|person–job fit]] (inner-to-outer match), argues for cross-cultural generalizability (the match may be a universal experience, not a Protestant legacy), and predicts calling will be uniquely tied to **resilience** and **rigidity** outcomes. Thompson & Bunderson note this balanced, destiny-acknowledging definition is **largely missing** from the existing literature — only 5 of 14 surveyed definitions explicitly include a sense of destiny/fate.
