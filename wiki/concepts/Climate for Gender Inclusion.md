# Climate for Gender Inclusion

The **cross-level linking mechanism** proposed by **[[kossek-su-wu-2017-opting-out-pushed-out|Kossek, Su & Wu (2017)]]** connecting antecedents (career-preference, bias, work–family) to **[[Women's Career Equality|career-equality]]** outcomes.

## Definition
The degree to which individuals and organizational social groups perceive and experience the work environment as involving social interactions, cultures, and structures that **support and effectively use the varied identities and values women bring to work**, fostering belongingness and the ability to leverage their talents.

Builds on climate theory (climates have specific referents; Schneider & Reichers 1983) and seminal diversity-climate work (Kossek & Zonia 1993). Distinguishes **psychological** (individual) from **organizational** (shared) climate.

## Three ordered dimensions
1. **Fairness & nondiscrimination** — practices and leader actions ensuring fairness in access, process, and outcomes; eliminating implicit and explicit bias. Most-researched; a **necessary-but-insufficient foundation**.
2. **Culture & strategy that leverage women's talents** — high **[[Person–Environment Fit|P–E fit]]** so the organization learns from and uses women's skills broadly (Ely & Thomas learning-and-effectiveness paradigm).
3. **Workplace support** — for women's values, voices, and needs; **[[Psychological Safety|belongingness and psychological safety]]**, plus tangible resources.

> [!tip] The ordering is a claim, not decoration
> Fairness must be in place **before** talent-leveraging and support can be enacted. Adopting mentoring, bias, or work–life policies *without* a supportive inclusion climate can backfire (e.g., reinforce stereotyping).

## Mechanism
Operates through **attraction-selection-attrition** (Schneider 1987): women are attracted to, select into, and stay in contexts they experience as gender-inclusive — linking individual career success to organizational representation.
