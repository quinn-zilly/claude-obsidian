# Subjective Career Success

## Definition

Subjective career success refers to one's own evaluation of career outcomes — how satisfied, fulfilled, or successful one perceives oneself to be — as distinguished from **objective career success** (pay, title, promotion speed, hierarchical level).

The distinction matters because objective and subjective career success can diverge significantly, and for minority employees, divergence is systematic: someone may achieve adequate objective markers while experiencing persistent subjective dissatisfaction due to [[Complex Visibility]] costs.

## Standard Dimensions

Classic subjective career success measures focus on:
- Job satisfaction
- Career satisfaction
- Perceived career success relative to personal goals
- Work-life balance / subjective well-being

## Seibert et al. (2024) — Eight-Dimension Saturation Map

[[seibert-akkermans-liu-2024-contemporary-career-success|Seibert, Akkermans & Liu (2024)]] argue SCS research has reached **saturation**: across the major conceptualizations (Dries et al. 2008; Shockley et al. 2016; Briscoe et al. 2021 / 5C), eight recurring dimensions emerge — (1) financial, (2) advancement in responsibility/status/influence, (3) interpersonal relations, (4) challenge & mastery, (5) meaning & impact, (6) self-development, (7) career opportunities & control, (8) the work-life interface — plus an overall satisfaction assessment.

Two open problems they flag:
- **Contamination / overlap** — SCS correlates ~.61 with job satisfaction; new dimensions overlap with job characteristics (Hackman & Oldham) and work-family balance. Scholars must show *incremental* validity beyond existing OB measures.
- **Dimensionality & use** — fine-grained multidimensional SCS for specific predictors/interventions; short global scales (Greenhaus et al. 1990) for broad theory testing.

They pair SCS with [[Objective Career Success]] and recommend including *both* unless there is a well-articulated reason not to.

## Beigi et al. (2025) — Expanded Dimensions for Minority Employees

Beigi et al. identify four additional subjective dimensions especially relevant to minority career experience:

| Dimension | Description |
|---|---|
| **Authenticity** | Ability to bring one's full self to work without identity concealment or managed visibility labor; feel that the version of self at work is genuine |
| **Survival** | Managing threat, hostility, and complex visibility burden without derailing career trajectory; being able to persist without burning out |
| **Collective good** | Sense of contributing to community — paving the way for others in one's group, serving as role model, advocacy within the organization |
| **Adjustability** | Capacity to adapt across contexts and career phases while maintaining core identity; different from chameleon-like managed visibility — this is strategic flexibility with authentic self intact |

## Why These Dimensions Matter

Standard career success measures were developed on (and validated with) predominantly majority-group samples. Applying them to minority careers misses systematically important outcomes:
- A minority executive paid fairly and promoted appropriately may score highly on objective success and standard subjective satisfaction, yet experience daily inauthenticity, persistent survival stress, and a felt obligation to carry the group — none of which standard scales capture.

## Cross-Domain Connections

- **[[On-the-Job Development]] / [[Learning Agility]]:** authenticity and psychological safety are prerequisites for the developmental risk-taking that OJD requires; managed visibility burden directly suppresses these
- **[[Motivated Reasoning]]:** majority-group managers don't typically track subjective career success beyond satisfaction scores, so minority-specific dimensions remain invisible to organizational metrics — ordinary cognitive blind spot
