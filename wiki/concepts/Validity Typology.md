# Validity Typology

Nav: [[index]] | [[log]]

The four-type framework for classifying inferences from experiments, canonical in Shadish, Cook & Campbell (2002). **Validity is a property of inferences, not of designs.** A method never guarantees a valid inference.

## The four types

| Type | Validity of inferences about… |
|---|---|
| [[Statistical Conclusion Validity]] | whether cause and effect **covary**, and how strongly |
| [[Internal Validity]] | whether that covariation reflects a **causal** relationship A→B |
| [[Construct Validity (Causal Generalization)]] | the **higher-order constructs** representing study operations |
| [[External Validity]] | whether the causal relationship **holds over variation** in persons, settings, treatments, outcomes |

## Four questions they answer

1. How large/reliable is the covariation? (SCV)
2. Is it causal, or would it have occurred without treatment? (IV)
3. Which general constructs are involved? (Construct)
4. How generalizable is the local relationship? (External)

## Lineage

Campbell (1957) coined internal vs external validity. Campbell & Stanley (1963) kept the two-type split. Cook & Campbell (1979) elaborated to four. SCC (2002) retained SCV/IV definitions (adding effect-size concerns) and broadened construct + external validity per Cronbach (1982) to cover all study elements (units, treatments, observations, settings).

## Key tension

Because methods map to validity types many-to-many, one design choice affects several types. **Randomization aids [[Internal Validity]] but can hamper [[External Validity]].** Diversifying participants aids external validity but can hurt statistical conclusion validity. No universal priority ranking exists — priority depends on the research question.
