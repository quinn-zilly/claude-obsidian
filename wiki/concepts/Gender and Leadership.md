# Gender and Leadership

A major research thread initiated in the Second Wave and still growing. Addresses differential leader emergence, leadership style, evaluation bias, and effectiveness by gender.

## Origins
**Schein (1973, 1975)** [Table 1, JAP]: "Think manager, think male" paradigm. Characteristics thought to describe men are more similar to characteristics of an effective middle manager than characteristics of women. Initiated decades of gender-leadership stereotype research.

**Megargee (1969)** [Table 1, JAP]: Women showed diminished leader emergence even when they had dominant personalities — situational and social pressure suppresses women's leadership expression.

## Four Research Literatures (meta-analyzed)

### 1. Leader Emergence
**Eagly & Karau (1991)**: Men emerge as leaders more often than women in initially leaderless groups; conditions that exacerbate or diminish this tendency identified.

### 2. Leadership Style
**Eagly & Johnson (1990)**: Women adopt more democratic and participative styles than men.
**Eagly, Johannesen-Schmidt & van Engen (2003)**: Women somewhat more transformational than men, especially in building supportive relationships; also more transactional in using rewards.

### 3. Evaluation Bias
**Rosen & Jerdee (1973)**: Limited evidence of bias in early study.
**Eagly, Makhijani & Klonsky (1992)**: Women devalued relative to equated men, especially when evaluated by men and when enacting autocratic/directive (culturally masculine) styles.

### 4. Effectiveness
**Eagly, Karau & Makhijani (1995)**: Leaders fare better in roles culturally congruent with or numerically dominated by their own sex — context effects.

## Role Congruity Theory
**Eagly & Karau (2002)**: General framework for understanding women's vulnerable status as leaders. Two forms of prejudice:
1. Less favorable evaluation of women's leadership potential (mismatch between female gender role and leader role)
2. Less favorable evaluation of actual female leadership behavior (agentic behavior violates communal expectations for women)

## Leadership Prototype Masculinity
**Koenig, Eagly, Mitchell & Ristikari (2011)** meta-analysis: Leadership prototype is still culturally masculine, but this has *ebbed* as interpersonal skill demands increased. Men regard leadership as more masculine than women do.
