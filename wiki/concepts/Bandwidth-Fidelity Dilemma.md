# Bandwidth-Fidelity Dilemma

The tension between **broad predictors** (wide bandwidth, lower fidelity) and **narrow predictors** (high fidelity, narrow bandwidth) in personality measurement. The term originates in **Shannon's information theory** (via Cronbach 1991) — a point [[hough-oswald-ock-2015-beyond-the-big-five|Hough, Oswald & Ock (2015)]] note is often forgotten.

Classic positions:

- **Cronbach & Gleser (1957)**: broad predictors predict broad criteria with moderate validity; maximizing validity requires **high theoretical/rational correspondence** between predictor and criterion, so specific criteria require specific (conceptually related) predictors.
- **[[Facet-Level Personality Measurement|Narrow measures]] often yield higher validity for narrower criteria** — a mountain of evidence (Ashton 1998; Dudley et al. 2006; Paunonen & Ashton; etc.).

> [!key-insight] Not a dilemma to resolve, but a tension to manage
> Hough, Oswald & Ock reframe it: broad-vs-narrow is **"a tension between theory (understanding) and practice (prediction) that should be continuously examined,"** not a dichotomy to be settled once. It becomes a genuine dilemma only when (a) predictor breadth can't be matched to criterion breadth, (b) there isn't time to reliably measure the full range of desired constructs, or (c) one is constrained by traditional validation models.

**Resolution via [[Synthetic Validity|synthetic validation]]**: assemble tailored batteries of *narrow* predictors matched to the criterion, achieving high validity while reducing testing time (drop constructs that don't add value). Sometimes broad/complex measures are unavoidable — especially on the **criterion** side, where performance complexity is deeply embedded and raters struggle to decompose it (hence *policy capturing*). Connects to the [[Criterion Problem]]: the criterion side is as much a bandwidth problem as the predictor side.
