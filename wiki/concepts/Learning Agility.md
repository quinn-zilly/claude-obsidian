# Learning Agility

**Learning agility** — the dispositional capacity to extract lessons from experience and apply them to novel challenges. One of the four dimensions of the [[Leadership Map]].

## Components (from Yost & Plunkett)

- Intellectual horsepower
- Openness to experience
- Integrity
- Comfort with ambiguity
- Openness to feedback

## Why It Matters

Not everyone who goes through a developmental experience emerges a better leader. The same stretch assignment produces variable outcomes depending on learning agility. Experience is necessary but not sufficient for [[On-the-Job Development]].

> Two people, same turnaround experience → different learning outcomes. Learning agility determines yield.

## Relationship to Other Constructs

- **Openness to experience** (Big Five) — strong overlap; learning agility is partly dispositional
- **Growth mindset** (Dweck) — related; learning agility includes behavioral as well as attitudinal components
- **Self-regulation** — transition effectiveness literature (Ashforth et al., 2007) emphasizes self-regulatory skills during high-change moments

## Connections

- [[On-the-Job Development]] — learning agility determines OJD yield from experiences
- [[Leadership Map]] — fourth dimension
- [[Organizational Learning]] — individual learning agility aggregates to org-level learning capacity
- [[Transition Checklist]] — transitions especially require learning agility

## Three-Way Relationship with Stretch Experiences
([[mccauley-yost-2021-comfort-zone]])

Learning agility relates to [[Stretch Experiences]] simultaneously as:
- **Antecedent**: learning-agile leaders *seek* stretch (change readiness, curiosity, learning goal orientation)
- **Moderator**: learning agility buffers negative consequences (emotional regulation prevents turnover; self-efficacy prevents laissez-faire leadership) and amplifies developmental gains
- **Outcome**: repeated stretch builds learning agility (self-insight, resilience, flexibility, feedback seeking)
