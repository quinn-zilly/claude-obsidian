# Growth Mindset

The belief that intelligence is **not fixed but malleable** — it can grow with effort and help from others (Dweck; Blackwell, Trzesniewski & Dweck 2007). Students with a growth mindset interpret academic challenge as an *opportunity to learn* rather than *evidence of fixed inability*, and respond to difficulty by trying harder rather than giving up. A canonical [[Wise Interventions|wise intervention]].

In the Blackwell et al. (2007) study, an eight-workshop growth-mindset intervention produced a **rebound in math grades** while the control group continued to decline.

## The precision caveat

[[Walton-2014-Wise-Psychological-Interventions|Walton (2014)]] uses growth mindset to illustrate that wise interventions depend on **wise theories**. If misunderstood, a growth-mindset message can be confused with the lay belief that students should be praised for *existing* intelligence ("You're so smart!"). That praise ironically induces a **fixed** mindset, which undermines motivation when students struggle (Mueller & Dweck 1998). Precise theory is what separates the effective message from the counterproductive one.
