# Distributive Justice

**Distributive justice** is the perceived fairness of *outcomes* — how rewards, resources, and burdens are allocated among people. It is one of the [[Four Justice Dimensions]] of [[Organizational Justice]], distinguished from procedural justice (fairness of the *process*), interpersonal justice (fairness of *enactment*), and informational justice (fairness of *explanations*). Where procedural justice asks "was the decision made fairly?", distributive justice asks "is the outcome itself fair?"

## The Three Allocation Rules

Distributive justice is judged against three allocation rules (Adams 1965; Leventhal 1976; see [[Four Justice Dimensions]], Colquitt & Rodell 2015 Table 8.1):

| Rule | Outcomes are fair when allocated by… | Typical context |
|------|--------------------------------------|-----------------|
| **Equity** | contribution / input (the equity ratio) | performance-based reward, pay-for-performance |
| **Equality** | equally, regardless of input | team cohesion, egalitarian settings |
| **Need** | need, regardless of input | welfare, hardship, care contexts |

Which rule feels fair is context-dependent: equity dominates in market/economic exchanges, equality in solidarity-oriented groups, need in caring relationships.

## Equity Theory Roots

The equity rule descends from **Adams' (1965) equity theory**: people compare their own outcome/input ratio to a referent's. Perceived *inequity* (over- or under-reward) produces tension the person is motivated to reduce — by adjusting inputs, distorting perceptions, changing the referent, or leaving. Distributive justice generalizes this by admitting equality and need as alternative fairness standards beyond strict equity.

## Place in the Justice Structure

- Distributive and procedural justice are the more **structural** dimensions (Greenberg): relatively stable over time and more organization-focused, versus the more day-to-day, supervisor-focused social dimensions (interpersonal, informational). See [[Four Justice Dimensions]].
- In the **two-factor model** (Sweeney & McFarlin), distributive justice predicts *outcome-referenced* dependent variables (pay satisfaction, reward satisfaction), while procedural justice predicts *system-referenced* ones (organizational commitment, trust in the institution).
- The four dimensions are highly intercorrelated (avg r ≈ .49; [[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al. 2013]]), which complicates isolating distributive justice's unique effects — see [[Justice Measurement Taxonomy]].

## Descriptive vs. Normative: A Blind Spot in I/O

I/O psychology has overwhelmingly studied *perceived* distributive justice — whether employees **feel** an allocation is fair — rather than the normative question of which distributions actually **ought** to be fair. [[lefkowitz-2008-values-organizational-psychology|Lefkowitz (2008)]] makes this a central indictment: the field is "invariably ethical but often **amoral**," studying perceived procedural and interactional justice while sidestepping distributive justice and the normative question of just distribution altogether. The remedy he proposes — the [[Scientist-Practitioner-Humanist (S-P-H) Model]] — calls on I/O psychologists to adopt a normative stance and engage distributive justice, CSR, and multiple-stakeholder concerns rather than only measuring perceptions.

This connects organizational-behavior distributive justice to the older political-philosophy tradition (Rawls, Nozick) where "distributive justice" names the normative theory of how a society's benefits and burdens should be shared.

## Why It Predicts Behavior

Like the other justice dimensions, distributive justice shapes attitudes and behavior largely through [[Social Exchange Theory|social exchange]]: fair outcomes signal a high-quality exchange relationship, which employees reciprocate with commitment, citizenship, and performance. It also operates partly through affect. See [[Organizational Justice]] for the two-lens (exchange vs. affect) account.
