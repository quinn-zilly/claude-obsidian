# Means-Ends Inversion

The "morally anemic technocratic view" that the applied psychologist supplies **means** to whatever **ends** the employer chooses, and is "not burdened" with judging those ends. Traced by [[Joel Lefkowitz|Lefkowitz]] (2008) to Munsterberg (1913):

> [!quote] Munsterberg (1913)
> "Economic psychotechnics may serve certain ends of commerce and industry, but whether these ends are the best ones is not a care with which the psychologist has to be burdened."

Lefkowitz counters with Roback (1917), who in the *first* volume of the *Journal of Applied Psychology* argued the applied psychologist — unlike the bridge-building engineer — *must* consider the ethical implications of the task: "As no purpose is ultimate ... there will be a tendency to rule out all ends and to ignore every consideration but what is expedient."

Because organizations are enormously powerful and their actions have societal consequences, the psychologist should be "more than just a hired hand" and engage with the **ends**, not only technically competent **means**. This is the fifth indictment underpinning the [[Scientist-Practitioner-Humanist (S-P-H) Model]].
