# General Factor in Job Performance

The hypothesis that rated job-performance dimensions share a large common factor — employees good on one dimension tend to be good on all (a positive manifold), plausibly because the same underlying traits (general cognitive ability, conscientiousness) drive performance across dimensions (Feldman 1981; Hulin 1982; Motowidlo et al. 1997).

## The controversy

Factor analyses almost always find one big general factor in ratings. Two explanations:

1. **Artifact** — it's just [[Halo Error|halo error]] (Landy, Vance & Barnes-Farrell). If so, you should *partial it out* of ratings.
2. **Substance** — a true general factor exists independent of halo (Harvey, Hulin, Murphy). Partialling it out would then destroy valid variance.

You cannot tell the two apart without controlling for **all four** rating error sources: halo, rater leniency, random response error, transient error.

## The resolution (Viswesvaran, Schmidt & Ones 2005)

After netting out all four, a **real general factor remains at the construct level, accounting for ~60% of total variance**. So the factor is substantive, not pure halo — but [[Halo Error|halo still inflates]] observed interdimension correlations (+33% supervisor, +63% peer). Supervisory and peer ratings are **construct-equivalent**.

> [!warning] Construct ≠ true performance
> The general factor is in *ratings-true* scores (what's shared across raters), which may correlate <1.0 with actual job performance. And **shared halo** — erroneous impressions common to raters — could still inflate even between-rater estimates; evidence is conflicting.

## Why it matters

- Supports a hierarchical model of performance with a meaningful general factor at the top.
- Justifies *not* mechanically partialling the general factor out of ratings.
- Stable performance structure underwrites the rebuttal that selection equations don't go stale (see [[Mechanical vs Clinical Prediction]]).
