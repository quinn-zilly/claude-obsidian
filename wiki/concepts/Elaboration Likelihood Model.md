# Elaboration Likelihood Model

Petty & Cacioppo's (1986) dual-route theory of persuasion. How carefully a message is processed depends on the recipient's **ability** and **motivation**:

- **High elaboration → central route:** careful scrutiny of argument quality. Attitudes formed this way are **persistent, resistant to change, and more predictive of behavior**.
- **Low elaboration → peripheral route:** reliance on simple cues/**heuristics** (source credibility, message length, others' opinions, "good workplaces make good ads").

## Application to recruitment

Both [[uggerslev-et-al-2012-recruiting-through-stages|Uggerslev et al. (2012)]] and [[breaugh-2013-employee-recruitment|Breaugh (2013)]] use ELM to explain *why* predictor importance shifts across **[[Recruitment Stages (Barber)|recruitment stages]]**:

- **Early:** applicants have little info (low ability) and skim many options (low motivation) → **low EL** → rely on **[[Recruiter Behaviors|recruiter]] cues and [[Signaling Theory (Recruitment)|signals]]**.
- **Later:** more info + rising commitment → **high EL** → effortful scrutiny of **job/organizational information** and **procedural-justice** aspects of the process; heuristic recruiter cues lose persuasive force.

Because personally relevant, high-stakes messages invite effortful processing, **job/organizational information tends to be a stronger predictor of attraction than recruiter/process cues** — while the controllable cues still add unique early-stage variance.

## Related persuasion models (Breaugh 2013)

Hovland et al. (1953, source credibility) and **McGuire's (1968)** six-stage sequence (presentation → attention → comprehension → yielding → retention → behavior). Chaiken's heuristic-systematic model parallels ELM's peripheral/central split.
