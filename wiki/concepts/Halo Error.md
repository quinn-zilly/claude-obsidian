# Halo Error

A rating measurement error first named by Thorndike (1920): a rater's **overall impression** of a ratee bleeds into their ratings of every specific dimension, making the dimensions look more correlated than they are.

## Process, not effect

Best conceptualized as a **psychological process**, not a single observable effect (Stanley 1961; Wells 1907). Before rating, each rater forms an overall impression of a ratee. Part is *shared* with other raters (true variance); part is *idiosyncratic* to that rater — the **Rater × Ratee interaction** — and that idiosyncratic part **is** halo error. It's directly analogous to the Subject × Item specific-factor error in a test.

## The asymmetry (key for measurement)

Because halo is uncorrelated *across* raters:

- **Inflates** within-rater reliabilities and within-rater interdimension correlations.
- **Deflates** between-rater reliabilities and between-rater interdimension correlations.

This asymmetry is what lets [[viswesvaran-schmidt-ones-2005-general-factor-job-performance|Viswesvaran et al. (2005)]] *isolate* halo: the gap between within-rater-corrected and between-rater-corrected interdimension correlations = halo's inflationary effect. Measured size: **+33% supervisor, +63% peer**.

## Terminology cautions

- **"True halo"** is a misnomer — halo is by definition error. Use *actual correlations* for the real-world cross-dimension relationships.
- **Shared halo** ≠ halo error: an erroneous impression *common* to raters would look like true variance under classical test theory and would not be removed by between-rater correction. It's a form of criterion contamination and must be studied separately.

## Connection

Halo is the artifact the [[General Factor in Job Performance|general-factor]] debate had to overcome; removing it (plus leniency, random, transient error) is what reveals the ~60% substantive general factor.

Historically halo was the first-named member of the [[Rater Error Measures|"holy trinity" of rater errors]] (halo, leniency, central tendency) used to judge [[Performance Appraisal|appraisal]] quality — until Murphy & Balzer (1986) showed error measures don't track accuracy.
