# Five Design Characteristics (OJD)

The five "5S" design principles Yost (2014) derives from interviews with 16 senior HR professionals about OJD interventions that survived 10+ years. Each characteristic increases the probability that an initiative will be adopted, adapted, and sustained across the organization without top-down mandate.

**Source:** Yost et al. (2011). HR interventions that go viral. *Journal of Business and Psychology, 26*, 233–239; synthesized in Yost (2014) Ch. 6.

---

## 1. Strategic

**Core question:** Does the initiative align with and support the business strategy?

Principles:
- Frame in business language, not HR-speak ("how will your success be measured?" not "set performance goals")
- Position OJD as how the business achieves its strategy — not as an HR add-on
- Leaders develop when they are put in roles where they **execute the business strategy and develop themselves at the same time** (Yost & Plunkett 2009)
- Connect developmental experiences to specific future strategic challenges senior leaders have already articulated

Evidence base: Becker & Huslid (2006); Lawler (2003); McCall (2010) — HR processes significantly more accepted when closely aligned with business strategy.

---

## 2. Simple

**Core question:** Are the tools easy to use? Do they make the leader's job easier?

Principles:
- Maximum one page for tools users will see
- Clear, concrete actions the leader can take
- User-interface simplicity can hide back-end complexity ("think Apple products")
- Avoid HR jargon entirely
- Short tools get used; comprehensive tools get filed

> The temptation is to make the checklist comprehensive. Resist it. A short checklist will be used. A long one won't.

---

## 3. Sneeze-able

**Core question:** Do people want to use it AND pass it on?

**Sticky** (Gladwell 2000; Heath & Heath 2007): people want to use it. Tools should be so compelling that leaders "would be stupid not to use them."

**Sneeze-able** (Godin 2001): people want to transmit it. Tools should:
- Stand alone without requiring the user to consult other materials
- Be written in first person (focus on what the user wants)
- Include items that prompt interaction with others (networking, feedback-seeking)
- Be self-contained enough that forwarding makes immediate sense

Test: *Is this a tool I would use as I start my next job? Would I pass it on?*

Classic examples:
- Leadership competency **card sorts**: leaders asked "Can I get a set of these?" after assessment sessions
- "**Development Plans That Aren't a Waste of Time**" quiz: went viral when managers forwarded it to teams during annual performance cycle; visceral low-end message ("you may finish the year less employable than you started") drove sharing

---

## 4. Systemic

**Core question:** Can this tool plug into other HR processes?

> The World Wide Web may be a better metaphor than a hierarchy — separate entities that complement, combine, and sometimes compete with each other. Or think Legos™ or hamster Habitrail components.

Principles:
- Design tools so they work standalone AND attach to adjacent processes (orientation, manager training, leadership programs)
- Use language broad enough to apply across leadership levels and geographies
- Post URL links directly in the forms/documents where the tool will be needed ("just in time")
- Identify the other talent management processes where it might be used BEFORE finalizing the tool

Tension with sneeze-ability: tools must be standalone (sneeze-able) but also designed to plug in (systemic). Resolve by designing the tool to complement other processes without *requiring* them as context.

---

## 5. Sustainable

**Core question:** Did I build this in a way that will outlast me?

Principles:
- Minimize ongoing energy and resources required to maintain
- Build in self-adaptation mechanisms (stakeholder input, pilot feedback loops)
- Proactively recruit champions in other business units before scaling
- Create a "pull" from pilots — visibility + early success → demand from elsewhere
- Engage key stakeholders (HR generalists, direct users) in rollout design

Evidence base (Yost et al. 2011 interviews): top-down factors were important but were *proactively cultivated* by change leaders from the middle — sponsors were recruited, not awaited. Classic example: HR professional plants an idea in a senior leader conversation that eventually becomes "the leader's idea."

Weick (1984) small wins as the operational model: discrete, high-P(success) actions that compound iteratively.

## Application Matrix

| HR System | Strategic | Simple | Sneeze-able | Systemic | Sustainable |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Succession management | Define experiences by strategic need | Experience × lessons matrix (1 page) | Post on intranet; anyone can access | Cascade experience definitions to lower levels | Pilot in high-profile BU |
| Performance management | Business language in dev plan instructions | 1-page dev idea list by challenge type | Link in dev plan form; managers forward | URL embeds; surfaces "just in time" | Managers trained alongside employees |
| Communities of Practice | Senior leader as sponsor | Pilot → model for others | Easy on-boarding for outsiders | Shared leadership structure | Self-sustaining governance |
