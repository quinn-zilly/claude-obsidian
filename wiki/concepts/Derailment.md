# Derailment

**Definition**: the involuntary cessation of a once-advancing career. Derailment is distinct from voluntary career change or planned plateau — it is the leader being stopped against their will or expectation.

Originally studied at the Center for Creative Leadership (McCall & Lombardo, 1983; Van Velsor & Leslie, 1995).

## When It Happens

Derailment is most likely during **transitions**:
- New boss with different priorities and values
- New job (especially lateral moves or promotions to unfamiliar domains)
- Environmental shift (new market, reorganization, strategic pivot)

The mechanism: strategies that worked before stop working. Past **strengths become weaknesses** in the new context. The leader who succeeded through collaboration in a staff role may derail in a fast-execution sales role using the same tactics.

## The 10 Deadly Sins (Yost & Plunkett, 2009)

| # | Sin | Core failure |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Poor performance | Unclear success criteria; bad fit; eyes on next job |
| 2 | Failure to adapt | Can't adjust when environment changes |
| 3 | Arrogance | Self-confidence tips into ignoring feedback |
| 4 | Blind spots | Overreliance on strengths; can't see what others see |
| 5 | Failure to build a strong team | Weak talent; clones of self; no conditions for success |
| 6 | Failure to build a network | Too tied to one sponsor; invisible in succession |
| 7 | Poor working relationships | Drive for results → abrasive; OR tries to please everyone |
| 8 | Avoiding stretch assignments | Doesn't prove self "in the fire" |
| 9 | Unethical behavior | Drift in gray areas |
| 10 | Bad luck | Wrong place, wrong time — but still examine your role |

## Connection to Leadership Pipeline

In the [[Leadership Pipeline]] model (Charan), derailment often happens when a leader skips a passage or fails to complete a passage fully. The leader carries forward the wrong skills, time applications, and work values for their current level. This is structural derailment — not character failure, but developmental incompleteness.

## Connection to Stretch Experiences

Avoiding [[Stretch Experiences]] (#8 above) reduces future promotion chances and is itself a derailment risk. Yet stretch also *causes* derailment risk when the leader lacks the emotional regulation or self-efficacy to manage the stress. The solution is not avoiding stretch but **surrounding it with support**: learning goals, coaching, feedback, reflection.

## Recovery Process

Recovery is hard and takes time. The key steps:
1. Find your part in the story — neither over-blame nor under-own
2. Seek outside perspectives — including from people who are not your fans
3. Come to terms with what was unfair AND with your role
4. Decide who you want to be going forward

> [!key-insight] Derailment can be growth
> Leaders who do the hard reflective work often become better leaders on the other side. Plateauing can force a shift from defining value by job title to finding meaning in contribution and legacy.

## Organizational Prevention

- Structured onboarding for leadership transitions
- 360-degree feedback and performance systems as early warning
- Pre-move interviews at senior levels (Microsoft, IBM)
- Define success criteria explicitly with the manager — don't leave it to chance
- Technical career paths so management is reserved for willing leaders (derailment-resistant culture)
