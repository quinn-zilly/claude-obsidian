# Knowledge Management

## Definition

Knowledge management (KM) is the deliberate organizational effort to identify, create, represent, distribute, and enable adoption of knowledge — both tacit and explicit — within and across organizations. The goal is to leverage knowledge assets for competitive advantage.

**Alavi & Leidner (2001):** KM is the process of creating, storing, transferring, and applying organizational knowledge, supported by information technologies (knowledge management systems, KMS).

## The Four KM Processes

From Alavi & Leidner (2001):

1. **Knowledge Creation** — learning, R&D, knowledge combination and conversion
2. **Knowledge Storage & Retrieval** — organizational memory, expert systems, repositories
3. **Knowledge Transfer** — communication, socialization, IT-mediated sharing
4. **Knowledge Application** — using knowledge in decision-making and problem-solving

IT can support each process differently. The process view avoids treating KMS as a single monolithic system.

## Five Views of Knowledge (and Their KMS Implications)

| Perspective | Knowledge is... | KMS should... |
|---|---|---|
| State of mind | Personal understanding | Support individual internalization |
| Object | Storable, manipulable thing | Build repositories and retrieval |
| Process | Knowing and acting simultaneously | Support workflows and practice |
| Access | Condition of having information | Connect people to expertise |
| Capability | Capacity for effective action | Enable performance and competence |

## Data → Information → Knowledge (and the Inversion)

Common hierarchy: raw data → processed information → authenticated knowledge.

Tuomi (1999) inverts this: knowledge must exist before information can be formulated, and before data can be measured. "Raw data" don't exist — even the most elementary data point reflects prior knowledge that led to identifying and collecting it.

Practical implication: KMS cannot simply be information systems with a "knowledge" label. They must support the cognitive and social processes through which individuals convert information into personal knowledge.

## Tacit vs. Explicit — The Central Tension

The most consequential design decision in KM is how to handle tacit knowledge — experiential, context-specific, difficult to articulate. Most IT tools are better at capturing explicit knowledge (documents, databases). Tacit knowledge transfers through socialization, apprenticeship, communities of practice.
