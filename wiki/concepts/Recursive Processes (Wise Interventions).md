# Recursive Processes (Wise Interventions)

The self-reinforcing loops that make brief [[Wise Interventions]] produce effects that **compound and grow over time**. Central mechanism in [[Walton-2014-Wise-Psychological-Interventions|Walton (2014)]].

## Snapshot vs. movie

A wise intervention changes not a moment in time (a "snapshot") but a *process that unfolds over time* (a "movie"; Kenthirarajah & Walton 2013). In a relationship or a school career, every interaction builds on the previous one, so altering the loop early puts the person in a **new situation** downstream.

## Proximal outcomes carry effects forward

Real-world factors (friendships, school ties, a better-disposed spouse) are **not just noise that decays effects** — they act as *proximal outcomes* that facilitate longer-term gains. This is why the [[Social Belonging Intervention|social-belonging]] and perspective-taking interventions produced their **largest** benefits at the most distal time point assessed, and why wise interventions cause the biggest long-term gains in **inherently recursive contexts** (relationships, school).

## Timing and affordances

Recursion cuts both ways. [[Values Affirmation]] delivered *early* prevents downward threat cycles; delivered *after* a cycle is established, the same exercise is weaker (Cook et al. 2012). Effects also require the context to provide affordances for the loop to continue (a belonging intervention only helps if ties can actually form).
