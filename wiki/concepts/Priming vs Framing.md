# Priming vs Framing

## The Distinction

One of Cornelissen & Werner's (2014) two core theoretical contributions is the distinction between **priming** and **framing** — constructs that the literature frequently conflates, creating measurement, theoretical, and ontological confusion.

| | Priming | Framing |
|---|---|---|
| **Direction** | Top-down (theory-driven) | Bottom-up (meaning construction) |
| **Process** | Accessing/activating an existing cognitive schema | Actively constructing new meaning in context |
| **Mechanism** | Cue → retrieves frame from memory | Language/interaction → new frame emerges |
| **Locus** | Individual cognition | Social interaction |
| **Stability** | Frames as relatively stable knowledge structures | Frames as dynamic, socially situated products |
| **Level** | Micro (individual cognition) | Meso/macro (social, organizational) |

## Why the Conflation Happens

Both processes involve "frames" in some sense, and acts of framing in language often *incorporate* priming — an utterance that frames a situation may simultaneously trigger the retrieval of a well-established cognitive schema. The reciprocal connection between the two is real and important: language invokes cognitive frames; new frames are established through creative extensions in language.

The problem arises when researchers treat these as the same construct, which leads to:
- **Reification:** dynamic meaning construction is reduced to a stable packaged output
- **Static bias:** researchers identify frames in texts rather than studying framing processes
- **Level confusion:** micro cognitive models are applied to meso/macro social phenomena without theoretical justification

## Priming (Micro Focus)

Sherman et al. (1990): priming = the activation of a cognitive frame as a knowledge structure, and the speed with which it is accessed, based on observable cues. May be conscious (assigning a cue to a prototypical frame) or unconscious (automatic associative retrieval).

The focus of most cognition-in-organizations research: Hodgkinson et al. (1999), Kennedy & Fiss (2009), March & Simon (1958), Seo et al. (2010). These studies examine how gain/loss framing, reference points, or other cues prime cognitive frames that then create assimilation effects — the entire context is processed consistent with the prototypical content of the primed frame.

## Framing (Meso/Macro Focus)

Goffman (1974): framing as the active task of figuring out what is going on — without which no utterance or gesture could be interpreted. Tannen (1985): frames are not merely accessed; they are socially constructed and may be novel. Language and cognition are recursive: language makes active use of implied cognitive frames while simultaneously constructing new ones.

The focus of symbolic interactionist, social movement, and neo-institutional research.

## Mutual Recursion

Cornelissen & Werner argue the two are **reciprocally and recursively interconnected**:
1. Acts of framing (language) invoke and prime existing cognitive frames
2. Activated cognitive frames constrain the available framings an actor can enact
3. Over time, framing-in-interaction can establish new shared cognitive frames (Tannen 1985)

The distinction is analytically useful — it marks: (a) instances where available frames are primed vs. (b) instances where meanings are actively constructed. Making it explicit helps researchers choose appropriate methods and make appropriate claims.

## Methodological Implication

Research focused on priming → experimental and quasi-experimental designs measuring cue → frame activation → behavioral outcome.

Research focused on framing-as-process → ethnographic, interaction analytic, and conversation analytic methods that capture meaning construction in real time.
