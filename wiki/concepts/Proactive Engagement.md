# Proactive Engagement

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Motivation Meta-Framework (Kanfer)]]

A dynamic, self-directed syndrome of cognitive, affective, and motivational states characterized by **vigor, dedication, and absorption** that propel and sustain goal-directed activities (Kanfer, Frese & Johnson 2017; Grant & Parker 2009).

## Components

- **Vigor** — high energy and mental resilience at work
- **Dedication** — sense of significance, enthusiasm, inspiration, pride, challenge
- **Absorption** — full concentration and engrossment in work

Related to but distinct from work engagement (Schaufeli & Bakker 2010), proactive behavior (Parker et al. 2006), and personal initiative (Frese & Fay 2001).

## Personal Initiative

Frese & Fay (2001): personal initiative = self-starting, proactive, persistent work behavior that overcomes barriers. Requires:
1. Goal-setting that is self-concordant
2. Proactive planning (developing Plan B and C)
3. Active error management and feedback seeking
4. Persistence in face of obstacles

Initiative is distinct from compliance: initiative implies going beyond prescribed role requirements.

## Job Crafting

Wrzesniewski & Dutton (2001): employees actively reshape jobs to better fit their motives, strengths, and identity. Job crafting = micro-level proactive work redesign. Connected to SDT autonomy need and self-determination.

## Why Proactive Engagement Matters Now

Changes in the nature of work: service jobs cannot be fully formalized; accelerated technology requires preemptive preparation; career responsibility shifted to individual. Creates need for research on intersection of proactivity and engagement.

## Resources and Well-Being

Investigation requires identifying job resources (autonomy, social support) enabling proactive engagement and consequences for well-being (burnout, work–family conflict). Evidence of link between proactive work engagement and well-being in later adulthood (Schooler et al. 2004).
