# Social Exchange Theory (SET)

One of the most influential paradigms in organizational behavior for explaining workplace behavior. **Core essence** (on which all variants converge): social exchange is **a series of interdependent, contingent interactions that generate obligations** and, over time and under the right conditions, produce high-quality relationships of trust, loyalty, and mutual commitment.

## Roots

Interdisciplinary: anthropology (Malinowski 1922, Mauss 1925), social psychology (Homans 1958, [[Alvin Gouldner|Gouldner]] 1960, Thibaut & Kelley 1959), sociology ([[Peter Blau|Blau]] 1964). Homans' founding move: exchanges include **symbolic** value (approval, prestige), not just material goods.

## Three Conceptual Pillars (Cropanzano & Mitchell 2005)

[[cropanzano-mitchell-2005-social-exchange-theory|Cropanzano & Mitchell (2005)]] argue SET is influential but **underspecified**, and organize its explanatory power into three ideas — each historically under-defined.

1. **Rules and norms of exchange** — the guidelines of exchange. Management over-focuses on [[Norm of Reciprocity|reciprocity]] and neglects Meeker's (1971) other five rules (rationality, altruism, group gain, status consistency, competition) and **negotiated** rules.
2. **Resources exchanged** — [[Foa & Foa Resource Theory]]: six resources on particularism × concreteness dimensions; collapsed in OB into **economic vs. socioemotional** outcomes.
3. **Social exchange relationships** — the most-researched pillar. Blau's signature: social exchange involves **unspecified, diffuse future obligations** and engenders trust and gratitude, unlike economic exchange. The relationship typically functions as a **mediator** between fair treatment and effective work behavior.

## Economic vs. Social Exchange (Blau 1964)

| | Economic exchange | Social exchange |
|---|---|---|
| Obligations | Specified, bargained | Unspecified, diffuse |
| Time frame | Short, defined | Open-ended |
| Trust | Not required | Engendered |
| Resources | Concrete/universal | Often particularistic/symbolic |

## The Causal-Order Question

Do exchanges create relationships or do relationships shape exchanges? Cropanzano & Mitchell's **ladder metaphor**: development is iterative; a past transaction's output becomes a future transaction's input. Relational benefits (trust, commitment) are **both result and resource**. Lab work (Molm) captures early-stage formation; field work (Eisenberger, Bishop) captures mature relationships.

## Operationalizations of "Exchange Quality"

The contemporary justice literature measures exchange-relationship quality with four constructs:

- [[Perceived Organizational Support]] (POS) — employee↔organization
- [[Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)]] — employee↔supervisor
- **Trust** — confident positive expectations about a trustee
- [[Organizational Commitment]] — affective attachment as repayment for support

[[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al. (2013)]] meta-analytically confirmed these four mediate justice→performance and justice→OCB (but not justice→CWB).

## Connection to Justice

SET is the **dominant lens** explaining why [[Organizational Justice|justice]] predicts behavior: justice is a symbolic, particularistic **resource**; reciprocity is the **rule**; trust/commitment/POS/LMX index the **relationship**. See [[Organizational Justice]].
