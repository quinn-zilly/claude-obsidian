# Differentiated Evaluations

The **pro-ratings** rationale in [[adler-et-al-2016-getting-rid-of-ratings|Adler et al. (2016)]]: quantifying individual performance differences and **differentially rewarding** on that basis contributes to individual and organizational performance. A quantitative index is what enables merit-based differentiation; abandoning ratings collapses it into unreliable narrative judgment.

## The four converging findings (sorting effects)

1. **Strong performers are differentially attracted** to firms that recognize individual contribution (Trank, Rynes & Bretz 2002; Cadsby et al. 2007).
2. **Strong performers leave** firms that don't differentiate (low salary growth → top-performer turnover; Trevor, Gerhart & Boudreau 1997).
3. **Weak performers leave** firms that strongly reward high performance (Lazear 1986).
4. **Weak performers stay** where pay-for-performance differentiation is weak.

Together these are **sorting/incentive effects**: differentiation shapes *who joins and stays*, not just current effort. CEB (2014) adds that Millennials are *more* competitive and want relative differentiation.

> [!contradiction] Differentiation vs collaboration
> The pro-ratings side argues differentiation drives performance and desirable sorting. The anti-ratings side counters that differentiated ratings tied to rewards can **hurt** motivation and performance, especially in collaborative settings (Casas-Arce & Martinez-Jerez 2009). Same mechanism, opposite valence depending on work interdependence. See [[Rating-less Reviews]].
