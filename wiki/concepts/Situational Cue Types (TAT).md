# Situational Cue Types (TAT)

Nav: [[index]] | [[concepts/_index]]

TAT's unique contribution to interactionism: six *functionally distinct* types of trait-relevant situational features, classified by how they moderate trait–outcome relationships. First 5: Tett & Burnett (2003). 6th: Tett et al. (2013).

## The Six Types

### 1. Demands
Cues where *responding is positively valued*. Meeting a demand is good.

- Function: activates the trait; yields **positive** trait–outcome correlation
- Example: A manager's job requiring frequent social interaction is a demand for sociability → high sociability → better performance
- Most studied: 60% of TAT empirical tests

### 2. Distracters
Cues where *responding is negatively valued* (at least for primary work outcomes).

- Function: activates the trait; yields **negative** trait–outcome correlation (for positively valued outcomes)
- If the outcome is itself negative (e.g., CWBs), direction reverses
- Example: An informal coworker gathering *distracts* highly sociable workers from task completion → sociability → lower task performance
- Note: demands and distracters are both **trait activators** — they differ only in valence of the response

### 3. Constraints
Situational features that *reduce trait relevance*, thereby weakening the trait–outcome relationship.

- Function: attenuates (moderates toward zero)
- Example: Physical distance between manager and subordinates *constrains* the relevance of sociability for predicting performance
- Strong situations often function as constraints

### 4. Releasers
Counter constraints; operate as *intermittent activators*.

- Function: restores trait relevance that was suppressed by a constraint
- Example: A company retreat releases sociability cues for the physically isolated manager
- Rarely studied (1% of TAT tests) — partly because they are temporary by definition

### 5. Facilitators
Uniquely **multiplicative** — enhance the salience or potency of trait-relevant cues *already present*.

- Function: amplifies existing demands or distracters
- Example: An ad promoting Las Vegas's social opportunities amplifies sociability cues already present at the retreat
- Distinct from releasers: facilitators enhance, releasers restore

### 6. Discretionary Cues
Trait expression opportunities *untethered to valued outcomes* — neither demanded, rewarded, nor punished.

- Function: drive **intrinsic motivation** from need satisfaction, independent of performance
- Related to work autonomy / weak situations, but trait-specific (not general autonomy)
- Example: A production supervisor position does not *require* creativity for performance, but may *offer* creative opportunities (developing training games) that engage creative workers' needs
- Key: these cues motivate engagement with core work demands even when not definitionally part of the job
- Account for 13% of task-level tests despite being "optional"

## Summary Table

| Type | Activates Trait? | Tied to Valued Outcome? | Effect on r(trait, perf) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Demand | Yes | Yes (positive) | Positive ↑ |
| Distracter | Yes | Yes (negative) | Negative ↓ |
| Constraint | No (suppresses) | N/A | Attenuates → 0 |
| Releaser | Restores | Depends on prior context | Restores |
| Facilitator | Amplifies | Yes | Amplifies |
| Discretionary | Yes | No | Motivational only |

## Operational Level Distribution

Demands and facilitators are mainly task-level; distracters and constraints more often social-level. Discretionary cues: 100% task-level in current data.

## Significance Rates (2011–2019 review)
At task level (154 non-releaser cases): distracters = 100% significant (most potent!), demands = 54%, facilitators = 67%, constraints = 63%, discretionary = 45% (weakest — weak situations by definition).

## Relationship to Situation Strength

[[Situation Strength]] operates at the trait-*general* level (strong situations suppress all traits). Discretionary cues operate at the trait-*specific* level. Both predict trait expression, but situation trait relevance > situation strength as a predictor of personality validity (Judge & Zapata 2015 meta-analysis).
