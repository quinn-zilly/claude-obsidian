# Learning and Development Perspective on Work Design

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]] | [[Relational Job Design]]

Contemporary, integrative work design perspective advanced by Parker (2014): work design drives **learning, development, and identity**, not just motivation and satisfaction. Highlighted (alongside [[Relational Job Design]]) as an integrative perspective in [[parker-morgeson-johns-2017-100-years-work-design|Parker et al. (2017)]].

## Core Idea

Jobs with certain characteristics — high demands + control (Karasek & Theorell, 1990), autonomy (Wall, Jackson & Davids, 1992), and complex work with low supervision (Kohn & Schooler, 1982) — promote **worker learning and development**. This is distinct from the traditional motivational focus of the [[Job Characteristics Model|JCM]]: work design can foster **moral, cognitive, and personality** development.

## Evidence

- Job demands + control predict development of a more **proactive personality**, which has lagged beneficial effects on work characteristics (Li, Fay, Frese, Harms & Gao, 2014).
- Job autonomy predicts development of a more internal **locus of control**, which then predicts later autonomy (Wu, Griffin & Parker, 2015) — a reciprocal, agency-building loop.

## Links to Careers & Identity

Dovetails with the careers field: "stretch work" builds skills over a career (O'Mahony & Bechky, 2006); "smart jobs" enhance adaptive capability and self-identity (Hall & Heras, 2010). Parker et al. (2017) extend this into a proactive agenda: enriched work can build **adaptive identities** (callings, future work selves; Strauss, Griffin & Parker, 2012) that are assets in a changing world of work — whereas deskilling technology can provoke an identity schism.

## Significance

Part of the paper's central call to **expand the criterion space** — to study what work design *achieves* (learning, identity, adaptation to automation) rather than only its effect on a fixed set of outcomes.
