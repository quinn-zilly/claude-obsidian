# Intentional Distortion (Faking)

Deliberate positive self-presentation on self-report personality measures in high-stakes settings — "the issue that still haunts" personality testing ([[hough-johnson-2013-personality-in-work-settings|Hough & Johnson 2013]]).

## Findings that have stood the test of time

- **People can fake when instructed** (more dramatically in the negative direction); social-desirability scales are sensitive to it (Hough et al. 1990; Viswesvaran & Ones 1999).
- **Distortion is larger in directed-faking studies than in real high-stakes settings** (Hough 1998).
- Applicant **factor structures do not collapse** the way instructed-faking structures do (Ellingson, Smith & Sackett 2001).
- Applicants score higher than incumbents, but **less than commonly thought** — moderate differences on Emotional Stability and Conscientiousness only (Birkeland et al. 2006).

> [!key-insight] Faking does not appear to be corrosive of criterion-related validity
> Even in high-stakes real-life testing, predictive validities with applicants seem **unaffected** by social-desirability scores. Corrections to content scales (e.g., Conscientiousness) based on "Unlikely Virtues" scores do **not** improve — and may **differentially disadvantage Hispanics**, who score ~0.5 SD higher on Unlikely Virtues.

## Warnings and consequences

- **Warnings + consequences reduce distortion; warnings alone do not** (Dwight & Donovan 2003). Warnings are effective in real applicants but not in faking studies (Stanush 1997).
- Warnings do **not** harm criterion validity (validities remain intact).
- Norms for comparison must be built from applicant data gathered under the **same instructions**.

## Design mitigations

- **Forced-choice / ideal-point** measures and **computer adaptive** tests (NCAPS, TAPAS) resist faking (NCAPS avoids ipsativity via same-trait pairs; TAPAS uses MDPP IRT).
- **Conditional reasoning tests** hide their purpose but are easily faked once known.
- **Subtle items** resist faking better than obvious items; idiosyncratic-response-pattern scales (Kuncel & Borneman 2007) are a promising detection strategy.
- **Coaching** can defeat traditional social-desirability detection (Hurtz & Alliger 2002).

Newer process models of faking (McFarland & Ryan; Goffin & Boyd 2009) incorporate attitudes toward faking, perceived control, norms, and contextual moderators. The authors are **leery of more lab studies with students** given poor generalization to real settings.

## The lab-vs-applicant distinction

[[ones-et-al-2007-personality-assessment-support|Ones et al. (2007)]] make the same generalization point structural: see [[Directed Faking vs Applicant Response Distortion]]. Their conclusion — that the impact of response distortion among real applicants is **overrated**, because criterion-related *and* construct validity hold up in applicant samples, and that score corrections give "minimal effects" — converges with the findings above.
