# Motivated Reasoning

The cognitive process by which people arrive at conclusions they are motivated to reach, often unconsciously using reasoning to justify desired outcomes rather than follow evidence objectively.

## Definition

Motivated reasoning occurs when:
- We have directional goals (desired conclusions)
- We deploy selective reasoning to justify those goals
- We may be unaware that our reasoning is biased toward the desired conclusion
- The bias emerges from normal human cognition, not deliberate deception

## Evidence

**Bastardi, Uhlmann, & Ross (2011); Ditto & Lopez (1992); Lord, Ross, & Lepper (1979); Pyszczynski & Greenberg (1987):**

We are more likely to:
- Accept evidence uncritically when it confirms our hypothesis
- Scrutinize heavily when it disconfirms
- Use confirmation bias to interpret ambiguous evidence as supporting our view

## In Research Context

[[nosek-2012-scientific-utopia-ii|Nosek et al. 2012 - Scientific Utopia II]] argues that motivated reasoning is particularly influential in research when:

1. **The situation is complex**: Many reasonable research design choices exist
2. **Available information is ambiguous**: Multiple interpretations are defensible
3. **Legitimate reasons can be generated for multiple actions**: We can justify our preferred choice on technical grounds

### Manifestations in Research Practice

When researchers are motivated to publish (especially novel, positive findings), motivated reasoning leads to:

- **Accepting questionable practices as defensible**: "My analysis strategy is legitimate" (when many other choices could have been made)
- **Selective use of flexibility**: "I chose these exclusion criteria based on methodological principles" (conveniently, they produce publishable results)
- **Post-hoc justification**: Reconstructing research history to match findings (see [[HARKing]])
- **Forgetting details**: After months, remembering the gist but not whether we truly hypothesized the moderator, justifying selective memory as recollection

### The Difference from Dishonesty

Motivated reasoning is **ordinary, unconscious product of human cognition**. It doesn't require that researchers:
- Know they're doing it
- Intend to bias results
- Are uniquely flawed or unethical

Instead, it's what normal people do under conditions of:
- Directional motivation
- Ambiguous information
- Legitimate flexibility in decision-making

## Why It Matters

Motivated reasoning helps explain how [[Publication Bias]] and [[P-hacking|questionable research practices]] become systematized despite researchers' accuracy motives and ethical intentions. It bridges the gap between:

- What scientists **intend** (do good science)
- What **incentives encourage** (publish positive findings)
- What **actually happens** (subtle biases across many decision points)

## Solutions

Rather than assuming researchers will overcome motivated reasoning through willpower or education, [[nosek-2012-scientific-utopia-ii|Nosek et al. 2012 - Scientific Utopia II]] proposes structural solutions:

1. **Reduce flexibility**: Preregister hypotheses and analysis plans to limit motivated choice-points
2. **Increase transparency**: Open workflow makes motivated reasoning visible; knowing someone could look reduces likelihood of engaging in it
3. **Align incentives**: Reward accurate practices (replication, negative results) as much as novel positive findings
