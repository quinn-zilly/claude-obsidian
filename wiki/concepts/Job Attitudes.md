# Job Attitudes

**Definition (Judge & Kammeyer-Mueller 2012):** Evaluations of one's job that express one's feelings toward, beliefs about, and attachment to one's job.

Encompasses both cognitive (beliefs, contentment) and affective (feelings) components. These components need not be in exact correspondence — affective and cognitive attitudes can diverge and predict different behaviors.

> [!key-insight] Core Insight
> Job attitudes have both **traitlike** (stable, between-person) and **statelike** (fluctuating, within-person) properties. ESM research shows 1/3–1/2 of variance in job satisfaction is within-person — one-shot designs treat this as error, but it is substantively meaningful.

## Structure

Job attitudes are hierarchically organized:
- **Global job attitude** (most general — composite of satisfaction + commitment)
- **Major attitudes**: overall job satisfaction, organizational commitment
- **Facets**: work, pay, supervision, coworkers, promotion; specific dimensions of commitment

Can be treated as *latent* (top-down: general attitude causes facet correlations) or *manifest* (bottom-up: facets compose the overall). Both framings are valid depending on research goals.

## Distinctions from Related Constructs

Job attitudes require an **explicit evaluative appraisal** linked to personal values. They differ from:
- **Perceptions/descriptions** (e.g., role clarity, most org. justice scales) — these are antecedents to attitudes
- **Intentions** (planned behavior) — shaped by opportunities and social norms beyond attitudes
- **Engagement/motivation** — reflects direction/quantity of energy, not evaluation

## Cognitive vs. Affective Bases

Historically, research oscillated between emphasizing cognition and affect. Neuropsychology shows the two are inseparable: affect requires cognitive input; cognition is colored by affect (Adolphs & Damasio 2001; Drevets & Raichle 1998).

Recent shift: **experience-sampling methodology** (ESM) allows studying ephemeral affective reactions as they occur — moving beyond one-shot cognitive appraisals.

## Outcomes

Job attitudes predict a broad composite of work behaviors (Harrison et al. 2006). Bandwidth-fidelity principle: use global attitudes to predict global behaviors, specific attitudes to predict specific behaviors.

Outcomes include: task performance, citizenship behavior (OCB), withdrawal/turnover, counterproductivity, creative performance, and unit-level organizational performance.

See [[Job Performance (Outcomes of Job Attitudes)]].

## Key Measures

- **JDI (Job Descriptive Index)** — Smith et al. (1969): 5 facets (work, supervision, coworkers, pay, promotion); heavily evaluative despite name
- **Faces scale** — Kunin (1955): affect-centric global satisfaction
- **Job in General scale** — Ironson et al. (1989): cognitive global measure
- **MSQ (Minnesota Satisfaction Questionnaire)** — broader facets including job security, social status
