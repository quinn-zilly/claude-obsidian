# Autonomous Work Groups

Nav: [[index]] | [[Sociotechnical Systems Theory]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]]

Teams that carry out a relatively **whole task**, whose members hold a range of skills, and that have **autonomy over when and how** work is done (Cummings, 1978). Also called semiautonomous work groups or self-managing teams. The team-level expression of [[Sociotechnical Systems Theory]] — together the largest (red) cluster in [[parker-morgeson-johns-2017-100-years-work-design|Parker et al. (2017)]].

## Origins

Grew from sociotechnical systems work: Trist & Bamforth's (1951) Longwall coal-mining studies showed that self-managing miner groups completing whole tasks alleviated the dysfunction of rigid, sequenced work. Most popular in Europe — especially Scandinavia, with its industrial-democracy tradition — then spread to the US and beyond. Interdependence is a defining team feature (Kiggundu, 1983).

## Evidence (Mixed)

- **Positive reviews:** Pasmore et al. (1982, 134 US experiments) — enhanced productivity, quality, satisfaction, lower cost (though technical-system change was rare). Further positive: Cohen & Bailey (1997); team empowerment (Kirkman & Rosen, 1999).
- **Inconsistencies:** Wall et al. (1986) found satisfaction benefits but not productivity. Attributed partly to neglect of levels-of-analysis issues (van Mierlo et al., 2005; Langfred, 2000).
- **Boundary conditions:** AWGs may help within-group but not across-group performance (Parker, 2014); depend on task interdependence fit (Wageman, 1995), operational uncertainty (Cordery et al., 2010), and organizational support (Morgeson et al., 2006). Implementation often fails (Parker & Wall, 1998).
- **Critical view:** Barker (1993) "tightening the iron cage" — members in autonomous teams exercised intense concertive control over each other's behavior.

## Significance

Highlighted group dynamics as central to work design, feeding 1990s team-effectiveness models (Campion, Medsker & Higgs, 1993) that added leadership, composition, processes, and context as drivers beyond task characteristics. Notably **under-represented in JAP** (which rarely publishes team/qualitative work), one reason the JAP map omits this cluster.
