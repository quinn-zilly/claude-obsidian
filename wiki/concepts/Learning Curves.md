# Learning Curves

## Core Phenomenon

As organizations accumulate experience producing a product or service, performance (typically unit cost or time) improves at a **decreasing rate**. This is variously called the learning curve, experience curve, progress curve, or "learning by doing."

First documented at the organizational level by **Wright (1936)**, who showed that labor required to produce an aircraft decreases as a decreasing function of cumulative aircraft produced.

## Progress Ratio

The learning curve is often characterized by a **progress ratio**: the proportional reduction in unit costs with each doubling of cumulative output.

> An 80% progress ratio means: each time cumulative output doubles, unit cost falls to 80% of its previous level.

## Where Learning Curves Appear

Found across domains:
- Manufacturing: aircraft, semiconductors, chemicals, hardware
- Services: hospitals (surgical outcomes), software development, biotech
- Knowledge-intensive work: design, professional services

The productivity gains are typically very large (Levitt et al., 2013).

## Key Complications

### Knowledge is Not Simply Cumulative

The **Lockheed L-1011 case** (Argote & Epple, 1990; Benkard, 2000): when Lockheed slowed production of the L-1011, costs rose back *above* the level achieved before the slowdown — and remained elevated. This showed that accumulated learning knowledge depreciates when not actively practiced. Knowledge acquired from experience is not permanent.

Similarly, Douglas Aircraft failed to achieve the expected 80% progress ratio on the DC-9, leading to the McDonnell-Douglas merger (1967).

### Learning Rate Varies

Organizations vary significantly in how fast they learn. This variation is explained by:
- **Dimensions of experience** — depth vs. breadth, direct vs. vicarious, recency
- **Organizational context** — structure, culture, turnover, technology
- **Type of knowledge** — tacit vs. explicit, how codifiable it is

### Level of Analysis Matters

Learning occurs simultaneously at individual, team, and organizational levels — each contributing independently. Reagans et al. (2005) showed that individual experience, team experience, and hospital experience all improved surgical team performance, with different relative weights.

## Strategic Uses

- **Operational planning** — production schedules, workforce assignments, delivery commitments
- **Competitive strategy** — firms can use learning curves to predict competitor costs, decide on market entry, set pricing (the "experience curve" strategy)
- **Monitoring** — detect when actual learning diverges from expected curve
