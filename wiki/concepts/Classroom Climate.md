# Classroom Climate

## Definition (CMU Eberly Principle 6)

The **social, emotional, and intellectual environment** of a course. Classroom climate interacts with students' current developmental level to shape learning outcomes.

Students are intellectual, social, *and* emotional beings — still developing across all three dimensions. Instructors cannot control developmental pace, but can shape climate dimensions:

- **Intellectual** — rigor, openness, depth of discussion
- **Social** — belonging, inclusion, peer dynamics
- **Emotional** — psychological safety, stereotype threat management
- **Physical** — space, accessibility, comfort

## Evidence Base

- **Negative climate** → impedes learning and performance
- **Positive climate** → energizes learning
- **Stereotype threat** (Steele & Aronson 1995): situational cues that activate group-based performance fears depress test performance for African American students — and the mechanism is cognitive load / working memory drain
- **Belonging** (Walton & Cohen 2007): students uncertain about social fit expend resources on belonging-uncertainty rather than learning; brief belonging interventions have durable effects on GPA

## Instructional Implications

- Foster belonging through transparent inclusive practices
- Reduce stereotype threat: affirm values, use growth mindset framing, diversify examples
- Create intellectual safety — students must feel errors are learning opportunities
- Monitor climate signals (participation patterns, office hour attendance, classroom body language)
