# Foa & Foa Resource Theory

A typology of **what gets exchanged** in social exchange (Foa & Foa 1974, 1980). Six resource classes arranged on a two-dimensional matrix; the structure explains *which exchange rules apply to which resources* and *which resources fit which relationships*.

## Six Resources

**Love, status, information, money, goods, services.**

## Two Dimensions

1. **Particularism** — does a resource's worth depend on *who* provides it?
   - Low (universal): **money** — value is constant regardless of source.
   - High (particularistic): **love** — value depends heavily on the source.
2. **Concreteness** — how tangible/specific is the resource?
   - Concrete: goods, services.
   - Symbolic: status, information — convey meaning beyond objective worth.

## Exchange Implications

- **Less particularistic + more concrete** → exchanged short-term, quid-pro-quo (e.g., money for a good).
- **More particularistic + symbolic** → exchanged open-endedly (e.g., love, status).

## Collapse Into Economic vs. Socioemotional

Organizational science usually collapses the six into two:

- **Economic outcomes** — address financial needs; tangible.
- **Socioemotional outcomes** — address social/esteem needs; symbolic and particularistic; signal that a person is valued and treated with dignity.

## Relevance to Justice

[[colquitt-et-al-2013-justice-millennium-decade-later|Colquitt et al. (2013)]] use this theory to classify **justice itself as a symbolic, particularistic resource** — which is why fair treatment is exchanged in the open-ended, diffuse manner of social exchange and reciprocated with particularistic resources like [[Organizational Citizenship Behavior|OCB]]. (Caveat: distributive justice depends partly on **money**, a concrete/universal resource — but the resulting *fairness* still carries socioemotional meaning.) The theory also helps resolve [[Social Exchange Theory|SET]]'s causal-order question: universal resources dominate casual associations; particularistic resources dominate close relationships.
