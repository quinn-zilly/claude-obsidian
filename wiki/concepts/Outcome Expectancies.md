# Outcome Expectancies

Nav: [[index]] | [[Social Cognitive Theory]] | [[Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)]]

From Bandura's (1977) **social learning theory**: beliefs about the probability that one's behavior will produce favorable or unfavorable outcomes.

## Distinction from Self-Efficacy

| | Self-Efficacy | Outcome Expectancies |
|--|--------------|----------------------|
| **Question** | "Can I do it?" | "Will doing it produce good outcomes?" |
| **Object** | Capability to execute behavior | Consequences of behavior |
| **Bandura's claim** | Necessary condition | Insufficient alone |

People act on efficacy beliefs *when* they believe behavior will be beneficial. But high outcome expectancies + low SE → no behavior change. Low SE negates the motivating potential of outcome expectancies (Barling & Abel 1983; Godding & Glasgow 1985; Williams & Watson 1985).

## Subtypes

- **External:** perceived rewards/punishers from supervisors, peers, org policy
- **Self-evaluative:** internal affective outcomes — self-satisfaction when performance meets goals; self-dissatisfaction when falling short → increased effort (Bandura & Cervone 1983)

SLT emphasizes both; most measurement focuses on external only. Future research implication: measure self-evaluative outcomes explicitly.

## Empirical Note (Frayne & Latham 1987)

Outcome expectancies showed **no significant change** after self-management training — ceiling effect: employees already believed attendance would produce good outcomes. This ruled out OE as the active mechanism; [[Self-Efficacy (Goal Setting)|perceived self-efficacy]] was the operative mediator.
