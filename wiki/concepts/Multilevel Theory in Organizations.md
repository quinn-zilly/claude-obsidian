# Multilevel Theory in Organizations

Nav: [[index]] | [[Multilevel Theory & Methods MOC]]

**Organizations are multilevel systems.** Micro phenomena (individual cognition, affect, behavior) are embedded in macro contexts (groups, organizations, environments), and macro phenomena often emerge from the interaction of lower-level elements. Multilevel ("meso" — House, Rousseau & Thomas-Hunt 1995) theory bridges the **micro–macro gap** that fragments organizational science into separate disciplines.

> [!key-insight]
> "The organization may be an integrated system, but organizational science is not." Neither a pure micro nor a pure macro stance is adequate: macro neglects *how* individual action gives rise to higher-level phenomena (risking anthropomorphism — "organizations do not behave; people do"); micro neglects contextual constraints on individual differences.

## The two directions of linkage

- **[[Cross-Level Models|Top-down]] (contextual):** higher-level units exert **direct** or **moderating** effects on lower levels. Every phenomenon is embedded in a higher-level context.
- **[[Emergence (Organizational)|Bottom-up]] (emergent):** lower-level properties combine — via interaction, exchange, amplification — into collective phenomena at higher levels.

Most rich models contain **both**.

## Building a multilevel theory: what, how, where, when, why (and why not)

Kozlowski & Klein organize theory-building around six questions:

| Question | Concern |
|---|---|
| **What** | Start from the **endogenous construct** (the phenomenon to explain), not the antecedents. Multilevel models are unnecessary only if the phenomenon is uninfluenced by higher units, doesn't reflect lower-unit action, or is unexplored — *"Proceed with caution!"* |
| **How** | Specify the **linking mechanism**: top-down (direct/moderating) and/or bottom-up ([[Composition vs Compilation|composition/compilation]]). |
| **Where** | Specify the **focal entities/units** (formal vs informal) and the **[[Bond Strength and Coupling|strength of ties]]** across levels. |
| **When** | Specify **temporal** assumptions: time as boundary/moderator, differential time scales across levels, [[Entrainment]]. |
| **Why / Why not** | Argue by logic, not assertion. Explaining *why not* (e.g., why members' perceptions might *not* converge) refines the model. |

## Model specification: align constructs, measures, design, analysis

The core discipline is **avoiding misalignment** among the level of a construct, its measurement, and its analytic representation. Misalignment produces aggregation bias, ecological correlation, and misspecification (see [[Levels of Analysis Fallacies]]). The remedy runs through [[Global, Shared, and Configural Unit Properties|precise construct typing]], theory-driven [[Aggregation Justification Indices|aggregation]], multi-unit [[Multilevel Research Design and Sampling|sampling]], and matched analysis (HLM, WABA, contextual regression).

> [!note] The overarching principle
> Where general principles conflict, **theory takes precedence.** No analytic technique establishes "the" level of a phenomenon atheoretically.
