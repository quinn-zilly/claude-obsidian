# Social Comparison

Nav: [[index]] | [[Sources of Self-Efficacy]]

Comparing oneself with others (Festinger 1954) as a source of information about one's abilities and a **[[Sources of Self-Efficacy|source of self-efficacy]]**. Observing *similar* others succeed raises observers' efficacy and motivation; observing similar others fail lowers it. Passing one's peers builds efficacy; inferior performance erodes it.

> [!note] Similarity is the moderator
> The efficacy impact of social comparison depends on perceived **similarity** to the comparison target — a competitive student may even gain efficacy from being outperformed.

## Link to the Dunning–Kruger account

> [!connection] Social comparison as a route out of miscalibration
> [[kruger-dunning-1999-unskilled-and-unaware|Kruger & Dunning (1999)]] show a *failure* of social comparison in the unskilled: because incompetent individuals cannot recognize competence in others, they **cannot use social comparison information** to correct inflated self-assessments (their Prediction 3). Social comparison only recalibrates those who have the metacognitive skill to read others' performances — which is why the top quartile revised downward after grading peers while the bottom quartile did not.

Source: [[schunk-usher-2012-social-cognitive-theory-motivation|Schunk & Usher (2012)]].
