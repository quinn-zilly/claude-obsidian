# Judgment of Learning

A judgment of learning (JOL) is a metacognitive assessment made by a learner about how well they have learned or will be able to recall a piece of information in the future. JOLs are a primary measure of metacognitive monitoring in learning research.

## Core Problem: JOLs Are Often Inaccurate

Learners' JOLs are systematically miscalibrated — particularly in the context of [[Desirable Difficulties]]. Because massed practice produces high current fluency (high retrieval strength), learners *feel* they have learned well, even though long-term retention is poor. Conversely, spaced practice and [[Testing Effect|testing]] produce lower fluency in the moment, leading learners to underestimate their learning — when in fact retention is stronger.

## Relationship to the [[Learning vs Performance Distinction]]

This miscalibration is central to the learning vs. performance distinction: current performance (fluency, ease of recall right now) is a poor proxy for long-term learning (storage strength). JOLs track performance, not learning.
