# Job Crafting

Nav: [[index]] | [[Work Design (Concept)]] | [[Job Characteristics Model]]

Employee-initiated, bottom-up customization of one's own work to fit personal needs, values, skills, and abilities (Wrzesniewski & Dutton 2001). A **major departure** from classic job design, where management designed jobs and imposed them top-down. The contemporary frontier of work-design research (Oldham & Fried 2016, §5.1).

## Original Typology (Wrzesniewski & Dutton 2001)

Three approaches:
- **Cognitive crafting** — changing how one perceives the job (its meaning/purpose).
- **Task crafting** — changing the content/scope of work tasks.
- **Relational crafting** — changing the quality and amount of interaction with others.

## JD-R Typology (Tims, Bakker & Derks 2012)

Four activity types tied to the [[Job Demands-Resources Model|JD-R framework]], each with a validated measure:
1. Increasing **social** job resources (maximizing feedback from the social context).
2. Increasing **structural** job resources (developing personal capabilities).
3. Increasing **challenging** job demands (taking on new projects).
4. Decreasing **hindering** job demands (organizing work to minimize stress).

## Individual vs. Collaborative

- **Individual crafting** — a single employee reshapes their own work.
- **Collaborative crafting** — informal group members jointly shape shared work to meet shared objectives (Leana, Appelbaum & Shevchuk 2009).

## Evidence: Mixed Outcomes

Effects on the crafter's own behavioral/attitudinal outcomes are **inconsistent**:
- Increasing structural resources and challenging demands → positively related to performance; increasing social resources often unrelated (Bakker et al. 2012).
- General crafting measures have shown *negative* or nonsignificant relations to job satisfaction and commitment in some studies (Leana et al. 2009).
- Work engagement mediates crafting → performance (Tims, Bakker, Derks & Van Rhenen 2013).

Oldham & Fried argue mixed results may stem from over-reliance on JD-R-derived activities; JCT-derived activities (e.g., contacting beneficiaries to boost significance) might produce stronger, more consistent effects.

## Antecedents

Proactive personality (Bakker et al. 2012), self-efficacy (Vough & Parker 2008), autonomy/discretion, interdependence and supportive supervision (for collaborative crafting; Leana et al. 2009), and supervisor trust (Clegg & Spencer 2007).

## Open Questions (Oldham & Fried 2016)

- Effects of crafting on **coworkers and the organization** (one person's crafting can raise others' workloads → conflict and burnout; Tims et al. 2015).
- Whether crafting actually alters formal organizational structure.
- Cross-cultural differences (collaborative crafting may be more common in collectivist cultures).
- Crafting under **new work arrangements** (remote work → more opportunity to craft, less supervisor visibility).
- Crafting as part of a **dynamic** model of person–job fit requiring *continuous* crafting (Berg, Wrzesniewski & Dutton 2010).

- [[Job Characteristics Model]] — crafting can boost the same characteristics (significance, feedback) JCT specifies, but employee-driven.
