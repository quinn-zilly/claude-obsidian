# Conflict of Interest in Science

A fundamental tension in academic science between **personal incentives** (career advancement through publication) and **institutional goals** (accurate knowledge accumulation).

## The Core Problem

Scientists have two often-contradictory motivations:

1. **Accuracy motive**: Learn and publish true things about nature
2. **Professional motive**: Succeed and thrive in academic career

Under current incentive structures, publishing itself is rewarded regardless of whether published findings are true. This creates a conflict where personal interests (publication) are decoupled from scientific interests (accuracy).

[[nosek-2012-scientific-utopia-ii|Nosek et al. (2012)]] frame this as an unavoidable fact of human psychology, not a question of dishonesty:

> "Openness is not needed because we are untrustworthy; it is needed because we are human."

## Manifestations

When publication incentives dominate, researchers are motivated to:

- Favor [[P-hacking|flexible analysis]] that produces publishable results
- Practice [[HARKing]]: report discoveries as if they were confirmatory hypotheses
- Selectively report positive findings ([[Publication Bias]])
- Avoid direct [[Replication]] (seen as unrewarding novelty-wise)
- Use motivated reasoning to justify decisions that advance careers

## Why Incentive Misalignment Matters

The consequences are not fraud, but systematic bias:

- **Short-term payoff**: Publishing is immediate, concrete, and tied to hiring/promotion
- **Long-term payoff**: Accuracy has long-term career benefit but is abstract and distal
- **Result**: When the two conflict, the temporal difference favors publication

For early-career scientists especially, the urgency of publication creates relative urgency for markers of achievement.

## Solutions

Addressing this conflict requires:

1. **Making accuracy competitive** with publication: elevation of the abstract, long-term goal to match concrete, short-term goal
2. **Restructuring incentives**: Reduce emphasis on publication count; reward replications, negative results, open practices
3. **Transparency**: [[Open Workflow|Open workflow]], [[Open Data|open data]], preregistration make practices visible and create accountability
4. **Distributed responsibility**: Individual choice (open practices) + systemic change (journal policies, evaluation criteria)

## Related Concepts

- [[Motivated Reasoning]]: The psychological mechanism by which conflict of interest influences decision-making
- [[File-Drawer Problem]]: A consequence of misaligned incentives (what doesn't get published)
- [[Replication Crisis]]: Systemic outcome of incentive misalignment
- [[Open Science]]: Structural response to conflict of interest
