# Entrainment

Nav: [[index]] | [[Multilevel Theory & Methods MOC]]

The **"when"** of multilevel theory. Time is rarely modeled, yet it shapes most organizational phenomena. Kozlowski & Klein identify three temporal considerations.

## 1. Time as boundary condition or moderator
The apparent **direction** of a cross-level process depends on the time point assumed. Organizational **culture** looks bottom-up (emergent) early in the life cycle or during upheaval, and top-down (constraining) once institutionalized. Neither is the "true" model — both are veridical. Theory must state its temporal reference points; unstated assumptions produce spurious debates about a phenomenon's "true" level.

## 2. Differential time scales across levels
Lower-level phenomena have **faster dynamics**; higher-level/emergent phenomena change **more slowly**. Consequences:

> [!key-insight]
> Top-down contextual effects manifest **quickly** → detectable with cross-sectional or short-term longitudinal designs. Bottom-up **emergent** effects unfold **slowly** → require long-term longitudinal / time-series designs. This asymmetry is one reason top-down models dominate the literature (e.g., TQM training transfers fast to individuals but takes far longer to emerge as organizational quality).

## 3. Entrainment
**Entrainment** = the rhythm, pacing, and synchronicity that link levels (Ancona & Chong 1997). Coupling **tightens during periods of entrainment** — driven by task cycles, workflows, budget cycles. Levels ordinarily **loosely coupled** become **tightly coupled** during synchronous, coordinated episodes, then decouple again.

Research designs for entrained phenomena must sample on the **theoretically specified time cycle** — capturing both entrained and non-entrained periods.

Connects to [[Bond Strength and Coupling]] (entrainment is time-varying coupling) and [[Emergence (Organizational)]] (why emergence needs longitudinal detection).
