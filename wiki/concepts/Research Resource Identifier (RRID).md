# Research Resource Identifier (RRID)

A persistent identifier for research reagents (antibodies, organisms, cell lines, tools) that remains stable even if companies merge or stock centers move. Cited alongside reagent name, catalog/stock number, and company name.

## The problem it solves

A single antibody catalog can now list 100,000+ products, with hundreds targeting the same protein. Citing only company + target is ambiguous and, surprisingly often, leads scientists to waste money optimizing the *wrong* reagent. RRIDs disambiguate — a **methods transparency** win.

## Adoption and impact

- Launched via meetings with editors of 25 major neuroscience journals, NIH officers, and nonprofits; began as a 3-month pilot.
- Now in its ninth year (as of the 2023 source); 1,000+ journals request RRIDs.
- By 2020, ~half of antibody references were trackable, up from **15% in the 1990s**.
- Side benefit: asking for RRIDs nudged researchers to double-check reagents, reducing antibody errors and problematic cell-line use — at no extra cost to journals.

> [!key-insight] Grassroots vs funded
> [[thibault-2023-open-science-2-0|Thibault et al. (2023)]] use RRID as their central case study of a **grassroots success that took ~a decade of volunteer persistence** — and argue such wins would be far more common if backed by standard funding mechanisms instead of depending on dedicated volunteers.
