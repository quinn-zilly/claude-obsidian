# Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET)

Subtheory of [[Self-Determination Theory]] (Deci & Ryan 1985). Specifies social and environmental factors that facilitate vs. undermine [[Intrinsic Motivation]].

## Core Logic

Intrinsic motivation is inherent; will flourish *if circumstances permit*. CET identifies what circumstances those are by focusing on two needs: [[Competence (SDT)]] and [[Autonomy (SDT)]].

## Two Functional Aspects of External Events

1. **Informational aspect** — provides competence-relevant feedback → affects perceived competence → affects intrinsic motivation
2. **Controlling aspect** — pressures behavior toward specific outcomes → shifts perceived locus of causality externally → undermines intrinsic motivation

## Key Propositions

- Social-contextual events conducive to feelings of competence → enhance intrinsic motivation
- Competence alone insufficient — must co-occur with sense of autonomy (internal perceived locus of causality)
- Positive feedback → ↑ intrinsic motivation (mediated by perceived competence; Vallerand & Reid 1984)
- Negative feedback → ↓ intrinsic motivation
- **All expected tangible rewards contingent on task performance reliably undermine intrinsic motivation** (Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999 comprehensive meta-analysis; settled question despite Eisenberger & Cameron 1996 contrary claims)
- Threats, deadlines, directives, pressured evaluations, imposed goals → ↓ intrinsic motivation
- Choice, acknowledgment of feelings, opportunities for self-direction → ↑ intrinsic motivation
- Relatedness plays distal supporting role

## Limits

CET principles only apply to activities that hold intrinsic interest. For uninteresting activities, see [[Organismic Integration Theory]] and [[Internalization (SDT)]].
