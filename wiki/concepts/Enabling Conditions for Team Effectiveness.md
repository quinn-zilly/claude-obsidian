# Enabling Conditions for Team Effectiveness

Nav: [[index]] | [[concepts/_index]]

Hackman's model (Hackman 1987, 2002; Hackman & Wageman 2005), operationalized by the [[Team Diagnostic Survey]]. **Unconventional**: it posits no direct input→output causal links. Teams are **semi-autonomous social systems** that evolve idiosyncratically; the five conditions, when present, *raise the likelihood* — but do not guarantee — of effectiveness.

## The Five Conditions

1. **Real team** — clear boundaries (members vs non-members), interdependence for a common purpose with collective responsibility, and at least moderate membership **stability**. (A "team in name only" fails here.)
2. **Compelling direction** — purpose that is **challenging** (energizes), **clear** (orients), and **consequential** (engages full talents). Best practice: specify **ends** insistently but leave **means** to the team (specifying both under-employs the team; neither risks anarchy; means-only prompts disengagement).
3. **Enabling structure** — three structural features: **task design** with high motivating potential (whole/meaningful work, autonomy, knowledge of results; Hackman & Oldham 1980); **team composition** (as small as possible, ample task + interpersonal skills, moderate diversity — neither too similar nor too different); **core norms of conduct** established early.
4. **Supportive organizational context** — reward system (rewards team excellence; individual rewards shouldn't disincentivize collaboration), education system (technical/educational assistance on request), information system (data + projections), plus material resources.
5. **Available, expert coaching** — task-focused coaching that helps effort, performance strategy, and knowledge/skill. Coaching **cannot compensate for a badly flawed design**; it helps teams minimize process losses and capture process gains. Delivered by leaders *and* peers.

## Why "Conditions," Not "Causes"

Because the conditions and their subcomponents legitimately vary independently, a team can score high on some and low on others — an **empirical diagnostic finding**, not a measurement flaw.
