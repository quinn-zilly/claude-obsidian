# Contest-Mobility vs Sponsored-Mobility

Turner's (1960) two ideal-type systems of upward mobility, used by [[ng-eby-sorensen-feldman-2005-career-success-meta-analysis|Ng et al. (2005)]] as the theoretical scaffold for classifying career-success predictors. Not mutually exclusive — an institution can lean one way without excluding the other.

## Contest mobility

Everyone competes openly and fairly; advancement goes to those who demonstrate the greatest accomplishments through their own ability and effort. A slow starter can still win by putting in time and energy; established elites can't predetermine winners. **Predictor family: [[Human Capital (Career Success)|human capital]]** (experience, education, skills) — because human capital is rewarded in an open labor market. (Cf. the career-as-tournament framing; see [[Tournament Theory (Compensation)]].)

## Sponsored mobility

Elites identify high-potential members early and provide sponsoring activities to help them win; early success begets sponsorship, and the non-sponsored are excluded. Individuals have less personal discretion over the outcome. Supported by internal-labor-market theory (Spilerman 1977). **Predictor families: [[Organizational Sponsorship|organizational sponsorship]] + sociodemographic status** — because sociodemographic characteristics (gender, race, marital status, age) are often used as the criteria for *allocating* sponsorship.

## Empirical upshot (Ng et al. 2005)

Human-capital + sociodemographic predictors → stronger with **objective** success (salary, promotion); organizational-sponsorship predictors → stronger with **subjective** success (career satisfaction). Both systems operate simultaneously in real careers.
