Making smart investments in training:
The devil is in the details
Kimberly A. Smith-Jentsch
Employee training and development is often one of an orga-
nization’s largest budget items. Too often, however, organiza-
tions make decisions about how to invest that money without a
systematic review of details that are critical in ensuring a
return on that investment. There is a science to employee
training and development. Informed by this science, by dec-
ades of experience implementing, customizing, and evaluat-
ing training programs, I offera concrete and practical roadmap
for making sound investments in training.
In the first major section, I offer questions that need to be
asked and answered in order to estimate the likelihood that a
commercial off-the-shelf product or service is likely to be as
beneficial in your organization as it has been elsewhere. In the
second section, I detail questions to be asked and answered in
order to determine why a training program (homegrown or
commercial off-the-self) that was once deemed successful in
your organization appears to have become less effective over
time,andwhatto do about it. In the final sectionof this article,
I offer proactive steps that can be taken to set a new training
program up for success and to protect the viability of an
existing training program over time.
WILL IT WORK FOR US?
INTER-ORGANIZATIONAL BENCHMARKING
Off-the-shelf training is usually less expensive than devel-
oping a homegrown training program from scratch. More-
over, there is typically an assumption that the training has
already been proven to be effective for clients before you.
Or has it? The first question you should ask a vendor when
considering the purchase of a training product or service is,
“What evidence exists to show that other organizations have
benefitted from this training in the past?” And, how speci-
fically did they benefit? What did the training change? Atti-
tudes? Knowledge?

follows a single group of employees from the start to the
end of training and sometimes beyond. It may or may not
involve the collection of any quantitative data. In fact,
evidence of training effectiveness derived from case studies

(e.g., on-the-job experience) operating outside of the train-

training effectiveness comes from studies in which employ-
ees are randomly assigned to receive different types of
training or to a control group and quantitative measures
between the two groups are statistically compared. In addi-
tion to the type of studies conducted one should ask about
the depth of the data collected.
Trainee reactions are the easiest and most frequently
collected evidence of training effectiveness. Typically, reac-
tions ask about trainees’ enjoyment of training, their per-
ceptions of the usefulness of training, and their confidence in
using trained knowledge and skills on-the-job. Positive reac-
tions to training have been linked to trainees’ level of
engagement and motivation to learn. However, they are
by no means guarantees that learning has occurred. Thus,
trainee reactions to a training program are only the first level
(and the weakest evidence) of training effectiveness. The
next level involves directly measuring learning. Learning
criteria demonstrate that trainees’ knowledge, attitudes,
or skills have improved due to a training program (either

Learning measures may involve surveys, written or psycho-
motor tests, and behavioral role plays or simulations. It is
important to note that evidence of learning reflects what
employees “can do” after training but not necessarily what
they “will do.” Employees are often not able or willing to
apply what they learn in training once back on their jobs.
Therefore, a third level of training evaluation involves the
assessment of on-the-job behavior. Measures of on-the-job
behavior should be fairly specific to the content of training to
be convincing. Again, pre-post comparisons may be made for
a single group of employees or comparisons between sepa-
rate groups of trained and untrained employees may have
been made. Ultimately, an organization is interested in the
final level of training evaluation: results. Results criteria
reveal whether training impacted desired outcomes (e.g.,
sales or productivity). It is possible that employees both
learn and apply knowledge or skills taught in training yet this
does not impact an organization’s bottom line. When this
occurs the organization has mis-identified their training
needs and provided unnecessary training. Therefore, it is
not enough to know that training has been impactful for
other organizations. One must compare or “benchmark”
their own organization relative to those for whom the train-
ing has been successful in the past. But what exactly should
be benchmarked? The following sections describe important
and often overlooked details. A list of specific questions to
ask is presented in Table 1.
Do Our Employees Have the Same Training
Needs?
When benchmarking your employees’ training needs against
the needs of those who have benefitted from a training
product or service in the past, two questions need to be
answered. First, how similar is the work performed by the
two groups of employees? Second, how does your employees’
current standing on these knowledge, skills, and attitudes
compare to the pre-training levels held by those who ben-
efitted from a product or service previously?
A job analysis is a systematic study of the tasks performed
on a particular job, the relative importance of those tasks,
and the knowledge, skills, and attitudes that are critical for
the performance of those tasks. The data from a job analysis
can be used to compare the training needs of the employees
in your organization who would receive a new product or
service with those who benefitted from that training in the
past. In this respect, it is important to benchmark both the
underlying knowledge, skills, and attitudes requirements
as well as the more superficial look and feel of the tasks
performed. The “face validity” of course materials, or the
appearance that they are directly relevant, from your trai-
nees’ perspective can have a substantial impact on their
motivation to engage and their ability to transfer what they
learn. Therefore, it is relevant to ask a vendor how much
customization of the training was done for or by their prior
clients. It is important to note that this may also involve
eliminating material that is irrelevant for a particular group
of trainees.
As an example, I have found that when implementing
the same team training program in different organizations,
trainees react far more positively when I tailor terms to

experience? Is there any reason to be concerned that the
training may be too easy or too difficult for your employees?

Benchmarking the details of training delivery should
begin with determining how prior organizations have mar-
keted the training internally. Internal marketing, the art of
making employees aware of and excited about new initia-
tives, can significantly impact trainees’ motivation to attend
or to actively engage in that training. Therefore, it is rele-
vant to ask a vendor how organizations that have successfully
employed their training in the past communicated its
purpose and value to prospective trainees. For instance,
employees may have been told that a course was added
to address deficiencies responsible for a recent incident or
failure (e.g., loss of an important client, sexual harassment
scandal)? Alternatively, they may have been told that the
training was needed to prepare for an upcoming change or
challenge (e.g., economy, merger, new product or equip-
ment. Or perhaps, the training was introduced simply to
keep up with their competitors who are using it.
It’s also important to know what trainees were told about
who advocated for the training. Was it a faction internal to
the organization (e.g., union, management, HR, external
government monitor), or perhaps an external regulatory
agency? If your organization plans to internally market a
training program in a manner that is different from the way it
was done by organizations for whom the training was pre-
viously successful, this alone could cause you to have dif-
ferent results.
Beyond the internal marketing of a training program or
service, it is important to benchmark details of the training
process itself. It is common for organizations to modify the
delivery of the training in ways that are more practical and
less costly for them. Unfortunately, they almost always do
this without data to suggest that the training will remain
effective after these modifications are made. Typically, the
first things to be cut or substantially reduced are opportu-
nities for active practice (e.g., role-plays, simulations). This
is because active practice adds significant time and labor
(e.g., role-players) and these costs become multiplied as
class size increases.
I found this to be the case for organizations that adopted
an assertiveness training course my colleagues and I devel-
oped for junior aircrew members. The instructor guide we
prepared for the course specifically stated that role-play was
an essential component. However, we learned that when the
training was implemented many instructors simply asked for
volunteers to role play while the rest of the class watched.
Observing and doing are not comparable activities when it
comes to learning. Research has repeatedly shown that
active practice producing verbal responses is critical for skill
acquisition. So, reducing opportunities for practice in train-
ing is highly likely to reduce the effectiveness of that train-
ing. And, notably, those who volunteer to practice in front of
the class are likely to be those who needed practice the
least.
Another common way organizations modify a training
course is to chop it up into multiple shorter sessions. This
is typically done to avoid taking employees off of their jobs
for an extended number of consecutive hours. However,
doing so may also disrupt learning as it breaks up the
intellectual flow and increases the likelihood that some
participants will skip a session or two. It is rare that vendors
have quantitative data that conclusively show how various
changes to their training will impact learning. If they
validation
have
data at all, it is most likely from a single study
where the training process was controlled. Therefore, when
considering the purchase of a training program, ask the
vendor for details regarding the manner in which the training
was delivered when it’s effectiveness was formally evalu-

In sum, when estimating the likelihood that an off-the-
shelf training product or service will be as effective for your
organization as it has been for others you must consider how
similarly you plan to implement the training. How will your
internal marketing efforts compare to those adopted by
previously satisfied customers? Is it likely that practical or
political issues within your organization will require you to
shorten, modify, or break up training in a different manner?
Does your organization have the resources to customize the
training to the degree it has been in the past?
WHY HAS OUR TRAINING STOPPED WORKING?
INTRA-ORGANIZATIONAL BENCHMARKING
Whether a training program was purchased from a vendor or
developed in house, it is fairly common for organizations to
stop measuring its effectiveness once it has been deemed to
be “effective.” However, continual monitoring using quan-
titative metrics allows downward trends to be noted quickly
(and agreed upon by multiple stakeholders). The sooner such
trends are identified the easier it will be to pinpoint changes
in process, instructors, timing, trainees, etc. that coincided
with a drop in effectiveness. In the absence of such data,
problems are likely to go on much longer without being
noticed. This makes it far more difficult to disentangle
the root cause since multiple factors are likely to have
changed since a training program’s inception. Ultimately,
organizations often eliminate an entire training course when
it only needed some updating; essentially throwing out the
baby with the bathwater.
The first thing to determine when a training program
appears to have become less effective is when exactly it
stopped being as effective. This question can only be
answered with confidence if successive cohorts of trainees
have been assessed on the knowledge, skills, and attitudes
targeted in training; and using the same metrics. Are trai-
nees coming out of training with lower scores than they did in
the past? Which metrics are lower? Trainee reactions? Learn-
ing? Transfer to the job? Results (e.g., sales or readiness for
promotion)?
Once the pivot point has become clear, the next step is to
do some intra-organizational benchmarking. What changed
in close proximity to the drop in training effectiveness? The
following sections describe some of the most common cul-
prits. A list of specific questions is presented in Table 2.

Employees’ needs and preparedness for training can also
be affected when an organization changes when or how
frequently they offer a course. Again, this can cause a
long-standing training program to become less effective
because at the time employees receive the training they
are more or less prepared for it. Similarly, the effectiveness
of one training course can be negatively impacted when
an organization adds, eliminates, reduces or delays giving
another related or complementary course. The addition of a
new (seemingly unique) course may eliminate the need for
an existing one, or may inadvertently present employees
with information or strategies that conflict with those pre-
sented in an existing one. Reducing, eliminating or delaying a
course that normally precedes another can leave employees
unprepared to learn from the downstream course. Such
ripple effects are often not considered or are under-
estimated. In fact, when responsibility for training is dis-
tributed rather than centralized, changes in one program
may not even be communicated to those responsible for
another program.
In sum, if a longstanding training program is believed to
have become less effective over time, it is important to
consider whether employees’ need for the training or their
preparedness for the training has changed. This often occurs
as a result of differences in the knowledge and skills pos-
sessed by new hires, or changes in the amount and type of
experience employees have at the time they participate
in the training. Finally, changes in other related courses
upstream in the training pipeline can lead to redundancies,
omissions, or conflicting guidance.

Training?
Often an abrupt change in training effectiveness will coin-
cide with a changing of the guard with respect to those who
deliver the training. This is particularly true when those who
deliver a training program in its inception pass the baton.
The initial instructors to deliver a training course tend to
have been involved in advocating for the training need and/
or developing the training content. Typically, this leads them
to have an enthusiasm for and commitment to maintaining
the integrity of the training process that is not shared by
those who deliver the same course at a later date. When
a new group of instructors take over there is always a
danger that they will try to “fix something that isn’t broken”
simply to make the course their own. Although it is possible
that training will be enhanced by such updates, it is also
possible the training will become less effective. Thus, if a

Changes in the physical and social training environment
may also render training less effective over time. For
instance, when training is delivered off-site employees typi-
cally have fewer work-related distractions competing for
their attention than they do when it is delivered on-site. This
can have a significant impact on engagement and ultimately
learning. Other variables such as class size typically impact
the opportunities that individual trainees have to participate
in class discussions as well as their willingness to ask ques-
tions. Changes in the past or future relationships (or lack of)
that exist between employees who attend training together
can also have a significant impact on the training dynamic.
For instance, training members of intact teams together can
facilitate goal setting and planning for transfer to the job. It
can also build cohesion and trust particularly if the content
of training is interpersonal in nature. Coworkers who attend
training together can serve as reminder cues and can hold
one another accountable for transfer as well. Similarly,
changes in the credibility (e.g., technical expertise) or
the organizational relationship between the individual(s)
who teaches a course (e.g., internal human resources,
external consultant, managers) and those who take the
course can affect trust and openness during training, which
may lower learning outcomes.
For instance, much has been written about the growing
gig economy, where workers engage in various forms of
contingent work arrangements for a defined period of time
without any long-term relationship with a particular busi-
ness. Contingent employees who perform the same jobs that
were previously performed by permanent employees may
respond differently to the training provided to them. Addi-
tionally, if an organization chooses to employ contingent
employees to provide training that was previously delivered
by permanent organizational members, this may affect
the dynamic between trainees and their trainers. As an
example of this, over the past decade NASA has substantially
increased their use of external contract employees to deliver
training that was once delivered by an internal government
employee.
Finally, it has become popular to deliver part or all of
courses that were once solely face-to-face using some sort of
distance learning technology. Although the cost savings
associated with making such changes are easy to estimate,
the costs in terms of training effectiveness are not. There-
fore, organizations often have little or no guidance in deter-
mining what training objectives can be appropriately met in
a distance learning environment. In an online setting,
employees often have the freedom to break up lessons into
pieces separated by enough time so as to interrupt the
intellectual flow. They may also engage with an online
training system in an environment that is full of distractions
(e.g., kids, pets). To the extent that interaction between
trainees and between trainees and a trainer are integral to
learning, moving training to an online platform has a strong
potential to reduce training effectiveness.

Has Our Transfer Environment Changed?
All other factors being equal, it is possible for a training
program to become less effective over time within the same
organization simply because the transfer environment no
longer motivates or enables employees to use what they
have learned once back on their jobs. In this case, the
training process itself may be just as effective at enhancing
what employees “can do,” yet not what they “will do” after
training. In this section, I highlight three aspects of the
transfer environment that are most likely to change over
time and to weaken the effects of training.
First, when it comes to job performance, you get what
you measure. Employees are motivated to demonstrate
behaviors that they are held accountable for and rewarded
for. When they perceive a direct link between the content of
training and the recognition and rewards they desire, apply-
ing what they learn in training becomes instrumental to their
success. In this way, an organization’s performance appraisal
and compensation policies partially determine whether
employees will transfer what they have learned in training
to their jobs. When changes are made to the criteria used to
evaluate and compensate employees this has the potential
to reduce the effectiveness of a particular training program.
For instance, making a performance appraisal more out-
come-focused weakens the perceived link between demon-
strating specific behaviors or strategies and receiving
desired rewards. Therefore, if a once successful training
program appears to have become less effective over time,
an organization should consider whether this may have
occurred because training-related competencies are no
longer being formally evaluated or rewarded.
Second, research has shown that the frequency and tim-
ing of opportunities employees have to practice their newly
learned skills on-the-job partially determines how effective
training will be. Knowledge and skills will often decay if they
are not practiced. Moreover, motivation to attempt transfer
is highest immediately following training. Changing when a
particular training course is given to employees in the con-
text of their professional development has the potential to
reduce, increase, delay, or accelerate the opportunities they
have to apply newly learned knowledge and skills on-the-
job. For example, my colleagues and I found that new
Navy officers benefitted more from classroom leadership
training if they had been given some limited shipboard
experience prior to that training than they did if the class-
room training came before any shipboard experience. The
officers reported that without some relevant on-the-job
experience it was more difficult to visualize the concepts
taught in the classroom training. In this way, the same
training program may become more or less effective for
consecutive cohorts of trainees if there is a change in the

Third, research has repeatedly demonstrated that super-
visory support for the use of trained knowledge and skills on-
the-job is a key determinant of transfer. In fact, employees
are highly sensitive to informal cues that suggest what they
learned in training is accepted and expected in their immedi-
ate work team. Supervisors cannot be effective at reinfor-
cing transfer if they themselves are not familiar with the
training their subordinates receive. If a previous cohort of
supervisors participated in or perhaps even helped to
develop a training program designed for their subordinates
and a later cohort of supervisors did not, this could weaken
the informal reinforcement of training transfer over time. In
this way, significant managerial turnover in an organization
could cause a once impactful training program to become
less effective over time for subsequent cohorts of employ-
ees, simply because transfer of training is no longer sup-
ported by immediate supervisors.
In sum, when a training program appears to have
become less effective for cohorts of trainees over time,
it is important to compare past and present performance
appraisal systems, supervisory staff and their commitment
to the training, and opportunities that trainees have to
apply what they have learned on-the-job. Such benchmark-
ing may uncover the root cause for a drop in training
effectiveness.

Summary
Billions of dollars each year are spent on employee training
and development. The present article was written to help
organizational decision-makers to be more systematic in
making and protecting those investments. I have made the
case that “the devil is in the details” and highlighted a
number of variables that are often overlooked when pur-
chasing, modifying, or discontinuing a training program.
Finally, I have offered a number of proactive measures that
can be taken to improve the return on investments organiza-
tions make in training products and
