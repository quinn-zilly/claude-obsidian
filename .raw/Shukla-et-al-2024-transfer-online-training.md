Training employees in new competencies is crucial for

ensuring organizational sustainability and success. Conse-

quently, organizations allocate significant resources to training

initiatives. As training constitutes a substantial investment, it

becomes imperative to identify and categorize factors that

enhance the transferability of training. This necessity becomes

particularly pronounced in the context of online training, which

is being increasingly adopted by organizations for various rea-

sons, including cost efficiency and flexibility. While the adop-

tion of online training is on the rise, research on factors that

enhance the effectiveness of such training remains limited.

This study aims to address this gap by introducing a taxonomy

of factors that influence the transfer of online training. These

factors are categorized into four broad themes: computer (digi-

tal)

literacy and attitudes,

learner characteristics,

training

design and planning, and the learning environment along with

organizational factors. Additionally, the study maps these fac-

tors to the intended outcomes of training (knowledge, skills,

and attitude) and the participants engaged in the training ses-

sions, providing a comprehensive overview of ongoing

research and identifying areas for future investigation. Draw-

ing insights from an evaluation of existing literature, the

research not only points toward potential research directions

but also offers practical implications for designing more effec-

tive online training sessions.

I N T RO DU CT I O N

Continuous upskilling has become imperative for companies to thrive in today's knowledge economy. Consequently,

organizations are dedicating significant resources to a diverse range of training activities (Baldwin et al., 2009;

Caporarello et al., 2019; Choi et al., 2021; Dolezalek, 2005). However, despite these investments, concerns persist

within organizations regarding the transferability of training to the workplace (Baldwin & Ford, 1988; Garavan

et al., 2019; Hutchins et al., 2010). The provision of training through novel mediums, such as online platforms, is

likely to intensify these concerns due to limited insights into the factors influencing transferability within this domain.

Even though the movement toward online training was expedited by technological advancements and the Covid-19

pandemic-related restrictions (Akdere et al., 2023; Coman et al., 2020; Dhawan, 2020; Mulder, 2021; Sobaih

et al., 2020), the benefits and efficiencies of online training over face-to-face methods still persist in the post-

pandemic era (Patino & Naffi, 2023). It is now essential for HRD professionals to identify and categorize the factors

affecting the transferability of online training, enabling the understanding, differentiation, and adoption of best

practices.

Effectively ensuring training transfer necessitates meticulous planning and strategizing (Machin, 2002; Yamnill &

McLean, 2001). This, in turn, calls for an understanding of the factors influencing training transfer. Despite a growing

emphasis on online training transfer, there remains a dearth of comprehensive studies that systematically synthesize

the existing literature and discern the factors that facilitate successful online training transfer. This study aims to

bridge this gap by proposing a taxonomy of factors based on a comprehensive synthesis of existing research. This

taxonomy would serve as a guide for future research, aiding researchers in identifying potential new factors based

on their relation to existing factors or their alignment with the broader categories identified in this study. It would

also provide an indispensable checklist for HRD practitioners seeking to enhance online training transfer in real-

world organizations.

By aligning the factors affecting training transfer with the purpose of training (knowledge, skills, and attitude)

and the profile of trainees (industry types), this paper assists HRD professionals in designing effective online training

programs tailored to the content of training and the intended audience.

The specific research questions addressed in this study are: RQ1: How can the existing literature on factors

influencing the transfer of online training be systematically categorized into a coherent framework? RQ2: How can

these factors be employed to provide a comprehensive overview of the current state of literature and to propose

directions for future research?

To address these questions, this study employs a systematic literature review (SLR). SLR is a recommended

method for summarizing and organizing existing literature in a specific area (Makarem & Wang, 2020). SLR is well-

suited for addressing targeted research questions that necessitate content synthesis and comparison across articles

(Snyder, 2019). Given that this article seeks to synthesize existing research in the evolving field of online training

transfer for specific goals (constructing a taxonomy of factors), SLR is a suitable methodology. The SLR encompassed

articles published between April 2001 and May 2021, a timeframe chosen based on the emergence of online training

phenomena in the early 2000s.

Initial keyword-based exploration uncovered pertinent articles alongside unrelated subjects such as technology

and material training transfers. To ensure accuracy and a systematic approach, PRISMA guidelines were adhered

to. These guidelines facilitate a methodical and rigorous assessment of the current literature (Page et al., 2021).

PRISMA is a widely used methodology for SLRs, and its application has demonstrated enhanced reporting quality

| T R A N S F E R O F T R A I N I N G A N D O N L I N E M O D E

Transfer of training refers to the degree to which the knowledge, skills, and attitudes acquired in a training program

are effectively applied to enhance job performance (Laker & Powell, 2011, p. 112). Improving job performance

necessitates the practical utilization of these KSAs (knowledge, skills, and attitudes)

in real-world scenarios

(Grossman & Salas, 2011; Schoeb et al., 2021). Therefore, the transfer of training assesses the extent to which

trainees can apply the acquired KSAs from training to their job roles.

Research on training transfer has substantially expanded in recent times. However, discussions persist

around defining, measuring, and contextualizing training transfer. A consensus definition and universally

accepted measurement scale for training transfer have not emerged (Schoeb et al., 2021). This conceptual and

operational uncertainty results in varied definitions and measurement tools employed by different researchers,

leading to limited generalizability across studies. The implications of the lack of consensus and measurement

tools are even more pronounced within digital media due to the diverse nature of online environments. For

instance, digital devices differ in terms of size, processing power, and features, potentially impacting the trans-

ferability of digitally received training.

The rapid surge in online training due to technological advancements and Covid-19-driven restrictions (Coman

et al., 2020; Dhawan, 2020; Sobaih et al., 2020) has given rise to diverse conceptual understandings of online train-

ing. Beyond conceptual and operational challenges, debates also center around the impact of different media on

training effectiveness. While certain studies point out transfer-related distinctions between online and offline media

(Kimball & Holyoak, 2000), meta-analyses have struggled to confirm these findings. For instance, Kaplan et al. (2021)

found no significant differences in transfer effectiveness between extended-reality (XR) training and traditional

mediums, except for physical tasks. This suggests that simulations in extended reality might be more effective for

physical skill training.

The perceived influence of the training's goal on its effectiveness has also been explored. Blume et al. (2010)

found that transfer magnitude is higher for open skills (focused on understanding and internalization) compared to

closed skills (emphasizing specific behaviors). While research has not explicitly linked online training to better acquisi-

tion of closed skills (Kaplan et al., 2021), this could be due to inconsistencies in reporting training transfer studies

(Blume et al., 2010) and the lack of control for contextual variables (Kaplan et al., 2021), warranting further sophisti-

cated meta-analysis.

A related understudied area is the impact of the purpose of training, such as the transfer of knowledge, skills,

and attitude, on transfer effectiveness. The author(s) failed to identify studies in this domain. The lack of granular

reporting on the content of training, whose transfer was studied, has been identified by existing SLRs (Schoeb et al.

(2021). Besides the lack of granularity, the SLR by Schoeb et al. (2021) also highlighted the overall absence of

reporting on parameters in studies exploring the transfer of training. However, some studies have explored context-

specific factors promoting transferability. For leadership training, trainer characteristics (Richter & Kauffeld, 2020)

and evidence-based pedagogy (Seeg et al., 2022) have proven effective. Regarding the transfer of technical training,

content and supervisor support have been found to play significant roles in training transfer (Richter &

Kauffeld, 2020). Concerning entrepreneurship training, demographic factors and prior experience with such training

are important for training transfer (Minja et al., 2022).

The differential impact of industry type on the transfer of training is also being investigated. Simosi (2012)

examined the influence of self-efficacy on training transfer in the service industry, and attitudes on training transfer

on transferability in the oil and gas industry. Yaqub et al. (2021) and Kodwani (2017) identified the positive impact of

self-efficacy and organizational factors, respectively, on training transfer in public sector companies. Scholars are also

identifying new factors that affect the transfer of training, such as team-based learning (Hart et al., 2019), culture

(Brion, 2020; Gil et al., 2021), trainer conduct (Creon & Schermuly, 2022), job resources and demands (Salamon

et al., 2022), learner readiness (Ghosh, 2022), implementation intention (Greenan, 2023), and the evaluation of train-

ing (Arabi & Garza, 2023).

Studies have attempted to identify the relative contribution of specific environmental conditions to training

transfer (Hughes et al., 2020) and have employed more sophisticated statistical techniques. Studies are increasingly

using sequential mediation techniques (Andoh et al., 2023; Blume et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2019), leading to a better

understanding of the mechanisms. These novel factors generate substantial discussion and offer potential avenues

for future research in the field of training transfer. Longitudinal studies are also being utilized, enhancing the validity

of suggested causal relationships (Deckers et al., 2022).

In conclusion, Cheng and Hampson's (2008) clarion call for research on training transfer still remains valid.

Although some research has been conducted to address the concerns they voiced, substantial issues in clarifying and

measuring the construct and addressing questions regarding the impact of context, such as the medium and content

of training, still persist. This study focuses on gaining an understanding of training in the online medium by identify-

ing and classifying the factors that lead to transfer effectiveness in online training. This would enable a comparison

of differences and similarities with research advancements in the more established domain of offline/face-to-face

training (Clark, 2020; Fauth & González-Martínez, 2021a, 2021b; Galoyan et al., 2021; Martins et al., 2019;

Stefaniak, 2020). The study also classifies earlier studies in terms of the purpose of training (knowledge, skills, and

attitude) and the industry/domain of trainees. This classification can assist future researchers in conceptually linking

factors to training content and participant profiles, driving specific and high-impact empirical research in the field of

online training. HRD professionals can also use this classification to develop more customized training programs.

Systematic literature review

Literature reviews serve as essential tools for synthesizing existing knowledge within a domain and identifying gaps

(Xiao & Watson, 2019). They play a crucial role in the advancement of research (Borrego et al., 2014) by providing

an avenue to amalgamate and reflect upon prior research, thus establishing a robust foundation for knowledge pro-

gression (Paré et al., 2015). In instances where a literature review lacks rigor and comprehensiveness, doubts may

arise regarding the validity of its conclusions. As a result, scholars advocate for a systematic and thorough approach

in the literature review process to address concerns pertaining to reliability, validity, and replicability. To this end, this

study employs a systematic literature review (SLR) to synthesize existing knowledge concerning the transfer of

online training.

3.1.1

| Research design

Conducting SLRs necessitates the adoption of standard frameworks to guide the analysis. An initial examination of

databases using specific search terms revealed a substantial body of literature covering diverse subject matters shar-

ing common keywords. Consequently, the need for a methodical approach, guided by clear exclusion and inclusion

criteria, was emphasized. This study employs the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and

3.1.2

|

Search strategy

The search encompassed articles from three databases: Google Scholar, Web of Science, and EBSCO. The search

employed the following keywords: Transfer of e-learning, Transfer of online learning, transfer of computer-based

learning, transfer of mobile learning, and transfer of online education. The search scope was limited to the titles of

the articles. The keywords were deliberately not enclosed in quotation marks to ensure the inclusion of the maxi-
mum possible number of papers. Consequently, if any portion of a keyword such as “online” or “mobile” appeared in

the title, the paper was deemed suitable for further evaluation. The selection of all three databases utilized in the

study was driven by their substantial article repositories, and their selection was motivated by their size and accessi-

bility to the authors (refer to Table 1).

Inclusion and exclusion criteria

The inclusion and exclusion criteria were developed initially, after which the authors engaged in a two-stage process.

In the first stage, the articles were sorted based on abstract reading, followed by further scrutiny of the full text in

the second stage. All authors participated in discussions and finalized the articles, first after assessing the abstracts

and then after reviewing the full text, reaching a consensus on the articles to be included and excluded (refer to

Figure 1). Articles were included in the systematic literature review (SLR) if they met the following criteria: (a) The

article focused on factors determining the transfer of training, and (b) The study was conducted in an online medium,

which encompassed e-learning, online learning, computer-based learning, mobile learning, and online education. The

SLR systematically excluded studies that were: (a) Not relevant to the transfer of training or learning, (b) Not con-

ducted in an online medium, (c) Did not primarily focus on the transfer of training despite containing the required

4.1

| Developing a taxonomy of factors enabling transfer of online training

4.1.1

|

Identification of factors

A total of 33 articles were shortlisted for the review. Among these articles, 18 reported the results of quantitative

studies, and 6 described qualitative studies. Eight articles presented literature reviews, while one article detailed a

mixed-method study that employed both qualitative and quantitative techniques. For the quantitative studies, fac-

tors were identified by analyzing hypotheses and results. Factors hypothesized to facilitate the transfer of online

training were considered as such if they were empirically supported by the hypotheses.

Various factors were identified from quantitative studies, including computer anxiety (Park & Wentling, 2007),

organizational support, and supervisor support (Martins et al., 2019). In the case of the mixed-method study, factors

were similarly identified from the text of the study. In qualitative studies, factors were identified based on the

author(s)' use of qualitative techniques like interviews, focus groups, or anthropological studies. Attention was partic-

ularly given to sections discussing study results and findings. Factors identified from qualitative studies encompassed

the design of training (Fauth & González-Martínez, 2021b) and teaching pedagogy (Galoyan et al., 2021).

Literature reviews on related topics provided another valuable source for identifying factors affecting the trans-

fer of online training. Close reading of these reviews led to the identification of additional factors, such as technology

readiness, infrastructure, organizational culture, instructional decisions, peer support, training content, communica-

tion between learners and trainers, opportunities for use, content delivery style, perceived utility, computer literacy

barriers, networking objectives, and the need for networking (Clark, 2020; David, 2013; Fauth & González-

Martínez, 2021a; Huffaker & Calvert, 2003; Kohn et al., 2010; Lim, 2012; Stefaniak, 2020).

4.1.2

| Categorization of taxonomy of factors (taxonomy)

Several studies have developed taxonomies of factors that influence the transfer of training in face-to-face mediums

(Baldwin & Ford, 1988; Burke & Hutchins, 2008; Lim & Morris, 2006; Park & Kim, 2016). However, as far as our

knowledge goes, similar taxonomies have not yet been established for online training. The creation of a taxonomy

for factors affecting online training would facilitate a comparison between factors influencing online and offline

training. This comparison could also help pinpoint future research directions in online training and empower practi-

tioners to leverage the well-developed literature on the transfer of offline training when designing training programs.

Consequently, it is logical to investigate the factors identified in the transfer of online training and derive a taxonomy

based on this study to guide future research (as depicted in Tables 2 and 3).

4.2

| Description of the categories

4.2.1

| Computer (digital) literacy and attitudes

Several articles (e.g., Lim, 2012; Pham & Huynh, 2018; Ronen, 2008) have explored the positive influence of learner

competencies in effectively using digital devices and exhibiting favorable attitudes toward digital mediums in facili-

tating the transfer of online training. Building on this insight, the factor of digital literacy and attitudes was classified

as contributing to the successful transfer of online training. Digital literacy encompasses the knowledge and skills

required for the efficient operation of digital devices, while digital attitudes encompass individuals' behaviors and

attitudes toward digital devices and their usage, which can be either positive or negative.

Variables falling within this category include knowledge of technological advancements in the realm of digital

devices (Clark, 2020; Jin et al., 2021; Kohn et al., 2010), strong computer confidence, perceived usefulness of

computers, affinity for computer usage, and computer anxiety levels (Park et al., 2010; Park &

Wentling, 2007). Similarly, this category encompasses attitudes specific to the digital realm, such as computer

self-efficacy and low computer anxiety (Pham & Huynh, 2018; Ronen, 2008). The factors observed within this

category are distinctive to the transfer of online training and serve as differentiating factors from traditional

face-to-face training. The importance of positive attitudes toward computing devices for successful online

training suggests the need to develop confidence and self-efficacy of learners in a digital environment. One
way to achieve the same is by improving usability of the human–computer interface, especially for older

learners (Chou & Hsiao, 2007).

4.2.2

|

Learner characteristics

Learner characteristics have been recognized as relevant in the context of face-to-face training (Sookhai &

Budworth, 2010), and the findings from this systematic review of studies (e.g., Huffaker & Calvert, 2003; Ramli

et al., 2010; Ronen, 2008) further suggest that learner-specific attributes play a crucial role in determining the effec-

tiveness of online training transfer. Thus, learner characteristics have been identified as a factor influencing the

transfer of online training. Learner characteristics refer to stable behavioral patterns and general attitudes toward

the learning process and pedagogy, which impact overall learning outcomes.

Various characteristics of learners contribute to the effective transfer of training, including self-regulation (Barak

et al., 2016; Fauth & González-Martínez, 2021a, 2021b), self-efficacy (Gunawardena et al., 2010; Park et al., 2010;

Trombley, 2004), motivation for transfer (Chee et al., 2018; Zia-ur-Rehman & Shahzadi, 2014), motivation to learn

(Fauth & González-Martínez, 2021a, 2021b; Ramli et al., 2010), internal values (Zia-ur-Rehman & Shahzadi, 2014),

and active engagement in training (Granger & Levine, 2010). Additional factors such as trainee's reactions (Martins

et al., 2019), learner control (Granger & Levine, 2010), enjoyment (Peters et al., 2012), learner satisfaction with train-

ing (Pineda-Herrero et al., 2011), cognitive skills (Barak et al., 2016), and perceived usefulness of online training (All

et al., 2016; Fauth & González-Martínez, 2021a, 2021b; Kohn et al., 2010; Trombley, 2004) also hold significance for

the effective transfer of online training.

|

The review of the selected articles (e.g., Chee et al., 2018; Fauth & González-Martínez, 2021a, 2021b; Lim, 2012)

suggests that the proper development and delivery of instructional material contribute to the effective transfer of

online training. As a result, instructional design, defined as the process of systematically creating, developing, and
delivering instructional materials and experiences—both digital and face-to-face—was identified as a factor

4.2.4

Learning environment and organizational support

Organizational climate has already been found relevant in the transfer of face-to-face training (Cromwell &

Kolb, 2004; Egan et al., 2004; Martin, 2010; Rouiller & Goldstein, 1993; Xiao, 1996), and this section aims to

assess its relevance in the context of online training transfer. The physical, social, and cultural settings also

impact the effectiveness of the transfer of online training (as seen in Lim, 2012; Van Doorn & Van

Doorn, 2014; Zia-ur-Rehman & Shahzadi, 2014). These physical, social, and cultural settings, including teacher/

trainer characteristics and organizational factors, define the learning environment and organizational factors.

The learning environment and organizational factors are significant categories of variables that influence the

transfer of online training.

Several variables have been identified under this category, including technology (Lim, 2012; Van Doorn & Van

Doorn, 2014),

infrastructure (Kohn et al., 2010), organizational culture (Fraj & Mbarek, 2017), peer support

(David, 2013; Martins et al., 2019), interaction opportunities (Clark, 2020; Everett, 2010; Hansen, 2008), organiza-

tional support (Herrington et al., 2009; Joo et al., 2011; Ronen, 2008), learning environment (Zia-ur-Rehman &

Shahzadi, 2014), opportunity to use (Fauth & González-Martínez, 2021a, 2021b), organizational climate

(Hennebury, 2007), organizational policies (Merkley & Nichols, 2007), and organizational readiness (Lim, 2012).

Exploring the current state of research on contextual parameters

The study's objectives involve a comprehensive exploration of the existing literature landscape and the identification

of potential research directions (RQ2). Existing literature has identified the absence of reporting on parameters as a

limitation in the current literature on training transfer. Hence, this study explores whether existing literature has

(1) captured the contextual parameters of training and (2) measured the differential impact of any factor in differing

contexts. Two contextual factors were identified as most relevant by the authors: purpose of training and purpose of

training. Many studies did not mention any or both of the parameters. No study measured the impact of context on

the potency of a factor. The details are mentioned in Table 3 and discussed in subsequent sections. This approach

has the potential to provide further layers of insight that can aid in refining research focus, addressing current

research gaps, and uncovering possible paths for future investigation.

to consider the training's intended objectives. In the conducted literature review, five studies focused on skill

4.3.2

| Aligning identified factors to profile of participants

The impact of various factors on training transfer is contingent upon the institutional setting, as evidenced by differ-

ential effects observed among participants from industry and educational institutions (Argote et al., 1990; Cree &

Macaulay, 2000). Therefore, this study identifies the importance of considering the institutional context in analyzing

the factors affecting online training. Furthermore, this research aims to discern the type of participants (i.e., from

industry or educational institutes) involved in online training and examines the range of factors studied across the

articles included in the literature review.

The literature review incorporates a total of 33 studies, among which 11 were conducted in industry settings,

providing training to employees in various sectors such as oil and gas, financial services, electronics, technology, and

public banking. Some studies did not specify a particular industry as they involved participants from diverse sectors.

Additionally, 13 studies were conducted in the education sector, focusing on training programs within institutes or

universities, where the participants were students. The remaining eight studies were literature reviews that

encompassed a variety of different research.

A closer examination of the types of industries involved in the studies (as shown in Table 3) reveals that studies

concentrating on knowledge acquisition predominantly took place in educational institutes and universities, with stu-

dents as the participants. On the other hand, studies focusing on skill development were conducted with employees

from diverse industries, such as oil and gas, construction, and banking.

Another noteworthy observation is that the first category, “computer liking and attitude,” received more atten-

tion in industry settings, whereas the other three categories were studied in both industry and academic institutes.

Despite the findings presented in Table 3, there remains ample room for further research on this subject in various

other industries and regions around the world.

characteristics; online learning design and planning; and learning environment and organizational support. This com-

prehensive categorization can aid HRD professionals in developing checklists to better design and execute online

Comparing this taxonomy with earlier ones based on the transfer of training in offline mediums revealed the

The taxonomy of factors developed in this study includes many elements, such as self-efficacy, peer support,

learning strategy, and training usefulness, that are common to both online and offline training transfer. Given these

serve as a foundation for further qualitative and quantitative studies on the subject, contributing to the expansion of

the current knowledge base.

In addition to identifying commonalities in the transfer of online and offline training, the study underscores the
dearth of context-specific studies in this domain. For more than half of the studies, the specific training purpose—
knowledge transfer, skill acquisition, attitude change, or behavioral transformation—was not identified. The study

correlated the training purpose with the identified factors wherever feasible. This correlation can facilitate future

researchers in exploring the nuanced relationship between training purpose and specific factors. Understanding

these micro-relationships would aid institutions in better planning their training sessions by pinpointing and prioritiz-

ing the most crucial factors. The study also linked specific factors to participant types, as explored in the existing lit-

erature. This linkage can help identify specific research gaps for future investigations and potentially determine the

institution-specific significance of factors.

Limitations and future research directions

The scope of the literature review was confined to three databases: Google Scholar, Web of Science, and EBSCO.

Considering that all three databases are major repositories of scientific literature, the author(s) are confident that the

literature search effectively captured a wide array of articles. However, it is important to acknowledge the possibility

of overlooking relevant articles from other databases. Similarly, even though the author(s) meticulously selected key-

words, the potential for missing pertinent keywords cannot be completely dismissed. Another limitation was the

restriction of your keyword search to only the titles of articles, potentially leading to the omission of articles that lack

keywords in their titles.

The study revealed that existing literature has employed various definitions and operationalizations of the cen-
tral construct—transfer of training. However, very few studies have delved into examining the equivalence among

different scales utilized. Consequently, establishing the convergent validity of these diverse measures presents an

area ripe for future research. Subsequent investigations could also explore avenues for refining the existing scales

(Schoeb et al., 2021).

Drawing on insights from the literature review, other potential areas for research have also come to light. One

notable gap pertains to the dearth of research assessing the ease of transferring various types of training content.

This absence is somewhat surprising since the ease of transferability should ideally wield considerable influence over

the determination of training content. The study suggests employing the KSA framework to broadly classify training

| C O N CL U S I O N

Online provision of training has expanded rapidly due to technological advancements and the impact of the Covid-

19 pandemic. However, there has been no comprehensive overview of the factors that promote the transfer of train-

ing in the online medium. The study aimed to overcome the same by identifying and classifying the factors promot-

ing the transfer of online training (RQ1) and using the identified factors to evaluate the existing literature and

suggest new research directions (RQ2). To achieve the objectives, a SLR was performed.

Based on the SLR, 33 relevant articles were identified and analyzed. The analysis of the articles led to the identi-

fication of 42 factors, which were classified into four categories: computer literacy and attitudes; learner characteris-

tics;

learning environment and organizational support; and online learning design and planning. One notable

contribution of the study is the identification of computer literacy and attitudes as a new category, specifically rele-

vant to online training.

An analysis of current literature on these factors suggests that few factors such as usefulness of training; learn-

ing strategy and design and organizational support have been explored in multiple studies. On the other hand, rela-

tively few studies have explored the factors categorized under computer literacy and attitudes. As this category is

unique to online training, there is scope for future research in this area. The study also explored whether existing lit-

erature has examined the impact of contextual parameters, such as the purpose of training and participant profile, on

the efficacy of the factors. The findings reveal that reporting on contextual parameters needs strengthening with

many studies providing no information on either or both of the parameters. No study examined the possible moder-

ating impact of the contextual parameters. Future studies are advised to report the contextual parameters and evalu-

ate their impact on the efficacy of factors for gaining an improved understanding of the phenomenon.

6.1

| Practical implications

The advent of the information technology revolution has made online training an attractive option due to its exten-

sive reach, cost-effectiveness, and convenience (Szymkowiak et al., 2021). Moreover, the ongoing Covid-19 pan-

demic has accelerated the adoption of online training. Considering that training constitutes a substantial investment

for organizations, its effectiveness becomes a crucial concern (Almahasees et al., 2021; Paudel, 2021). Researchers

have raised questions about the effectiveness of training transfer for online versus traditional mediums (Liu

et al., 2019). The challenge of training transfer has been a longstanding issue, and it becomes even more critical for

Human Resource Development (HRD) professionals as learning transitions to the online mode, where significant

efforts, time, and resources could potentially be at stake (Hutchins et al., 2010).

This literature review delves into the factors that can influence the transfer of online training, encompassing

both specific and analogous elements found in face-to-face environments. Noteworthy specific factors identified for
