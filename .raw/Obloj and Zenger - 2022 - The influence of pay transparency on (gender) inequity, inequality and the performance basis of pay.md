The influence of pay transparency on (gender)
inequity, inequality and the performance basis
of pay
Tomasz Obloj 1 ✉ and Todd Zenger 2
Recent decades have witnessed a growing focus on two distinct income patterns: persistent pay inequity, particularly a gender
pay gap, and growing pay inequality. Pay transparency is widely advanced as a remedy for both. Yet we know little about the
systemic influence of this policy on the evolution of pay practices within organizations. To address this void, we assemble a
dataset combining detailed performance, demographic and salary data for approximately 100,000 US academics between 1997
and 2017. We then exploit staggered shocks to wage transparency to explore how this change reshapes pay practices. We find
evidence that pay transparency causes significant increases in both the equity and equality of pay, and significant and sizeable
reductions in the link between pay and individually measured performance.

Study description This study examines longitudinal quantitative data involving the performance and pay of nearly all faculty from PhD granting institutions
in eight US states before and after a shock to the transparency of pay.
Research sample The sample includes essentially all academics from 139 public PhD granting institutions in eight US states (California, New York, Florida,
Texas, Pennsylvania, Connnecticut, Virgnia, West Virginia) for which salary data can be obtained. We believe this sample represents
essentially the entire population of academics in these 139 institutions, and that these institutions represent nearly all public PhD
granting institutions in these states.
Sampling strategy Our approach has been to obtain what amounts to essentially the population of academics from public PhD granting institutions in these
eight states, rather than a sample of these individuals. These states represent more than 41% of the US population, but there could be
an issue of how representative these institutions are of the broader US and world population of academics.
Data collection Data were obtained from several archival sources. One key source of data is from a consulting firm that has aggregated performance
data for all academics employed in PhD-granting institutions in the US. The other data sources are US state comptroller offices or
individual academic institutions which have provided us salary data in response to Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) requests for data.
Timing The data were all assembled in 2018-2019, but encompass archival data from 1997 to 2017.
Data exclusions In general we did not exclude data except clearly described winsorizing, a common strategy dealing with salary data to account for
outliers. As reported in the paper, we drop observations with missing data only when these data are required for the specific analysis
performed. We also test all of our results with a restricted sample that has all data required for all analyses.
Non-participation This question is not relevant for our analysis. All data was publicly available, though extraordinarily difficult to compile. Thus, there was
no option to opt out or in.
Randomization This question is also not relevant as we are dealing essentially with the population of relevant individuals and not a sample.
